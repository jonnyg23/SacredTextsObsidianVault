 <p><a name="page_xxiv"><font size="1" color="green">p. xxiv</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">POSTSCRIPTUM.</h3>  
 <p>At the last moment of going to press I have chanced upon three remarkable variants of the <i>swastika</i> which the Count Goblet d’Alviella wishes me to reproduce here. The first is from a sepulchral stone at Meigle in Perthshire, and the second, which is a sinister <i>swastika</i>, from a Cross at St. Vigeans in Forfarshire.</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img__02400"></a>
 <span align="CENTER">
 <a href="img/_02400.jpg"><img src="tn/_02400.jpg"><br><font size="-3">Click to enlarge</font></a></span></p>  
 <p>Both these are illustrated in Stuart's <i>Sculptured Stones of Scotland</i>. The third is from one of the old Mahometan buildings of the Mo(n)gol period at Lahore.</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img__02401"></a>
 <span align="CENTER">
 <a href="img/_02401.jpg"><img src="tn/_02401.jpg"><br><font size="-3">Click to enlarge</font></a></span></p>  
 <p>This is also ignorantly rendered as the inauspicious <i>suwastika;</i> and twisted into a legend, which I read as, <i>ya Fattah</i>, "O Opener," "Beginner," "Leader," "Victorious," "Conqueror," and so forth.</p>  
 <p align="right"><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Geo. B.</span></p>
