 <p> </p>  
 <p><small>THIS poem was obtained in 1891 from Malcolm Macmillan, crofter, Grimnis, Benbecula. Macmillan was then an old man. He heard this and many other poems when a boy from old people who, when evicted in Uist, emigrated to Prince Edward's Island, Nova Scotia, Cape Breton, and other parts of the Canadian Dominion, and <a name="page_163"><font size="1" color="green">p. 163</font></a> to Australia. These old people took great quantities of traditional Gaelic lore with them to their new homes, some of which still lingers among their descendants. Many original and translated songs of the Highlands and Islands are sung among these settlers, whose hearts still yearn towards their motherland.</small></p>

 <p>CHAIDH Eosai is   Mairi<br>   Chon aireamh a suas,<br>   ’S chaidh eoin an geall caithream<br>   Ann an caille nan cuach.</p>   
 <p>Bha ’n   dithis a siubhal slighe,<br>   Gon a ranuig iad coille tiugh,<br>   Is anns a choille bha miosan<br>   Bha co dearg ris na subh.</p>   
 <p>Sin an t-am an   robh ise torrach,<br>   Anns an robh i giulan nigh nan gras,<br>   Is ghabh i miann air na miosan<br>   Bha air sliosrach an aigh.</p>   
 <p>Is labhair   Mairi ri Eosai,<br>   Le guth malda, miamh,<br>   'Tabhair miosan domh, Eosai,<br>   Gon caisg mi mo mhiann.'</p>   
 <p>Is labhair   Eosai ri Mairi,<br>   ’S an cradh cruaidh na chom,<br>   'Bheir mi ’uit miosan, a Mhairi,<br>   Ach co is athair dha d’ throm?'</p>   </td>   <td valign="top" align="top">   
 <p> </p>   </td>   <td valign="top" align="top">   
 <p>JOSEPH and Mary   went<br>   To the numbering up,<br>   And the birds began chorusing<br>   In the woods of the turtle-doves.</p>   
 <p>The two were   walking the way,<br>   Till they reached a thick wood,<br>   And in the wood there was fruit<br>   Which was as red as the rasp.</p>   
 <p>That was the   time when she was great,<br>   That she was carrying the King of grace,<br>   And she took a desire for the fruit<br>   That was growing on the gracious slope.</p>   
 <p>Then spoke Mary   to Joseph,<br>   In a voice low and sweet,<br>   'Give to me of the fruit, Joseph,<br>   That I may quench my desire.'</p>   
 <p>And Joseph   spoke to Mary,<br>   And the hard pain in his breast,<br>   'I will give thee of the fruit, Mary,<br>   But who is the father of thy burthen?'</p>   </td>  </tr>  <tr>   <td valign="top" align="top">

 <p>Sin ’d   uair labhair an Leanabh,<br>   A mach as a bru,<br>   'Lub a sios gach geug aluinn,<br>   Gon caisg mo Mhathair a ruth.'</p>   
 <p>’S o   ’n mheanglan is airde,<br>   Chon a mheanglan is isde,<br>   Lub iad a sios gon a glun,<br>   ’S ghabh Mairi dhe na miosan<br>   Ann am fearann fiosraidh a ruin.</p>   
 <p>An sin thuirt   Eosai ri Mairi,<br>   ’S e lan aithreachais trom,<br>   'Is ann air a ghiulan a tasa,<br>   Righ na glorach ’s nan grasa.<br>   Beannaicht thu, Mhairi,<br>        Measg mnai gach fonn.<br>   Beannaicht thu, Mhairi,<br>        Measg mnai gach fonn.'</p>   </td>   <td valign="top" align="top">   
 <p> </p>   </td>   <td valign="top" align="top">   
 <p>Then it was   that the Babe spoke,<br>   From out of her womb,<br>   'Bend ye down every beautiful bough,<br>   That my Mother may quench her desire.'</p>   
 <p>And from the   bough that was highest,<br>   To the bough that was lowest,<br>   They all bent down to her knee,<br>   And Mary partook of the fruit<br>   In her loved land of prophecy.</p>   
 <p>Then Joseph   said to Mary,<br>   And he full of heavy contrition,<br>   'It is carrying Him thou art,<br>   The King of glory and of grace.<br>   Blessed art thou, Mary,<br>        Among the women of all lands.<br>   Blessed art thou, Mary,<br>        Among the women of all lands.'</p>   </td>  </tr> </table>  </div>
