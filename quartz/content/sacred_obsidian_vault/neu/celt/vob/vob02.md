 <p><a name="page_2"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 2</font></a></p>   <h3 align="center">The Voyage <a name="fr_6"></a><a href="#fn_6"><font size="1">1</font></a> of Bran son of   Febal, and his Expedition <a name="fr_7"></a><a href="#fn_7"><font size="1">2</font></a> here below</h3>   
 <p>1. ’TWAS   fifty quatrains the woman from unknown lands sang on the floor of the house   to Bran son of Febal, when the royal house was full of kings, who knew not whence   the woman had come, since the ramparts were closed.</p>   
 <p>2. This is the   beginning of the story. One day, in the neighbourhood of his stronghold, Bran   went about alone, when he heard music behind him. As often as he looked back,   ’twas still behind him the music was. At last he fell asleep at the   music, such was its sweetness. When he awoke from his sleep, he saw close by   him a branch <a name="fr_8"></a><a href="#fn_8"><font size="1">3</font></a> of</p>

 <p>And she said:</p>

 <p>5. 'A delight   of the eyes, a glorious range,<br>   Is the plain on which the hosts hold games:<br>   Coracle contends against chariot<br>   In southern Mag Findargat. <a name="fr_15"></a><a href="#fn_15"><font size="1">7</font></a></p>

 <p>6. 'Feet of   white bronze under it<br>   Glittering through beautiful ages. <a name="fr_16"></a><a href="#fn_16"><font size="1">1</font></a><br>   Lovely land throughout the world's age,<br>   On which the many blossoms drop.</p>   
 <p>7. 'An ancient tree   there is with blossoms,<br>   On which birds call <a name="fr_17"></a><a href="#fn_17"><font size="1">2</font></a> to the Hours. <a name="fr_18"></a><a href="#fn_18"><font size="1">3</font></a><br>   ’Tis in harmony it is their wont<br>   To call together every Hour.</p>   
 <p>8. 'Splendours   of every colour glisten<br>   Throughout the gentle-voiced plains.<br>   Joy is known, ranked around music,<br>   In southern Mag Argatnél. <a name="fr_19"></a><a href="#fn_19"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>   
 <p>9. 'Unknown is   wailing or treachery <a name="fr_20"></a><a href="#fn_20"><font size="1">5</font></a><br>   In the familiar cultivated land,<br>   There is nothing rough or harsh, <a name="fr_21"></a><a href="#fn_21"><font size="1">6</font></a><br>   But sweet music striking on the ear.</p>   
 <p>10. 'Without   grief, without sorrow, without death,<br>   Without any sickness, without debility, <a name="fr_22"></a><a href="#fn_22"><font size="1">7</font></a><br>   That is the sign of Emain <a name="fr_23"></a><a href="#fn_23"><font size="1">8</font></a>--<br>   Uncommon is an equal marvel.</p>

 <p>11. 'A beauty   of a wondrous land,<br>   Whose aspects are lovely,<br>   Whose view is a fair country,<br>   Incomparable is its haze.</p>

 <p>15. 'Yellow   golden steeds are on the sward there,<br>   Other steeds with crimson hue,<br>   Others with wool upon their backs<br>   Of the hue of heaven all-blue.</p>

 <p>16. At sunrise   there will come<br>   A fair man illumining level lands;<br>   He rides upon the fair sea-washed <a name="fr_31"></a><a href="#fn_31"><font size="1">1</font></a> plain,<br>   He stirs the ocean till it is blood.</p>   
 <p>17. 'A host   will come across the clear sea,<br>   To the land they show their rowing;<br>   Then they row to the conspicuous stone,<br>   From which arise a hundred strains.</p>   
 <p>18. 'It sings a   strain unto the host<br>   Through long ages, it is not sad,<br>   Its music swells <a name="fr_32"></a><a href="#fn_32"><font size="1">2</font></a> with choruses of hundreds--<br>   They look for neither decay nor death.</p>

 <p>31. Thereupon the   woman went from them, while they knew not whither she went. <a name="fr_45"></a><a href="#fn_45"><font size="1">1</font></a> And she   took her branch with her. The branch sprang from Bran's hand into the hand of   the woman, nor was there strength in Bran's hand to hold the branch.</p>   
 <p>32. Then on the   morrow Bran went upon the sea. The number of his men was three companies of   nine. One of his foster-brothers and mates <a name="fr_46"></a><a href="#fn_46"><font size="1">2</font></a> was set over each of the   three companies of nine. When he had been at sea two days and two nights, he   saw a man in a chariot coming towards him over the sea. That man also sang   thirty <a name="fr_47"></a><a href="#fn_47"><font size="1">3</font></a> other quatrains to him, and made himself known to him, <a name="fr_48"></a><a href="#fn_48"><font size="1">4</font></a>   and said that he was Manannan the son of Ler, and said that it was upon him   to go to Ireland after long ages, and that a son would be born to him, even   Mongan son of Fiachna--that was the name which would be upon him.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_93"></a><a href="vob02.htm#fr_93">30:1</a> Zimmer,   adopting the corrupt reading of <i>R</i> (<i>na mná</i> instead of <i>nammá</i>) renders: 'sondern   blickte die frauen an.' No women have been mentioned.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_94"></a><a href="vob02.htm#fr_94">30:2</a> Zimmer   renders 'ehepaar.' But there is no reason for being so particular.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_95"></a><a href="vob02.htm#fr_95">30:3</a> For   this use of <i>écmaing</i> = 'it really   was,' cf. Ir. Texte iii. p. 17:</p>   
 <p></p><dir><dir>'Andarlium ba   slúaiged fer,<br>   Góidil co ler iar n-gail gairg:<br>   eccmuing ba rí. Midi máir<br>   doluid do dáim óenaig aird.'</dir>   
 <p>'Methought it   was a hosting of men,<br>   Gaels in numbers after fierce prowess;<br>   But it was the king of great Meath,<br>   Going to the company of a noble gathering.'</p></dir>   
 <p></p>
 <p><a name="fn_96"></a><a href="vob02.htm#fr_96">30:4</a> <i>i.e.</i> every man found in his food and   drink the taste that he especially desired, a common incident in Irish   story-telling.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_97"></a><a href="vob02.htm#fr_97">32:1</a> He was the   hero of a tale, the title of which figures in the list of sagas in LL. p. 170   b as <i>Echtra Nectain maic Alfroinn</i>.   This tale is not now known to exist; it probably contained the incidents here   narrated.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_98"></a><a href="vob02.htm#fr_98">32:2</a>   O’Curry, MS. Mat. p. 477, note 15, says that there are two places of   this name--one in the west of Kerry, the other, now called Staoove or Shruve   Brin, at the entrance to Lough Foyle, a little to the south of Inishowen   Head. As the ancient Irish imagined Mag Mell to be in the south or south-west   of Ireland (see Stokes, Rev. Celt. xv. p. 438), it seems natural that Bran   coming from there should arrive at a place in Kerry. Otherwise, from Bran's   connection with Lough Foyle, so called from his father Febal, the latter   place might seem to be meant. See its dindsenches in Rev. Celt xv. p. 450,   where Srub Brain is said to mean 'Raven's Stream.' Stokes thinks that this   Srub Brain is the place in Donegal; but, considering that numbers 50 to 53 of   the Rennes Dindsenchas all refer to places in Kerry, I believe the West Kerry   place is meant.</p>
