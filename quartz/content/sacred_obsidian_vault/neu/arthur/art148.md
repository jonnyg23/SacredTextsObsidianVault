 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">An Arthurian Miscellany at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <center> <h2 align="CENTER">THE LAMENT OF SIR ECTOR DE MARIS</h2>  <h3 align="CENTER">by</h3>  <h2 align="CENTER">ERNEST RHYS</h2>  </center>  <br>                                   I<br> <br>  Thro' waste and steep, this seven year<br>    Sir Ector seeks Sir Launcelot,<br>  His flower of knights, his brother dear.<br>  <br>  From Calabre to Gwent, he sought;<br>    Last home to Joyous Garde, he hight<br>  By roads that once Sir Launcelot brought.<br>  <br> <br>                                 II<br> <br>  What lights are they that burn all night,<br>    Within the quire of Joyous Garde?<br>  Sir Ector draws rein at the sight.<br>  <br>  What songs are they to heaven's Lord?<br>    What singing men, that sing and pause?<br>  Sir Ector doffs his helm and sword.<br>  <br>  They sing no song of "Deo Laus":<br>    Sir Ector de Maris knows them not:<br>  Yet, well they know his thrice-scarr'd brows.<br>  <br>  "There lies they brother, Launcelot,"<br>    Sir Bors says, standing by the bier,<br>  "That thou this seven year hath sought!"<br>  <br>  Through waste and steep, this seven year,<br>    He sought; and now he swoons to see<br>  That face he sought lie on the bier.<br>  <br>  The kneeling knights rise silently:<br>    They bear him forth to the cool night-air<br>  The summer night drinks from the sea.<br>  <br> <br>                                 III<br> <br>  When Ector woke, what anguish there<br>    He made, what pity in him wrought:<br>  His cries were more than heart can bear.<br>  <br>  "Oh Launcelot, oh Launcelot,<br>    Of Christian knight, the flower and head,<br>  And there thou liest, Sir Launcelot.<br>  <br>  "I will dare say," Sir Ector said,<br>    "Of all men thou wert courtliest,<br>  And the truest knight ever love bested.<br>  <br>  "Of a sinful man, thou wert the best,<br>    That ever loved; and of all, did ride<br>  The lists full-arm'd, the goodliest.<br>  <br>  "And thou were first in the battle-tide<br>    To meet the spears; yet, the gentlest man <br>  That sate in hall by the ladies' side.<br>  <br>  "And thou wert always the meekest one<br>    That served thy lord in Camelot:<br>  And the sternest knight, since wars began,<br>  <br>  "Put spear in rest, or ever fought<br>    With thy mortal foe: and there, how low,<br>  How low, thou liest, Sir Launcelot!"<br>  <br>                  *                *                *<br>  <br>  The sorrow then, no man can know:<br>  Weeping, complaining, without end,<br>  For the noblest knight, the truest friend,<br>  That ever into the grave did go.<br> <br>
