 <span class="splashtitle">Sources of Lord of the Rings</span>
 </td>
 </tr>
 </table>
 </center>
 
 
 <hr>
 
 <p>
 J.R.R. Tolkiens' Middle Earth did not come out of thin air.
 Tolkien, an academic linguist, drew on the following
 source materials to inspire his world-building exercise.
 The texts presented at this site are complete and in some
 cases in the original languages.
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <a href="../neu/kveng/index.htm">The Kalevala. [English]</a><br>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <a href="../neu/kvfin/index.htm">The Kalevala. [Finnish]</a><br>
 This Finnish saga, replete with battling wizards and heroes, quests and
 magical objects has been cited as one of the early inspirations
 for Tolkien.  The languages of Middle Earth also have more than a
 passing resemblance to Finnish.
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <a href="../neu/poe/index.htm">The Elder (Poetic) Edda.</a><br>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <a href="../neu/pre/index.htm">The Younger (Prose) Edda.</a><br>
 These Icelandic collections of epic poetry are key sources for
 the study of Norse mythology.
 Our plane of existence is called 'Middle Earth' in this mythology.
 Tolkien also took many of the names of key
 characters from this text; particularly one sequence early on in
 the <a href="../neu/poe/poe03.htm">Voluspo</a>, including Gandalf, and
 all of the Dwarves from <cite>the Hobbit</cite>.
 Also found in the Eddas is the forest of Myrkwood; Bilbo's party
 traversed the vast Mirkwood forest in the Hobbit.
 </p><p>
 
 </p><p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <a href="../neu/ron/index.htm">Wagner's Ring of the Niblung</a><br>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <a href="../neu/vlsng/index.htm">The Volsung Saga</a><br>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <a href="../neu/nblng/index.htm">The Nibelungenlied</a><br>
 The Volsung saga is the Icelandic version of the 
 story which was later re-told in the Germanic Nibelungenlied.
 This was the basis for Wagner's Ring cycle.
 The cursed ring of doom is likewise a central theme of Tolkiens' mythology.
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="../cdshop/index.htm"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0" alt="this text is on the sacred-texts CD-ROM"></a>
 <a href="../neu/heim/index.htm">Heimskringla or The Chronicle of 
 the Kings of Norway</a><br>
 by Snorri Sturlson. tr. Samuel Laing [1844]<br>
 
 Gandalf is not only mentioned in the Voluspo,
 but in the Heimskringla Sagas:
 Gandalf was the name of the last king to rule over Alfheim,
 He was killed by Harald Harfagra in the year 866.
 Gandalf gets mentioned in 
 <a href="../neu/heim/02ynglga.htm">The Ynglinga Saga</a>,
 <a href="../neu/heim/03halfdn.htm">Halfdan, the Black Saga</a>,
 and
 <a href="../neu/heim/04harfgr.htm">Harald Harfager's Saga</a>.
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="../neu/beo/index.htm">Beowulf [Modern English]</a></span><br>
 </span>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="../neu/asbeo.htm">Beowulf [Anglo Saxon]</a></span><br>
 <span class="c_b">The original monster story, in the original Old English</span>.
 Tolkien was a distinguished scholar of Middle and Old English.
 The Beowulf saga is one of the treasures of this branch of literature,
 and Grendel, the monster of this tale, is a prototype for many of the
 darker creatures that populate Middle Earth.
 </span>
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="../neu/celt/mab/index.htm">The Mabinogion</a></span><br>
 tr. Lady <span class="c_a">Charlotte Guest</span> [<span class="c_d">1877</span>]<br>
 <span class="c_b">The Original Red Book.</span>
 Among the sources for Tokien's languages was Welsh, and the Welsh
 stories of the 'Red Book of Hergest' appear to have also
 had an influence on the narratives
 of Tolkien's fictional 'Red Book of Westmarch'.
 </span>
 </p>
 
 
 <h3>Modern Fantasy</h3>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <a href="../neu/eng/fq/index.htm">The Faerie Queene</a>,
 by Edmund Spenser [1596]<br>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <a href="../neu/eng/sfq/index.htm">Stories from the Faerie Queene</a>,
 by Mary Macleod [1916]<br>
 <span class="c_b">The Faerie Queene is one of the wellsprings of modern Fantasy writing.</span>
 Spenser, like Tolkien, did not simply retell classical or medieval stories.
 He used similar themes and elements but put the pieces together
 in an entirely new way, constructing an invented reality with a consistent
 internal logic.
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <img src="../new2.gif">
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="../neu/dun/index.htm">The Works of Lord Dunsany</a></span><br>
 by <span class="c_a">Edward Plunkett, Lord Dunsany</span> [<span class="c_d">1905-22</span>]<br>
 <span class="c_b">Lord Dunsany paved the way for Tolkien with his delightful internally consistent fantasy worlds.</span>
 </span>
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="wbw/index.htm">The Wood Beyond the World</a></span><br>
 by <span class="c_a">William Morris</span> [<span class="c_d">1894</span>]<br>
 <span class="c_b">The Wood Beyond the World is one of Morris' masterpieces which he originally published using hand-set type.</span>
 One of the recognized forerunners of Tolkien was <a href="../neu/morris/index.htm">William Morris</a>,
 skilled artist, pioneering Socialist, translator of Icelandic sagas,
 and fantasy writer.
 </span>
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="two/index.htm">The Worm Ouroboros</a></span><br>
 by <span class="c_a">E. R. Eddison</span> [<span class="c_d">1922</span>]<br>
 <span class="c_b">"The greatest and most convincing writer of
 'invented worlds' that I have read&amp;quot</span> -- [<a href="two/index.htm#fn1">J.R.R. Tolkien *</a>]
 </span>
 </p>
