 <p><a name="an_ch_67"></a></p>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER LXVII.</h3>  
 <p>It was once generally affirmed that the greater the Self the more impossible it was to compare it with anything else. <a name="fr_221"></a><a href="#fn_221"><font size="1">1</font></a> Now it is just this greatness which makes it incomparable; should, however, a comparison be demanded, it would have to be described as the eternal, which is imperceptible. Now the Self has three treasures, to which it clings as to inseparables—the first is compassion, <a name="fr_222"></a><a href="#fn_222"><font size="1">2</font></a> the second, self-restraint, the third, nowhere venturing to claim precedence.</p>  
 <p>Compassionate—therefore irresistible! <a name="fr_223"></a><a href="#fn_223"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>Self-restrained—therefore enlarged!</p>  
 <p>Nowhere venturing to claim precedence—therefore efficient! <a name="fr_224"></a><a href="#fn_224"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>Now-a-days men cast compassion on one side, yet expect to be irresistible! They discard self-restraint, yet look for enlargement; They forget to retire, yet demand precedence!—this is death. <a name="fr_225"></a><a href="#fn_225"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>  
 <p>As regards compassion, rely on it when you would contend, and you will overcome; rely on it when you would protect, and you will succeed.</p>
