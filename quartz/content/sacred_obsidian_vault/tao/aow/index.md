 <p><small><cite>If you know neither the enemy nor yourself, you will succumb in every battle</cite>.--p. 25, II:18.</small></p>
 <p><small><cite>Anger may in time change to gladness;<br>
 Vexation may be succeeded by content.<br>
 But a kingdom that has once been destroyed<br>
 Can never come again into being;<br>
 Nor can the dead ever be brought back to life.<br>
 Hence the enlightened ruler is heedful,<br>
 And the good general full of caution.<br>
 This is the way to keep a country at peace and an army intact</cite>.<br>
    --p. 159, XII:20-22.
 </small></p>
 <p>
 Attributed to the legendary Chinese general,
 Sun Tzu, the <cite>Art of War</cite> also has a strong spiritual component.
 The work blends Taoism with the art of statecraft,
 applying concepts such as 'doing without doing' to the battlefield.
 Traditionally written during the Spring and Autumn period (722-481 BCE),
 others date it, based on historical and internal evidence,
 to the Warring States Period (403-221 BCE).
 Sun Tzu is traditionally identified with an historical figure who lived
 from 544-496 BCE, a general of the King of Wu.
 Since it first came to the attention of Westerners in the 19th century,
 the <cite>Art of War</cite> has influenced leaders from Napoleon to Norman Schwarzkopf.
 Mao tse Tung attributed it as a source of his thinking on
 guerilla warfare.
 The work is considered a masterpiece of military theory.
 </p>
