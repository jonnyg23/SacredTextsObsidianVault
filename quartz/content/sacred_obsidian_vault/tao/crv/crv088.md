 <h1 align="CENTER">COMMENTS AND ALTERNATIVE READINGS.</h1>
 <h3 align="CENTER">CHAPTER I.</h3>
 <p>The phrase '<i>yiu ming</i>, "having name" (or simply <i>ming</i>, "name") means that which the definition of a name involves, and as such the term represents the actualized types of things. However <i>wu ming</i>, "not name" or "the Unnamable," corresponds to Plato's conception of the prototype of things before they have been actualized. Lao-tze speaks with reverence of the Unnamable, <a name="fr_21"></a><a href="#fn_21"><font size="1">1</font></a> which closely corresponds to the "Ineffable" of Western mystics.</p>
 <p>The words "these two things" apparently refer to the Unnamable and the Namable.</p>
 <p>What Lao-tze calls "the Name" or "the</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_132"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 132</font></a></p>
 <p><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font> Namable" is in Spinoza's language <i>natura naturata</i>, while "the Unnamable" is <i>natura naturans</i>. In either system the two are one; they are two aspects of one and the same thing which in Lao-tze's taoism is the Tao and in Spinoza's cosmotheism is God as the eternal substance.</p>
