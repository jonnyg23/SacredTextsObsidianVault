 <p><a name="page_28"><font size="1" color="green">p. 28</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">THIRD LECTURE,</h3>  <h6 align="center">CALLED</h6>  <h6 align="center">HOT AND COLD.</h6>  <h4 align="center">FIRST LESSON.</h4>  
 <p>The unwise sleep, the sages always wake. Know, that in this world the (cause of) misery <a name="fr_153"></a><a href="#fn_152"><font size="1">1</font></a> brings forth evil consequences! Knowing the course of the world <a name="fr_154"></a><a href="#fn_153"><font size="1">2</font></a>, one should cease from violent acts. He who correctly possesses <a name="fr_155"></a><a href="#fn_154"><font size="1">3</font></a> these (sensual perceptions), viz. sounds, and colours, and smells, and tastes, and touches (1), who self-possessed, wise, just, chaste, with right comprehension understands the world, he is to be called a sage, one who knows the law, and righteous. He knows the connection of the whirl (of births) and the current (of sensation with love and hate). Not minding heat and cold, equanimous against pleasure and pain, the Nirgrantha does not feel the austerity of penance. Waking and free from hostility, a wise man, thou liberatest (thyself and others) from the miseries. (2)</p>  
 <p>But a man always benighted, subject to old age and death, does not know the law. Seeing living beings suffering, earnestly enter a religious life <a name="fr_156"></a><a href="#fn_155"><font size="1">4</font></a>. Considering this, O prudent one, look!</p>  
 <p></p><dir>Knowing the misery that results from action,<br> The deluded and careless one returns to life;</dir>  
 <p>

 <p><a name="page_29"><font size="1" color="green">p. 29</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><dir><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Disregarding sounds and colours, upright,<br> Avoiding Mâra one is liberated from death <a name="fr_157"></a><a href="sbe2217.htm#fn_156"><font size="1">1</font></a>.</dir>  
 <p>Carefully abstaining from pleasures and ceasing from bad works he is a hero, guarding himself, who is grounded in knowledge <a name="fr_158"></a><a href="sbe2217.htm#fn_157"><font size="1">2</font></a>. (3) He who knows the violence done for the sake of special objects, knows what is free from violence <a name="fr_159"></a><a href="sbe2217.htm#fn_158"><font size="1">3</font></a>; he who knows what is free from violence, knows the violence done for special objects. For him who is without karman, there is no appellation <a name="fr_160"></a><a href="sbe2217.htm#fn_159"><font size="1">4</font></a>. The condition of living beings arises from karman.</p>  
 <p>Examining karman and the root of karman, viz. killing <a name="fr_161"></a><a href="sbe2217.htm#fn_160"><font size="1">5</font></a>, examining (it) and adopting its contrary <a name="fr_162"></a><a href="sbe2217.htm#fn_161"><font size="1">6</font></a>, he is not seen by both ends <a name="fr_163"></a><a href="sbe2217.htm#fn_162"><font size="1">7</font></a>. Knowing this, a wise man who knows the world and has cast off the idea of the world, should prudently conquer the obstructions to righteousness <a name="fr_164"></a><a href="sbe2217.htm#fn_163"><font size="1">8</font></a>. Thus I say. (4)</p>
