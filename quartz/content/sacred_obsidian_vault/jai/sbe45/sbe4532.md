 <p><a name="page_174"><font size="1" color="green">p. 174</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">THIRTIETH LECTURE.</h3>  <h6 align="center">THE ROAD OF PENANCE.</h6>  
 <p>Now hear with concentrated mind, how a monk destroys by austerities the bad Karman which he had acquired by love and hatred. (1)</p>  
 <p>By abstaining 1. from destroying life; 2. from lying; 3. from taking anything which is not given; 4. from all sexual indulgence; 5. from having any property; and 6. from eating at night, the soul becomes free from Âsravas <a name="fr_466"></a><a href="#fn_464"><font size="1">1</font></a>. (2)</p>  
 <p>By possessing the five Samitis and the three Guptis, by freedom from passions, by subduing the senses, by vanquishing conceit <a name="fr_467"></a><a href="#fn_465"><font size="1">2</font></a>, and by avoiding delusions, the soul becomes free from Âsravas. (3)</p>  
 <p>Hear attentively how a monk destroys (the Karman) acquired by love and hatred in the absence of the above-mentioned (virtues). (4)</p>  
 <p>As a large tank, when its supply of water has been stopped, gradually dries up by the consumption of the water and by evaporation, so the Karman of a monk, which he acquired in millions <a name="fr_468"></a><a href="#fn_466"><font size="1">3</font></a> of births, is annihilated by austerities, if there is no influx of bad Karman. (5, 6)</p>  
 <p>Austerities are of two kinds: external and internal;</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_175"><font size="1" color="green">p. 175</font></a></p>  
 <p>external austerities are of six kinds, and internal are of six kinds <a name="fr_469"></a><a href="#fn_467"><font size="1">1</font></a>. (7)</p>  
 <p>External austerities are:</p>  
 <p>1. ana<i>s</i>ana, fasting; 2. avamôdarikâ, abstinence <a name="fr_470"></a><a href="#fn_468"><font size="1">2</font></a>; 3. bhikshâ<i>k</i>aryâ, collecting alms; 4. rasaparityâga, abstention from dainty food; 5. kâyaklê<i>s</i>a, mortification of the flesh; 6. sa<i>m</i>lînatâ, taking care of one's limbs <a name="fr_471"></a><a href="#fn_469"><font size="1">3</font></a>. (8)</p>  
 <p>1. Fasting is of two kinds: <i>a</i>. itvara, temporary, and <i>b</i>. mara<i>n</i>akâla, fasting which precedes, and ends with death. Temporary fasting is either such in which a desire (for food) is present, or such in which no such desire exists. (9)</p>  
 <p><i>a</i>. The temporary fasting is briefly of six kinds: 1. in the form of a line <a name="fr_472"></a><a href="#fn_470"><font size="1">4</font></a>; 2. in the form of a square; 3. in the form of a cube; 4. of a sixth power; 5. of a twelfth power; 6. of any arrangement. Temporary fasting (can be practised) for different objects which one has in mind. (10, 11)</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_176"><font size="1" color="green">p. 176</font></a></p>  
 <p><i>b</i>. Fasting which is to precede death, is of two kinds with regard to the motions of the body: with change (of position) and without change. (12)</p>  
 <p>And again it is twofold: admitting of relief <a name="fr_473"></a><a href="#fn_471"><font size="1">1</font></a>, or not; one may either leave the place (which one has chosen to die in), or not leave it; in both cases one may not take any food. (13)</p>  
 <p>2. Abstinence is briefly of five kinds: with regard to <i>a</i>. substance; <i>b</i>. place; <i>c</i>. time; <i>d</i>. state of mind; <i>e</i>. development. (14)</p>  
 <p><i>a</i>. He who takes less food than he usually does <a name="fr_474"></a><a href="#fn_472"><font size="1">2</font></a>, in the extreme case but one mouthful, performs abstinence with regard to substance. (15)</p>  
 <p><i>b</i>. (Place means) a village, a scotfree town <a name="fr_475"></a><a href="#fn_473"><font size="1">3</font></a>, a capital, a camp of merchants <a name="fr_476"></a><a href="#fn_474"><font size="1">4</font></a>, a mine, a settlement of a wild tribe <a name="fr_477"></a><a href="#fn_475"><font size="1">5</font></a>, a place with an earth wall <a name="fr_478"></a><a href="#fn_476"><font size="1">6</font></a>, a poor town <a name="fr_479"></a><a href="#fn_477"><font size="1">7</font></a>, a town with a harbour <a name="fr_480"></a><a href="#fn_478"><font size="1">8</font></a>, a large town <a name="fr_481"></a><a href="#fn_479"><font size="1">9</font></a>, an isolated town <a name="fr_482"></a><a href="#fn_480"><font size="1">10</font></a>, and an open town <a name="fr_483"></a><a href="#fn_481"><font size="1">11</font></a>. (16)</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_177"><font size="1" color="green">p. 177</font></a></p>  
 <p>In a hermitage, a vihâra <a name="fr_484"></a><a href="#fn_482"><font size="1">1</font></a>, a halting-place for procession <a name="fr_485"></a><a href="#fn_483"><font size="1">2</font></a>, a resting-place for travellers <a name="fr_486"></a><a href="#fn_484"><font size="1">3</font></a>, a station of herdsmen, a camp on high ground, a caravan's camp, a fortified place of refuge. (17)</p>  
 <p>In gardens, on roads, in houses--all this is meant by place. In these and similar places he may (wander about). In this way he performs abstinence with regard to place. (18)</p>  
 <p>1. pê<i>t</i>â, 2. ardhapê<i>t</i>â, 3. gômûtrikâ, 4. pataṅgavîthikâ, 5. <i>s</i>ambûkâvartta, 6. âyata<i>m</i>-gatvâ-pratyâgata <a name="fr_487"></a><a href="#fn_485"><font size="1">4</font></a>. (19)</p>  
 <p><i>c</i>. Abstinence with reference to time (is observed by him) who goes about in that time of the four Paurushîs of the day (which he selects for that purpose). (20)</p>  
 <p>Or if he collects alms in a part of the third Paurushî, or in its last quarter, then he observes abstinence with reference to time. (21)</p>  
 <p><i>d</i>. Abstinence with reference to state of mind (is observed by him) who accepts alms from a woman or man, from an adorned or unadorned person, from one of any age or dress, of any temper or colour: if that person does not change his disposition or condition <a name="fr_488"></a><a href="#fn_486"><font size="1">5</font></a>. (22, 23)</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_178"><font size="1" color="green">p. 178</font></a></p>  
 <p><i>e</i>. A monk who observes abstinence according to the particulars which have been enumerated with regard to substance, place, time, and state of mind, observes abstinence with regard to development <a name="fr_489"></a><a href="#fn_487"><font size="1">1</font></a> too. (24)</p>  
 <p>3. With regard to collecting alms there are the eight principal ways <a name="fr_490"></a><a href="#fn_488"><font size="1">2</font></a> how to collect them; the seven êsha<i>n</i>âs (or modes of begging) and other self-imposed restrictions. (25)</p>  
 <p>4. Abstention from dainty food means abstention from such highly nourishing <a name="fr_491"></a><a href="#fn_489"><font size="1">3</font></a> food and drink as milk, curds, ghee, &amp;c. (26)</p>  
 <p>5. Mortification of the flesh consists in the different postures as Virâsana, &amp;c., which benefit the soul, and which are difficult to perform. (27)</p>  
 <p>6. Using unfrequented lodgings and beds consists in living and sleeping in separate and</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_179"><font size="1" color="green">p. 179</font></a></p>  
 <p>unfrequented places where there are neither women nor cattle. (28)</p>  
 <p>Thus external austerities have been briefly explained; I shall now explain internal austerities in due order. (29)</p>  
 <p>Internal austerities are:</p>  
 <p>1. prâya<i>s</i><i>k</i>itta, expiation of sins;</p>  
 <p>2. vi<i>n</i>aya, politeness;</p>  
 <p>3. vaiyâv<i>ri</i>tya, serving the Guru;</p>  
 <p>4. svâdhyâya, study;</p>  
 <p>5. dhyâna, meditation;</p>  
 <p>6. vyutsarga <a name="fr_492"></a><a href="#fn_490"><font size="1">1</font></a>, abandoning of the body. (30)</p>  
 <p>1. Expiation of sins is tenfold, what must be confessed <a name="fr_493"></a><a href="#fn_491"><font size="1">2</font></a>, &amp;c.; this is to be strictly observed by a monk; this is called expiation of sins. (31)</p>  
 <p>2. Politeness consists in rising (from one's seat), folding of the hands, offering of a seat, loving the Guru, and cordial obedience. (32)</p>  
 <p>3. There are ten <a name="fr_494"></a><a href="#fn_492"><font size="1">3</font></a> kinds of service, as serving the Â<i>k</i>ârya, &amp;c. <a name="fr_495"></a><a href="#fn_493"><font size="1">4</font></a>; doing service consists in giving one's assistance as well as one is able. (33)</p>  
 <p>4. Study is fivefold: 1. saying or learning one's lesson; 2. (questioning the teacher about it); 3. repetition; 4. pondering; 5. religious discourse. (34)</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_180"><font size="1" color="green">p. 180</font></a></p>  
 <p>5. Abstaining to meditate on painful and sinful things <a name="fr_496"></a><a href="#fn_494"><font size="1">1</font></a>, one should, with a collected mind, engage in pure meditations on the Law; this the wise call meditation. (35)</p>  
 <p>6. If a monk remains motionless when lying down, sitting, or standing upright, this is called abandoning of the body, which is the sixth kind (of internal austerities). (36)</p>  
 <p>If a sage truly performs these two kinds of austerities, he will soon be thoroughly released from the Circle of Births. (37)</p>  
 <p>Thus I say.</p>
