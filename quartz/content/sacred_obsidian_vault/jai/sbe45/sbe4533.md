 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>Jaina Sutras, Part II (SBE45)</i>, tr. by Hermann Jacobi, [1895], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">THIRTY-FIRST LECTURE.</h3>  <h6 align="center">MODE OF LIFE <a name="fr_497"></a><a href="#fn_495"><font size="1">2</font></a>.</h6>  
 <p>I shall declare the mode of life that benefits the soul; by practising it many souls have crossed the ocean of Sa<i>m</i>sâra. (1)</p>  
 <p>One should desist from one thing, and practise another: desist from neglect of self-control, and practise self-control. (2)</p>  
 <p>Love and hatred are two evils which produce bad</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_181"><font size="1" color="green">p. 181</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Karman; if a monk always avoids them, he will not stand within the circle (of transmigration). (3)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always avoids the thrice threefold hurtful, conceited, and delusive acts <a name="fr_498"></a><a href="#fn_496"><font size="1">1</font></a>, will not stand in the circle (of transmigration). (4)</p>  
 <p>A monk who well bears calamities produced by gods, animals, or men, will not stand, &amp;c. (5)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always avoids the (four) different kinds of praises <a name="fr_499"></a><a href="#fn_497"><font size="1">2</font></a>, passions, expressions (of the emotions) <a name="fr_500"></a><a href="#fn_498"><font size="1">3</font></a>, and (of the four) meditations the two sinful ones, will not stand, &amp;c. (6)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always exerts himself <a name="fr_501"></a><a href="#fn_499"><font size="1">4</font></a> with regard to the (five) vows, the (five) objects of sense, the (five) Samitis, and (five) actions <a name="fr_502"></a><a href="#fn_500"><font size="1">5</font></a>, will not stand, &amp;c. (7)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the six lê<i>s</i>yâs <a name="fr_503"></a><a href="#fn_501"><font size="1">6</font></a>, the six kinds of bodies, and the six (regular functions as) eating <a name="fr_504"></a><a href="#fn_502"><font size="1">7</font></a>, will not stand, &amp;c. (8)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the (seven) rules of accepting alms <a name="fr_505"></a><a href="#fn_503"><font size="1">8</font></a>, and the seven causes of danger (to other men) will not stand, &amp;c. (9)</p>

 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the (eight) objects of pride <a name="fr_507"></a><a href="#fn_504"><font size="1">1</font></a>, to that which protects his chastity <a name="fr_508"></a><a href="#fn_505"><font size="1">2</font></a>, and to the tenfold Law of the monks <a name="fr_509"></a><a href="#fn_506"><font size="1">3</font></a>. (10)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the (eleven) duties of the upâsakas, and the (twelve) duties of the bhikshus <a name="fr_510"></a><a href="#fn_507"><font size="1">4</font></a>, will not stand, &amp;c. (11)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the (thirteen) actions (productive of Karman), to the various (fourteen) kinds of living beings, and the (fifteen) places of punishment of the wicked <a name="fr_511"></a><a href="#fn_508"><font size="1">5</font></a>, will not  stand, &amp;c. (12)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the sixteen Gâthâs <a name="fr_512"></a><a href="#fn_509"><font size="1">6</font></a>, and to the (seventeen kinds of) neglect of self-control, will not stand, &amp;c. (13)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the (eighteen kinds of) continence, to the (nineteen) <i>g</i><i>ñ</i>âtâdhyayanas <a name="fr_513"></a><a href="#fn_510"><font size="1">7</font></a>, and the (twenty) cases for not concentrating one's thoughts, will not, &amp;c. (14)</p>

 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the twenty-one forbidden <a name="fr_514"></a><a href="#fn_511"><font size="1">1</font></a> actions, and the twenty-two troubles <a name="fr_515"></a><a href="#fn_512"><font size="1">2</font></a>, will not stand, &amp;c. (15)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the twenty-three (lectures of the) Sûtrak<i>ri</i>tâṅga, and to the gods whose number exceeds by an unit <a name="fr_516"></a><a href="#fn_513"><font size="1">3</font></a> (the number of the lectures of the Sûtrak<i>ri</i>tâṅga), will not stand, &amp;c. (t 6)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the twenty-five clauses <a name="fr_517"></a><a href="#fn_514"><font size="1">4</font></a>, and (to the recitation of the twenty-six) chapters of the Da<i>s</i>âs, &amp;c. <a name="fr_518"></a><a href="#fn_515"><font size="1">5</font></a>, will not stand, &amp;c. (17)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the (twenty-seven) virtues of the laity, and the (twenty-eight lectures of the) Prakalpa <a name="fr_519"></a><a href="#fn_516"><font size="1">6</font></a>, will not stand, &amp;c. 08)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the (twenty-nine) causes of wrong knowledge, and the (thirty) causes of delusion, will not stand, &amp;c. (19)</p>  
 <p>A monk who always exerts himself with regard to the (thirty-one) qualities of Siddhas, &amp;c., the (thirty-two)</p>

 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Yogas <a name="fr_520"></a><a href="#fn_517"><font size="1">1</font></a>, and thirty-three Â<i>s</i>âtanâs <a name="fr_521"></a><a href="#fn_518"><font size="1">2</font></a>, will not stand, &amp;c. (20)</p>  
 <p>A clever monk who always exerts himself with regard to the above-mentioned points, will soon be thoroughly released from the Circle of Births (21)</p>  
 <p>Thus I say.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_495"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_497">180:2</a> This lecture offers many difficulties to the translator, as it contains scarcely more than a dry list of articles of the <i>G</i>aina faith. To fully understand or interpret it would require an accurate knowledge of the complete religious system of the <i>G</i>ainas, to which we can lay no claim at present. The order in which the articles are given follows the number of their subdivisions. In some cases the number is not given in the Sûtra, but is supplied by the commentary.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_496"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_498">181:1</a> Compare XIX, 91, and XXX, 3. Hurtful acts (da<i>n</i><i>d</i>a) are threefold, as referring to thoughts, words, and acts; conceited acts (gârava), as pride of riches, of taste (rasa), and of pleasure or fashion (sâtâ); delusive acts (<i>s</i>alya), as mâyâ, nidâna, and mithyâdar<i>s</i>ana.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_497"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_506">181:2</a> Vikatthâ.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_498"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_500">181:3</a> Sa<i>m</i><i>g</i><i>ñ</i>â.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_499"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_501">181:4</a> Yatatê 'exerts himself;' supply 'to avoid, to know, or to do,' as the case may require.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_500"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_502">181:5</a> Kriyâ; they are: 1. kâyikî; 2. adhikara<i>n</i>ikî; 3. prâdvêshikî; 4. paritâpanikî, and 5. prâ<i>n</i>âtipâtikî.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_501"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_503">181:6</a> On the lê<i>s</i>yâs see Thirty-fourth Lecture, p. 196 ff.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_502"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_504">181:7</a> From the commentaries I learn two more of these six kâra<i>n</i>as: vêdana and vaiyâv<i>ri</i>tya. I cannot say which are the remaining three.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_503"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_505">181:8</a> They are enumerated in note  <a name="fr_506"></a><a href="#fn_497"><font size="1">2</font></a> on XXX, 25, <a href="sbe4532.htm#page_178">p. 178</a>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_504"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_507">182:1</a> Viz. caste, family, beauty, &amp;c.; see Sûtrak<i>ri</i>t. II, 2, 17.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_505"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_508">182:2</a> Brahmagupti. This is of nine kinds.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_506"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_509">182:3</a> Bhikshudharma. It consists of Nos. 46-49, 26, 27, of Lecture XXIX, truth, purity, poverty, and chastity.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_507"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_510">182:4</a> The details given in the commentary (Dêvêndra) partly differ from the description of the twelve duties of <i>S</i>râvakas, and the ten duties of Bhikshus given by Bhandarkar from the Kârttikêyânuprêkshâ, see his Report, p. 114 ff.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_508"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_511">182:5</a> Paramâdhârmika. My translation is based on the enumeration of fifteen words, among which the names of some well-known hells occur.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_509"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_512">182:6</a> The sixteen lectures of the first part of the Sûtrak<i>ri</i>tâṅga, the last of which is called Gâthâ, are meant by the sixteen Gâthâs. The whole book contains twenty-three lectures as stated in verse 16.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_510"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_513">182:7</a> The first <i>s</i>rutaskandha of the <i>G</i><i>ñ</i>âtâdharmakathâ, which contains nineteen adhyayanas, is intended by <i>g</i><i>ñ</i>âtâdhyayana.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_511"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_514">183:1</a> <i>S</i>abala, because they 'variegate' the conduct. The actions meant are sitting on an unwiped seat, &amp;c.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_512"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_515">183:2</a> Parîsaha, see above, <a href="sbe4504.htm#page_9">p. 9</a> ff.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_513"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_516">183:3</a> Rûpa. The twenty-four gods are: ten Bhavanapatis, eight Vyantaras, five <i>G</i>yôtishkas, one Vaimânika; or the 24 prophets.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_514"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_517">183:4</a> Bhâvanâ, the subdivisions of the five great vows, see part i, p. 189 ff.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_515"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_518">183:5</a> The Da<i>s</i>â<i>s</i>rutaskandha, B<i>ri</i>hat Kalpa, and Vyavahâra Sûtras are meant, which together contain twenty-six uddê<i>s</i>as.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_516"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_519">183:6</a> I.e. the Â<i>k</i>ârâṅga Sûtra; it now contains but twenty-four lectures, but is said to have originally contained four more, see part i, introduction, p. xlix f. These four lectures were: Mahâparinnâ, Ugghâya, A<i>n</i>ugghâya, Ârôva<i>n</i>â.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_517"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_520">184:1</a> The pure operations of mind, speech, and body.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_518"></a><a href="sbe4533.htm#fr_521">184:2</a> As far as I can make out from the enumeration in the commentary, they are articles regulating the intercourse between monks, especially pupils and teacher.</p>
