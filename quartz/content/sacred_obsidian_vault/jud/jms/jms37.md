Thorndike, II, 973, IV, 317; Maimonides, <i>Mishneh Torah</i>, <i>Akkum</i>, 11, 8; <i>Nishmat Ḥayim</i>, III, 21; Moses of Tachau lent his support to this Maimonidean position in his anti-Maimonidean polemic (<i>Oẓar Neḥmad</i>, III, 82), and severely criticized "those men to whom the spirit of <i>Torah</i> is foreign, who busy themselves with astrology and believe in it and make it their creed, and thereby bring harm to others." See <i>JE</i>, II, 243 ff. and <i>EJ</i>, III, 578 ff., for a general survey of the rabbinic material. The use of the word <i>mazal</i>, "star," "constellation," to signify "luck" may be seen in the Talmud; in the Middle Ages it was more frequent. "We call good fortune, good <i>mazal</i>, and the reverse, bad <i>mazal</i> . . . in German it is <i>Glück</i> and in Italian <i>ventura</i>" (Levita, <i>Tishbi</i>, s. v. <i>mazal</i>). This usage of the word did not displace its astrological sense in the vernacular until modern times.</p>

 <p><a name="an_16_13">13</a>. Ginzberg, <i>Legends</i>, VI, 204, n. 109; Perles, <i>Etym. Studien</i>, 73; Grunwald, <i>MGJV</i>, V (1900), 84 ff.; <i>JJV</i>, I (1923), 217; Schudt, II, 29:13, p. 10b; Güd. I, 206; Wuttke, 104. In Northern Italy the peasants went out into the fields on Midsummer Day to seek "the oil of St. John," which had wonderful properties, on oak leaves. Brück, 45 ff., suggests that the Jewish belief may be connected with the Phoenician Adonis rites, celebrated during the midsummer season at a river near the Syrian Byblos, when the river ran red with the blood of the dying god.</p>
