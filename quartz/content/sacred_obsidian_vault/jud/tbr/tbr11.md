 <p><a name="page_4"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 4</font></a></p>  <h4 align="center">The Time of Reciting the Shma‘ in the Morning.</h4>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><b>M</b>.</font></span>I. 4 (2). At what time does one begin to recite the Shma‘ in the mornings? As soon as one can distinguish between blue and white. <a name="fr_24"></a><a href="#fn_24"><font size="1">1</font></a> R. Eliezer says: Between blue and leek-green, <a name="fr_25"></a><a href="#fn_25"><font size="1">2</font></a> and one may finish it until the sun first shines out.</p>  
 <p>R. Joshua <a name="fr_26"></a><a href="#fn_26"><font size="1">3</font></a> says: Until [the end of] the third hour, <a name="fr_27"></a><a href="#fn_27"><font size="1">4</font></a> for such is the practice of kings’ sons, <a name="fr_28"></a><a href="#fn_28"><font size="1">5</font></a> to arise at the third hour. He who recites from that time and later has no loss; <a name="fr_29"></a><a href="#fn_29"><font size="1">6</font></a> he is like a man who reads in the Law. <a name="fr_30"></a><a href="#fn_30"><font size="1">7</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><dir><b>T</b>. I. 2. At what time does one begin to recite the Shma‘ in the mornings? Others <a name="fr_31"></a><a href="#fn_31"><font size="1">8</font></a> say: When it is light enough to recognize one's companion <a name="fr_32"></a><a href="#fn_32"><font size="1">9</font></a> at the distance of four cubits. Its legal recitation is at the moment that the sun first shines out, so that a man may join the <i>Ge’ullah</i> <a name="fr_33"></a><a href="#fn_33"><font size="1">10</font></a> to the <i>Tephillah</i>, <a name="fr_34"></a><a href="#fn_34"><font size="1">11</font></a> and thus be praying the Tephillah in the daytime.</dir>

 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><b>T</b>.</font></span></p><dir>R. Judah <a name="fr_35"></a><a href="tbr12.htm#fn_35"><font size="1">1</font></a> said: Once I was walking behind R. <i>T</i>. Aqiba <a name="fr_36"></a><a href="tbr12.htm#fn_36"><font size="1">2</font></a> and behind R. Eleazar ben Azariah, <a name="fr_37"></a><a href="tbr12.htm#fn_37"><font size="1">3</font></a> and the time of reciting the Shma‘ arrived. I supposed that they would neglect reciting it because they were occupied with the requirements of the congregation. I recited and said my mishna portion, and, after this, they began, and the sun was already visible over the tops of the mountains.</dir>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_24"></a><a href="tbr11.htm#fr_24">4:1</a> <i>blue and white</i>. Between the colours of the threads in the Tsitsith, the "Fringe" of the Prayer-scarf (Tallith).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_25"></a><a href="tbr11.htm#fr_25">4:2</a> <i>blue and leek-green</i>. <i>i.e.</i> rather later, because the two colours require a better light to distinguish them.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_26"></a><a href="tbr11.htm#fr_26">4:3</a> <i>R. Joshua</i>. R. J. ben Chananiah, c. 90-130 A.D.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_27"></a><a href="tbr11.htm#fr_27">4:4</a> <i>the third hour</i>. c. 9 a.m. See note, <a href="tbr25.htm#page_28">p. 28</a>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_28"></a><a href="tbr11.htm#fr_28">4:5</a> <i>kings’ sons</i>. <i>i.e.</i> persons who are not obliged to rise early.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_29"></a><a href="tbr11.htm#fr_29">4:6</a> <i>has no loss</i>. For he may still recite the Benedictions that precede and follow the Shma‘ (Bartenora).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_30"></a><a href="tbr11.htm#fr_30">4:7</a> <i>in the Law</i>. And he receives the same reward (Bartenora).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_31"></a><a href="tbr11.htm#fr_31">4:8</a> <i>Others</i>. <i>i.e.</i> than those mentioned in <i>M</i>. I. 4.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_32"></a><a href="tbr11.htm#fr_32">4:9</a> <i>to recognize one's companion</i>. Compare <i>The Pilgrimage of Etheria</i>, written in the end of the fourth century. "The arrival in Jerusalem thus takes place at the hour when one man begins to be able to recognize another, that is, close upon but a little before daylight." (English translation, 1919, p. 53; see also p. 72.)</p>
 <p><a name="fn_33"></a><a href="tbr11.htm#fr_33">4:10</a> <i>the Ge’ullah</i>. The one long Benediction that follows the Shma‘ in the morning (Staerk, pp. 6 <i>sq</i>.; SA, pp. 42-44,) and the first of the two that follow it in the evening (Staerk, pp. 8 <i>sq</i>.; SA, pp. 98 <i>sq</i>.). It is called Ge’ullah (redemption) because the latter part recalls the redemption from Egypt.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_34"></a><a href="tbr11.htm#fr_34">4:11</a> <i>the Tephillah</i>. <i>i.e.</i> <i>the Prayer par excellence</i>. It consists of <a name="page_5"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 5</font></a> nineteen separate prayers, or Benedictions, the original number being <i>Eighteen</i>, whence the name <i>Shemoneh Esreh</i>. It is often (though not in the Mishna or Tosephta of <i>Berakoth</i>) called the <i>Amidah</i>, because it was said standing. It is essentially pre-Christian, and perhaps pre-Maccabean. Both the Palestinian (the earlier) and the Babylonian (the later and normal) forms are given in Dalman's <i>Worte</i>, Appendix, Staerk, pp. 9 <i>sqq</i>., see further SA, pp. lv. <i>sqq</i>.</p>
