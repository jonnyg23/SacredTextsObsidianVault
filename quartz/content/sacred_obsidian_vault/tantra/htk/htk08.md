 <p><a name="page_53"><font size="1" color="green">p. 53</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">VERSE 4</h3>  
 <p>O DESTRUCTRESS of the sins of the three worlds, auspicious <a name="fr_34"></a><a href="#fn_34"><font size="1">1</font></a> Kālikā, who in Thy upper lotus-like left hand holdest a sword. <a name="fr_35"></a><a href="#fn_35"><font size="1">2</font></a> and in the lower left hand a severed head; <a name="fr_36"></a><a href="#fn_36"><font size="1">3</font></a> who with Thy upper right hand maketh the gesture which dispels fear, <a name="fr_37"></a><a href="#fn_37"><font size="1">4</font></a> and with Thy lower right hand that which grants boons; they, O Mother with gaping mouth, <a name="fr_38"></a><a href="#fn_38"><font size="1">5</font></a> who reciting Thy name, meditate in this way <a name="fr_39"></a><a href="#fn_39"><font size="1">6</font></a> upon the greatness of Thy mantra, possess the eight great powers <a name="fr_40"></a><a href="#fn_40"><font size="1">7</font></a> of the Three-Eyed One <a name="fr_41"></a><a href="#fn_41"><font size="1">8</font></a> in the palm of their hands. <a name="fr_42"></a><a href="#fn_42"><font size="1">9</font></a></p>

 <p align="center">COMMENTARY</p>  
 <p><i>'Sword' (Kṛpāṇam)</i></p>  
 <p>The sword is knowledge (Jñāna) by which the bonds of ignorance of the desire-free Sādhaka are severed. See Śivadharmottara.</p>  
 <p><i>'Severed head' (Chinna-muṇdaṁ)</i></p>  
 <p>The human head is the seat of Tattvajñāṇa free of attachment.</p>  
 <p><i>'Terrible countenance' (Prakatita-radane)</i></p>  
 <p>Her white teeth indicative of the white self-manifesting Sattva-Guṇa bite the red lolling tongue indicative of Rajas Guṇa and suppress both Rajas and Tamas by Sattva.</p>  
 <p><i>'Precious Mantrās' (Manu-vi-bhavaṁ)</i></p>  
 <p>The three 'Krīṁ' Bījas represent the Cidghana aspect of Devī, the two Hūm Bījas the Sattva-Guṇa aspect and the two 'Hrīm' Bījas the Rajah-pradhāna-sattva-Guṇa aspect.</p>  
 <p>[Durgārāma-Siddhāntavāgīśa explains this in the following different ways: (<i>a</i>) Manuvibhava = the Vibhava or Saṁpatti (precious possession) of Manus or Mantras. This precious possession is the</p>  
 <p><a name="page_54"><font size="1" color="green">p. 54</font></a></p>  
 <p>name in the vocative case 'Dakshiṇe Kālike.' The meaning of the passage then is that those who recite Thy name Dakshiṇe Kālike, which is the precious possession of Mantras, and meditate on this Thine appearance possess the Powers and so forth. (<i>b</i>) Manuvibhava is the Vibhava of the Manu that is the twenty-two syllabled Mantra of Kālī. This possession is the name Dakshiṇā Kālikā. (<i>c</i>) Manuvibhava = Manu (Mantra) vibhava (Ghataka) of which (the Devī's body) is the body of which Mantra is the generator. The bodies of the Devatās are produced by their Mantras. The passage thus means that, they who recite Thy name Dakshiṇā Kālikā and meditate on this Thine appearance generated by Mantra possess the virtues mentioned above.]</p>  
 <p>See last Verse.</p>  
 <p><i>Kālikā (Kālike)</i></p>  
 <p><i>'Three eyed one (Tryaṁbaka)'</i></p>  
 <p>[The same commentator (Durgārāma) offers three explanations of the term Tryaṁbaka used for Śiva (<i>a</i>) He who has three Aṁbakas or eyes is Tryaṁbaka, (<i>b</i>) He who has three Mothers or Aṁbās is Tryaṁbaka. The Kālikāpurāṇa says, 'As Hara is born of three Mothers He is known, even amongst Devas, by the title Tryaṁbaka.<font size="-1" color="green">'</font> (<i>c</i>) Todala-Tantra says 'the Vidyā Bhuvaneśvarī is in Heaven, Earth, and the Nether world (Pātāla). He who delights in the Devī as threefold in three places is called Tryaṁbaka. He is with Śakti and is worshipped in all Tantras.']</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_34"></a><a href="htk08.htm#fr_34">53:1</a> <a name="page_54"><font size="1" color="green">p. 54</font></a> <i>Dakshiṇā</i>, the beneficent grantor of <i>Nirvāṇa</i>. (V)</p>
 <p><a name="fn_35"></a><a href="htk08.htm#fr_35">53:2</a> <i>Khadga</i>, the peculiar heavy sword with the blade curved at the tip so named, used to behead the sacrificial animals.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_36"></a><a href="htk08.htm#fr_36">53:3</a> The Devī is the destroyer of the wicked.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_37"></a><a href="htk08.htm#fr_37">53:4</a> The Devī is the dispeller of all fear, and makes with Her hand the <i>mudrā</i>. The right upper hand makes the gesture of dispelling fear, or the gesture of assurance of safety (<i>Abhayamudrā</i>) and the right lower hand makes the gesture of granting boons (<i>Varamudrā</i>). (V) The <i>Sādhaka</i> seeks fearlessness, which is the great gift of the Goddess, who is <i>Bhayāpahā</i>, 'remover of fear.' 'If thou art remembered in times of <a name="page_55"><font size="1" color="green">p. 55</font></a> difficulty, Thou takest away all fear' (Mārkaṇdeya-Purāṇa). At the same time it is she who fills the ignorant with terror (Paśuloka-bhayaṁkarī) that is, those devoid of the knowledge of non-duality, for 'fear comes when there is duality' (Br. Up. 1-4-2, Lalitā, v. 99).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_38"></a><a href="htk08.htm#fr_38">53:5</a> <i>Prakatita-vadane</i> (see '<i>Daśa-Mahāvidyā Upāsanārahasya</i>,' by Prasanna-Kumāra-Śāstrī). <i>Vimalānanda</i> reads <i>Prakatitaradane</i>, that is, with big protruding teeth. The <i>Yoginī-Tantra</i> says, 'Supreme eternal, large-toothed, smeared with blood.' The <i>Tārākalpa</i> speaks of '<i>Syāmā</i> of the colour of a new (freshly formed) cloud, with large breasts, terrible with protruding teeth.<font size="-1" color="green">'</font> (V)</p>
 <p><a name="fn_39"></a><a href="htk08.htm#fr_39">53:6</a> As statedthat is, <i>Krīṁ Krīṁ Krīṁ Hūṁ Hūṁ Hrīṁ Hrīṁ</i> which with <i>Dakṣiṇe</i> makes ten syllables.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_40"></a><a href="htk08.htm#fr_40">53:7</a> <i>Siddhi</i>that is, <i>Aṇimā</i>, <i>Laghimā</i>, <i>Garimā</i>, <i>Prāpti</i>, <i>Prākāṁya</i>, <i>Iśitva</i>, <i>Vaśitva</i>, <i>Kāmāvasāyitā</i> the power of becoming small, great, heavy, light, etc., which are inherent in Īśvara, and are attainable by Yogis who become Īśvara and gain <i>Āiśvarya</i>. By realization of the self, that Divine state which is the universal Self is manifested, as also the eightfold manifestation of the Divine power.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_41"></a><a href="htk08.htm#fr_41">53:8</a> <i>Tryaṁbaka</i> or <i>Śiva</i>. According to Tarkālaṁkāra's Commentary on Mahānirvāṇa-Tantra, <i>Tryaṁbaka</i> means the father of the three Devas, Brahmā, Viṣṇu, and Rudra. The <i>Ṛgvidhāna</i> uses it as an equivalent of Mahādeva. The Mahānirvāṇa-Tantra says: 'As She surveys the entire universe, which is the product of time, with Her three eyesthe Moon, Sun, and Firetherefore She is endowed with three eyes' (<i>Ullāsa</i> xiii, verse 8) The Moon, Sun, and Fire are the <i>Icchā</i>, <i>Kriyā</i>, <i>Jñāna</i> and other <i>Śaktis</i> (see the Ṣatcakranirūpaṅa of Pūrnānaṇda-Śvāmī) and Serpent Power by A. Avalon.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_42"></a><a href="htk08.htm#fr_42">53:9</a> 'By him who carries a flower its odour is enjoyed without seeking. By him who looks upon himself as the universal Self the powers (of Brahmā, etc.) are enjoyed' (Commentary of Sureśvarācārya on tenth Sloka of <i>Dakṣināmūrti Stotra</i>).</p>
