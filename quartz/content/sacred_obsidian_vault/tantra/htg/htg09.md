 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_204"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_204">43:1</a> From the Mahānirvāṇa Tantra, Seventh Ullāsa, verses 12 <i>et seq</i>. This hymn to the primordial Kālī contains a hundred of her names all beginning with "K." Thus Kālī, Karālī, Kalyānī, Kalāvatī, Kamalā, Kalidarpaghni, Kaparddīśakripanvitā, etc. <i>Kādi</i> is that which has "Ka" in the beginning. In the Tantrarāja, Devī says to Śiva: "The syllable 'Ka' is in Thy form, and that Śakti confers all <i>siddhis</i>" (see Lalitā Sahasranāma, where a number of the following names occur).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_205"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_205">43:2</a> The <i>Māyābīja</i> (see Fifth Ullāsa, verse 10).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_206"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_206">43:3</a> Kālī (see Fourth Ullāsa, verses 30 <i>et seq</i>.). She is thus called <i>Kālakarshinī</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_207"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_207">43:4</a> <i>Bīja</i> of Lakṣmī, Devī of prosperity or beauty.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_208"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_208">43:5</a> Karālī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_209"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_209">43:6</a> The Bījābhidāna says <i>Ka</i> = Kālī, <i>Ra</i> = Brahmā, <i>Ī</i> = Mahāmāyā. The half circle of <i>candrabindu</i> is the universal Mother, and the point is the destroyer of misery.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_210"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_210">43:7</a> Kalyāṇī, or She who bestows peace and happiness (see the Lalitā, verse 73). According to the Padmapurāṇa, Devī is worshipped as Kalyāṇī in the Malaya mountains.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_211"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_211">44:1</a> Kalāvatī (see the Lalitā, verse 74). The <i>Kālā</i>, or arts, are sixty-four in number. The Śakti should always be <i>Kalāvatī</i>. Devī is also called <i>Kalāmālā</i>, or garland of the arts. Kalāvatī may also mean possessed of all arts complete.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_212"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_212">44:2</a> A name of Lakṣmī-Devī is Kamala, for She is all Śaktis. In verse 73 of the Lalitā, Devī is called Kāmakalārūpā, on which Bhāskararāya says that there are three <i>bindus</i> and the <i>hārdakala</i>. The first <i>bindu</i> is called Kāma, and the last Kalā; but according to the rule <i>pratyāhāra</i>, Kamalā includes all four. Kālikā Purāṇa says, Devī, is alone indicated by Kāma.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_213"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_213">44:3</a> Kalidarpaghnī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_214"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_214">44:4</a> Kapardīśakripanvitā. <i>Kapardīśa</i> is a title of Śiva derived from his matted hair.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_215"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_215">44:5</a> Kālikā, because She devours Śiva as Mahākāla (see Mahānirvāṇa Tantra, Fourth Ullāsa, verse 31).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_216"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_216">44:6</a> Kālamātā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_217"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_217">44:7</a> Kālānalasamadyuti.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_218"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_218">44:8</a> Kapardinī. Spouse of Śiva, called <i>Kapardi</i> from his matted hair (see Lalitā, verse 151). The Viśva says that <i>Kaparda</i> means the matted hair of Śiva and the cowdung cakes. When Śiva incarnated, as Mailāra, his spouse was decked with a garland of cowdung-cakes.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_219"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_219">44:9</a> Karālāsyā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_220"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_220">44:10</a> Karunāmritasāgarā (see Lalitā, verse 73).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_221"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_221">45:1</a> Kripāmayī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_222"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_222">45:2</a> Kripādhārā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_223"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_223">45:3</a> Kripāpārā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_224"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_224">45:4</a> Kripāgamā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_225"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_225">45:5</a> Kṛṣānu. <i>Kṛṣānuretas</i> is an epithet of Śiva whose male seed is fire.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_226"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_226">45:6</a> Kapilā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_227"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_227">45:7</a> Kṛṣṇā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_228"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_228">45:8</a> Kṛṣṇānandavivardhinī. Kṛṣṇa is here the supreme Lord.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_229"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_229">45:9</a> Kālaratrī. The Lalitā, verse 101, speaks of the Devī as attended by hosts of Śaktis, Kālarātrī, and others--that is the twelve Śaktis from Kālarātrī to Tankārī, one in each petal of the <i>anahāta padma</i>. The Varāha Purāṇa says that Raudrī, who was born <a href="errata.htm#5">from</a> darkness and went to the Blue Mountain to perform penance (the Śakti causing destruction) is called Kālarātrī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_230"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_230">45:10</a> Kāmarupā (<i>ibid</i>., verse 73), Kalika Purāṇa--says that Devī is called Kāma because She came to the secret place in the Blue peak of the great mountain (Kāilāsa) along with Śiva, for the sake of desire, and because She fulfils desires and destroys and restores the body of Kāma.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_231"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_231">45:11</a> Kāmapāśavimocinī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_232"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_232">45:12</a> Kādambinī. In the heat of India the rain-cloud is welcome, and in some of Her forms She is dark.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_233"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_233">46:1</a> Kalādhārā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_234"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_234">46:2</a> Kalikalmaṣanāśinī (see Lalitā, verse 113), Kūrma Purāṇa says that the repetition of the names of Devī destroys the multitude of sins in the Kālī Age.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_235"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_235">46:3</a> Kumāripūjanapritā. The <i>Kumārī pūjā</i>, or worship of virgins, is a <i>pūjā</i> common in Bengal. The worship is by men, whereas the <i>sadhavapūjā</i>, or worship of married women, is done by women. Or Kumārī may directly refer to the Devī Herself. She is known as the unmarried Kumārī. A <i>sūtra</i> of the Śiva Sūtras runs <i>Iccāśaktih Umā kumārī</i>. The energy of desire is Umā, the unmarried. Bhāskararāya (Commentary, Lalitā, verses 25, 40) says: As play She creates the universe, hence She is Kumārī, or She destroys (<i>mārayate</i>) the ground (<i>ku</i>) of the great illusion. Hence She is Kumārī; Kumārī is the enjoyer and not to be enjoyed, as She is one with the <i>Yogi</i>, the enjoyer.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_236"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_236">46:4</a> Kumārī pūjakālayā, or "who art the refuge of such worshippers."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_237"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_237">46:5</a> Kumāribhojanānanda. The <i>Kumārīs</i> are feasted at the <i>Kumārī pūjā</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_238"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_238">46:6</a> Kumārīrūpadhārinī. So a very young marriageable girl is called Gauri.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_239"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_239">46:7</a> Kadambavanasanchārā (see Hymn to Tripurasundari, <i>post</i>).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_240"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_240">46:8</a> Kadambapuṣpasantoṣā (see Lalitā, verse 73).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_241"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_241">47:1</a> Kadambavanavāsinī (see the Lalitā, verse 23). The palace of <i>Cintāmani</i> is surrounded by a gallery of gems (<i>maṇimaṇḍapa</i>). Around this is the grove of Kadamba trees, which in the Purāṇās are said to be seven yojanas in height in the space between the walls of gold and silver. The Bhairavayāmala says the abode of Bindu is the ocean of nectar. The five <i>yonis</i>--that is, the five Śakti angles in the <i>śricakra</i>--are the divine trees. There is the grove of <i>Nīpa</i> trees. Within that is the gallery of gems. Within that is the palace of <i>Cintāmaṇi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_242"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_242">47:2</a> Kadambapuṣpamālinī. The Lalitā, verse 8, speaks of Devī as decked above her ears with clusters of Kadamba flowers.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_243"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_243">47:3</a> Kishorī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_244"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_244">47:4</a> Kālakaṇṭhā. Devī is also called Kālakaṇṭhī, or wife of Kālakaṇṭha, a name of Śiva whose throat was coloured by the poison he swallowed at the churning of the ocean. According to Devī purāṇa, Kālakaṇṭha was worshipped at Kālanjara.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_245"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_245">47:5</a> Kalanādaninādinī. Water-birds which sing to one another at night--the male to the female and the latter to the male--sitting on opposite banks of the river. Their passionate devotion is often alluded to by the poets.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_246"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_246">47:6</a> Kādambaripānaratā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_247"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_247">47:7</a> Kādambarīpriyā. <i>Kādambarī</i> is mead. Bhāskararāya (Commentary, Lalitā), dealing with the Tripurā Upanishad, which prescribes that mead, fish, flesh, and cooked cereals should be offered to the Devatā, says that it enjoins those who are allowed to take wine, flesh, etc., that they should do so after first dedicating them to the Brahman, and minimize the habit by gradation (see the same idea expressed in Manu v., 56, Bhāg. Pr. XI., 5-11).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_248"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_248">48:1</a> Kapālapātraniratā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_249"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_249">48:2</a> Kamkālamālyadhārini.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_250"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_250">48:3</a> Kamalāsanasantuṣṭā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_251"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_251">48:4</a> Kamalāsanavāsinī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_252"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_252">48:5</a> Kamalālayamadhyasthā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_253"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_253">48:6</a> Kamalāmodamodinī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_254"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_254">48:7</a> Kalahamsagatī. <i>Hamsa</i> is variously translated goose, swan, flamingo. A swaying waddle like that of a duck is admired. As the swans live in the celestial lake called Mānasa, so She lives in the minds (<i>mānasa</i>) of Her devotees.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_255"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_255">48:8</a> Klaibyanāśinī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_256"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_256">48:9</a> Kāmarūpinī (see also <i>post</i>).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_257"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_257">48:10</a> Kāmarūpakritavāsā (see next note).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_258"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_258">48:11</a> Kāmapīthavilāsinī. Kāmarūpa, the great Tantrika centre in Assam, one of the <i>Mahāpīthas</i>, where the genital organ of the Devī fell on the severance of Her dead body by Viṣṇu after the <i>Dakṣa Yajna</i>. Kāmarupa is also one of the <i>Ādibhuta</i>, which are in the <i>Mūlādhāra</i> and other tattvik centres (see the Lalitā, verse 82).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_259"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_259">48:12</a> Kamanīyā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_260"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_260">48:13</a> Kalpalatā. The <i>Kalpa</i> tree is one of the celestial trees in the heaven of India, which yielded whatever one desired. Woman is likened to a creeper (<i>latā</i>) embracing and depending on her <a name="page_49"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 49</font></a> husband (see also the Lalitā, where the Devī is called <i>Bhaktimatkalpalatikā</i>, the <i>kalpa</i> creeper of the devotee).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_261"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_261">49:1</a> Kamanīyavibhūṣanā, or "who art the possessor of beautiful ornaments."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_262"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_262">49:2</a> Kamanīyagunārādhyā; or it may mean that the Devī is to be worshipped by the worshipper with all best and tender feeling.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_263"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_263">49:3</a> Komalāngī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_264"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_264">49:4</a> Krishodarī. Literally, small of belly (see Bhuvaneśvarī <i>stotra</i>, <i>apivarastanatating tanuvrittamadhyām</i>, and the Lalitā, verse 79, <i>tanumadhyā</i>).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_265"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_265">49:5</a> Kāranāmritasantoshā. <i>Kārana</i> is one of the technical terms given to the purified wine consumed as an element of the Tāntrika pancatattva.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_266"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_266">49:6</a> Kāranānandasiddhidā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_267"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_267">49:7</a> Kāranānandajapeshtā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_268"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_268">49:8</a> Kāranārchchanaharshitā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_269"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_269">49:9</a> Kāranārnavasangmagnā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_270"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_270">49:10</a> Kāranavratapālinī. <i>Vratas</i> are ritual acts and devotional exercises which do not belong to the obligatory (<i>nitya</i>) <i>karma</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_271"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_271">50:1</a> Kastūrīsaurabhāmodā. Musk is used in worship to scent <i>candan</i>, etc.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_272"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_272">50:2</a> Kastūrītilakojjvalā. The <i>tilak</i> is the mark worn by sectarian Hindus on the forehead.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_273"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_273">50:3</a> Kastūrīpūjanaratā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_274"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_274">50:4</a> Kastūrīpūjakapriyā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_275"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_275">50:5</a> Kastūrīdāhajananī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_276"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_276">50:6</a> Kastūrīmrigatoshinī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_277"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_277">50:7</a> Kastūrībhojanapritā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_278"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_278">50:8</a> Karpūrāmodamoditā. Camphor is burnt in worship during <i>ārati</i>, and is used to scent the <i>pādya</i>, betel leaf, etc., offered to the Devī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_279"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_279">50:9</a> Karpūramālābharanā. Balls of camphor are strung together in a garland.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_280"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_280">50:10</a> Karpūracandanākshitā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_281"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_281">50:11</a> Karpūrakāranāhladā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_282"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_282">50:12</a> Karpūrāmritapāyinī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_283"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_283">51:1</a> Karpūrasāgarasnatā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_284"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_284">51:2</a> Karpūrasāgarālayā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_285"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_285">51:3</a> Kūrchchabījajapapritā. <i>Hūm</i> is the <i>Kūrchhabīja</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_286"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_286">51:4</a> Kūrchchajapaparāyayanā--that is, She who mutters "<i>Hūm</i>" when conquering the demons. The Devī then constantly uttered the <i>hūmkāra</i> with its threatening, roaring sound.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_287"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_287">51:5</a> Kulīna. <i>Kula</i>, according to the Tantra, means Śakti. <i>Akula</i> = Śiva. The union of <i>Kula</i> with <i>Akula</i> is called Kaula, the essence common to both Śiva and Śakti. Hence Devī is <i>Kaulinī</i> (see Lalitā, verse 37). Both <i>Kula</i> and <i>Akula</i> are in the <i>Sahasrāra</i>. <i>Kulācāra</i> is the way of the <i>Kaula</i> division of Tāntrik worshippers.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_288"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_288">51:6</a> Kaulikārādhyā. The <i>Kaulikas</i> are followers of <i>Kulācāra</i> (see last note). So also in the Lalitā, verse 17, the Devī is addressed as "adored by <i>Dakṣinas</i> and <i>Adakṣinas</i>."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_289"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_289">51:7</a> Kaulikapriyakārīnī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_290"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_290">51:8</a> Kulacārā, <i>v. ante</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_291"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_291">51:9</a> Kantukinī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_292"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_292">51:10</a> Kulamārgapradarshinī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_293"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_293">51:11</a> Kaśīśvarī. Kāśī is Benares, the sacred city of Śiva.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_294"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_294">51:12</a> Kartahartrī; and thus the Lalitā speaks of the Devī as the moon-light which soothes those burned by the triple fires of misery.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_295"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_295">52:1</a> Kāśīśavaradayinī ("Lord" is Śiva).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_296"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_296">52:2</a> Kāśīśvarakritāmodā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_297"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_297">52:3</a> Kāśīśvaramanoramā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_298"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_298">52:4</a> Kalamanjīracaranā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_299"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_299">52:5</a> Kvanatkāncīvibhūṣanā.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_300"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_300">52:6</a> Kāncanādrikritāgarā (the mountain Kāncana or Sumeru). Durvāsa says in his Lalitāstavaratna (verses 2-4): "Let the gold mountain be victorious whose body is the universe resounding with the music of celestial women living in the golden bowers of creepers of the mountain peak. We salute the three peaks which are the seats of Brahmā, Viṣṇu, and Śiva, extending to the four quarters of the earth. In their midst is another peak 400 yojanas (a yojana is eight or nine miles) in height, making the place beauteous with the golden rays of its flowers, and I worship it." The Lalitā Sahasranāma (verse 22) also addresses Devī as dwelling on the middle peak of Mount Sumeru.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_301"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_301">52:7</a> Kāncanācalakaumudi.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_302"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_302">52:8</a> Kāmabījajapānanda. The <i>Kāma bīja</i> is "<i>klīm</i>." When the "<i>Ka</i>" and "<i>La</i>" are eliminated, the remaining "<i>īm</i>" is called <i>kāmakalā</i>, which is in the <i>turīya</i> state. In the Lalitā, verse 225, Devī is called creatrix of the <i>mantra klīm</i> ("<i>klīmkarī</i>"). <i>Klīmkāra</i> is <i>Śivakāma</i>, and She is His wife.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_303"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_303">52:9</a> Kāmabījasvarūpinī--that is, She is "<i>klīm</i>" itself.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_304"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_304">53:1</a> Kumatighnī. Devī is also (Lalitā, verse 78) <i>Sadācāra pravarttakā</i>, because She makes men move to right action.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_305"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_305">53:2</a> Kulīnārtināśinī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_306"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_306">53:3</a> Kulakāminī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_307"></a><a href="htg09.htm#fr_307">53:4</a> <i>Krīm, Hrīm, Śrīm mantravarṇena kālakanṭakaghātinī</i>.</p>
