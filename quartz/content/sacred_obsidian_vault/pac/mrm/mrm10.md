 <h1 align="CENTER">APPENDIX.</h1>
 <h3 align="CENTER">MAORI TERMS OF RELATIONSHIP.</h3>
 <p>TUPUNA. <i>An ancestor--male or female</i>.</p>
 <p>MATUA. <i>A father, or uncle either patruus or avunculus</i>.</p>
 <p>PAPA. <i>The same</i>.</p>
 <p>WHAEA. <i>A mother, or aunt on either side</i>.</p>
 <p>TAMA. <i>Eldest nephew</i>.</p>
 <p>TAMAHINE. <i>Eldest niece; also used more generally</i>.</p>
 <p>TAMAITI. <i>Son, or nephew</i>.</p>
 <p>TAMAROA. <i>The same</i>.</p>
 <p>TUAKANA. <i>Elder brother of males, elder sister of females; also elder brother's children in reference to younger brother's children, elder sister's children in reference to younger sister's children</i>.</p>
 <p>TEINA. <i>The younger brother of males, the younger sister of females; also the younger brother's children in reference to elder brother's children, the younger sister's children in reference to elder sister's children</i>.</p>
 <p>TUNGANE. <i>A sister's brother</i>.</p>
 <p>TUAHINE. A brother's sister.</p>
 <p>IRAMUTU. <i>A nephew, or niece</i>.</p>
 <p>HUNGAWAI. <i>A father-in-law, or mother-in-law</i>.</p>
 <p>HUNAONGA. <i>A son-in-law, or daughter-in-law</i>.</p>
 <p>TAOKETE. <i>A man's brother-in-law, or sister's sister-in-law</i>.</p>
 <p>AUTANE. <i>A woman's brother-in-law</i>.</p>
 <p>AUWAHINE. A man's sister-in-law.</p>
 <p>POTIKI. <i>A brother's children, or sister's children; also the youngest child of a family</i>.</p>
 <p>MOKOPUNA. <i>A grand-child, or child of a nephew or niece</i>.</p>
 <p>HUANGA. <i>A relation in general</i>.</p>
 <p>WHANAUNGA-TUPU. <i>A blood relation</i>.</p>
 <p>{p. 107}</p>
 <p>ARIKI. <i>The first born male or female</i>.</p>
 <p>WAEWAE. <i>A man's younger brother: literally the foot</i>.</p>
 <p>HAMUA. <i>Syn. tuakana</i>.</p>
 <p>MARONUI. <i>A married man or woman</i>.</p>
 <p>TAKAKAU. <i>A single man or woman</i>.</p>
 <p>POUARU. <i>A widow</i>.</p>
 <p>PUHI. <i>A betrothed female, also a female of rank restricted from marriage</i>.</p>
 <p>HE WAHINE TAUMARO. <i>A betrothed female</i>. N.B.--There is a distinction between a <i>Puhi</i> and a <i>wahine taumaro</i>. The betrothed female is a <i>Puhi</i> in reference to her father's act of consent, and a <i>wahine taumaro</i> in reference to her future father-in-law's act of consent to the arrangement.</p>
 <h3 align="CENTER">VOCABULARY OF SOME MAORI WORDS REQUIRING EXPLANATION.</h3>
 <p>IHI has the sense of <i>tapu</i> when occurring in <i>karakia</i>, or invocations of spirits.</p>
 <p>KAHUKAHU, the spirit of the germ of a human being: also called <i>Atua</i> noho-whare, or house-dwelling <i>Atua</i>. Verbi <i>kahukahu</i> significatio simplex est panniculus; et panniculus quo utitur femina menstrualis nomine <i>kahukahu</i> dicitur {Greek <i>kat? e?ksoxh`n</i>}. Apud populum Novæ Zelandæ creditur sanguinem utero sub tempus menstruale effusum continere germina hominis; et secundùm præcepta veteris superstitionis panniculus sanguine menstruali imbutus habebatur sacer (<i>tapu</i>), haud aliter quàm si formam humanam accepisset: mulierum autem mos est</p>
 <p>{p. 108}</p>
 <p>hos panniculos intra juncos parietum abdere; et hâc de causâ paries est domûs pars adeo sacra ut nemo illi innixus sedere audeat.</p>
 <p>KARAKIA. This word generally rendered by 'charm,' does not signify what the word charm would mean, in its popular sense. The word 'invocation' conveys more correctly its meaning; for it is a prayer addressed to spirits of deceased ancestors, in form somewhat like a litany.</p>
 <p>KAUPAPA, One whom the spirit of an ancestor visits, and who is its medium of communication with the living.</p>
 <p>PUKENGA, a spirit, the author or first teacher of any <i>karakia</i>.</p>
 <p>TAPAIRU, any very sacred ancestral Spirit: also sometimes applied to the female.</p>
 <p>TAUIRA, a person who is being instructed by a <i>tohunga</i>, or by the spirit of a parent or ancestor. He had to submit to a strict fast of several days before he was taught any important <i>karakia</i>.</p>
 <p>TIPUA, or TUPUA, the spirit of one who when living was noted for powerful <i>karakia</i>.</p>
 <p>TIRI, a strip of flax leaf or <i>toetoe</i> so placed as to serve as an imaginary pathway for an <i>Atua</i>. In sickness a <i>tiri</i> is suspended above the head of the sick person to facilitate the departure of the <i>Atua</i> who causes the disease. A <i>tiri</i> is also suspended near the <i>kaupapa</i>, when he desires his <i>Atua</i> to visit him. It is also applied to signify the <i>karakia</i> used on such occasions.</p>
 <p>TOHUNGA, a person skilled in <i>karakia</i>, also one skilled in any craft.</p>
 <p>{p. 109}</p>
 <p>TUAHU, a sacred place where offerings of food--first fruits--for the <i>Atua</i> were deposited.</p>
 <p>WANANGA, the spirit of anyone who when living had learned the <i>karakia</i> of his ancestors: thus when a <i>tauira</i> died he became a <i>wananga</i>.</p>
