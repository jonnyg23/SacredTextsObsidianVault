 <p><a name="page_33"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 33</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">SECTION IV.</h3>  <h4 align="center">DEUS LUNUS.</h4>  
 <p>THE moon-power, owing to the influence of the Greek Artemis-Selênê, the Latin Diana-Luna, is generally feminine in our thoughts; but this aspect, though also occasionally occurring elsewhere, <i>e.g.</i>, in Peru, is really exceptional. Thus among the Germanic nations the moon is masculine and the sun feminine. It is the daughter of Sôl, the Norse Sun-goddess, who in the regenerated world 'shall ride on her mother's track when the gods are dead'; <a name="fr_131"></a><a href="#fn_131"><font size="1">1</font></a> and it is the god Mâni, <a name="fr_132"></a><a href="#fn_132"><font size="1">2</font></a> who at Ragnarok, 'the-Twilight-of-the-gods,' shall be devoured by the Wolf of darkness, Managarmr, 'Moon-swallower,' a reduplication of the terrible wolf Fenrir. <a name="fr_133"></a><a href="#fn_133"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>In Egypt again, 'Chons is the personification of the moon, and in this character he is called Chonsaah or Chons the moon. His name seems to mean</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_34"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 34</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> "the chaser," or "pursuer,"' <a name="fr_134"></a><a href="#fn_134"><font size="1">1</font></a> the Unicorn who, as we shall see, <a name="fr_135"></a><a href="#fn_135"><font size="1">2</font></a> chases the Lion-sun. Another Kamic-lunar personage is Teti (Thoth), the weighing and measuring god, lord of knowledge and writing. <a name="fr_136"></a><a href="#fn_136"><font size="1">3</font></a> 'The crescent is found followed by the figure of Thoth in several hieroglyphic legends, with the phonetic name Aah.' <a name="fr_137"></a><a href="#fn_137"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>'The Arabs to this day, consider the moon masculine, and not feminine.' <a name="fr_138"></a><a href="#fn_138"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>  
 <p>'In Sanskrit the most current names for the moon, such as Kandra, Soma, Indu, Vidhu, are masculine. The names of the moon are frequently used in the sense of month, and these and other names for month retain the same gender.' <a name="fr_139"></a><a href="#fn_139"><font size="1">6</font></a></p>  
 <p>In Asia Minor was widely established the cult of the Moon-god Mên, <a name="fr_140"></a><a href="#fn_140"><font size="1">7</font></a> the Lunus of the Romans, who, to a great extent suppressed his ritual.</p>  
 <p>The Babylonian and Assyrian Moon-god is Sin, <a name="fr_141"></a><a href="#fn_141"><font size="1">8</font></a> whose name probably appears in Sinai. The expression, 'From the origin of the god Sin,' was used by the Assyrians to mark remote antiquity; because as chaos preceded order, so night preceded day, and the enthronement of the moon as the Night-king marks the commencement of the annals of kosmic order.</p>

 <p>the Semitic Sin, is Aku, 'the Seated-father,' as chief supporter of kosmic order, styled 'the-Maker-of-brightness,' En-zuna, 'the-Lord-of-growth,' and Idu, the-Measuring-lord,' <a name="fr_142"></a><a href="#fn_142"><font size="1">1</font></a> the Aïdês of Hesychios. <a name="fr_143"></a><a href="#fn_143"><font size="1">2</font></a> <i>Idu</i> is the equivalent of the Assyrian <i>Arkhu</i>, 'month,' Heb. <i>Yerakh</i>; and is expressed in archaic Babylonian by the ideograph <a name="img_03500"></a>
 <img src="img/03500.jpg"> = the circle, (solar or) lunar + <a name="img_03501"></a>
 <img src="img/03501.jpg"> (10 + 10 + 10), <i>i.e.</i>, the thirty days of the month. <a name="img_03501"></a>
 <img src="img/03501.jpg"> also stands for the Moon-god as the god-thirty. Amongst the Finns Kuu is 'the male god of the moon,' <a name="fr_144"></a><a href="#fn_144"><font size="1">3</font></a> and exactly corresponds with A-ku. It is singular to find also Kua as a moon-name in Central Africa. <a name="fr_145"></a><a href="#fn_145"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>'Among the Mbocobis of South America, the moon is a man and the sun his wife.' <a name="fr_146"></a><a href="#fn_146"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>  
 <p>Amongst the Mexicans, Metztli, the Moon, was a hero. <a name="fr_147"></a><a href="#fn_147"><font size="1">6</font></a></p>  
 <p>According to an Australian legend, 'Mityan, the Moon, was a native cat [male], who fell in love with some one else's wife, and was driven away to wander ever since.' <a name="fr_148"></a><a href="#fn_148"><font size="1">7</font></a></p>  
 <p>'The Khasias of the Himalaya say that the moon [male] falls monthly in love with his mother-in-law, who throws ashes in his face, whence his spots.' <a name="fr_149"></a><a href="#fn_149"><font size="1">8</font></a></p>  
 <p>Ra Vula, the Figian Moon, is male. <a name="fr_150"></a><a href="#fn_150"><font size="1">9</font></a></p>  
 <p>The Ahts of Vancouver's Island regard 'the Moon as husband and the Sun as wife.' <a name="fr_151"></a><a href="#fn_151"><font size="1">10</font></a></p>
