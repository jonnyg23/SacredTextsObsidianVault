 <p><a name="page_47"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 47</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">SECTION VII.</h3>  <h4 align="center">MEDOUSA THE GORGÔ.</h4>  
 <p>FROM the Triple-moon and the Unicorn-horse-moon I pass on to the Serpentine-full-moon, the victim of the solar Perseus, another version of the oft-recurring story. Careful study of the Homerik Poems reveals the intrinsically archaic nature and high antiquity of the majority of their ideas, and in the consideration of any mythic personage a passage in Homer, if available, almost always supplies an excellent starting-point. It is generally, but not quite accurately stated that 'Homer knows only one Gorgo.' The passages are as follows;</p>  
 <p>'On it [the <i>aigis</i> of Athenaiê] was a Gorgeian head of a dreadful portent.' <a name="fr_186"></a><a href="#fn_186"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>  
 <p>'Hektôr, having the eyes of a Gorgô.' <a name="fr_187"></a><a href="#fn_187"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>'An awful-looking Gorgô' <a name="fr_188"></a><a href="#fn_188"><font size="1">3</font></a> was the device upon the shield of Agamemnôn.</p>  
 <p>Odysseus fears 'lest Persephoneia from Hades should send a Gorgeian head of a dreadful portent.' <a name="fr_189"></a><a href="#fn_189"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_48"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 48</font></a></p>  
 <p>From these passages we gather:</p>  
 <p>1. That whilst there was certainly one Gorgô, there may also have been others.</p>  
 <p>2. That its eye constituted the chief terror of the appearance. <a name="fr_190"></a><a href="#fn_190"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>  
 <p>3. That this appearance, originally portentous, <a name="fr_191"></a><a href="#fn_191"><font size="1">2</font></a> became, or was considered to be, monstrous. <a name="fr_192"></a><a href="#fn_192"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>4. That, though having a bright eye, it is connected with Darkness and the Underworld. And</p>  
 <p>5. Was used heraldically as arms upon a shield.</p>  
 <p>Fick would connect the obscure word Gorgô with the European root <i>garg</i>, 'to cry,' and compares the Sk. <i>garj</i>, 'to emit a deep sound;' <a name="fr_193"></a><a href="#fn_193"><font size="1">4</font></a> but the idea of sound is so truly out of place in the myth (a circumstance which we are bound to consider), that I am compelled to reject this derivation. I had deemed the term as possibly an intensive variant of <i>orgê</i>, 'natural impulse,' primarily 'swelling' (first physics, then meta-physics), as applied to the swollen, full-faced Moon; for from Homer alone it is not very</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_49"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 49</font></a></p>  
 <p>difficult to gather that Gorgô = Luna. But the detail of a myth is the true test by which to try various etymologies of the name of its protagonist, especially when in the abstract several distinct derivations appear to have an almost equal claim to acceptance. Now the Gorgon-power (as will more fully appear) = Nocturnal-darkness + Moon, not darkness merely or the moon merely. Darkness, it will be remembered, is frequently (like Chaos) depicted in monstrous form; but especially is it a Devourer or Swallower. <a name="fr_194"></a><a href="#fn_194"><font size="1">1</font></a> The Proto-Aryan root <i>gar</i>, 'to swallow, gulp,' appears in the intensive form <i>gargar</i>, <a name="fr_195"></a><a href="#fn_195"><font size="1">2</font></a> the Gk. variant of which would be Gorgô, the earliest form of the word in that language. Gorgô is 'the Swallower,' the devouring darkness which has a bright headthe Moon, a head capable of being cut off. Hence the combined beauty and horror (hideousness) of the Gorgô, a hideousness which does not arise in the first instance from the lunar-serpent-rays, and hence <i>the open mouth</i>, so marked a feature in the Gorgoneion and one not in the least lunar. Mr. Dennis observes;</p>  
 <p>The most remarkable type on the coins of Populonia is the Gorgoneion; not here "the head of the fair-cheeked Medusa" <a name="fr_196"></a><a href="#fn_196"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"A woman's countenance with serpent locks,"</font></dir>  
 <p>as it is represented by the sculptors of later Greece</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_50"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 50</font></a></p>  
 <p>and of Etruria; but a monstrous fiend-like visage, with snaky hair, gnashing tusks, and tongue lolling out of</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"The open mouth that seemed to containe<br>  A good full peck within the utmost brim,<br>  Appearing <i>like the mouth</i> of Orcus griesly grim." <a name="fr_197"></a><a href="#fn_197"><font size="1">1</font></a></font></dir>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> From this open mouth issue two huge curved teeth, the lunar horns. The protruded tongue and gnashing teeth were familiar to the author (probably Hesiod <a name="fr_198"></a><a href="#fn_198"><font size="1">2</font></a>) of the <i>Aspis Herakleous</i>. <a name="fr_199"></a><a href="#fn_199"><font size="1">3</font></a> And this leads us to the Hesiodik phase of the myth, according to which <a name="fr_200"></a><a href="#fn_200"><font size="1">4</font></a> there are three Gorgones (= Hekatê Triformis), Medousa 'the Ruler' (= the King or Queen-moon), Stheinô or Sthenô 'the Strong' (= the general Nocturnal-potency), and Euryalê 'the Wide-wandering' (= the Moon 'wandering companionless' <a name="fr_201"></a><a href="#fn_201"><font size="1">5</font></a>), a phase which corresponds with the solar Bellerophôn in the same Aleian Field. <a name="fr_202"></a><a href="#fn_202"><font size="1">6</font></a> Do not hastily charge the intricate myth with inconsistency. The Night is dark and not-dark, lunar and not-lunar; and so is the Gorgô; so are the Gorgones. And that the Gorgô is one as well as three, is shown clearly by a writer as late as</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_51"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 51</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Euripides. <a name="fr_203"></a><a href="#fn_203"><font size="1">1</font></a> The home of the Gorgones lies as of course in or beyond the western darkness; <a name="fr_204"></a><a href="#fn_204"><font size="1">2</font></a> with the Euemerism which first connected them with Libyê <a name="fr_205"></a><a href="#fn_205"><font size="1">3</font></a> as a western region, and has subsequently identified them with apes of some kind, gorilla or ourang-oötan, I am not here concerned. An early Vase shows the solar Herakles, who for the purpose is the equivalent of the solar Perseus, 'killing the threefold Gorgon.' <a name="fr_206"></a><a href="#fn_206"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>As Hekatê is Perseis or Perseia <a name="fr_207"></a><a href="#fn_207"><font size="1">5</font></a> and daughter of Perses, so Hekatos is Perseus, 'the solar hero, son of Zeus (heaven), in the form of a gleaming golden shower, and his son Perses is the mythic sire of the Persians, the lords of the "sun-stricken plains" <a name="fr_208"></a><a href="#fn_208"><font size="1">6</font></a> of the East.' <a name="fr_209"></a><a href="#fn_209"><font size="1">7</font></a> Perseus naturally engages to attack the Gorgô as the Lion the Unicorn; and assisted by</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_52"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 52</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Athenê (the Dawn-light) and Hermes (the Wind-power upon the clouds <a name="fr_210"></a><a href="#fn_210"><font size="1">1</font></a>), sets forth upon the perilous expedition. The helmet of Hades ('the Unseen') renders him invisible, <i>i.e.</i> the condition of the Nocturnal-sun as concealed in the Underworld; and from the two Graiai <a name="fr_211"></a><a href="#fn_211"><font size="1">2</font></a> he seizes the solar eye <a name="fr_212"></a><a href="#fn_212"><font size="1">3</font></a> and lunar tooth, <a name="fr_213"></a><a href="#fn_213"><font size="1">4</font></a> which he will not restore until they tell him where to find the implements necessary to complete his task. This eye and tooth the sisters are wont to hand from one to the other, <i>i.e.</i> from morn to eve, from eve to morn. The hero having obtained the other requisites, 'Hermes added the knife (<i>harpê</i>) with which he had cut off the head of Argos;' <a name="fr_214"></a><a href="#fn_214"><font size="1">5</font></a> and this same potency which put out the starry eyes, now puts out the lunar eye, or, to change the imagery, cuts off the bright head of the dark Gorgô; but the light veiled for a moment, soon reappears on the <i>aigis</i> of the</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_53"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 53</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Dawn-queen. The Sun has done the deedtechnically called the Gorgotomybut he has to fly, pursued by Euryalê, the Reappearing-moon; and Stheinô, whom Sir G. W. Cox well describes as 'the eternal abyss of darkness.' <a name="fr_215"></a><a href="#fn_215"><font size="1">1</font></a> The petrifying stare of Medousa is the moon-glare on the darkness when the colour, sound, and motion of the world of day have gone.</p>  
 <p>This myth alone might well form the subject of a monograph, but I can "here only notice one other of its many incidentsthe weapon of Perseus, the <i>harpê</i>, in shape a sickle or scimetar. Now the tradition that this was the special weapon used on the occasion, is a very ancient one, for Pherekydes, B.C. 540, who 'according to the concurrent testimony of antiquity was the teacher of Pythagoras,' and 'did not receive instruction from any master but obtained his knowledge from the secret books of the Phoenicians,' <a name="fr_216"></a><a href="#fn_216"><font size="1">2</font></a> expressly names it as used by Perseus in the Gorgotomy. <a name="fr_217"></a><a href="#fn_217"><font size="1">3</font></a> It is the same 'portentous sickle' (πελώριον ἅρπην) which Kronos took in his right hand when he assailed Ouranos, <a name="fr_218"></a><a href="#fn_218"><font size="1">4</font></a> for one of 'his peculiar adjuncts is the crescent-shaped sickle, which he somewhat singularly holds over his head in a scene where he is</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_54"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 54</font></a></p>  
 <p>receiving from Rhea the stone supposed to be Zeus.' <a name="fr_219"></a><a href="#fn_219"><font size="1">1</font></a> Lastly, we find that 'the scimetar with which Merodach [or Bel] is armed [when about to fight with the Dragon] is shown by the cylinders and bas-reliefs to have been of the shape of a sickle, and is therefore [as had also occurred to me] the same as the <i>harpê</i> or <i>khereb</i> with which the hero Perseus was armed.' <a name="fr_220"></a><a href="#fn_220"><font size="1">2</font></a> Now Bel and Merodach fight against chaos and also against darkness, and the chief weapon of the god who maintains nocturnal kosmic order is, as of course, the sickle-shaped moon. Perseus, in accordance with the Principle of Reduplication above noticed, armed with the crescent-moon cuts off the Gorgô-head or full-moon; just as another mighty Babylonio-Akkadian divinity is described as being armed with the sun. <a name="fr_221"></a><a href="#fn_221"><font size="1">3</font></a> It is evident therefore that Perseus, who was supposed to have slain the sea-monster at Joppa, and who in a passage of Herodotos, difficult to explain, is said to have had a temple and ritual in Egypt, <a name="fr_222"></a><a href="#fn_222"><font size="1">4</font></a> was more or less connected with the non-Aryan East. Lenormant <a name="fr_223"></a><a href="#fn_223"><font size="1">5</font></a> gives an extract from a Babylonian Fragment of which he says, 'Cest le prototype de lhistoire de Persée et dAndromède;' and he thinks that Perseus may be another variant form of the word represented by the Parsoudos of</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_55"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 55</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Ktesias, and is therefore in origin a Babylonian name. Very likely; but I do not doubt that it is also an Aryan name, that is to say, here probably, as in many other instances, an Aryan and a non-Aryan name, of somewhat similar sound, have become united like a double star. The sire of Andromedê, <a name="fr_224"></a><a href="#fn_224"><font size="1">1</font></a> Kepheus the Aithiop king and son of Belos, <a name="fr_225"></a><a href="#fn_225"><font size="1">2</font></a> is a personage altogether non-Aryan and Euphratean; and Hellanikos, B.C. 490-10, chief of the Greek logographers, mentions Kepheus and the Kephenians (Ethiopians or Kushites) in connexion with Babylon. <a name="fr_226"></a><a href="#fn_226"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>Lastly, in the dread Gorgô, originally Darkness + Moon, then more distinctly lunar, we have the origin of the myth of the Face in the Moon. We know otherwise that this myth was archaic, for Epigenes of Sikyôn, 'the most ancient writer of tragedy,' <a name="fr_227"></a><a href="#fn_227"><font size="1">4</font></a> in a lost work called <i>The Poetry of Orpheus</i>, says that the Theologer called 'the moon Gorgonian on account of the face in it;' <a name="fr_228"></a><a href="#fn_228"><font size="1">5</font></a> and Serapiôn, an Alexandrine physician of the third century B.C., thought that 'the Face seen in the moon is the soul of the Sibylla.' <a name="fr_229"></a><a href="#fn_229"><font size="1">6</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_56"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 56</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> According to the doctrine set forth by Plutarch, <a name="fr_230"></a><a href="#fn_230"><font size="1">1</font></a> evil souls, on attempting to enter the tranquil lunar region, are driven away by the dread Face in the Orb. <a name="fr_231"></a><a href="#fn_231"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>With respect to Gorgonian art, Sir G. Wilkinson is of opinion that 'the monster Medusa evidently derived its form from the common Typhonian figure of Egypt;' <a name="fr_232"></a><a href="#fn_232"><font size="1">3</font></a> and M. Clermont-Ganneau, in a most interesting work, has elaborated a theory which connects a beautiful female Gorgon with Hathor and Tanit, and a hideous male Gorgon with the Kamic Bes. <a name="fr_233"></a><a href="#fn_233"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>Speaking of Etruscan temple-tombs, Mr. Dennis observes, 'The pediments terminate on each side in a volute, within which is a grim, grinning face, <i>with prominent teeth</i>, a Gorgon's head, a common sepulchral decoration.' <a name="fr_234"></a><a href="#fn_234"><font size="1">5</font></a> On the hollowed bottom of the famous Etruscan bronze lamp in the Museum of Cortona is 'a huge Gorgon's face, all horror. The visage of a fiend, <i>with eyes starting from their sockets, a mouth stretched to its utmost, with gnashing tusks</i>and the whole rendered more terrible by a wreath of serpents bristling around it.' <a name="fr_235"></a><a href="#fn_235"><font size="1">6</font></a> Well may Mr. Dennis add, 'It is a libel on the fair face of Dian, to say that this hideous visage symbolises the moon.' This difficulty I have fully explained.</p>  
 <p>On the ceiling of a chamber in the cemetery of Perugia is 'an enormous Gorgon's head, hewn from</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_57"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 57</font></a></p>  
 <p>the dark rock, with eyes upturned in horror, gleaming from the gloom, <i>teeth bristling whitely in the open mouth</i>, wings on the temples, and snakes knotted over the brow.' <a name="fr_236"></a><a href="#fn_236"><font size="1">1</font></a> The Etruscans evidently fully shared in the Akkadian horror of darkness.</p>  
 <p>On the back of the late Mr. Cooper's edition of Lenormant's <i>Chaldean Magic</i> is represented (I presume from some Chaldean original) a Gorgoneion, apparently a black face, radiate, with wide and open grinning mouth. This presents a remarkable combination of moon and darkness.</p>  
 <p>Greek vases were occasionally moulded in the shape of the leg of Gorgô. <a name="fr_237"></a><a href="#fn_237"><font size="1">2</font></a> A Vase in the British Museum <a name="fr_238"></a><a href="#fn_238"><font size="1">3</font></a> shows a Gorgô in connexion with Lions. She holds upon either side a lion by the fore paw; the lions standing on their hind legs, fling back their heads. The design may of course be mere sportive art, but it appears to be Assyrian in origin <a name="fr_239"></a><a href="#fn_239"><font size="1">4</font></a> and may signify the Gorgonian Night stationed harmoniously between two leonine Days.</p>  
 <p>Another Vase <a name="fr_240"></a><a href="#fn_240"><font size="1">5</font></a> shows Perseus, wearing the <i>petasos</i> and <i>talaria</i>, plunging the <i>harpê</i>, which he holds in his right hand, into the neck of the Gorgô, who has four wings, two snakes on each side of her head, and two round her waist. 'Her face has the usual Gorgon type, with curls symmetrically ranged [an Assyrian characteristic], and a wide, open mouth showing the</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_58"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 58</font></a></p>  
 <p>teeth and tongue.' Another Vase <a name="fr_241"></a><a href="#fn_241"><font size="1">1</font></a> shows the rare design of Perseus flying over the Libyan mountains, pursued by Stheinô and Euryalê. 'The wild pursuit of the immortal Gorgons seems to be the chase of Darkness after the bright Sun who, with his golden sandals, just escapes their grasp as he soars into the peaceful morning sky.' <a name="fr_242"></a><a href="#fn_242"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>In Canon Spano's very interesting work, <i>Mnemosine Sarda ossia Ricordi e memorie di varii Monumenti Antichi con altre rarita dell isola de Sardegna</i> (Cagliari, 1864), several good examples of the Gorgon-type are given, the most remarkable of which shows three Gorgon-faces radiate, with open mouths and protruded tongues, in a circlethe lunar orb. Here the three Gorgon sisters are connected with the one Moon.</p>
