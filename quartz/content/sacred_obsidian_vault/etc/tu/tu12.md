 <p><a name="page_69"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 69</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">SECTION X</h3>  <h4 align="center">ASPECTS OF THE MOON.</h4>  
 <p>LIGHT being pleasant to man and Darkness more or less awful, the original aspect of the Moon is a friendly and favourable one as the head of nocturnal kosmic order, the beneficent Unicorn, the 'Righteous' Ass of the <i>Bundahis</i>, who is hated and warred against by the powers of evil. But the Moon may be the friend as well as the enemy of Night, and as such becomes Gorgonian and terrific, connected with witchcraft, evil demons, 'wicked apparitions,' <a name="fr_279"></a><a href="#fn_279"><font size="1">1</font></a> and all the power and horror of great darkness; whilst its changing form admits of monstrous concrete representation in art and fancy.</p>  
 <p>With reference to the Sun; the Moon may with almost equal propriety appear as the sire, mother, brother, sister, husband, bride or nurse of the mighty star; friendly to the Sun, as Inô or Sin; hostile as the Unicorn; pursued by or pursuing the Sun.</p>  
 <p>When civilization progresses sufficiently to possess a Calendar, the Moon, as time-measurer, lends invaluable assistance, and marks the months.</p>  
 <p>As lord of moisture and humidity, the Moon is</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_70"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 70</font></a></p>  
 <p>connected with growth and the nurturing power of the peaceful night.</p>  
 <p>The Moon too, like the Sun, speaks of a future life, so that even the rude Congo Negro claps his hands and cries, 'So may I renew my life as thou art renewed;' <a name="fr_280"></a><a href="#fn_280"><font size="1">1</font></a> and in the famous Namaqua-myth 'the Moon once sent the Hare to Men to give this message, "Like as I die and rise to life again, so you also shall die and rise to life again."' <a name="fr_281"></a><a href="#fn_281"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>According to the anthropomorphic principle the Moon appears in male or female form, and is symbolically connected with the Bull or Cow, Unicorn or Horse, Serpent, Dog and Cat, with the latter animal probably on account of phenomena of periodicity, cats eyes shining in the dark, etc. <a name="fr_282"></a><a href="#fn_282"><font size="1">3</font></a> It is also at times a pearl or a good fairy. <a name="fr_283"></a><a href="#fn_283"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>Regarded as a locality, it often appears as the abode of departed souls. So in the Kamic <i>Book of the Respirations</i>, which is probably of the epoch of the Ptolemies, the wish is expressed respecting the deceased,</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">'That his soul may rise to heaven in the disk of the Moon.' <a name="fr_284"></a><a href="#fn_284"><font size="1">5</font></a></font></dir>  
 <p>Such are some of the principal mythological lunar aspects. If the savage at times regards her</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_71"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 71</font></a></p>  
 <p>as cleft in sunder by the angry sun, the poet at times also has his mere fanciesfancies as distinguished from the ordinary growths of mythologyand compares her to a lunatic and dying lady, tottering forth</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">                             'Led by the insane<br> And feeble wanderings of her fading brain.' <a name="fr_285"></a><a href="#fn_285"><font size="1">1</font></a></font></dir>  
 <p>But in health we do not speak thus, and so to this same great singer in a nobler moment she is an orbed maiden with white fire [white gold] laden.'</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_07100"></a>
 <img src="img/07100.jpg" alt="FIG. 4"></p>  
 <p>More grandly did Milton see her, in his stately vision, throwing 'her silver mantle oer the dark,' even as Homer and Tennyson saw</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">                  'The stars about the moon<br> Look beautiful, when all the winds are laid,<br> And every height comes out, and jutting peak<br> And valley, and the immeasurable heavens<br> Break open to their highest.'</font></dir>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_72"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 72</font></a></p>  
 <p>I append two Figures <a name="fr_286"></a><a href="#fn_286"><font size="1">1</font></a> illustrating the origin of the terms Caput and Cauda Draconis as applied to the moon's nodes (knots), or the two points in the heavens where the moon's orbit intersects the plane of the ecliptic.</p>  
 <p>The circling path of the sun becomes similarly the</p>

 <img src="img/07200.jpg" alt="FIG. 5."></p>
