 <p>This is a book of essays by a Zen Buddhist Abbot who visited the
 United States in 1905-6, translated by another figure who was instrumental
 in introducing Buddhism to the West, Daisetz Teitaro Suzuki.
 Originally titled <cite>Sermons of a Buddhist Abbot</cite>, it has been
 reprinted in numerous editions as <cite>Zen for Americans</cite> and is
 currently in print under that title.</p>
 <p>
 The book includes a translation of
 <cite>The Sutra of Forty-Two Chapters</cite>,
 which was the first Buddhist document translated into Chinese, and which
 had a huge influence on the development of Zen.
 Many of the essays are introductions to various Buddhist topics for
 Christians, and all of them are ideal for a general audience.
 The two essays which close out the book which discuss the
 Buddhist attitude towards war and peace, and are of historical interest
 because they were referenced by Leo Tolstoy in his anti-war declaration.
 </p>
