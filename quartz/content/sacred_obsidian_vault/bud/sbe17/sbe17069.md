   <h3 align="center">2.</h3>  
 <p>At that time the king of Kâsi <a name="fr_351"></a><a href="#fn_346"><font size="1">2</font></a> sent to <i>G</i>îvaka Komârabha<i>k</i><i>k</i>a a woollen garment made half of Benares cloth . . .  <a name="fr_352"></a><a href="#fn_347"><font size="1">3</font></a>. Then <i>G</i>îvaka Komârabha<i>k</i><i>k</i>a</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_196"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 196</font></a></p>  
 <p>took that woollen garment made half of Benares cloth and went to the place where the Blessed One was; having approached him, and respectfully saluted the Blessed One, he sat down near him. Sitting near him, <i>G</i>îvaka Komârabha<i>k</i><i>k</i>a said to the Blessed One: 'Lord, this woollen garment made half of Benares cloth. . . . <a name="fr_353"></a><a href="#fn_348"><font size="1">1</font></a> has been sent to me by the king of Kâsi. May the Blessed One, Lord, accept this woollen garment, which may be to me a long time for a good and a blessing.' The Blessed One accepted that woollen garment.</p>  
 <p>And the Blessed One taught (&amp;c., as in chap. I, 34, down to:) and went away.</p>  
 <p>And the Blessed One, after having delivered a religious discourse in consequence of that, thus addressed the Bhikkhus:</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to use woollen garments.'</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_346"></a><a href="sbe17069.htm#fr_351">195:2</a> Buddhaghosa: 'This king was Pasenadi's brother, the same father's son.' He appears to have been a sub-king of Pasenadi, for in the Lohi<i>k</i><i>k</i>a-sutta it is stated that Pasenadi's rule extended both over Kâsi and Kosala ('Râ<i>g</i>â Pasenadi Kosalo Kâsikosala<i>m</i> a<i>g</i><i>gh</i>âvasati').</p>
 <p><a name="fn_347"></a><a href="sbe17069.htm#fr_352">195:3</a> Our translation of a<i>d</i><i>dh</i>akâsika<i>m</i> kambala<i>m</i> is merely <a name="page_196"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 196</font></a> conjectural. Buddhaghosa has the following note: 'A<i>d</i><i>dh</i>akâsiya<i>m</i>, here kâsi means one thousand; a thing that is worth one thousand, is called kâsiya. This garment was worth five hundred; therefore it is called a<i>d</i><i>dh</i>akâsiya. And for the same reason it is said, upa<i>d</i><i>dh</i>akâsina<i>m</i> khamamâna<i>m</i>.' Perhaps vikâsika<i>m</i> at VI, 15, 5 may have some connection with the word used here.</p>
