 <p><a name="page_62"><font size="1" color="green">p. 62</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER VII</h3>  <h3 align="center">ERRORS IN THE ROMAN TRIALS</h3>  
 <p>Mr. Rollins <a name="fr_97"></a><a href="#fn_97"><font size="1">97</font></a> says Rome had "nothing to do with the arrest of Christ other than to furnish the soldiers to accompany the accusers" and then goes on in a homiletical style, to review the public career of Jesus as a background for the tria1, <a name="fr_98"></a><a href="#fn_98"><font size="1">98</font></a> and after a brief discussion of the Sanhedrists and their actions, <a name="fr_99"></a><a href="#fn_99"><font size="1">99</font></a> devotes the remainder of his book <a name="fr_100"></a><a href="#fn_100"><font size="1">100</font></a> to a discussion of Pilate, Herod and Pilate's wife, in narrative fashion and without citation of authorities. Mr. Chandler <a name="fr_101"></a><a href="#fn_101"><font size="1">101</font></a> devotes considerable space to extraneous discussions of Roman Law and Procedure and concludes that while Pilate commenced the hearings in proper dignity and reached an opinion that would result in an acquittal, he <a href="errata.htm#4">vacillated</a> into compliance with the wishes of the Jewish mob. We confine ourselves to</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_63"><font size="1" color="green">p. 63</font></a></p>  
 <p>technicality as to the issues involved, and refer the reader to Chandler for a more abstract discussion of Roman Jurisprudence.</p>  
 <p>There are two documents in ancient Roman Law that are basic, The Laws of The Twelve Tables <a name="fr_102"></a><a href="#fn_102"><font size="1">102</font></a> and the Code of Justinian. <a name="fr_103"></a><a href="#fn_103"><font size="1">103</font></a> We need concern ourselves only with the former to convict Pilate of legal error in his treatment of Jesus. <a name="fr_104"></a><a href="#fn_104"><font size="1">104</font></a> We will cite table and sentence from the Twelve Tables as we have cited Chapter and Verse from The Bible.</p>  
 <p>The Gospels give the Roman trial considerable mention, <a name="fr_105"></a><a href="#fn_105"><font size="1">105</font></a> and yet only a few statements are made which we need examine in order to establish that Roman government was supreme over the Sanhedrists and that Christ "was condemned to death in the reign of Tiberius by the Procurator Pontius Pilate." <a name="fr_106"></a><a href="#fn_106"><font size="1">106</font></a></p>  
 <p>NowWhat does the record show and wherein did Pilate err?</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_64"><font size="1" color="green">p. 64</font></a></p>  
 <p>1. Pilate did not hold hearing until dawn, <a name="fr_107"></a><a href="#fn_107"><font size="1">107</font></a> but some say he should have compelled the Sanhedrin to obey the Jewish and Roman law against night trials. <a name="fr_108"></a><a href="#fn_108"><font size="1">108</font></a></p>  
 <p>2. Pilate erred in permitting the Sanhedrists to examine Jesus in private <a name="fr_109"></a><a href="#fn_109"><font size="1">109</font></a> when trials had to be public in those days <a name="fr_110"></a><a href="#fn_110"><font size="1">110</font></a> as now. <a name="fr_111"></a><a href="#fn_111"><font size="1">111</font></a></p>  
 <p>3. Pilate erred in not requiring the testimony of witnesses, Roman Law agreeing with the Jewish and our law on this point. <a name="fr_112"></a><a href="#fn_112"><font size="1">112</font></a></p>  
 <p>4. Pilate should have released Jesus when he found "no harm" in Him. <a name="fr_113"></a><a href="#fn_113"><font size="1">113</font></a> On this point the Law of the Twelve Tables is crystal clear. Archeologists have not found all of the stone fragments of The Twelve Tables, but Professor Conant explains that all have been collated by Bruns from other sources and</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_65"><font size="1" color="green">p. 65</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Conant translates as Table IX, verse 6, "The decrees of the Twelve Tables forbid any uncondemned man whomever be put to death." <a name="fr_114"></a><a href="#fn_114"><font size="1">114</font></a> All of the best writers agree that Pilate, finding no harm in Jesus, should have released him then and there. <a name="fr_115"></a><a href="#fn_115"><font size="1">115</font></a></p>
