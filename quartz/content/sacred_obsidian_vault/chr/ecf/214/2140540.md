 <p> <a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii"></a>
 </p><p class="c34"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p1"></a> <a name="page_450"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 450</font></a> <a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-Page_450"></a><span class="c4"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p1.1"></a>Canon XVI.</span></p> 
 <p class="c35"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p2"></a>That no bishop, presbyter or deacon should be a “conductor;” and that Readers should take wives; and that the clergy should abstain from usury; and at what age they or virgins should be consecrated.</p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p3"></a><span class="sc"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p3.1"></a>Likewise</span> it seemed good that bishops, presbyters, and deacons should not be “conductors” or “procurators;” nor seek their food by any base and vile business, for they should remember how it is written, “No man fighting for God cumbereth himself with worldly affairs.”</p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p4"></a>Also it seemed good that Readers when they come to years of puberty, should be compelled either to take wives or else to profess continence.</p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p5"></a>Likewise it seemed good that if a clergyman had lent money he should get it back again, but if kind (<i>speciem</i>) he should receive back the same kind as he gave.</p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p6"></a>And that younger than twenty-five years deacons should not be ordained, nor virgins consecrated.</p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p7"></a>And that readers should not salute the people.</p> 
 <p class="c31"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p8"></a><span class="c4"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p8.1"></a>Notes.</span></p> 
 <p class="c36"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p9"></a><span class="sc"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p9.1"></a>Ancient Epitome of XVI.</span></p> 
 <p class="c35"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p10"></a>A bishop, presbyter, and deacon may not be a “conductor” or a “procurator.”  A reader when he comes to puberty must contract marriage or profess continence.</p> 
 <p class="c39"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p11"></a>A cleric who has lent to someone, what he gave let him receive, or as much.</p> 
 <p class="c39"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p12"></a>Let not him be a deacon, who is made a deacon being under twenty-five.</p> 
 <p class="c39"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p13"></a>And let not readers salute the people.</p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p14"></a>This canon is made up of Canons xv., xviij., and xxj., and added to these Canon j. of the same Second Series of the synod of Hippo, <span class="sc"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p14.1"></a>a.d.</span> 393.</p> 
 <p class="c36"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p15"></a><span class="sc"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p15.1"></a>Johnson.</span></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p16"></a>Zonaras says this was never observed anywhere but in Africa.  See Can. Afr. 19 (27).</p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="xv.iv.iv.xvii-p17"></a>Du Pin turns the Latin, <i>saluto</i>, by “addressing his speech to the people.”</p>
