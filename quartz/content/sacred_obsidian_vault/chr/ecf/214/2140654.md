 </p><p class="c34"><a name="xv.iv.iv.cxxxi-p1"></a> <a name="page_505"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 505</font></a> <a name="xv.iv.iv.cxxxi-Page_505"></a><span class="c4"><a name="xv.iv.iv.cxxxi-p1.1"></a>Canon CXXX.  (Greek cxxxi.)</span></p> 
 <p class="c35"><a name="xv.iv.iv.cxxxi-p2"></a>That he who has failed to prove one charge shall not be allowed to give evidence to another.</p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="xv.iv.iv.cxxxi-p3"></a><span class="sc"><a name="xv.iv.iv.cxxxi-p3.1"></a>So</span>, too, it seemed good that as often as many crimes were laid to clerics by their accusers, and one of the first examined could not be proved, <a name="fr_483"></a><a href="#fn_483"><font size="1">484</font></a>
  they should not be allowed to go on giving evidence on the other counts.</p> 
 <p class="c36"><a name="xv.iv.iv.cxxxi-p5"></a><span class="sc"><a name="xv.iv.iv.cxxxi-p5.1"></a>Ancient Epitome of Canon CXXX.</span></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="xv.iv.iv.cxxxi-p6"></a><i>He who makes many accusations and proves nothing [is not to give witness].</i></p>
