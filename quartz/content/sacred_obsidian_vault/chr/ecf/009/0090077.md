 <p class="c33"><a name="viii.i-p3"></a><span class="c10"><a name="viii.i-p3.1"></a>Introduction.</span></p> 
 <p class="Centered"><a name="viii.i-p4"></a> ————————————</p> 
 <p class="c12"><a name="viii.i-p5"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.i-p5.1"></a>The</span> translation is from Mr. M. R. Jamess text in <i>Texts and Studies</i>, ii. 3, p. 130ff., published from a fifteenth century <span class="sc"><a name="viii.i-p5.2"></a>ms.</span> in the Bodleian Library.  The original, Mr. James conjecturally assigns to the tenth or eleventh century.  It is notable for its close resemblance in several passages to 4 Esdras, to the Greek original of which the author seems to have had direct access.</p> 
 <p class="c13"><a name="viii.i-p6"></a>Like the Apocalypse of Esdras it deals with the subject of intercession for sinners and the reluctance of the seer to die.  The parallel passages in 4 Esdras and the Apocalypse of Esdras are pointed out in the margin of the translation.</p> 
 <p class="c13"><a name="viii.i-p7"></a>Chapter I. consists of a few lines from the beginning and end of a homily on love which appears in the <span class="sc"><a name="viii.i-p7.1"></a>ms.</span> at the beginning of the Apocalypse, but which Mr. James regards as “quite unimportant and quite irrelevant.”</p>
