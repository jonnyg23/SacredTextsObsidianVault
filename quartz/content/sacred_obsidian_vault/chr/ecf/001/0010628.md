 <p> <a name="viii.vi.xviii"></a>  </p><h3 align="center"><a name="viii.vi.xviii-p0.1"></a>Chapter XVIII.—Testimony of Sophocles.</h3>  
 <p><a name="viii.vi.xviii-p1"></a><a name="viii.vi.xviii-p1.1"></a>And if it is needful that we add testimonies concerning one God, even from the dramatists, hear even Sophocles speaking thus:—</p>  <blockquote><a name="viii.vi.xviii-p1.2"></a>“There is one God, in truth there is but one,<br>  Who made the heavens and the broad earth beneath,<br>  The glancing waves of ocean and the winds<br>  But many of us mortals err in heart,<br>  And set up for a solace in our woes<br>  Images of the gods in stone and wood,<br>  Or figures carved in brass or ivory,<br>  And, furnishing for these our handiworks,<br>  Both sacrifice and rite magnificent,<br>  We think that thus we do a pious work.”<br> </blockquote>  
 <p><a name="viii.vi.xviii-p2"></a>Thus, then, Sophocles.</p>
