 <p> <a name="ix.ix.vii"></a>  </p><h4 align="center"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p0.1"></a>Elucidations.</h4>  <h5 align="center"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p0.2"></a>I.</h5>  
 <p class="Header"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p1"></a>(The Bidding Prayer, etc., p. 485.)  </p>  <h4 align="center"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p1.2"></a>The Pauline Norm. <a name="fr_3826"></a><a href="#fn_3826"><font size="1">3827</font></a>
 </h4>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p3"></a>1. <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p3.1"></a>Supplications</span>.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p4"></a>2. <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p4.1"></a>Prayers, Psalms, Hymns, and Spiritual Songs</span>.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p5"></a>3. <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p5.1"></a>Intercessions</span>.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p6"></a>4. <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p6.1"></a>General Thanksgiving</span>. The Kiss of Peace.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p7"></a>5. <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p7.1"></a>Anaphora.</span> <a name="fr_3827"></a><a href="#fn_3827"><font size="1">3828</font></a>
    </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p9"></a>The Lord <a name="ix.ix.vii-p9.1"></a>Jesus the same night in which He was betrayed took bread:  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p10"></a>And when He had given thanks, He brake it,  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p11"></a>And said, Take, eat: this is my Body, which is broken for you:  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p12"></a>This do in remembrance of Me.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p13"></a>After the same manner also He took the cup, when He had supped,  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p14"></a>Saying, This cup is the New Testament in my Blood:  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p15"></a>This do ye, as oft as ye drink it, in remembrance of Me.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p16"></a>For as often as ye eat this Bread, and drink this Cup, ye do show the Lords death till He come.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p17"></a>6. <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p17.1"></a>Our Father</span>, etc. <a name="fr_3828"></a><a href="#fn_3828"><font size="1">3829</font></a>
    </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p19"></a>7. <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p19.1"></a>Communion</span>.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20"></a>Let us note also that the Apostle had “delivered” unto the Corinthians (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/co1011.htm#023">1 Cor. xi. 23</a><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.1"></a>), as doubtless to others (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/co1007.htm#017">1 Cor. vii. 17</a><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.2"></a>), certain institutions which he <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.3"></a>ordained</span> in all the churches, and for departing from which he censures the Corinthians in this place (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/co1007.htm#017">I Cor. vii. 17</a><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.4"></a> compared with <a href="../../../bib/kjv/co1007.htm#002">I Cor. vii. 2</a><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.5"></a>) in certain particulars. In chap. <a href="../../../bib/kjv/co1014.htm#040">I Cor. xiv. 40.</a><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.6"></a>, he refers to these ordinances as a <span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.7"></a>τα</span><span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.8"></a>́</span><span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.9"></a>ξις</span>, in the performance of which they were to proceed (<span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.10"></a>κοσμι</span><span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.11"></a>́</span><span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.12"></a>ως</span>) with due order, <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p20.13"></a>becomingly</span>; not with mere decency, but with a beautiful decorum of service.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p21"></a>Finally, let me suggest that there are fragments of the Apostles (<span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p21.1"></a>παρα</span><span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p21.2"></a>́</span><span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p21.3"></a>δοσεις</span>) instructions everywhere scattered through his Epistles, such as the minute canon <a name="fr_3829"></a><a href="#fn_3829"><font size="1">3830</font></a>
  concerning the veiling of women in acts of worship, insisting upon it with a length of argument which in one of the Apostolic Fathers would be considered childish. He also insisted that his <span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p22.2"></a>τα</span><span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p22.3"></a>́</span><span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p22.4"></a>ξις</span> is from the Lord. <a name="page_507"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 507</font></a> <a name="ix.ix.vii-Page_507"></a>  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p23"></a>Fragments of the primitive hymns are also scattered through the Apostles writings, as, e.g.,—  </p>  <blockquote><a name="ix.ix.vii-p23.1"></a>   <span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p23.3"></a>́Εγειραι ὁ καθεύδων,</span><br>    <span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p23.5"></a>καὶ ἀνάστα ἐκ τω̑ν νεκρω̑ν</span><br>    <span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p23.7"></a>καὶ ἐπιφαύσει σοι ὀ Χριστός.</span> <a name="fr_3830"></a><a href="#fn_3830"><font size="1">3831</font></a>
  <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="Continue"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p25"></a>Of such passages the formula (<span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p25.1"></a>διὸ λέγει</span>) “It saith” seems to be a frequent index.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p26"></a>May we not conclude also that the sublime prayer and doxology of <a href="../../../bib/kjv/eph003.htm#014">Eph. iii. 14-21</a><a name="ix.ix.vii-p26.1"></a> is a quotation from the Apostles own eucharistic <span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p26.2"></a>τάξις</span> for the whole state of Christs Church militant?  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p27"></a>Might not the same be more constantly used in our days as an intercession for the whole flock of the one Shepherd?  </p>  <h5 align="center"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p27.1"></a>II.</h5>  
 <p class="Header"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p28"></a>(Fulfil His constitution, p. 489.)  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p29"></a>The Pauline Norm being borne in mind, we shall best comprehend this <a name="ix.ix.vii-p29.1"></a>Clementine liturgy, as to its primitive claims, by taking the testimony of <a name="ix.ix.vii-p29.2"></a>Justin, writing in Rome to the Antonines <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p29.3"></a>a. d.</span>160. Referring to the <a name="ix.ix.vii-p29.4"></a>Apology in our first volume, we observe that the order kept up in his day was this:—  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p30"></a>1. Prayers for all estates of men.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p31"></a>2. The kiss of peace.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p32"></a>3. Oblation of bread and wine.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p33"></a>4. Thanksgiving.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p34"></a>5. Words of institution.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p35"></a>6. The prayer ending with <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p35.1"></a>Amen</span>.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p36"></a>7. Communion.  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p37"></a>Now, a century later, we may suppose the <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p37.1"></a>original</span> of this <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p37.2"></a>Clementine</span> to have taken a fuller shape; of which still later this <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p37.3"></a>Clementine</span> is the product. <a name="fr_3831"></a><a href="#fn_3831"><font size="1">3832</font></a>
   </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p39"></a>Bear in mind that the early Roman use was (Greek) borrowed wholly from the East; <a name="fr_3832"></a><a href="#fn_3832"><font size="1">3833</font></a>
  and, comparing the testimony of <a name="ix.ix.vii-p40.3"></a>Justin with the Pauline Norm, may we not suppose that this norm in Rome was augmented by the <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p40.4"></a>Eastern uses</span>, and so preserves a true name in that of the first Bishop of Rome, who accepted it from Jerusalem or Antioch?  </p>  <h5 align="center"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p40.5"></a>III.</h5>  
 <p class="Header"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p41"></a>(That He may show this bread, etc., p. 489.)  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p42"></a>From a recent essay by Dr. <a name="ix.ix.vii-p42.1"></a>Williams, the erudite bishop of Connecticut, I am permitted to cite, as follows:—  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p43"></a>  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p44"></a>Compare the original texts thus:—  </p>     
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p45"></a><span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p45.1"></a>Clementine.</span> <a name="fr_3833"></a><a href="#fn_3833"><font size="1">3834</font></a>
   </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p47"></a><span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p47.1"></a>Irenæus.</span> <a name="fr_3834"></a><a href="#fn_3834"><font size="1">3835</font></a>
   </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p49"></a>  </p>    
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p50"></a><span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p50.1"></a>ὀπως αποφηνῃ τοὸν ὰρτον του̑τον σω̑μα του̑ Χριστου̑ σου καὶ τὸ ποτήριον του̑τον αἰμα του̑ Χριστου̑ σου ινα οἱ μεταλαβόντες, κ.τ.λ.</span>  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p51"></a><span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p51.1"></a>ὅ</span><span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p51.2"></a>πως ἀποφηνῃ τὴν θυσίαν ταύτην, καὶ τὸν ἄ́ρτον σω̑μα του̑ Χριστου̑, καὶ τὸ ποτήριον τὸ αἰμα του̑ Χριστου̑ ἴνα οἱ μεταλαβόντες, κ.τ.λ.</span>  </p>     
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p52"></a><a name="page_508"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 508</font></a> <a name="ix.ix.vii-Page_508"></a>  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p53"></a>Bishop <a name="ix.ix.vii-p53.1"></a>Williams then proceeds to inquire:—  </p>  
 <p class="Blockquote"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p54"></a> “How is this striking agreement to be explained? Does <a name="ix.ix.vii-p54.1"></a>Irenæus quote from the Clementine, or the Clementine from him? Or is it not much more likely that they are independent witnesses to primitive uses, going back to the period of the persecutions, and extending far beyond the limits of Syria or Palestine?” <a name="fr_3835"></a><a href="#fn_3835"><font size="1">3836</font></a>
    </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p56"></a>I shall recur to these passages in the elucidations to <a name="ix.ix.vii-p56.1"></a>Early Liturgies (<span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p56.2"></a>infra</span>): but here I beg the reader to consult <a name="ix.ix.vii-p56.3"></a>Pfaff, to whom we owe the discovery of the fragment cited from <a name="ix.ix.vii-p56.4"></a>Irenæus; also <a name="ix.ix.vii-p56.5"></a>Grabe, in the same volume of <a name="ix.ix.vii-p56.6"></a>Pfaff, whom I have already introduced to the reader. <a name="fr_3836"></a><a href="#fn_3836"><font size="1">3837</font></a>
    </p>  <h5 align="center"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p57.5"></a>Postscript.</h5>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p58"></a><span class="sc"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p58.1"></a>The</span> American editor had been promised the aid of his beloved friend the Rev. Dr. <a name="ix.ix.vii-p58.2"></a>Hobart in the elucidation of the liturgies; but a sudden and almost fatal prostration of his health has deprived the reader of the admirable comments with which he would have enriched these pages, had Providence permitted.   </p>

For this we have the important testimony of <a name="ix.ix.vii-p18.2"></a>Gregory the Great, as preserved to his day: that the Apostles (SS. <a name="ix.ix.vii-p18.3"></a>Peter and <a name="ix.ix.vii-p18.4"></a>Paul must have been primarily in his mind, of course) delivered no other “custom” to the churches (i.e., as essential) than the words of Institution and the Lords Prayer. He says:—“Orationem Dominicam, <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p18.5"></a>mox post precem</span>, dicimus, quia <span><a name="ix.ix.vii-p18.6"></a>mos Apostolorum erat</span>, ad ipsam solummodo orationem oblationis hostiam consecrare.”—<a name="ix.ix.vii-p18.7"></a>Epist. ad Joann. Now, for the sense of <span class="Foreign"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p18.11"></a>post precem</span> in the above, we have <a name="ix.ix.vii-p18.12"></a>Justin Martyr for a primitive witness of Roman usage. He speaks of the words of Institution expressly (vol. i. cap. lxvi. p. 185) as “the Prayer of the Logos” (<span class="Greek"><a name="ix.ix.vii-p18.14"></a>δι΄ ἐυχῆς Λόγου</span>), in the use of which he makes the essential act of the Oblation to consist. Liturgic fulness may or may not require more, but the essentials are thus simple. So far, the Roman Missal to this day sustains the words of <a name="ix.ix.vii-p18.15"></a>Gregory. It is overloaded with ceremonial, but does not include the noble features on which the Greeks lay so great stress: i.e., the conjoint Oblation and Invocation. See <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pe1002.htm#005">1 Pet. ii. 5</a><a name="ix.ix.vii-p18.16"></a>.  </p>
