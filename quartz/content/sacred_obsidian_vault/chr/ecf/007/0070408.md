 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <a name="fn_2368"></a><a href="0070408.htm#fr_2368">377:2369</a> <a name="viii.iii-p1.3"></a>  
 <p class="Footnote"><a name="viii.iii-p2"></a> <span><a name="viii.iii-p2.1"></a>The longer title is supposed to be the original one; the shorter, a popular abridgment. The latter has no real connection with </span><a href="../../../bib/kjv/act002.htm#042">Acts ii. 42</a><a name="viii.iii-p2.2"></a><span><a name="viii.iii-p2.3"></a>. Many hold that the term “nations” (or “Gentiles”) points to a Jewish Christian as the author (so </span><a name="viii.iii-p2.4"></a>Bryennios<span><a name="viii.iii-p2.5"></a>), though this is denied by others (so </span><a name="viii.iii-p2.6"></a>Brown<span><a name="viii.iii-p2.7"></a>). A similar diversity of opinion exists as to the class of readers; but, if the early date is accepted, the more probable theory is, that the first part at least of the manual was for the instruction of catechumens of Gentile birth (so </span><a name="viii.iii-p2.8"></a>Bryennios<span><a name="viii.iii-p2.9"></a>, </span><a name="viii.iii-p2.10"></a>Schaff<span><a name="viii.iii-p2.11"></a>). Others extend it to Gentile Christians.</span>  </p>
