 </p><p class="c26"><a name="ix.clxxxvii-p1"></a>  <span class="c18"><a name="ix.clxxxvii-p1.1"></a>Letter CLXXXVI. <a name="fr_2604"></a><a href="#fn_2604"><font size="1">2605</font></a>
 </span></p>  
 <p class="c55"><a name="ix.clxxxvii-p3"></a><i>To Antipater, the governor</i>. <a name="fr_2605"></a><a href="#fn_2605"><font size="1">2606</font></a>
 </p>  
 <p class="c20"><a name="ix.clxxxvii-p5"></a><span class="c14"><a name="ix.clxxxvii-p5.1"></a>Philosophy</span> is an excellent thing, if only for this, that it even heals its disciples at small cost; for, in philosophy, the same thing is both dainty and healthy fare.  I am told that you have recovered your failing appetite by pickled cabbage.  Formerly I used to dislike it, both on account of the proverb, <a name="fr_2606"></a><a href="#fn_2606"><font size="1">2607</font></a>
  and because it reminded me of the poverty that went with it.  Now, however, I am driven to change my mind.  I laugh at the proverb when I see that cabbage is such a “good nursing mother of men,” <a name="fr_2607"></a><a href="#fn_2607"><font size="1">2608</font></a>
  and has restored our governor to the vigour of youth.  For the future I shall think nothing like cabbage, not even Homers lotus, <a name="fr_2608"></a><a href="#fn_2608"><font size="1">2609</font></a>
  not even that ambrosia, <a name="fr_2609"></a><a href="#fn_2609"><font size="1">2610</font></a>
  whatever it was, which fed the Olympians.</p>
