 </p><p class="c47"><a name="iv.vi.ii.xxxviii-p1"></a>  <span class="c11"><a name="iv.vi.ii.xxxviii-p1.1"></a>Chapter XXXVIII</span>.—<i>In what Manner Requests should be made for these.</i></p>  
 <p class="c18"><a name="iv.vi.ii.xxxviii-p2"></a>“<span class="c13"><a name="iv.vi.ii.xxxviii-p2.1"></a>But</span> it is possible that those who have become masters of such property (if it be right or possible to allow them such a title) will assure us by way of apology for their conduct, that it was not in their power to abstain from this appropriation at a time when a spectacle of misery in all its forms everywhere met the view; when men were cruelly driven from their homes, slaughtered without mercy, thrust forth without remorse: when the confiscation of the property of innocent persons was a common thing, and <a name="page_510"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 510</font></a> <a name="iv.vi.ii.xxxviii-Page_510"></a>when persecutions and property seizures were unceasing. If any defend their conduct by such reasons as these, and still persist in their avaricious temper, they shall be made sensible that such a course will bring punishment on themselves, and all the more because this correction of evil is the very characteristic of our service to the Supreme God. So that it will henceforth be dangerous to retain what dire necessity may in time past have compelled men to take; especially because it is in any case incumbent on us to discourage covetous desires, both by persuasion, and by warning examples.</p>
