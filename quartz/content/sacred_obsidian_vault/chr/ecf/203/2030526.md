 <p> <a name="v.iv.vii"></a>
 </p><p class="c46"><a name="v.iv.vii-p1"></a>  <span class="c12"><a name="v.iv.vii-p1.1"></a>Chapter VI.</span></p>  
 <p class="c31"><a name="v.iv.vii-p2"></a><span class="c12"><a name="v.iv.vii-p2.1"></a>Heliodorus <a name="fr_2579"></a><a href="#fn_2579"><font size="1">2580</font></a>
 </span> the Presbyter wrote a book entitled <i>An introductory treatise on the nature of things,</i> in which he showed that the beginning of things was one, that nothing was coaeval with God, that God was not the creator of evil, but in such wise the creator of all good, that matter, which is used for <a name="fr_2580"></a><a href="#fn_2580"><font size="1">2581</font></a>
  evil, was created by God after evil was discovered, and that nothing material whatever can be regarded as established in any other way than by God, and that there was no other creator than God, who, when by His foreknowledge He knew that nature was to be changed, <a name="fr_2581"></a><a href="#fn_2581"><font size="1">2582</font></a>
  warned of punishment.</p>
