 </p><p class="c46"><a name="v.iv.lxxxi-p1"></a>  <span class="c12"><a name="v.iv.lxxxi-p1.1"></a>Chapter LXXX.</span></p>  
 <p class="c31"><a name="v.iv.lxxxi-p2"></a><span class="c12"><a name="v.iv.lxxxi-p2.1"></a>Musaeus, <a name="fr_2711"></a><a href="#fn_2711"><font size="1">2712</font></a>
 </span> presbyter of the church at Marseilles, a man learned in Divine Scriptures and most accurate in their interpretation, as well as master of an excellent scholastic style, on the request of Saint Venerius the bishop, selected from Holy Scriptures passages suited to the various feast days of the year, also passages from the Psalms for responses suited to the season, and the passages for reading. The readers in the church found this work of the greatest value, in that it saved them trouble and anxiety in the selection of passages, and was useful for the instruction of the people as well as for the dignity of the service. He also addressed to Saint Eustathius <a name="fr_2712"></a><a href="#fn_2712"><font size="1">2713</font></a>
  the bishop, successor to the above mentioned man of God, an excellent and sizable volume, a <i>Sacramentary,</i> <a name="fr_2713"></a><a href="#fn_2713"><font size="1">2714</font></a>
  divided into various sections, according to the various offices and seasons, Readings and Psalms, both for reading and chanting, but also filled throughout with petitions to the Lord, <a name="fr_2714"></a><a href="#fn_2714"><font size="1">2715</font></a>
  and thanksgiving for his benefits. By this work we know him to have been a man of strong intelligence and chaste eloquence. He is said to have also delivered homilies, which are, as I know, valued by pious men, but which I have not read. He died in the reign of Leo and Majorianus.</p>
