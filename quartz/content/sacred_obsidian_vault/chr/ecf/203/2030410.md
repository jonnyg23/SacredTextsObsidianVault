 <p> <a name="v.iii.xxix"></a>
 </p><p class="c46"><a name="v.iii.xxix-p1"></a>  <span class="c12"><a name="v.iii.xxix-p1.1"></a>Chapter XXVII.</span></p>  
 <p class="c31"><a name="v.iii.xxix-p2"></a><span class="c12"><a name="v.iii.xxix-p2.1"></a>Dionysius</span>, <a name="fr_2426"></a><a href="#fn_2426"><font size="1">2427</font></a>
  bishop of the church of Corinth, was of so great eloquence and industry that he taught not only the people of his own city and province but also those of other provinces and cities by his letters. Of these one is <i>To the Lacedæmonians,</i> another <i>To the Athenians,</i> a third <i>To the Nicomedians,</i> a fourth <i>To the Cretans,</i> a fifth <i>To the church at Amastrina and to the other churches of Pontus,</i> a sixth <i>To the Gnosians and to Pinytus bishop of the same city,</i> a seventh <i>To the Romans,</i> addressed to Soter their bishop, an eighth <i>To Chrysophora</i> a holy woman. He flourished in the reign of Marcus Antoninus Verus and Lucius Aurelius Commodus.</p>
