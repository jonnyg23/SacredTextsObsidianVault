 <p> <a name="iv.xi.iii"></a>
 </p><p class="c30"><a name="iv.xi.iii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="iv.xi.iii-p1.1"></a>Chapter III.—The Souls Origin Defined Out of the Simple Words of Scripture.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="iv.xi.iii-p2"></a>Would to God that no “heresies had been ever necessary, in order that they which are approved may be made manifest!” <a name="fr_1506"></a><a href="#fn_1506"><font size="1">1507</font></a>
  We should then be never required to try our strength in contests about the soul with philosophers, those patriarchs of heretics, as they may be fairly called. <a name="fr_1507"></a><a href="#fn_1507"><font size="1">1508</font></a>
  The apostle, so far back as his own time, foresaw, indeed, that philosophy would do violent injury to the truth. <a name="fr_1508"></a><a href="#fn_1508"><font size="1">1509</font></a>
  This admonition <i>about false philosophy</i> he was induced to offer after he had been at Athens, had become acquainted with that <i>loquacious</i> city, <a name="fr_1509"></a><a href="#fn_1509"><font size="1">1510</font></a>
  and had there had a taste of its huckstering wiseacres and talkers. In like manner is the treatment of the soul according to the sophistical doctrines of men which “mix their <a name="page_184"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 184</font></a> <a name="iv.xi.iii-Page_184"></a>wine with water.” <a name="fr_1510"></a><a href="#fn_1510"><font size="1">1511</font></a>
  Some of them deny the immortality of the soul; others affirm that it is immortal, and something more. Some raise disputes about its substance; others about its form; others, again, respecting each of its several faculties. One school of philosophers derives its state from various sources, while another ascribes its departure to different destinations. <i>The various schools reflect the character of their masters</i>, according as they have received their impressions from the dignity <a name="fr_1511"></a><a href="#fn_1511"><font size="1">1512</font></a>
  of Plato, or the vigour <a name="fr_1512"></a><a href="#fn_1512"><font size="1">1513</font></a>
  of Zeno, or the equanimity <a name="fr_1513"></a><a href="#fn_1513"><font size="1">1514</font></a>
  of Aristotle, or the stupidity <a name="fr_1514"></a><a href="#fn_1514"><font size="1">1515</font></a>
  of Epicurus, or the sadness <a name="fr_1515"></a><a href="#fn_1515"><font size="1">1516</font></a>
  of Heraclitus, or the madness <a name="fr_1516"></a><a href="#fn_1516"><font size="1">1517</font></a>
  of Empedocles. The fault, I suppose, of the divine doctrine lies in its springing from Judæa <a name="fr_1517"></a><a href="#fn_1517"><font size="1">1518</font></a>
  rather than from Greece. Christ made a mistake, too, in sending forth fishermen to preach, rather than the sophist. Whatever noxious vapours, accordingly, exhaled from philosophy, obscure the clear and wholesome atmosphere of truth, it will be for Christians to clear away, both by shattering to pieces the arguments which are drawn from the principles of things—I mean those of the philosophers—and by opposing to them the maxims of heavenly wisdom—that is, such as are revealed by the Lord; in order that both the pitfalls wherewith philosophy captivates the heathen may be removed, and the means employed by heresy to shake the faith of Christians may be repressed. We have already decided one point in our controversy with Hermogenes, as we said at the beginning of this treatise, when we claimed the soul to be formed by the breathing <a name="fr_1518"></a><a href="#fn_1518"><font size="1">1519</font></a>
  of God, and not out of matter. We relied even there on the clear direction of the inspired statement which informs us how that “the Lord God breathed on mans face the breath of life, so that man became a living soul” <a name="fr_1519"></a><a href="#fn_1519"><font size="1">1520</font></a>
 —by that inspiration of God, of course. On this point, therefore, nothing further need be investigated or advanced by us. It has its own treatise, <a name="fr_1520"></a><a href="#fn_1520"><font size="1">1521</font></a>
  and its own heretic. I shall regard it as my introduction to the other branches of the subject.</p>
