 <p> <a name="vi.iii.x"></a>
 </p><p class="c30"><a name="vi.iii.x-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="vi.iii.x-p1.1"></a>Chapter X.—Of Johns Baptism.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="vi.iii.x-p2"></a>We have spoken, so far as our moderate ability permitted, of the <i>generals</i> which form the groundwork of the sanctity <a name="fr_8635"></a><a href="#fn_8635"><font size="1">8636</font></a>
  of baptism. I will now, equally to the best of my power, proceed to the rest of its character, touching certain minor questions.</p> 
 <p class="c20"><a name="vi.iii.x-p4"></a>The baptism announced by John formed the subject, even at that time, of a question, proposed by the Lord Himself indeed to the Pharisees, whether that baptism were heavenly, or truly earthly: <a name="fr_8636"></a><a href="#fn_8636"><font size="1">8637</font></a>
  about which they were unable to give a consistent <a name="fr_8637"></a><a href="#fn_8637"><font size="1">8638</font></a>
  answer, <a name="page_674"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 674</font></a> <a name="vi.iii.x-Page_674"></a>inasmuch as they understood not, because they believed not. But <i>we</i>, with but as poor a measure of understanding as of faith, <i>are</i> able to determine that that baptism was <i>divine</i> indeed, (yet in respect of the command, not in respect of efficacy <a name="fr_8638"></a><a href="#fn_8638"><font size="1">8639</font></a>
  too, in that we read that John was <i>sent by the Lord</i> to perform this duty,) <a name="fr_8639"></a><a href="#fn_8639"><font size="1">8640</font></a>
  but <i>human</i> in its nature: for it conveyed nothing celestial, but it fore-ministered to things celestial; being, to wit, appointed over <i>repentance</i>, which is in mans power. <a name="fr_8640"></a><a href="#fn_8640"><font size="1">8641</font></a>
  In fact, the doctors of the law and the Pharisees, who were unwilling to “believe,” did not “repent” either. <a name="fr_8641"></a><a href="#fn_8641"><font size="1">8642</font></a>
  But if repentance is a thing human, its baptism must necessarily be of the same nature:  else, if it had been celestial, it would have given both the Holy Spirit and remission of sins. But none either pardons sins or freely grants the Spirit save God only. <a name="fr_8642"></a><a href="#fn_8642"><font size="1">8643</font></a>
  Even the Lord Himself said that the Spirit would not descend on any other condition, but that He should first ascend to the Father. <a name="fr_8643"></a><a href="#fn_8643"><font size="1">8644</font></a>
  What the Lord was not yet conferring, of course the servant could not furnish.  Accordingly, in the Acts of the Apostles, we find that men who had “Johns baptism” had not received the Holy Spirit, whom they knew not even by hearing. <a name="fr_8644"></a><a href="#fn_8644"><font size="1">8645</font></a>
  That, then, was no celestial thing which furnished no celestial (endowments):  whereas the very thing which <i>was</i> celestial in John—the Spirit of prophecy—so completely failed, after the transfer of the whole Spirit to the Lord, that he presently sent to inquire whether He whom he had himself preached, <a name="fr_8645"></a><a href="#fn_8645"><font size="1">8646</font></a>
  whom he had pointed out when coming to him, were “HE.” <a name="fr_8646"></a><a href="#fn_8646"><font size="1">8647</font></a>
  And so “the baptism of repentance” <a name="fr_8647"></a><a href="#fn_8647"><font size="1">8648</font></a>
  was dealt with <a name="fr_8648"></a><a href="#fn_8648"><font size="1">8649</font></a>
  as if it were a candidate for the remission and sanctification shortly about to follow in Christ: for in that John used to preach “baptism <i>for</i> the remission of sins,” <a name="fr_8649"></a><a href="#fn_8649"><font size="1">8650</font></a>
  the declaration was made with reference to <i>future</i> remission; if it be true, (as it is,) that repentance is antecedent, remission subsequent; and this is “preparing the way.” <a name="fr_8650"></a><a href="#fn_8650"><font size="1">8651</font></a>
  But he who “prepares” does not himself “perfect,” but procures for another to perfect.  John himself professes that the celestial things are not his, but Christs, by saying, “He who is from the earth speaketh concerning the earth; He who comes from the <i>realms</i> above is above all;” <a name="fr_8651"></a><a href="#fn_8651"><font size="1">8652</font></a>
  and again, by saying that he “baptized in repentance only, but that One would shortly come who would baptize in the Spirit and fire;” <a name="fr_8652"></a><a href="#fn_8652"><font size="1">8653</font></a>
 —of course because true and stable faith is baptized with <i>water</i>, unto salvation; pretended and weak faith is baptized with <i>fire</i>, unto judgment.</p>
