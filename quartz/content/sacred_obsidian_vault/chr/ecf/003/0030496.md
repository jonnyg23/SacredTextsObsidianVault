 </p><p class="c30"><a name="v.v.xxvii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="v.v.xxvii-p1.1"></a>Chapter XXVII.—Some Hair-Splitting Use of Words in Which His Opponent Had Indulged.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="v.v.xxvii-p2"></a>But you next praise your eyebrows, and toss back your head, and beckon with your finger, in characteristic disdain, <a name="fr_6384"></a><a href="#fn_6384"><font size="1">6385</font></a>
  and say: There is the <i>was</i>, looking as if it pointed to an eternal existence,—making its subject, of course, unbegotten and unmade, and on that account worthy of being supposed to be Matter. Well now, for my own part, I shall resort to no affected protestation, <a name="fr_6385"></a><a href="#fn_6385"><font size="1">6386</font></a>
  but simply reply that “<i>was</i>” may be predicated of everything—even of a thing which has been created, which was born, which once was not, and which is not <i>your</i> Matter. For of everything which has being, from whatever source it has it, whether it has it by a beginning or without a beginning, the word “<i>was</i>” will be predicated from the very fact that it exists. To whatever thing the first tense <a name="fr_6386"></a><a href="#fn_6386"><font size="1">6387</font></a>
  of the verb is applicable for <i>definition</i>, to the same will be suitable the later form <a name="fr_6387"></a><a href="#fn_6387"><font size="1">6388</font></a>
  of the verb, when it has to descend to <i>relation</i>. “Est” (it is) forms the essential part <a name="fr_6388"></a><a href="#fn_6388"><font size="1">6389</font></a>
  of a definition, “erat” (it was) of a relation.  Such are the trifles and subtleties of heretics, who wrest and bring into question the simple meaning of the commonest words. A grand question it is, to be sure, <a name="fr_6389"></a><a href="#fn_6389"><font size="1">6390</font></a>
  whether “the earth <i>was</i>,” which was made! The real point of discussion is, whether “being without form, and void,” is a state which is more suitable to that which was created, or to that of which it was created, so that the predicate (<i>was</i>) may appertain to the same thing to which the subject (<i>that which was</i>) also belongs. <a name="fr_6390"></a><a href="#fn_6390"><font size="1">6391</font></a>
 </p>
