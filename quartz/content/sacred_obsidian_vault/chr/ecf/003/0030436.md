 </p><p class="c30"><a name="v.iv.v.xxxvii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="v.iv.v.xxxvii-p1.1"></a>Chapter XXXVII.—Christ and Zacchæus. The Salvation of the Body as Denied by Marcion. The Parable of the Ten Servants Entrusted with Ten Pounds.  Christ a Judge, Who is to Administer the Will of the Austere Man, I.e. The Creator.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="v.iv.v.xxxvii-p2"></a>“Salvation comes to the house” of Zac<a name="page_412"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 412</font></a> <a name="v.iv.v.xxxvii-Page_412"></a>chæus even. <a name="fr_4961"></a><a href="#fn_4961"><font size="1">4962</font></a>
  For what reason? Was it because he also believed that Christ came by Marcion? But the blind mans cry was still sounding in the ears of all:  “Jesus, Thou Son of David, have mercy on me.” And “all the people gave praise unto God”—not Marcions, but Davids. Now, although Zacchæus was probably a Gentile, <a name="fr_4962"></a><a href="#fn_4962"><font size="1">4963</font></a>
  he yet from his intercourse with Jews had obtained a smattering <a name="fr_4963"></a><a href="#fn_4963"><font size="1">4964</font></a>
  of their Scriptures, and, more than this, had, without knowing it, fulfilled the precepts of Isaiah: “Deal thy bread,” said the prophet, “to the hungry, and bring the poor that are cast out into thine house.” <a name="fr_4964"></a><a href="#fn_4964"><font size="1">4965</font></a>
  This he did in the best possible way, by receiving the Lord, and entertaining Him in his house. “When thou seest the naked cover him.” <a name="fr_4965"></a><a href="#fn_4965"><font size="1">4966</font></a>
  This he promised to do, in an equally satisfactory way, when he offered the half of his goods for all works of mercy. <a name="fr_4966"></a><a href="#fn_4966"><font size="1">4967</font></a>
  So also “he loosened the bands of wickedness, undid the heavy burdens, let the oppressed go free, and broke every yoke,” <a name="fr_4967"></a><a href="#fn_4967"><font size="1">4968</font></a>
  when he said, “If I have taken anything from any man by false accusation, I restore him fourfold.” <a name="fr_4968"></a><a href="#fn_4968"><font size="1">4969</font></a>
  Therefore the Lord said, “This day is salvation come to this house.” <a name="fr_4969"></a><a href="#fn_4969"><font size="1">4970</font></a>
  Thus did He give His testimony, that the precepts of the Creator spoken by the prophet tended to salvation. <a name="fr_4970"></a><a href="#fn_4970"><font size="1">4971</font></a>
  But when He adds, “For the Son of man is come to seek and to save that which was lost,” <a name="fr_4971"></a><a href="#fn_4971"><font size="1">4972</font></a>
  my present contention is not whether <i>He</i> was come to save what was lost, <i>to whom</i> it had once belonged, and <i>from whom</i> what He came to save had fallen away; but I approach a different question. <i>Man</i>, there can be no doubt of it, is here the subject of consideration. Now, since he consists of two parts, <a name="fr_4972"></a><a href="#fn_4972"><font size="1">4973</font></a>
  body and soul, the point to be inquired into is, in which of these two man would seem to have been lost? If in his body, then it is his body, not his soul, which is lost. What, however, is lost, the Son of man saves. The body, <a name="fr_4973"></a><a href="#fn_4973"><font size="1">4974</font></a>
  therefore, has the salvation. If, (on the other hand,) it is in his soul that man is lost, salvation is designed for the lost soul; and the body which is not lost is safe. If, (to take the only other supposition,) man is wholly lost, in both his natures, then it necessarily follows that salvation is appointed for the entire man; and then the opinion of the heretics is shivered to pieces, <a name="fr_4974"></a><a href="#fn_4974"><font size="1">4975</font></a>
  who say that there is no salvation of the flesh. And this affords a confirmation that Christ belongs to the Creator, who followed the Creator in promising the salvation of the whole man. The parable also of the (ten) servants, who received their several recompenses according to the manner in which they had increased their lords money by trading <a name="fr_4975"></a><a href="#fn_4975"><font size="1">4976</font></a>
  proves Him to be a God of judgment—even a God who, in strict account, <a name="fr_4976"></a><a href="#fn_4976"><font size="1">4977</font></a>
  not only bestows honour, but also takes away what a man seems to have. <a name="fr_4977"></a><a href="#fn_4977"><font size="1">4978</font></a>
  Else, if it is the Creator whom He has here delineated as the “austere man,” who “takes up what he laid not down, and reaps what he did not sow,” <a name="fr_4978"></a><a href="#fn_4978"><font size="1">4979</font></a>
  my instructor even here is He, (whoever He may be,) to whom belongs the money He teaches me fruitfully to expend. <a name="fr_4979"></a><a href="#fn_4979"><font size="1">4980</font></a>
 </p>
