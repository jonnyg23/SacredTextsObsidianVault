 <p> <a name="iv.iv.xx"></a>
 </p><p class="c30"><a name="iv.iv.xx-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="iv.iv.xx-p1.1"></a>Chapter XX.—Concerning Idolatry in Words.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="iv.iv.xx-p2"></a>But, however, since the conduct according to the divine rule is imperilled, not merely by deeds, but likewise by words, (for, just as it is written, “Behold the man and his deeds;” <a name="fr_321"></a><a href="#fn_321"><font size="1">322</font></a>
  so, “Out of thy own mouth shalt thou be justified” <a name="fr_322"></a><a href="#fn_322"><font size="1">323</font></a>
 ), we ought to remember that, even in <i>words</i>, also the inroad of idolatry must be foreguarded against, either from the defect of custom or of timidity. The law prohibits the gods of the nations from being named, <a name="fr_323"></a><a href="#fn_323"><font size="1">324</font></a>
  not of course that we are not to pronounce their names, the speaking of which common intercourse extorts from us: for this must very frequently be said, “You find him in the temple of Æsculapius;” and, “I live in Isis Street;” and, “He has been made priest of Jupiter;” and much else after this manner, since even on <i>men</i> names of this kind are bestowed. I do not honour Saturnus if I call a man so, by his own name. I honour him no more than I do Marcus, if I call a man Marcus. But it says, “Make not mention of the name of other gods, neither be it heard from thy mouth.” <a name="fr_324"></a><a href="#fn_324"><font size="1">325</font></a>
  The precept it gives is <a name="page_74"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 74</font></a> <a name="iv.iv.xx-Page_74"></a>this, that we do not call them <i>gods</i>. For in the first part of the law, too, “Thou shalt not,” saith He, “use the name of the Lord thy God in a vain thing,” <a name="fr_325"></a><a href="#fn_325"><font size="1">326</font></a>
  that is, in an idol. <a name="fr_326"></a><a href="#fn_326"><font size="1">327</font></a>
  Whoever, therefore, honours an idol with the name of God, has fallen into idolatry.  But if I speak of them as gods, something must be added to make it appear that <i>I</i> do not call them gods. For even the Scripture names “gods,” but adds “their,” viz. “of the nations:” just as David does when he had named “gods,” where he says, “But the gods of the nations are demons.” <a name="fr_327"></a><a href="#fn_327"><font size="1">328</font></a>
  But this has been laid by me rather as a foundation for ensuing observations.  However, it is a defect of custom to say, “By Hercules, So help me the god of faith;” <a name="fr_328"></a><a href="#fn_328"><font size="1">329</font></a>
  while to the <i>custom</i> is added the <i>ignorance</i> of some, who are ignorant that it is an oath by Hercules. Further, what will an oath be, in the name of gods whom you have forsworn, but a collusion of faith with idolatry? For who does not honour them in whose name he swears?</p>
