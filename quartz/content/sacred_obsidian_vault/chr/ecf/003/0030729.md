 </p><p class="c30"><a name="vi.iii.vii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="vi.iii.vii-p1.1"></a>Chapter VII.—Of the Unction.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="vi.iii.vii-p2"></a>After this, when we have issued from the font, <a name="fr_8592"></a><a href="#fn_8592"><font size="1">8593</font></a>
  we are thoroughly anointed with a blessed unction,—(a practice derived) from the old discipline, wherein on entering the priesthood, <i>men</i> were wont to be anointed with oil from a horn, ever since Aaron was anointed by Moses. <a name="fr_8593"></a><a href="#fn_8593"><font size="1">8594</font></a>
  Whence Aaron is called “Christ,” <a name="fr_8594"></a><a href="#fn_8594"><font size="1">8595</font></a>
  from the “chrism,” which is “the unction;” which, when made spiritual, furnished an appropriate name to the Lord, because He was “anointed” with the Spirit by God the Father; as <i>written</i> in the Acts: “For truly they were gathered together in this city <a name="fr_8595"></a><a href="#fn_8595"><font size="1">8596</font></a>
  against Thy Holy Son whom Thou hast anointed.” <a name="fr_8596"></a><a href="#fn_8596"><font size="1">8597</font></a>
  Thus, too, in <i>our</i> case, the unction runs carnally, (<i>i.e.</i> on the body,) but profits spiritually; in the same way as the <i>act</i> of baptism itself too is carnal, in that we are plunged in water, <i>but</i> the <i>effect</i> spiritual, in that we are freed from sins.</p>
