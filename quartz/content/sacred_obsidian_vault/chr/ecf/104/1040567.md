 </p><p class="c41"><a name="v.v.iv.lxxx-p1"></a> <span class="c11"><a name="v.v.iv.lxxx-p1.1"></a>Chapter 80.—</span>175.  <span class="c11"><a name="v.v.iv.lxxx-p1.2"></a>Petilianus</span> said:  "Lastly, what is the justification of persecution?  I ask you, you wretched men, if it so be that you think that your sin rests on any authority of law."</p>  
 <p class="c10"><a name="v.v.iv.lxxx-p2"></a>176.  <span class="c11"><a name="v.v.iv.lxxx-p2.1"></a>Augustin</span> answered:  He who sins, sins not on the authority of the law, but against the authority of the law.  But since you ask what <a name="page_571"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 571</font></a> <a name="v.v.iv.lxxx-Page_571"></a>is the justification of persecution, I ask you in turn whose voice it is that says in the psalm, "Whoso privily slandereth his neighbor, him will I cut off." <a name="fr_2188"></a><a href="#fn_2188"><font size="1">2189</font></a>
   Seek therefore the reason or the measure of the persecution, and do not display your gross ignorance by finding fault in general terms with those who persecute the unrighteous.</p>
