 </p><p class="c41"><a name="v.iv.ix.xxx-p1"></a> <span class="c11"><a name="v.iv.ix.xxx-p1.1"></a>Chapter 30.—</span>58.  Another Julianus of Marcelliana <a name="fr_1838"></a><a href="#fn_1838"><font size="1">1839</font></a>
  said:  "If a man can serve two masters, God and mammon, <a name="fr_1839"></a><a href="#fn_1839"><font size="1">1840</font></a>
  then baptism also can serve two, the Christian and the heretic." <a name="fr_1840"></a><a href="#fn_1840"><font size="1">1841</font></a>
 </p>  
 <p class="c10"><a name="v.iv.ix.xxx-p5"></a>59.  Truly, if it can serve the self-restrained and the covetous man, the sober and the drunken, the well-affectioned and the murderer, why should it not also serve the Christian and the heretic?—whom, indeed, it does not really serve; but it ministers to them, and is administered by them, for salvation to those who use it right, and for judgment to such as use it wrong.</p>
