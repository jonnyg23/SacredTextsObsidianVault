 <p> <a name="vi.ii"></a> <a name="page_69"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 69</font></a> <a name="vi.ii-Page_69"></a>
 </p><p class="c20"><a name="vi.ii-p1"></a><span class="c19"><a name="vi.ii-p1.1"></a>Introductory Notice</span></p> 
 <p class="c25"><a name="vi.ii-p2"></a><span class="c9"><a name="vi.ii-p2.1"></a>to the</span></p> 
 <p class="c25"><a name="vi.ii-p3"></a><span class="c19"><a name="vi.ii-p3.1"></a>Pseudo-Clementine Literature.</span></p> 
 <p class="c25"><a name="vi.ii-p4"></a><span class="c9"><a name="vi.ii-p4.1"></a>By Professor M. B. Riddle, D.D.</span></p> 
 <p class="c2"><a name="vi.ii-p5"></a> ————————————</p> 
 <p class="c21"><a name="vi.ii-p6"></a><span class="c9"><a name="vi.ii-p6.1"></a>The</span> name “Pseudo-Clementine Literature” (or, more briefly, “Clementina”) is applied to a series of writings, closely resembling each other, purporting to emanate from the great Roman Father.  But, as Dr. Schaff remarks, in this literature he is evidently confounded with “Flavius Clement, kinsman of the Emperor Domitian.” <a name="fr_503"></a><a href="#fn_503"><font size="1">504</font></a>
   These writings are three in number:  (1) the <i>Recognitions</i>, of which only the Latin translation of Rufinus has been preserved; <a name="fr_504"></a><a href="#fn_504"><font size="1">505</font></a>
  (2) the <i>Homilies</i>, twenty in number, of which a complete collection has been known since 1853; (3) the <i>Epitome</i>, “an uninteresting extract from the <i>Homilies</i>, to which are added extracts from the letter of Clement to James, from the <i>Martyrium</i> of Clement by Simeon Metaphrastes, etc.” <a name="fr_505"></a><a href="#fn_505"><font size="1">506</font></a>
   Other writings may be classed with these; but they are of the same general character, except that most of them show the influence of a later age, adapting the material more closely to the orthodox doctrine.</p> 
 <p class="c22"><a name="vi.ii-p10"></a>The <i>Recognitions</i> and the <i>Homilies</i> appear in the pages which follow.  The former are given a prior position, as in the Edinburgh series.  It probably cannot be proven that these represent the earlier form of this theological romance; but the <i>Homilies</i>, “in any case, present the more doctrinally developed and historically important form of the other treatises, which are essentially similar.” <a name="fr_506"></a><a href="#fn_506"><font size="1">507</font></a>
   They are therefore with propriety placed after the <i>Recognitions</i>, which do not seem to have been based upon them, but upon some earlier document. <a name="fr_507"></a><a href="#fn_507"><font size="1">508</font></a>
 </p> 
 <p class="c22"><a name="vi.ii-p13"></a>The critical discussion of the <i>Clementina</i> has been keen, but has not reached its end.  It necessarily involves other questions, about which there is still great difference of opinion.  A few results seem to be established:—</p> 
 <p class="c22"><a name="vi.ii-p14"></a>(1)  The entire literature is of Jewish-Christian, or Ebionitic, origin.  The position accorded to “James, the Lords brother,” in all the writings, is a clear indication of this; so is the silence respecting the Apostle Paul.  The doctrinal statements, “though not perfectly homogeneous” (Uhlhorn), are Judaistic, even when mixed with Gnostic speculation of heathen origin.  This tendency is, perhaps, not so clearly marked in the <i>Recognitions</i> as in the <i>Homilies</i>; but both partake largely of the same general character.  More particularly, the literature has been connected with the Ebionite sect called the Elkesaites; and some regard the <i>Homilies</i> as containing a further development of their system. <a name="fr_508"></a><a href="#fn_508"><font size="1">509</font></a>
   This is not definitely established, but finds some <a name="page_70"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 70</font></a> <a name="vi.ii-Page_70"></a>support in the resemblance between the baptismal forms, as given by Hippolytus in the case of the Elkesaites, <a name="fr_509"></a><a href="#fn_509"><font size="1">510</font></a>
  and those indicated in the <i>Recognitions</i> and <i>Homilies</i>, especially the latter. <a name="fr_510"></a><a href="#fn_510"><font size="1">511</font></a>
 </p> 
 <p class="c22"><a name="vi.ii-p18"></a>(2)  The entire literature belongs to the class of fictitious writing “with a purpose.”  The Germans properly term the <i>Homilies</i> a “Tendenz-Romance.”  The many “lives of Christ” written in our day to insinuate some other view of our Lords person than that given in the canonical Gospels, furnish abundant examples of the class.  The Tübingen school, finding here a real specimen of the influence of party feeling upon quasi-historical literature, naturally pressed the <i>Clementina</i> in support of their theory of the origin of the Gospels.</p> 
 <p class="c22"><a name="vi.ii-p19"></a>(3)  The discussion leaves it quite probable, though not yet certain, that all the works are “independent elaborations—perhaps at first hand, perhaps at second or third—of some older tract not now extant.” <a name="fr_511"></a><a href="#fn_511"><font size="1">512</font></a>
   Some of the opinions held respecting the relations of the two principal works are given by the Edinburgh translator in his Introductory Notice.  It is only necessary here to indicate the progress of the modern discussion.  Neander, as early as 1818, gave some prominence to the doctrinal view of the <i>Homilies</i>.  He was followed by Baur, who found in these writings, as indicated above, support for his theory of the origin of historical Christianity.  It is to be noted, however, that the heterogeneous mixture of Ebionism and Gnosticism in the doctrinal views proved perplexing to the leader of the Tübingen school.  Schliemann <a name="fr_512"></a><a href="#fn_512"><font size="1">513</font></a>
  took ground against Baur, collecting much material, and carefully investigating the question.  Both authors give the priority to the <i>Homilies</i>.  While Baur went too far in one direction, Schliemann, perhaps, failed to recognise fully the basis of truth in the position of the former.  The next important step in the discussion was made by Hilgenfeld, <a name="fr_513"></a><a href="#fn_513"><font size="1">514</font></a>
  whose views are briefly given in the Notice which follows.  Hilgenfeld assigned the priority to the <i>Recognitions</i>, though he traced all the literature to an earlier work.  Uhlhorn <a name="fr_514"></a><a href="#fn_514"><font size="1">515</font></a>
  at first attempted to prove that the <i>Recognitions</i> were a revision of the <i>Homilies</i>.  Further contributions were made by Lehmann <a name="fr_515"></a><a href="#fn_515"><font size="1">516</font></a>
  and Lipsius. <a name="fr_516"></a><a href="#fn_516"><font size="1">517</font></a>
   The former discovered in the <i>Recognitions</i> two distinct parts by different authors (i.–iii., iv.–ix.), tracing all the literature to the <i>Kerygma</i> of Peter.  The latter finds the basis of the whole in the <i>Acta Petri</i>, which show a strong anti-Pauline tendency.</p> 
 <p class="c22"><a name="vi.ii-p26"></a>Influenced by these investigations, Uhlhorn modified his views.  Lechler, <a name="fr_517"></a><a href="#fn_517"><font size="1">518</font></a>
  while not positive in his convictions, makes the following prudent statement:  “An older work lies at the basis both of the <i>Homilies</i> and <i>Recognitions</i>, bearing the title, <i>Kerygmen des Petrus</i>. <a name="fr_518"></a><a href="#fn_518"><font size="1">519</font></a>
   To this document sometimes the <i>Homilies</i>, sometimes the <i>Recognitions</i>, correspond more faithfully; its historical contents are more correctly seen from the <i>Recognitions</i>, its doctrinal contents from the <i>Homilies</i>.”  Other views, some of them quite fanciful, have been presented.</p> 
 <p class="c22"><a name="vi.ii-p29"></a>The prevalent opinion necessarily leaves us in ignorance of the authors of this literature.  The date of composition, or editing, cannot be definitely fixed.  In their present form the several works may be as old as the first half of the third century, and the common basis may be placed in the latter half of the second century.</p> 
 <p class="c22"><a name="vi.ii-p30"></a>How far the anti-Pauline tendency is carried, is a matter of dispute.  Baur and many others think Simon is meant to represent Paul; <a name="fr_519"></a><a href="#fn_519"><font size="1">520</font></a>
  but this is difficult to believe, though we must admit <a name="page_71"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 71</font></a> <a name="vi.ii-Page_71"></a>the disposition to ignore the Apostle to the Gentiles.  As to the literary merit of these productions the reader must judge.</p> 
 <p class="c22"><a name="vi.ii-p32"></a>For convenience in comparison of the two works, the following table has been prepared, based on the order of the <i>Recognitions</i>.  The correspondences are not exact, and the reader is referred to the footnotes for fuller details.  This table gives a general view of the arrangement of the two narratives:—</p>

 <p><a name="vi.ii-p34"></a>I.……I., II.</p>
