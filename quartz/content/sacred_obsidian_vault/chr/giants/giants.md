 <h2 align="center">The Book of the Giants</h2>
 
 <h3 align="center">By W. B. HENNING</h3>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p align="center"><font size="1">{<a href="#page_60">skip to translations</a>}</font></p>
 
 <p>ISAAC DE BEAUSOBRE, the Huguenot author of one of the best books ever written on Manichæism (<i>Histoire critique de Manichée et du Manicheïsme</i>, Amsterdam, 1734, 1739), was the one to make the only sound suggestions on the sources used by Mani for the compilation of his <i>Book of the Giants:</i> the <i>Book of Enoch</i>, and the <i>Γραφὴ τω̑ν Γιγάντων</i> which Kenan, a great-grandson of Noah, discovered lying in a field (vol. i, 429, n. 6). The latter work has been identified by Alfaric (<i>Les Écritures Manichéennes</i>, ii, 32) with a book whose contents are briefly indicated in the <i>Decretum Gelasianum</i>, p. 54, ll. 298-9 (ed. Dobschütz): <i>Liber de Ogia</i><a name="page_52_fr_1"></a><a href="#page_52_note_1"><font size="1">1</font></a><i> nomine gigante qui post diluvium cum dracone ab hereticis pugnasse perhibetur apocryphus</i>. Of the <i>Book of Enoch</i>, which was composed in the Hebrew language in the second century <small>B.C.</small>, only an Ethiopic version, a few Greek fragments, and some excerpts made by the Byzantine chronographer Georgius Syncellus survive.<a name="page_52_fr_2"></a><a href="#page_52_note_2"><font size="1">2</font></a> Mani, who could hardly read the Hebrew, must have used an Aramaic edition based directly on the Hebrew text (see below, <i>Šhmyz’d</i>). He quotes mainly from the first part, which Georgius S. (p. 45, Fl.-R.) calls "the first book of Enoch on the Egrēgoroi", but shows himself acquainted also with the subsequent chapters.<a name="page_52_fr_3"></a><a href="#page_52_note_3"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>
 
 <p>   It is noteworthy that Mani, who was brought up and spent most of his life in a province of the Persian empire, and whose mother belonged to a famous Parthian family,<a name="page_52_fr_4"></a><a href="#page_52_note_4"><font size="1">4</font></a> did not make any use of the Iranian mythological tradition. There can no longer be any doubt that the Iranian names of <i>Sām</i>, <i>Narīmān</i>, etc., that appear in the Persian and Sogdian versions of the <i>Book of the Giants</i>, did not figure in the original edition, written by Mani in the Syriac language.<a name="page_52_fr_5"></a><a href="#page_52_note_5"><font size="1">5</font></a> His disciples, who, it is well known, were in the habit of
 
 <!------------------------page 53------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_53"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 53</font></a>
 
 translating every word of a text (including the names of months, deities, etc.), seen fit also to "translate" the names of the giants. Thus <i>Sām</i> is merely the translation of <i>Ohya</i>. However, they kept some of the original names (e.g. <i>Šhmyz’d</i>), and adapted some others (e.g. <i>Wrwgd’d</i>).<a name="page_53_fr_1"></a><a href="#page_53_note_1"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>
 
 <p>   The story of the fallen angels and their giant sons needed little adaptation to be fitted into Mani's system. Of course, the heavenly origin of the <i>B’nē-hā-Elōhīm</i><a name="page_53_fr_2"></a><a href="#page_53_note_2"><font size="1">2</font></a> of Genesis vi, 2, 4, the <i>’Εγρήγοροι</i>, of the <i>Book of Enoch</i>, did not square with Mani's conviction that no evil could come from good. Therefore he transformed them into "demons", namely those demons that when the world was being constructed had been imprisoned in the skies under the supervision of the <i>Rex Honoris</i>. They rebelled and were recaptured, but two hundred of them escaped to the earth. Mani also used the term <i>’Εγρήγοροι</i> (preserved in Coptic, see texts L, M, P, S), or rather <i>‘yr</i> in Aramaic (once in a Middle Persian fragment, text D), but in Eastern sources they are mostly referred to as "demons" (Pers. <i>dyw’n</i>, Parth. <i>dyw’n</i> in T 6, Sogd. <i>δywt</i> in G, H 17, K 7, <i>cytyt</i> in E, <i>δywt ZY ykšyšt</i> in H. 16).</p>
 
 <p>   The puzzling clause of Genesis vi, 4: "The Nephilim were on the earth those days," was interpreted by Mani in this fashion: "when the Egrēgoroi descended, the animals, or proto-animals, were already in existence." Mani confused <i>nəfīlīm</i> with <i>nefäl</i> (<i>näfäl</i>) = <i>ἔκτρωμα</i>: see Nöldeke, <i>ZDMG</i>., 43 (1889), 536, who rightly referred to the formula of abjuration (<i>P.Gr.</i>, i, 1461) where the giants and the "abortions" are mentioned in one breath. In Manichæan parlance, "abortion" (cf. also MPers. <i>’bg’ng</i>, Sogd. <i>pš’q</i>) is synonymous with "animal".</p>
 
 <p>   We are therefore left with the <i>Gibbōrīm</i>, understood by Mani<a name="page_53_fr_3"></a><a href="#page_53_note_3"><font size="1">3</font></a> as "giants". He probably used the equivalent Syriac word, <i>gabbārē</i> (<i>gnbr’</i>), which his disciples translated as <i>γίγαντες</i>, <i>al-ǰabābirah</i> in Arabic, MPers. and Parthian <i>k’w’n</i>, Sogd. <i>kwyšt</i> = <i>kawišt</i> (Sing. <i>qwy</i>, <i>kw’y</i> = <i>kawi</i>); cf. <i>Sb.P.A.W.</i>, 1934, 30. In Sasanian times the words derived from the Avestan <i>Kavi</i> were generally understood as "giant"; see Benveniste, <i>MO</i>., xxvi, 214, and Polotsky in <i>Mir.Man.</i>, iii, 901. Thus MPers. Parth. <i>k’w</i> is freely used in Manichæan texts, e.g. of the Father of Light (M 40), of solar deities, of leading Manichæans (both in <i>Mir.Man.</i>, iii), also of the First Man and Ahriman<a name="page_53_fr_4"></a><a href="#page_53_note_4"><font size="1">4</font></a> with reference to the First Battle (which therefore could have been described as a <i>γιγαντομαχία</i>).<a name="page_53_fr_5"></a><a href="#page_53_note_5"><font size="1">5</font></a>
 
 <!------------------------page 54------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_54"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 54</font></a>
 
 However, the word <i>k’w</i> is applied only to men and such beings as are imagined anthropomorphous. Where one would translate <i>γίγας</i> as <i>monster</i>, the Iranian equivalent is <i>mzn</i>, <i>Mazan</i>. Thus the <i>γίγας τη̑ς θαλάσσης</i> (<i>Kephalaia</i>, 113 and notes), whose breathing operations are responsible for ebb and flow (cf. also Beruni, <i>India</i>, 203, 10-11), is called <i>Mzn ‘y </i>(<i>z</i>)<i>rhyg</i><a name="page_54_fr_1"></a><a href="#page_54_note_1"><font size="1">1</font></a> in Middle Persian (M 99, V 22-3). Accordingly, MPers. <i>mzn</i> (adj.<a name="page_54_fr_2"></a><a href="#page_54_note_2"><font size="1">2</font></a> and noun) and the related words, Pahl. <i>mā̆zan</i>, <i>māzanīg</i>, Sogd. <i>mzny’n δyw</i>, Av. <i>māzainya-</i>,<a name="page_54_fr_3"></a><a href="#page_54_note_3"><font size="1">3</font></a> should be rendered as "monster", or "gigantic, monstrous".</p>
 
 <p>   The Egrēgoroi and their giant progeny are fought and vanquished by four archangels: Raphael, Michael, Gabriel, and Istrael (<i>Enoch</i>, 10, 1; or: Uriel, or: Fanuel). In the <i>Book of the Giants</i> they are called "the four angels". They are frequently invoked by name in Manichæan prayers (e.g. M 4 d 19, f 6; M 20), as <i>Rwp’yl</i>, <i>Myx’yl</i>, <i>Gbr’yl</i>, and <i>Sr’yl</i> ( = Istrael).</p>
 
 <p>   There were no details about individual feats of the giants in the <i>Book of Enoch</i>. Mani filled the gap with the help of the above-mentioned <i>Liber de Ogia nomine gigante</i>. This <i>Ogias</i> has been identified with <i>Og of Bashan</i>,<a name="page_54_fr_4"></a><a href="#page_54_note_4"><font size="1">4</font></a> who according to late sources lived five thousand years and managed to survive the Deluge, thanks to his giant size.<a name="page_54_fr_5"></a><a href="#page_54_note_5"><font size="1">5</font></a> But possibly stories that primarily appertained to <i>Ogias</i> were transferred to the better known <i>Og</i>, owing to the resemblance of their names. The name of <i>Ogias</i> is <i>’why’ </i>(<i>’wḥy’</i>) =<i> Ohyā̆ </i>(<i>Oḥyā̆</i>) in the Manichæan fragments, and this spelling is presumably more correct than that of <i>Ogias</i>. <i>Og</i> (<i>‘wg</i>) indubitably would appear as <i>’wg</i> (or: <i>‘wg</i>). Since Mani took <i>’why’</i> from an Aramaic text, the ending of <i>Ogias</i> cannot be regarded as a Greek addition.</p>
 
 <p>   Ogias fought with a <i>draco</i>, and so did Ohya; his enemy was the Leviathan (text N). Ohya and his brother Ahya were the sons of <i>Šhmyz’d</i> (text H), i.e. <i>Στμιαζα̑ς</i>, the chief of the Egrēgoroi in the <i>Book of Enoch</i>; hence, <i>Στμιαζα̑ς</i> is transcription of <i>šhm-</i> (or <i>šḥm</i> ?). In the Persian edition of the <i>Kawān</i> Ohya and Ahya are "translated" as <i>Sām</i> and <i>Narīmān</i>, but the original names are kept in one passage (A 60). The translator did well to choose Sām-Krsāsp, both with regard to Ogias' longevity (Sām is one of the "Immortals") and to his fight with the dragon (Sām is a famous dragon-killer). In the Sogdian
 
 <!------------------------page 55------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_55"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 55</font></a>
 
 fragments the name of Sām is spelt <i>S’hm</i> = <i>Sāhm</i>, as it is often in Pahlavi (<i>S’hm</i><a name="page_55_fr_1"></a><a href="#page_55_note_1"><font size="1">1</font></a> beside <i>S’m</i>); Ṭabari has <i>Shm</i>,<a name="page_55_fr_2"></a><a href="#page_55_note_2"><font size="1">2</font></a> cf. Christensen, <i>Kayanides</i>, p. 130. Sāhm's brother is <i>Pāt-Sāhm</i>. This name may have been invented by the Sogdian translator in order to keep the names of the brothers resembling each other. Narīmān was evidently not known in Sogdiana as a brother of Sām. According to the <i>Book of the Giants</i>, the main preoccupation of Sām-Sāhm was his quarrel the giant <i>Māhawai</i>,<a name="page_55_fr_3"></a><a href="#page_55_note_3"><font size="1">3</font></a> the son of <i>Virōgdād</i>, who was one of the twenty ers of the Egrēgoroi.</p>
 
 <p>   <i>The Book of the Giants</i> was published in not less than six or seven languages. From the original Syriac the Greek and Middle Persian versions were made. The Sogdian edition was probably derived from the Middle Persian, the Uygur from the Sogdian. There is no trace of a Parthian text.<a name="page_55_fr_4"></a><a href="#page_55_note_4"><font size="1">4</font></a> The book may have existed in Coptic. The presence of names such as Sām and Narīmān in the Arabic version proves that it had been translated from the Middle Persian. To the few surviving fragments (texts A-G) I have added two excerpts, the more important of which (H) probably derives from a Syriac epitome of the book. Naturally, Manichæan authors quoted the book frequently, but there is only one direct citation by a non-Manichæan writer (text O). With the exception of text O, all the passages referring to the <i>Book of the Giants</i> (texts J-T) go back to Syriac writings (apparently). They are, therefore, to be treated as quotations from the Syriac edition. E.g. the Parthian text N is not the product of a Parthian writer who might have employed a Parthian version of the book, but was translated from a Syriac treatise whose author cited the Syriac text.</p>
 
 <p>   In their journey across Central Asia the stories of the <i>Book of the Giants</i> were influenced by local traditions. Thus, the translation of Ohya as Sām had in its train the introduction of myths appertaining to that Iranian hero; this explains the "immortality" of Sā(h)m according to text I. The country of <i>Aryān-Vēžan</i> = <i>Airyana Vaēǰah</i>, in text G (26), is a similar innovation.<a name="page_55_fr_5"></a><a href="#page_55_note_5"><font size="1">5</font></a> The "Kögmän mountains" in text B may reflect the "Mount Hermon". The progeny of the fallen angels was confined in thirty-six towns (text S). Owing to the introduction of the Mount Sumeru, this number was changed
 
 <!------------------------page 56------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_56"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 56</font></a>
 
 (in Sogdiana) to <i>thirty-two</i> (text G, 22): "the heaven of Indra . . . is situated between the four peaks (cf. G 21) of the Meru, and consists of <i>thirty-two</i> cities of devas" (Eitel, <i>Handb. Chinese Buddhism</i>, 148, on <i>Trayastriṃśat</i>).</p>

 <p align="center">F<small>RAGMENTS OF THE</small> K<small>AWĀN</small></p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>A. Middle-Persian</i></p>
 
 <p>   M 101, <i>a</i> to <i>n</i>, and M 911, fifteen fragments of a book, throughout small pieces from the centre of the pages. It has proved impossible, so far, to re-establish the original order of the pages. On purely technical grounds (size of the fragments, appearance of the margins, relative position of tears, stains, etc.), I at first assumed the following sequence: l-j-k-g-i-c-e-b-h-f-a-d-m-M 911-n. Being unable to estimate the cogency of these technical reasons now, because of the absence of any photographic material, I have decided to change the order of the first six fragments in the following way: c-j-l-k-g-i, in view of their contents.<a name="page_56_fr_2"></a><a href="#page_56_note_2"><font size="1">2</font></a> Unfortunately we do not know in what order Mani had told the story of the giants. The task of finding the original order is made still more difficult by the fact that besides the <i>Kawān</i> the book contained one or two more treatises, namely: (1) Parables referring to the Hearers, and possibly (2) a discourse on the Five Elements (here (1) = lines 160 to the end, and (2) = lines 112-159). The only fragments that undoubtedly belonged to the <i>Kawān</i> are c-j-l-k-g-i, while the position of the fragments e-b-h is particularly doubtful. It must be borne in mind that whole folios may be missing between apparently successive pages. In order to enable the reader to judge for himself, all the fragments (including the parables) are published here. The text is based on a copy I made nearly ten years ago (referred to in the notes as: Copy); a revision is not possible under the present circumstances.</p>
