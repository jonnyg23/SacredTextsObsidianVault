 <h3 align="CENTER">29.</h3>
 <p align="CENTER"><a name="img_v102101"></a>
 <img src="img/v102101.jpg"></p>
 <p>
 Let the object in relief <i>t</i> be seen by both eyes; if you will look
 at the object with the right eye <i>m</i>, keeping the left eye <i>n</i> shut,
 the object will appear, or fill up the space, at <i>a</i>; and if you
 shut the right eye and open the left, the object (will occupy the)
 space <i>b</i>; and if you open both eyes, the object will no longer
 appear at <i>a</i> or <i>b</i>, but at <i>e</i>, <i>r</i>, <i>f</i>. Why will not a picture
 seen by both eyes produce the effect of relief, as [real] relief
 does when seen by both eyes; and why should a picture seen with one
 eye give the same effect of relief as real relief would under the
 same conditions of light and shade?
 </p><p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_23"></a><a href="0029.htm#fr_23">21:21</a> : In the sketch, <i>m</i> is the left eye and <i>n</i> the right,
 while the text reverses this lettering. We must therefore suppose
 that the face in which the eyes <i>m</i> and <i>n</i> are placed is opposite
 to the spectator.</p>
