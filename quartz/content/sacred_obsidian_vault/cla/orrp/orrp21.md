   <h1 align="center">VIII. THE TRANSFORMATION OF PAGANISM.</h1>  
 <p>BIBLIOGRAPHY: The history of the destruction of paganism is a subject that has tempted many historians. Beugnot (1835), Lasaulx (1854), Schulze (Jena, 1887-1892) have tried it with varying success (see Wissowa, <i>Religion der Römer</i>, pp. 84 ff.). But hardly any one has been interested in the reconstruction of the theology of the last pagans, although material is not lacking. The meritorious studies of Gaston Boissier (<i>La fin du Paganisme</i>, Paris, 1891) treat especially the literary and moral aspects of that great transformation. Allard (<i>Julien l'Apostat</i>, I, 1900, p. 39 ff.) has furnished a summary of the religious evolution during the fourth century.</p>

The Christians, who were opposed to paganism and astrology, consequently manifested an aversion to the profane sciences in general, and in that way they became responsible to a serious extent for the gradual extinction of the knowledge of the past (cf. <i>Rev. hist. litt. rel.</i>, <i>ibid.</i>, p. 431; Royer, <i>L'enseignement d'Ausone à Alcuin</i>, 1906, p. 130 ff.).  <p><a name="page_284"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 284</font></a></p>  
 <p><a name="xref_8_19"><font color="GREEN">8_19</font></a>. Ambrosiaster, <i>Comm. in Epist. Pauli</i>, p. 58 B: "Dicentes per istos posse ire ad Deum sicut per comites pervenire ad regem" (cf. <i>Rev. his. lit. rel.</i>, VIII, 1903, p. 427).--The same idea was set forth by Maximus of Turin (<i>Adv. pag.</i>, col. 791) and by Lactantius (<i>Inst. div.</i>, II, 16, § 5 ff., p. 168 Brandt); on the celestial court, see also Arnobius, II, 36; Tertullian, Apol., 24.--Zeus bore the name of king, but the Hellenic Olympus was in reality a turbulent republic. The conception of a supreme god, the sovereign of a hierarchical court, seems to have been of Persian origin, and to have been propagated by the magi and the mysteries of Mithra. The inscription of the Nemroud Dagh speaks of Διὸς Ὠρομάσδου θρόνους (<i>supra</i>, ch. VI, n.  <a href="orrp19.htm#xref_6_26"><font color="GREEN" size="1">6_26</font></a>), and, in fact, a bas-relief shows Zeus-Oramasdes sitting on a throne, scepter in hand. The Mithra bas-reliefs likewise represent Jupiter Ormuzd on a throne, with the other gods standing around him (<i>Mon. myst. Mithra</i>, I, p. 129; 11, p. 188, fig. 11); and Hostanes pictured the angels sitting around the throne of God (<i>supra</i>, ch. VI, n.  <a href="orrp19.htm#xref_6_38"><font color="GREEN" size="1">6_38</font></a>; see Rev. iv). Moreover, the celestial god was frequently compared, not to a king in general, but to the Great King, and people spoke of his satraps; cf. Pseudo-Arist., Περὶ κόσμου, c.6, p. 398a, 10 ff. = Apul., <i>De mundo</i>, c. 26; Philo, <i>De opif. mundi</i>, c. 23, 27 (p. 24, 17; 32, 24, Cohn); Maximus of Turin, X, 9; and Capelle, <i>Die Schrift von der Welt</i> (<i>Neue Jahrb. für das klass. Altert.</i>, VIII), 1905, p. 556, n. 6. Particularly important is a passage of Celsus (Origen, <i>Contra Cels.</i>, VIII, 35) where the relation of this doctrine to the Persian demonology is shown. But the Mazdean conception must have combined, at an early date, with the old Semitic idea that Baal was the lord and master of his votaries (<i>supra</i>, <a href="orrp08.htm#page_94">p. 94</a> ff.). In his <i>Neutestamentliche Zeitgeschichte</i> (2d. ed., 1906, p. 364 ff.), Holtzmann insists on the fact that the people derived their conception of the kingdom  of God from the pattern of the Persian monarchy. See also <i>supra</i>, <a href="orrp09.htm#page_111">p. 111</a>.</p>
