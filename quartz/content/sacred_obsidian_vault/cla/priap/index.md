 <a name="epigrams"><h3 align="CENTER">Epigrams</h3></a>
 <a href="prp00.htm">To the Reader</a><br>
 <a href="prp01.htm">1. In play, Priapus (thou canst testify)</a><br>
 <a href="prp02.htm">2. Darkly might I to thee say: Oh give me for ever and ever</a><br>
 <a href="prp03.htm">3. These tablets, sacred to the Rigid God</a><br>
 <a href="prp04.htm">4. All the conditions (they say) Priapus made with the youngling</a><br>
 <a href="prp05.htm">5. Though I be wooden Priapus (as thou see'st)</a><br>
 <a href="prp06.htm">6. Oft in my speech one letter is lost; for Predicate always</a><br>
 <a href="prp07.htm">7. Matrons avoid this site, for your chaste breed</a><br>
 <a href="prp08.htm">8. 'Why be my parts obscene displayed without cover?' thou askest</a><br>
 <a href="prp09.htm">9. Why laugh such laughter, O most silly maid?</a><br>
 <a href="prp10.htm">10. 'Ware of my catching! If caught, with rod I never will harm thee</a><br>
 <a href="prp11.htm">11. A she (than Hector's parent longer aged,</a><br>
 <a href="prp12.htm">12. Thou shalt be pedicate (lad!), thou also (lass!) shalt be rogered</a><br>
 <a href="prp13.htm">13. Here' Here! nor dare expect (whoe'er thou be)</a><br>
 <a href="prp14.htm">14. Charged to my charge the fieldlet who shall dare</a><br>
 <a href="prp15.htm">15. Rare as those apples wherewith Hippomenes Schoeneïs ravished</a><br>
 <a href="prp16.htm">16. What hast thou, meddling watch, with me to do?</a><br>
 <a href="prp17.htm">17. Aye in this prickle of ours the bonniest boon to be found is</a><br>
 <a href="prp18.htm">18. Will ever Telethusa, posture-mime</a><br>
 <a href="prp19.htm">19. Thunders are under Jove; with the trident weaponed is Neptune</a><br>
 <a href="prp20.htm">20. Wealth is my loss! Do thou vouchsafe lend aid to my prayer,</a><br>
 <a href="prp21.htm">21. An fro' me woman shall thieve or plunder me man or a man-child,</a><br>
 <a href="prp22.htm">22. Whoso of violets here shall pluck or rose,</a><br>
 <a href="prp23.htm">23. Here has the bailiff, now of this plentiful garden the guardian,</a><br>
 <a href="prp24.htm">24. This staff of office cut from tree as 'tis,</a><br>
 <a href="prp25.htm">25. Hither, Quirites! (here what limit is?)</a><br>
 <a href="prp26.htm">26. Well-known darling of folk in the Circus Maximus far famed,</a><br>
 <a href="prp27.htm">27. Thou, of unrighteous thought, that hardly canst</a><br>
 <a href="prp28.htm">28. Priapus! perish I an words obscene</a><br>
 <a href="prp29.htm">29. Dreadful wi' sickle and dire with thy greater part, O Priapus!</a><br>
 <a href="prp30.htm">30. Hie thee amid these vines whereof an thou gather a grape-bunch</a><br>
 <a href="prp31.htm">31. Long as thy wanton hand to pluck refrain</a><br>
 <a href="prp32.htm">32. A damsel drier than the raisin'd grape,</a><br>
 <a href="prp33.htm">33. Wont the Priapi of old were to have both Naiads and Dryads</a><br>
 <a href="prp34.htm">34. At holy offering to the Lustful GodThief, for first thieving shalt be swived, but an</a><br>
 <a href="prp35.htm">35. Thief, for first thieving shalt be swived, but an</a><br>
 <a href="prp36.htm">36. We all show special notes of bodily shape:</a><br>
 <a href="prp37.htm">37. Why on memorial tablet do they limn</a><br>
 <a href="prp38.htm">38. Simply to thee I say whatever to say shall behove me</a><br>
 <a href="prp39.htm">39. Form-charms in Mercury have might to please</a><br>
 <a href="prp40.htm">40. Yon Telethusa befamed amid the damsels Suburran</a><br>
 <a href="prp41.htm">41. Whoso comes hither shall a bard become</a><br>
 <a href="prp42.htm">42. Bailiff Aristagoras of his grapes high-pedigree'd boasting</a><br>
 <a href="prp43.htm">43. Refrain from deeming all my sayings be</a><br>
 <a href="prp44.htm">44. What shouldest say this spear (although I'm wooden) be wishing</a><br>
 <a href="prp45.htm">45. Whenas the Rigid God espied a wight</a><br>
 <a href="prp46.htm">46. Ho girl! no whiter-skinned than Moorish man</a><br>
 <a href="prp47.htm">47. Who of you people here shall come to sup</a><br>
 <a href="prp48.htm">48. Tho' see you drenchèd wet that part of me</a><br>
 <a href="prp49.htm">49. Thou, who art 'customed to view around the walls of our temple</a><br>
 <a href="prp50.htm">50. A certain person, an thou please (Priapus!)</a><br>
 <a href="prp51.htm">51. What be this pother? For what cause suspects</a><br>
 <a href="prp52.htm">52. Ho thou, which hardly thy rapacious hand</a><br>
 <a href="prp53.htm">53. Bacchus often is wont with a moderate bunch to be sated</a><br>
 <a href="prp54.htm">54. E, D, an thou write, conjoining the two with a hyphen</a><br>
 <a href="prp55.htm">55. Who could believe my words? 'Tis shame to confess that the sickle</a><br>
 <a href="prp56.htm">56. Thou too dost mock me, Thief! and the infamous</a><br>
 <a href="prp57.htm">57. A chough, a caries, an eld-worn grave</a><br>
 <a href="prp58.htm">58. Whatever thief shall trick my faith may he</a><br>
 <a href="prp59.htm">59. Know, lest due warning be denied by thee</a><br>
 <a href="prp60.htm">60. Hadst thou as many of apples as offers of verses (Priapus!)</a><br>
 <a href="prp61.htm">61. Why, cultivator, vainly moan to me</a><br>
 <a href="prp62.htm">62. Sleep, O ye watchdogs! safe, while aid in guarding the garden</a><br>
 <a href="prp63.htm">63. 'Tis not enough, my friends, I set my seat</a><br>
 <a href="prp64.htm">64. One than a goose's marrow softer far</a><br>
 <a href="prp65.htm">65. This, with his snout aye alert to uproot the lilies a-blowing</a><br>
 <a href="prp66.htm">66. Thou, who lest manly mark thy glances meet</a><br>
 <a href="prp67.htm">67. PEnelope's first syllable followed by firstling of DIdo</a><br>
 <a href="prp68.htm">68. An I rustical seem to have spoken somewhat unlearned</a><br>
 <a href="prp69.htm">69. What then? Had Trojan yard Taenerian dame and her Cunnus</a><br>
 <a href="prp70.htm">70. When the fig's honied sweet thy taste shall catch</a><br>
 <a href="prp71.htm">71. A starveling stranger made me laughing-stock</a><br>
 <a href="prp72.htm">72. An thou pluck of this orchard fruit to my guarding committed</a><br>
 <a href="prp73.htm">73. Of vergers diligent guard (Priapus!), threat</a><br>
 <a href="prp74.htm">74. Not to be moved am I; shouldst thou, Thief, venture on thieving</a><br>
 <a href="prp75.htm">75. Why, O ye pathic girls, with sidelong oglings observe me?</a><br>
 <a href="prp76.htm">76. Right through the middle of lads and of lasses a passage shall pierce</a><br>
 <a href="prp77.htm">77. Dodona is hallowed, Jupiter, to thee</a><br>
 <a href="prp78.htm">78. Though I be agèd now, though head and chin</a><br>
 <a href="prp79.htm">79. The Gods and Goddesses deny thy teeth</a><br>
 <a href="prp80.htm">80. Although with yard distent (Priapus!) weighted</a><br>
 <a href="prp81.htm">81. Know that this crass coarse yard nor lengthens nor stands as becomes it</a><br>
 <a href="prp82.htm">82. While there is life 'tis fitting to hope, O rustical guardian!</a><br>
 <a href="prp83.htm">83. Bailiff of house whilom, now I of fieldlet the tiller</a><br>
 <a href="prp84.htm">84. What news be here? what send those angry gods?</a><br>
 <a href="prp85.htm">85. Neither of garden nor of blessed vine</a><br>
 <a href="prp86.htm">86. Roses in spring in the autumn fruits and in summer they bring me</a><br>
 <a href="prp87.htm">87. I thuswise fashioned I by rustic art</a><br>
 <a href="prp88.htm">88. This place, O youths, I protect, nor less this turf-builded cottage</a><br>
 <a href="prp89.htm">89. This grove to thee devote I give, Priapus!</a><br>
 <a href="prp90.htm">90. Thou who with prickle affrightest men and passives with sickle!</a><br>
 <a href="prp91.htm">91. I am not hewèd of the fragile elm</a><br>
 <a href="prp92.htm">92. A robber famed for greed exceeding wonder</a><br>
 <a href="prp93.htm">93. Carved me no rustic boor his artless sickle a-plying</a><br>
 <a href="prp94.htm">94. An thou would fain go filled thou mayest devour our Priapus</a><br>
 <a href="prp95.htm">95. First a wild-fig-tree trunk was I, not useful as timber</a><br>
