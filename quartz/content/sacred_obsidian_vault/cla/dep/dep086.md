 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Discourses of Epictetus</i>, tr. by P.E Matheson, [1916], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h1 align="center">The Discourses.</h1>  <h3 align="center">Book III. Notes.</h3>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_28"></a><a href="dep060.htm#fr_28">3-1</a> 'The <i>pancratium</i> consisted of boxing and wrestling, and was supposed to test all a man's powers. The <i>pentathlon</i> included running, jumping, quoit-throwing, spear-throwing, and wrestling.' (Matheson)</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_29"></a><a href="dep060.htm#fr_29">3-2</a> 'Young men of 18-20, under military training.' (Matheson)</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_30"></a><a href="dep061.htm#fr_30">3-3</a> Cf. Book I, note 7.</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_31"></a><a href="dep066.htm#fr_31">3-4</a> 'Such special commissioners, sent to set in order the affairs of the "free cities", date from Trajan's reign.' (Matheson)</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_32"></a><a href="dep066.htm#fr_32">3-5</a> These men are otherwise unknown. Matheson suggests that they were freedmen who had risen to high position.</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_33"></a><a href="dep067.htm#fr_33">3-6</a> A technical Stoic term.</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_34"></a><a href="dep068.htm#fr_34">3-7</a> I.e., a representative of the town at Rome.</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_35"></a><a href="dep068.htm#fr_35">3-8</a> A very valuable material.</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_36"></a><a href="dep072.htm#fr_36">3-9</a> A reference to the Stoic doctrine, ultimately derived from Heraclitus, that the universe was periodically consumed by fire.</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_37"></a><a href="dep072.htm#fr_37">3-10</a> 'Trajan after his conquest of Dacia.' (Matheson)</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_38"></a><a href="dep072.htm#fr_38">3-11</a> 'It is the drivel of the sickly novice in philosophy.' (Matheson)</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_39"></a><a href="dep074.htm#fr_39">3-12</a> Cf. the <i>Manual</i>, chap. 29.</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_40"></a><a href="dep079.htm#fr_40">3-13</a> Probably a slave.</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_41"></a><a href="dep081.htm#fr_41">3-14</a> 'The Cynic whom Epictetus describes is the ideal Stoic teacher, who is distinguished from the ordinary Stoic by a more austere and isolated life.' (Matheson)</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_42"></a><a href="dep081.htm#fr_42">3-15</a> 'Used to get a smooth skin.' (Matheson)</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_43"></a><a href="dep082.htm#fr_43">3-16</a> Cf. Xenophon, <i>Memorabilia</i>, <i>init</i>.</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_44"></a><a href="dep083.htm#fr_44">3-17</a> 'Probably refers to the story that Nicocreon ordered Anaxarchus’ tongue to be cut out, whereupon he bit it off himself and spat it in Nicocreon's face. Cf. Diogenes Laertius, IX. 59.' (Matheson)</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_45"></a><a href="dep083.htm#fr_45">3-18</a> 'A saying attributed to Solon, Anaxagoras, and Xenophon.' (Matheson)</p></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><p><a name="fn_46"></a><a href="dep085.htm#fr_46">3-19</a> The name of a slave.</p></font></p>
