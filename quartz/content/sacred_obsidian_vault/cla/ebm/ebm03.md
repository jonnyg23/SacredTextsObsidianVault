 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px"><small>                      “Tis not merely<br> The human beings pride that peoples space <br> With life and mystical predominance, <br> Since likewise for the stricken heart of Love <br> This visible nature, and this common world <br> Is all too narrow; yea, a deeper import <br> Lurks in the legend told my infant years <br> That lies upon that truth, we live to learn, <br> For fable is Loves world, his home, his birthplace; <br> Delightedly he dwells mong fays and talismans, <br> And spirits, and delightedly believes <br> Divinities, being himself divine. <br> The intelligible forms of ancient poets, <br> The fair humanities of Old Religion, <br> The Power, the Beauty, and the Majesty, <br> That had their haunts in dale or piny mountain, <br> Or forests by slow stream, or pebbly spring, <br> Or chasms or watry depths;—all these have vanished. <br> They live no longer in the faith of Reason, <br> But still the heart doth need a language; still <br> Doth the old instinct bring back the old names.”</small></div> 
 <p align="right"><small><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Schiller</span>: <i>The Piccolomini</i>, Act. ii. Scene 4.</small></p>
