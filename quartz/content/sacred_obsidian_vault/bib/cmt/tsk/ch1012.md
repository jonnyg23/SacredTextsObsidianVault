 </p><p><a href="../../../bib/kjv/ch1012.htm#001">Ch1 12:1</a>, The companies that came to David at Ziklag; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/ch1012.htm#023">Ch1 12:23</a>, The armies that came to him at Hebron.

 <p>these are:  <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa1027.htm#002">Sa1 27:2</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa1027.htm#006">Sa1 27:6</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2001.htm#001">Sa2 1:1</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2004.htm#010">Sa2 4:10</a> 
 </p><p>while he yet:  etc. Heb. being yet shut up, Sometimes, in the East, when a successful prince endeavoured to extirpate the preceding royal family, some of them escaped the slaughter, and secured themselves in an impregnable fortress, or in a place of great secrecy; while others have been known to seek an asylum in a foreign county, from when they have occasioned, from time to time, great anxiety and great difficulties to the usurper of the crown.  The expression shut up, so often applied to the extermination of eastern royal families (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/deu032.htm#032">Deu 32:32</a>.  <a href="../../../bib/kjv/kg1014.htm#010">Kg1 14:10</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/kg1021.htm#021">Kg1 21:21</a>.  <a href="../../../bib/kjv/kg2009.htm#008">Kg2 9:8</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/kg2014.htm#026">Kg2 14:26</a>), strictly speaking, refers to the two first of these cases; but the term may be used in a more extensive sense, for those who, by retiring into deserts, or foreign countries, preserve themselves from being slain by the men who usurp the dominions of their ancestors.  Thus the term is here applied to David, though he did not shut himself up, strictly speaking, in Ziklag.  It is described as a town in the country, and was probably an unwalled town; and it is certain that he did not confine himself to it, but, on the contrary, was continually making excursions from thence.

 <p>with a perfect heart:  The meaning of this expression may be inferred from that of a double heart in <a href="../../../bib/kjv/ch1012.htm#033">Ch1 12:33</a>.  If a double heart be expressive of insincerity or duplicity, a perfect heart, which seems to be put in opposition to it, must signify a sincere, faithful, and entire attachment. <a href="../../../bib/kjv/kg1008.htm#061">Kg1 8:61</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/kg1011.htm#004">Kg1 11:4</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/kg2020.htm#003">Kg2 20:3</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa101.htm#002">Psa 101:2</a>

 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_040">ch1 12:40</a></font></span></p>
 <p>brought:  The Septuagint reads εφερον αυτιος, "brought (to) them" which is probably correct; the Hebrew lahem, "to them" might be easily mistaken for lechem, "bread."  The passage will then read, "bought them on asses, on camels, and on mules, and on oxen, meat, meal, cakes of figs," etc., which renders the introduction of and unnecessary.  From the mention of oil, figs, and raisins, Mr. Harmer thinks that this assembly was held in autumn. <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2016.htm#001">Sa2 16:1</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2017.htm#027">Sa2 17:27-29</a>
