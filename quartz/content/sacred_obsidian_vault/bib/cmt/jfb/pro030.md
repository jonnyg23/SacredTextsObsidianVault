Fausset and David Brown [1882] at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <h3 align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#001">Proverbs Chapter 30</a></h3> 
 <p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#001">Proverbs 30:1</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_001">pro 30:1</a></font></span></p>(Pro. 30:1-33)
 <p>
 </p><p>This is the title of this chapter (see Introduction). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>the prophecy--literally, "the burden" (compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa013.htm#001">Isa 13:1</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac009.htm#001">Zac 9:1</a>), used for any divine instruction; not necessarily a prediction, which was only a kind of prophecy (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/ch1015.htm#027">Ch1 15:27</a>, "a song"). Prophets were inspired men, who spoke for God to man, or for man to God (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen020.htm#007">Gen 20:7</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/exo007.htm#014">Exo 7:14-16</a>). Such, also, were the New Testament prophets. In a general sense, Gad, Nathan, and others were such, who were divine teachers, though we do not learn that they ever predicted. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>the man spake--literally, "the saying of the man"; an expression used to denote any solemn and important announcement (compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2023.htm#001">Sa2 23:1</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa036.htm#001">Psa 36:1</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa110.htm#001">Psa 110:1</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa001.htm#024">Isa 1:24</a>, &amp;c.). Ithiel and Ucal were perhaps pupils.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#002">Proverbs 30:2</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_002">pro 30:2</a></font></span></p>brutish--stupid, a strong term to denote his lowly self-estimation; or he may speak of such as his natural condition, as contrasted with God's all-seeing comprehensive knowledge and almighty power. The questions of this clause emphatically deny the attributes mentioned to be those of any creature, thus impressively strengthening the implied reference of the former to God (compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/deu030.htm#012">Deu 30:12-14</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa040.htm#012">Isa 40:12</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/eph004.htm#008">Eph 4:8</a>).
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#005">Proverbs 30:5</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_005">pro 30:5</a></font></span></p>(Compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa012.htm#006">Psa 12:6</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa119.htm#140">Psa 119:140</a>).
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#006">Proverbs 30:6</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_006">pro 30:6</a></font></span></p>Add . . . words--implying that his sole reliance was on God's all-sufficient teaching. 
 <p>
 </p><p>reprove thee--or, "convict thee"--and so the falsehood will appear.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#007">Proverbs 30:7</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_007">pro 30:7</a></font></span></p>A prayer for exemption from wickedness, and the extremes of poverty and riches, the two things mentioned. Contentment is implied as desired.
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#008">Proverbs 30:8</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_008">pro 30:8</a></font></span></p>vanity--all sorts of sinful acts (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/job011.htm#011">Job 11:11</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa005.htm#018">Isa 5:18</a>).
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#009">Proverbs 30:9</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_009">pro 30:9</a></font></span></p>be full . . . deny--that is, puffed up by the pride of prosperity. 
 <p>
 </p><p>take the name . . . vain--This is not (Hebrew) the form (compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/exo020.htm#007">Exo 20:7</a>), but "take" rather denotes laying violent hold on any thing; that is, lest I assail God's name or attributes, as justice, mercy, &amp;c., which the poor are tempted to do.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#010">Proverbs 30:10</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_010">pro 30:10</a></font></span></p>Accuse not--Slander not (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa010.htm#007">Psa 10:7</a>). 
 <p>
 </p><p>curse . . . guilty--lest, however lowly, he be exasperated to turn on thee, and your guilt be made to appear.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#011">Proverbs 30:11</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_011">pro 30:11</a></font></span></p>Four kinds of hateful persons--(1) graceless children, (2) hypocrites, (3) the proud, (4) cruel oppressors (compare on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#014">Pro 30:14</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa014.htm#004">Psa 14:4</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa052.htm#002">Psa 52:2</a>) --are now illustrated; (1) <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#015">Pro 30:15-16</a>, the insatiability of prodigal children and their fate; (2) <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#017">Pro 30:17</a>, hypocrisy, or the concealment of real character; (3 and 4) <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#018">Pro 30:18-20</a>, various examples of pride and oppression.
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#015">Proverbs 30:15</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_015">pro 30:15</a></font></span></p>horse leech--supposed by some to be the vampire (a fabulous creature), as being literally insatiable; but the other subjects mentioned must be taken as this, comparatively insatiable. The use of a fabulous creature agreeably to popular notions is not inconsistent with inspiration. 
 <p>
 </p><p>There are three . . . yea, four--(Compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro006.htm#016">Pro 6:16</a>).
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#017">Proverbs 30:17</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_017">pro 30:17</a></font></span></p>The eye--for the person, with reference to the use of the organ to express mockery and contempt, and also as that by which punishment is received. 
 <p>
 </p><p>the ravens . . . eagles . . . eat--either as dying unnaturally, or being left unburied, or both.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#018">Proverbs 30:18</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_018">pro 30:18</a></font></span></p>Hypocrisy is illustrated by four examples of the concealment of all methods or traces of action, and a pertinent example of double dealing in actual vice is added, that is, the adulterous woman. 
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#020">Proverbs 30:20</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_020">pro 30:20</a></font></span></p>she eateth . . . mouth--that is, she hides the evidences of her shame and professes innocence.
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#021">Proverbs 30:21</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_021">pro 30:21</a></font></span></p>Pride and cruelty, the undue exaltation of those unfit to hold power, produce those vices which disquiet society (compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro019.htm#010">Pro 19:10</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro028.htm#003">Pro 28:3</a>).
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#023">Proverbs 30:23</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_023">pro 30:23</a></font></span></p>heir . . . mistress--that is, takes her place as a wife (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen016.htm#004">Gen 16:4</a>).
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#024">Proverbs 30:24</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_024">pro 30:24</a></font></span></p>These verses provide two classes of apt illustrations of various aspects of the moral world, which the reader is left to apply. By the first (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#025">Pro 30:25-28</a>), diligence and providence are commended; the success of these insignificant animals being due to their instinctive sagacity and activity, rather than strength. The other class (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#030">Pro 30:30-31</a>) provides similes for whatever is majestic or comely, uniting efficiency with gracefulness.
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#026">Proverbs 30:26</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_026">pro 30:26</a></font></span></p>conies--mountain mice, or rabbits.
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#028">Proverbs 30:28</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_028">pro 30:28</a></font></span></p>spider--tolerated, even in palaces, to destroy flies. 
 <p>
 </p><p>taketh . . . hands--or, uses with activity the limbs provided for taking prey.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#032">Proverbs 30:32</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_032">pro 30:32</a></font></span></p>As none can hope, successfully, to resist such a king, suppress even the thought of an attempt. 
 <p>
 </p><p>lay . . . hand upon thy mouth--"lay" is well supplied (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/jdg018.htm#019">Jdg 18:19</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/job029.htm#009">Job 29:9</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/job040.htm#004">Job 40:4</a>).
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro030.htm#033">Proverbs 30:33</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_033">pro 30:33</a></font></span></p>That is, strife--or other ills, as surely arise from devising evil as natural effects from natural causes.
