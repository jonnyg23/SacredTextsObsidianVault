 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>The Scofield Bible Commentary</i>, by Cyrus Ingerson Scofield, [1917], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <h3 align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk001.htm#001">Luke Chapter 1</a></h3> 
 <p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk001.htm#003">Luke 1:3</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_003">luk 1:3</a></font></span></p>
 <p>
 </p><p>from 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>"From the very first": (Greek, "anōthen", "from above)." So translated in (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/joh003.htm#031">Joh 3:31</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/joh019.htm#011">Joh 19:11</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam001.htm#017">Jam 1:17</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/joh003.htm#015">Joh 3:15</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/joh003.htm#017">Joh 3:17</a>). In no other place is the Greek, "anōthen", translated "from the very first." The use by Luke of anothen is an affirmation that his knowledge of these things, derived from those who had been eye-witnesses from the beginning (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk001.htm#002">Luk 1:2</a>) was confirmed by revelation. In like manner, Paul had doubtless heard from the eleven the story of the institution of the Lord's Supper, but he also had it by revelation from the Lord (compare (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/co1011.htm#023">Co1 11:23</a>) and his writing, like Luke's "anōthen" knowledge, thus became first-hand, not traditional, merely. 
 </p><p>
