 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_008">zac 1:8</a></font></span></p>
 <p>
 </p><p>I saw 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>The "man" (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#008">Zac 1:8</a>) is the "my lord," "the angel that talked with me" (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#009">Zac 1:9</a>), and "the angel of the Lord" (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#010">Zac 1:10</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#011">Zac 1:11</a>). The "man" "stood among the myrtle trees" (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#008">Zac 1:8</a>). The prophet addresses him as "my lord" (compare (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen019.htm#002">Gen 19:2</a>), but when the "man" answers he perceives that he has addressed an angel -- "the angel that talked with me" (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#009">Zac 1:9</a>). In (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#010">Zac 1:10</a>). The being of the vision is again "the man that stood among the myrtle trees." In (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#011">Zac 1:11</a>). He is called "the angel of the Lord," and to him the (riders on the) "red horses, speckled with white" say: "We have walked to and fro," etc. Then (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#012">Zac 1:12</a>), "the angel of the Lord" (that is, the "man," "my lord," "the angel that talked with me") intercedes for the land against a world at ease. The date of the intercession was at the end of the 70 years' captivity of Judah. (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#009">Zac 1:9-12</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>Taken as a whole (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#008">Zac 1:8-17</a>) Zechariah's first vision reveals Judah in dispersion; Jerusalem under adverse possession; and the Gentile nations at rest about it. This condition still continues, and Jehovah's answer to the intercession of the angel sweeps on to the end-time of Gentile domination, when "the Lord shall yet comfort Zion," etc. (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#016">Zac 1:16</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#017">Zac 1:17</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa040.htm#001">Isa 40:1-5</a>).

 <p>
 </p><p>And the Lord 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>The word "charash", translated, "carpenter," is literally, carver, engraver. (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/zac001.htm#021">Zac 1:21</a>) makes it plain that, whatever the four carvers may be, they are used to "fray," or carve away (Hebrew, charad) in the sense of diminishing, enfeebling, the great Gentile world-powers. They may stand for Jehovah's "four sore judgments," the sword, famine, evil beasts, and pestilence (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/eze014.htm#021">Eze 14:21</a>); the four horses of Revelation 6.
