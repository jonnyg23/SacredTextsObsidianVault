 <p><a name="000"><b>Rei</b></a>     Friendly, one who maintained true allegiance to king David (<a href="../kjv/kg1001.htm#008">Kg1 1:8</a>) when Adonijah rebelled. 
 </p>
 <p><a name="001"><b>Reins</b></a>     The kidneys, the supposed seat of the desires and affections; used metaphorically for "heart." The "reins" and the "heart" are often mentioned together, as denoting the whole moral constitution of man (<a href="../kjv/psa007.htm#009">Psa 7:9</a>; <a href="../kjv/psa016.htm#007">Psa 16:7</a>; <a href="../kjv/psa026.htm#002">Psa 26:2</a>; <a href="../kjv/psa139.htm#013">Psa 139:13</a>; <a href="../kjv/jer017.htm#010">Jer 17:10</a>, etc.). 
 </p>
 <p><a name="002"><b>Rekem</b></a>     Embroidered; variegated.     (1.) One of the five Midianite kings whom the Israelites destroyed (<a href="../kjv/num031.htm#008">Num 31:8</a>). (2.) One of the sons of Hebron (<a href="../kjv/ch1002.htm#043">Ch1 2:43</a>, <a href="../kjv/ch1002.htm#044">Ch1 2:44</a>). (3.) A town of Benjamin (<a href="../kjv/jos018.htm#027">Jos 18:27</a>). 
 </p>
 <p><a name="003"><b>Remaliah</b></a>     Adorned by the Lord, the father of Pekah, who conspired successfully against Pekahiah (<a href="../kjv/kg2015.htm#025">Kg2 15:25</a>, <a href="../kjv/kg2015.htm#027">Kg2 15:27</a>, <a href="../kjv/kg2015.htm#030">Kg2 15:30</a>, <a href="../kjv/kg2015.htm#032">Kg2 15:32</a>, <a href="../kjv/kg2015.htm#037">Kg2 15:37</a>; <a href="../kjv/isa007.htm#001">Isa 7:1</a>, <a href="../kjv/isa007.htm#004">Isa 7:4</a>, <a href="../kjv/isa007.htm#005">Isa 7:5</a>, <a href="../kjv/isa007.htm#009">Isa 7:9</a>; <a href="../kjv/isa008.htm#006">Isa 8:6</a>). 
 </p>
 <p><a name="004"><b>Remeth</b></a>     Another form of Ramah (q.v.) or Ramoth (<a href="../kjv/ch1006.htm#073">Ch1 6:73</a>; <a href="../kjv/jos019.htm#021">Jos 19:21</a>), and probably also of Jarmuth (<a href="../kjv/jos021.htm#029">Jos 21:29</a>). 
 </p>
 <p><a name="005"><b>Remmon-methoar</b></a>     (<a href="../kjv/jos019.htm#013">Jos 19:13</a>), rendered correctly in the Revised Version, "Rimmon, which stretcheth unto Neah," a landmark of Zebulun; called also Rimmon (<a href="../kjv/ch1006.htm#077">Ch1 6:77</a>). 
 </p>
 <p><a name="006"><b>Remphan</b></a>     (<a href="../kjv/act007.htm#043">Act 7:43</a>; R.V., "Rephan"). In <a href="../kjv/amo005.htm#026">Amo 5:26</a> the Heb.  Chiun  (q.v.) is rendered by the LXX. "Rephan," and this name is adopted by Luke in his narrative of the Acts. These names represent the star-god Saturn or Moloch. 
 </p>
 <p><a name="007"><b>Rent</b></a>     (<a href="../kjv/isa003.htm#024">Isa 3:24</a>), probably a rope, as rendered in the LXX. and Vulgate and Revised Version, or as some prefer interpreting the phrase, "girdle and robe are torn [i.e., are 'a rent'] by the hand of violence." 
 </p>
 <p><a name="008"><b>Repentance</b></a>     There are three Greek words used in the New Testament to denote repentance.     (1.) The verb  metamelomai  is used of a change of mind, such as to produce regret or even remorse on account of sin, but not necessarily a change of heart. This word is used with reference to the repentance of Judas (<a href="../kjv/mat027.htm#003">Mat 27:3</a>). (2.)   Metanoeo , meaning to change one's mind and purpose, as the result of after knowledge. (3.)  This verb, with the cognate noun  metanoia , is used of true repentance, a change of mind and purpose and life, to which remission of sin is promised.    Evangelical repentance consists of (1) a true sense of one's own guilt and sinfulness; (2) an apprehension of God's mercy in Christ; (3) an actual hatred of sin (<a href="../kjv/psa119.htm#128">Psa 119:128</a>; <a href="../kjv/job042.htm#005">Job 42:5</a>, <a href="../kjv/job042.htm#006">Job 42:6</a>; <a href="../kjv/co2007.htm#010">Co2 7:10</a>) and turning from it to God; and (4) a persistent endeavour after a holy life in a walking with God in the way of his commandments. The true penitent is conscious of guilt (<a href="../kjv/psa051.htm#004">Psa 51:4</a>, <a href="../kjv/psa051.htm#009">Psa 51:9</a>), of pollution (<a href="../kjv/psa051.htm#005">Psa 51:5</a>, <a href="../kjv/psa051.htm#007">Psa 51:7</a>, <a href="../kjv/psa051.htm#010">Psa 51:10</a>), and of helplessness (<a href="../kjv/psa051.htm#011">Psa 51:11</a>; <a href="../kjv/psa109.htm#021">Psa 109:21</a>, <a href="../kjv/psa109.htm#022">Psa 109:22</a>). Thus he apprehends himself to be just what God has always seen him to be and declares him to be. But repentance comprehends not only such a sense of sin, but also an apprehension of mercy, without which there can be no true repentance (<a href="../kjv/psa051.htm#001">Psa 51:1</a>; <a href="../kjv/psa130.htm#004">Psa 130:4</a>). 
 </p>
 <p><a name="009"><b>Rephael</b></a>     Healed of God, one of Shemaiah's sons. He and his brethren, on account of their "strength for service," formed one of the divisions of the temple porters (<a href="../kjv/ch1026.htm#007">Ch1 26:7</a>, <a href="../kjv/ch1026.htm#008">Ch1 26:8</a>). 
 </p>
