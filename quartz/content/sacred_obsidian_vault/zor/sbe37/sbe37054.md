 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>Pahlavi Texts, Part IV (SBE37)</i>, E.W. West, tr. [1892], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER V.</h3>  
 <p align="center"><i>Sû</i>d<i>kar Nask</i>.</p>  
 <p>1. The fourth fargar<i>d</i>, Yânîm-manô <a name="fr_506"></a><a href="#fn_503"><font size="1">1</font></a>, is about where a gradual development (dêr-zahî<i>s</i>nîh) <i>of</i> that which is for the future existence is best; and, secondly, that which <i>occurs</i> now when the wisdom, instructed eloquence, diligence, and energetic effort, which are the utilizers of life, are with one, and these five misusers <i>of it</i>greediness, want of energy, indolence, defilement, and illicit intercourseare not with one. 2. This, too, that these five defects existed in Dahâk <a name="fr_507"></a><a href="#fn_504"><font size="1">2</font></a>, and owing to that, moreover, Frê<i>d</i>ûn <a name="fr_508"></a><a href="#fn_504"><font size="1">2</font></a> is irritated with <i>him</i>, and smites him in revenge for Yim <a name="fr_509"></a><a href="#fn_505"><font size="1">3</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>3. About the heinousness of these four vices, which are drunkenness, knavish companionship, apostasy, and selfishness, and the grievous results therefrom. 4. And this, too, that Yim drove away these four vices from the world, and then was able to prepare immortality. 5. About avoidance of him who, through any statement, is producing a thief as an orator (âkhûn), and <i>of</i> acquiescence with a hasty unoratorical statement of a companion. 6. And this, too, that <i>he</i> who propagates very evil commands in the world gives stout-heartedness to the fiend.</p>

 <p>7. About the clamour of a poor distressed <i>one for</i> a perfect remedy, and the repelling derangement (lakhvâr-pafshîri<i>s</i>nîh), unacceptableness, unblessedness, and want of Gâtha lore of the distresser arisen from the clamour of the distressed <i>one</i>. 8. About the connection of satisfying distress on true <i>and</i> reasonable complaint, and the reasonable complaining of true complainers, by him who <i>has</i> been an inferior judge, <i>and</i> gradually up to the highest adjudicator who is Aûha<i>r</i>ma<i>z</i><i>d</i>.</p>  
 <p>9. The excellence <i>of</i> righteousness is perfect.</p>
