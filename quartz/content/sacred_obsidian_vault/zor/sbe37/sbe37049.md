 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>Pahlavi Texts, Part IV (SBE37)</i>, E.W. West, tr. [1892], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER XLVI.</h3>  
 <p>1. The Gâthas of the Ya<i>s</i>t <a name="fr_480"></a><a href="#fn_477"><font size="1">1</font></a>, as the first offspring of the Ahunavair, are a recitation of the source of sources of the religion, and in the compass (parvastârîh) <a name="fr_481"></a><a href="#fn_478"><font size="1">2</font></a> <i>of</i> the Gâthas, every word (mârîk)</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_170"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 170</font></a></p>  
 <p>in it is the origin of a word. 2. The word ahû <a name="fr_482"></a><a href="#fn_479"><font size="1">1</font></a> of the beginning <a name="fr_483"></a><a href="#fn_480"><font size="1">2</font></a> is of a like kind with ahyâ <a name="fr_484"></a><a href="#fn_481"><font size="1">3</font></a>, the beginning of the Gâthas; the end word, which is vâstârem <a name="fr_485"></a><a href="#fn_482"><font size="1">4</font></a>, is <i>of a like kind</i> with vahyô <a name="fr_486"></a><a href="#fn_483"><font size="1">5</font></a>, the end of the Gâthas; and the wholewhich, though <i>its</i> nature is <i>of</i> one kind, is distributed (vakhtŏ) in <i>what is</i> selected therefromis stored up (a<i>v</i>argû<i>d</i>ŏ) in <i>this</i> compendium <a name="fr_487"></a><a href="#fn_484"><font size="1">6</font></a> of all parts of the Ma<i>z</i><i>d</i>a-worshipping religion.</p>  
 <p>3. Likewise the purport (<i>a</i><i>v</i>orî-hastân) <a name="fr_488"></a><a href="#fn_485"><font size="1">7</font></a> of its verse (gâh), and the particulars of the primitive Vîspêra<i>d</i> <a name="fr_489"></a><a href="#fn_486"><font size="1">8</font></a> are to procure homage and praise, oblation <i>and</i> invocation; <i>and</i> the blessing <a name="fr_490"></a><a href="#fn_487"><font size="1">9</font></a>, which is regulated by the sagacity of the creator, is adapted for the spiritual illustration of the lodgment of the ceremonial of the sacred beings therein. 4. All</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_171"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 171</font></a></p>  
 <p>three are provisions for the first <i>and</i> last presentations <a name="fr_491"></a><a href="#fn_488"><font size="1">1</font></a> which <i>one</i> utters by means of the Stô<i>d</i> Ya<i>s</i>t.</p>  
 <p>5. It is perfect <i>is</i> the excellence of righteousness; it is perfect excellence <i>that is</i> righteousness; with the copy revised (râyînî<i>d</i>ŏ).</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_477"></a><a href="sbe37049.htm#fr_480">169:1</a> Corresponding to the twenty-first word, vâstârem, in the Ahunavair, according to B. P. Riv.; but it is the first Nask in other Rivâyats. In Chap. I, 9, 12 it is called Stô<i>d</i>-ya<i>s</i>t, 'praise-ritual,' (Av. staota yêsnya); and Stûd-ya<i>s</i>t, or Ya<i>s</i>t, in the Rivâyats, which also state that it contains thirty-three kardah, or ʓûrat. In Sls. XIII, 1 we are told that Vîsâi v<i>e</i>-ameshâ-spe<i>n</i>tâ (Yas. XIV, 1) is the beginning of the Stôtân-yasnô; and, if we look for its end, we find Yas. LVIII, LIX both ending with special reverence of 'the whole collection of the Stôtân-yasnân.' We may therefore conclude that Yas. XIV-LIX, with its supplementary passages in Vîsp. V-XXIV, contains the whole of the Stô<i>d</i>-ya<i>s</i>t. But from this we must deduct Yas. XIX-XXI which are the first three fargar<i>d</i>s of the Bakŏ Nask, Yas. LII which is an interpolation, and Yas. LVI, LVII which are the Srôsh Ya<i>s</i>ts, lesser and greater; we must also consider the Yasna Haptanghâiti as a single section, in accordance with its treatment in Bk. IX, Chaps. XII, XXXV, LVII; and much of the Vîspêra<i>d</i> may not belong to the primitive text mentioned in § 3. Making these necessary deductions we have exactly thirty-three has of the Yasna left for the Stô<i>d</i>-ya<i>s</i>t, as stated in the Rivâyats.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_478"></a><a href="sbe37049.htm#fr_481">169:2</a> This word can also be read fravistârîh (Av. fra + vid), 'interpretation,' or frôstârîh, 'handing down.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_479"></a><a href="sbe37049.htm#fr_482">170:1</a> The Ahunavair begins with the words yathâ ahû vairyô. The word ahû, in the MS., is written ahî as usual in Irân.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_480"></a><a href="sbe37049.htm#fr_483">170:2</a> Assuming that ba<i>r</i>â stands for bûn.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_481"></a><a href="sbe37049.htm#fr_484">170:3</a> The first Gâtha, or sacred hymn, begins with the words ahyâ yâsâ nemanghâ (Yas. XXVIII, 1 a). There is, of course, no connection but that of sound between ahû, 'a spiritual lord,' and ahyâ, 'of this;' nor is there any other between the concluding words vâstârem, 'a protector,' and vahyô, 'better,' though the phrases in which these latter occur are of a very similar character, which fully justifies the comparison made in the text.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_482"></a><a href="sbe37049.htm#fr_485">170:4</a> The Ahunavair ends with the words yim drigubyô dada<i>d</i> vâstârem.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_483"></a><a href="sbe37049.htm#fr_486">170:5</a> The last Gâtha ends with the words yâ erezhe<i>g</i>yôi dâhî drigaovê vahyô (Yas. LIII, 9 d).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_484"></a><a href="sbe37049.htm#fr_487">170:6</a> The Gâthas apparently.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_485"></a><a href="sbe37049.htm#fr_488">170:7</a> Or <i>a</i><i>v</i>ar-gâstân, 'disseminations.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_486"></a><a href="sbe37049.htm#fr_489">170:8</a> The Vîspêra<i>d</i> service consists of the Yasna ritual with certain additional passages intermixed, which passages are called the Vîspêra<i>d</i> because the earlier ones invoke 'all the chiefs' (vîspê ratavô, Visp. II, 3) of creation.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_487"></a><a href="sbe37049.htm#fr_490">170:9</a> Possibly Yas. LV.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_488"></a><a href="sbe37049.htm#fr_491">171:1</a> Probably referring to Yas. XIV and LVIII.</p>
