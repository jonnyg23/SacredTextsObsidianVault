 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>Pahlavi Texts, Part IV (SBE37)</i>, E.W. West, tr. [1892], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER XXXVIII.</h3>  
 <p>1. One section of the <i>first</i> thirty of the Sakâ<i>d</i>ûm <a name="fr_300"></a><a href="#fn_299"><font size="1">1</font></a> <i>contains</i> particulars about reward <i>by</i> command of the religion, the bridge <i>judgment</i> of the destroyers of the well-commanding, <i>and</i> the provision for their destruction. 2. About the importance of a man, after fifteen years of age <i>and</i> when he has heard that there is a law <a name="fr_301"></a><a href="#fn_300"><font size="1">2</font></a> which is good, <i>having</i> sought that law <a name="fr_302"></a><a href="#fn_300"><font size="1">2</font></a> by <i>having</i> enquired <i>about it</i>. 3. About a man's scrutinizing an action before doing <i>it</i>, when he does not know whether <i>it be</i> a sin or a good work,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_122"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 122</font></a></p>  
 <p><i>and</i> when <i>it</i> is possible for him to set <i>it</i> aside <i>and</i> not to do <i>it</i>.</p>  
 <p>4. About advice as to <i>having</i> entered into a house in the night by the light <i>of</i> a fire, or when <i>one</i> has noticed it in this place, though he goes elsewhere; <i>also</i> the watchful destruction of an injured person, or animal, or garment, <i>and</i> the retribution for the injury. 5. About the extent of any glitter of the sparks (zakhsh-1-î parkân), <i>and</i> the width <i>and</i> height of the doors of the constructed work of that appointed place <i>of the fire</i>.</p>  
 <p>6. About a new-born child, as to how <i>one has</i> to provide its place, connected lawfully <i>with</i> illumination <a name="fr_303"></a><a href="#fn_301"><font size="1">1</font></a>, more particularly <i>for</i> the first three nights. 7. About bringing a fire to <i>drive</i> away the overpowering fiend, and making the child taste first the Hôm-juice, so far as collected within its precincts (varân), and, <a href="errata.htm#4">secondly</a>, the butter of Maî<i>d</i>ôk-zarem <a name="fr_304"></a><a href="#fn_302"><font size="1">2</font></a> which is to be brought forward for it; <i>also</i> the watchfulness of the father and mother over the child, and the extent of their retiring (na<i>v</i>istanŏ) from the two sides of the <i>new</i>-born. 8. About lawfully-made places of several kinds for the child, the limits <i>and</i> manner of the mother's giving milk to the child, <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject.</p>  
 <p>9. About carrying forth holy-water, <i>or</i> even a cooking-pot, to a fire, where the hands are purified <i>and</i> thoroughly washed; <i>and</i> the sin owing to an unpurified</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_123"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 123</font></a></p>  
 <p>hand, not thoroughly washed, carrying <i>them</i> forth. 10. About the preservation of the cooking-pot, and the rest of one's operations with the fire, from defilement; <i>but</i> when, through want of care, defilement occurs, <i>by</i> the inexperience of any one bringing <i>it</i> to the fire, he who is careless is thereby contaminated, <i>and</i> the cooking-pot is properly placed in its position.</p>  
 <p>11. Arranging about properly-made bed-places (gâsvârakŏ) in a house, those for children and those for adults; also a decision about <i>a case</i> when a carpenter (dûrgar) shall make a bed-place properly which one's own judgment considers improperly <i>made</i>, and when both <i>consider it</i> improperly, <i>or</i> when both <i>consider it</i> properly <i>made;</i> and more of whatever is on the same subject.</p>  
 <p>12. About what is the mode <i>of</i> producing seeing properly; <i>and</i>, when not seeing properly, the oculist (dî<i>d</i>pân) to intrust with it is <i>he</i> who informs people, who wish for it, <i>how</i> to extract the defect of sight; if not, the people go on and hurt; <i>also</i> the penalty for hurting, and whatever is on the same subject.</p>  
 <p>13. About the insubordination of those accustomed to work (kâr-khûgarân) to women and children; also that of a grown-up man who <i>has</i> been giving no food <a name="fr_305"></a><a href="#fn_303"><font size="1">1</font></a> three <i>times</i> in succession; he, too, it is who advanced the fourth <i>time</i> <a name="fr_306"></a><a href="#fn_304"><font size="1">2</font></a>, because, owing to giving no food a fourth <i>time</i>, the man is he who <i>has</i> to accomplish work unrestrictedly; <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_124"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 124</font></a></p>  
 <p>14. About the care of a pointed thing, that is, how <i>it</i> is to be carried to a dwelling in the world, how <i>it</i> is to be deposited, <i>and</i> the sin owing to keeping and depositing <i>it</i> otherwise. 15. And about every garment <a name="fr_307"></a><a href="#fn_305"><font size="1">1</font></a> and utensil, even including such as a scum-pot, an hour-glass, and a dining-tray; that is, how <i>they are</i> to be deposited in the dwelling, <i>and</i> the sin owing to variously <a name="fr_308"></a><a href="#fn_306"><font size="1">2</font></a> placing <i>and</i> taking care <i>of them</i>. 16. About a door which is properly made; how it is when it falls down, and a wound arises from it, the carpenter <i>being</i> innocent regarding it; <i>and</i> how it is when <i>he is</i> guilty.</p>  
 <p>17. About washing the head, the care of the water and the religious ritual therein, and whatever is on the same subject. 18. About the period <i>for</i> arranging the hair, in which they shave the hair. 19. About the shaving of a child the first time, and the ritual which is taught for it; the performance of shaving <i>by</i> an instructed barber and with a sharp razor, which is the appointed practice as regards the razor of adults, <i>and</i> that also for children <i>with</i> the children's razor, because <i>it is</i> settled healthfulness; his whetstone (<i>s</i>ôn), and also the care of the razor. 20. About the number of the positions of a man, in which a barber can perform shaving, and that of the positions of the barber; <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject.</p>  
 <p>21. About each one of those who are custodians (kîrûk-kârânŏ), and the rules of the market; also their abstaining from wounding each other with a pike (têkh), <i>or</i> other implement, with which they</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_125"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 125</font></a></p>  
 <p>shall perform <i>their</i> duty; <i>likewise</i> the sin owing to heedlessness. 22. About giving forth a pointed thing lawfully, and a wound owing to not giving <i>it</i> forth lawfully; lawfully taking and giving away a plate of broken victuals (pa<i>d</i>khûr), and a wound owing to <i>doing it</i> unlawfully; <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject. 23. About the appointed place (dâ<i>d</i>-gâh) of a horse-course <i>and its</i> distance from the middle of a town, the nature of the horse-course, the training (farhâng) <i>and</i> masters of manuvres (pa<i>d</i>ân-î farhângânŏ) when in it, the shooting of arrows on the horse-course, <i>and</i> the wound which occurs to man <i>or</i> animal, how it is when culpable, and how it is when not culpable. 24. About admitting a listener; where, why, <i>and</i> how <i>he is</i> to be admitted; <i>and</i> the guilt <i>or</i> innocence as regards a wound owing to him.</p>  
 <p>25. About the mode of making a <i>sacred thread-</i>girdle <a name="fr_309"></a><a href="#fn_307"><font size="1">1</font></a>, <i>and</i> the harm from an unusual formation of it. 26. About lawfully tying <i>it</i>, without the culpability (va<i>z</i>agîh) <i>of</i> unauthorised action; <i>also</i> when they do not tie <i>it</i> lawfully, <i>but</i> the girdling is knotted (viragî-aîtŏ) <i>and</i> twisted owing to culpability (va<i>z</i>agânîh); and whatever is on the same subject.</p>  
 <p>27. About lawfully scratching <i>with</i> the nails, and the harm from unlawfully scratching. 28. About lawfully attending to a fire on the road; and, when <i>one</i> arrives at a ford <i>through</i> water, the sin which arises, as to fire, from not lawfully caring about the fire.</p>  
 <p>29. About warriors who mingle together in panic (mazangîh) and darkness; injury happens <i>to</i> one</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_126"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 126</font></a></p>  
 <p>from the other, and the statement of the account published is <i>that there was</i> a state of terror; also whatever is on the same subject. 30. About the march of an army which is in fear, <i>and</i> that which is in <i>a state of</i> fearlessness which is the distinction of the army of Irân from those of foreigners. 31. About lawfully and habitually requiring a share, and the harm from unlawfully and unhabitually requiring <i>it</i>.</p>  
 <p>32. About carrying firewood, brought away from the hills, into the house; depositing <i>it</i> at first by the tongs (dast-pânakŏ); watching, turning, and inspecting <i>it, and</i> carrying <i>it</i> away to the fire; that is, how <i>to do it</i> lawfully, the sin owing to unlawfully performing <i>it, and</i> whatever is on the same subject. 33. About lawfully warming bull's urine <a name="fr_310"></a><a href="#fn_308"><font size="1">1</font></a> by the fire, <i>and</i> the sin when <i>it is</i> not lawfully <i>done</i>.</p>  
 <p>34. About selecting a pasture, one ranked above the others; that is, how <i>to do it</i> lawfully, the sin when <i>one</i> shall do <i>it</i> otherwise, and, owing to that, he is really injured, <i>or</i> occasions injury. 35. About what is the mode <i>of</i> construction of a lawfully-formed farm-house (da<i>s</i>t-ka<i>d</i>akŏ), the dwelling of the people, <i>and</i> the place of the beasts <i>of burden and</i> cattle; <i>also</i> the sin when <i>one</i> shall construct <i>it</i> otherwise, <i>and</i>, owing to that, he is really injured, <i>or</i> occasions injury.</p>  
 <p>36. A decision about <i>a case</i> when one <i>person has</i> lawfully to force <i>away</i> a beast <i>of burden</i> from a control unlawfully <i>exercised, and another</i> person intrudes unauthorisedly, <i>and</i> vexes the district authorities (pa<i>d</i>-dihânân). 37. Also when <i>being</i> done unlawfully,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_127"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 127</font></a></p>  
 <p>and the beast <i>being away</i> from its control unlawfully <i>exercised</i>, the <i>other</i> person intrudes lawfully; and when both <i>persons act</i> unlawfully, <i>or</i> when both <i>act</i> lawfully. 38. About lawfully tying, whereby things are hung up; <i>and</i> the sin when, <i>through</i> an unlawfully-tied fastening, anything is injured, <i>or</i> occasions injury. 39. About unlawfully keeping horses in a stable (âkhûr), <i>and</i> the sin owing to the unlawfulness. 40. And, as regards the cutting of trees and shrubs, where <i>and</i> how <i>it is</i> lawfully <i>done</i>, and the harm <i>and</i> sin owing to not lawfully cutting. 41. About the mode of washing clothing, and the sin owing to different modes. 42. About the mode of walking in, <i>and</i> the sin owing to unusual walking in. 43. About the custom of a man of the sagacious (dânâkvarân) on passing through water, and the harm <i>and</i> sin owing to acting otherwise.</p>  
 <p>44. About the kinds of canals (nâî) <a name="fr_311"></a><a href="#fn_309"><font size="1">1</font></a> and fords, from those for two men passing, up to those for many; the dimensions of those <i>which are</i> large, and how much <i>they are</i> each separately <i>sunk</i> into the ground, without collecting water, when the ground is hard, and how much when <i>it is</i> soft. 45. The extent of their outer <a name="fr_312"></a><a href="#fn_310"><font size="1">2</font></a> banks, <i>and</i> the inspection as to the banks when the water is brackish, warm, <i>and</i> flowing; how far when outside of the water, and how far when in the water. 46. When <i>it is</i> brackish, cold, and flowing; or brackish, warm, <i>and</i> stagnant; or sweet, warm, <i>and</i> flowing; how far when in the water, and how far when outside. 47. And, when</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_128"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 128</font></a></p>  
 <p>brackish, cold, <i>and</i> stagnant; or sweet, cold, <i>and</i> flowing; or sweet, warm, and stagnant; how far when in the water, and how far when outside of the water. 48. What is the customary operation as regards the inspection of the banks; how is the stagnation (astînî<i>d</i>anŏ) within a pool dammed up (zarêh-stânŏ-aê), and the stone<i>-work</i> inside, from the canal which is for ten men passing, up to that for many; <i>and</i> how is the damming up inside of the canal, the stagnation within the pool dammed up, or the reedy jungle (vê<i>s</i>akŏ) when distributed and it becomes tall.</p>  
 <p>49. What are the mode <i>and</i> means of maintaining the supervision of a canal; which is that <i>which one</i> should maintain over the water of the canal when half is distributed, <i>or</i>, when not, one-third; and which is that when one-third is distributed, <i>or</i>, when not, one-fourth; <i>a supervision</i> which is animate <i>or</i> inanimate, <i>and</i> after those which are inanimate <i>means are provided</i> <a name="fr_313"></a><a href="#fn_311"><font size="1">1</font></a>, the former animate <i>ones</i> are then at rest; <i>and</i> the harm <i>and</i> sin when they shall act otherwise. 50. And, as regards the same, what is the mode <i>of</i> passage of animals of various species, by swimming across the water; <i>and</i> the sin, owing to acting otherwise, when harm occurs. 51. About the trampling down at a ford <i>through</i> water, when <i>one</i> is newly completing <i>it</i>, and when <i>the water is</i> brackish <i>and</i> flowing, when <i>it is</i> brackish <i>and</i> stagnant, when <i>it is</i> sweet <i>and</i> flowing, and when <i>it is</i> sweet <i>and</i> stagnant; the reason of passing through on it, <i>and</i> such <i>and</i> such <i>ways</i> for proceeding at</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_129"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 129</font></a></p>  
 <p>will thereon; so, also, observation as to the water which <i>has</i> remained behind for flowing, and the harm <i>and</i> sin when <i>one</i> does not properly observe <i>it, but</i> walks on.</p>  
 <p>52. About two of the warriors who meet together on the road, which <i>of them</i> was <i>busy</i> about the protection <i>of his</i> horse, <i>and</i> which about the preparation of food; <i>also</i> the usage and other <i>things</i> in similar matters. 53. The sin of <i>having</i> eaten food for refreshment on the road, that is, how the custom is a sin when they can act otherwise.</p>  
 <p>54. About the remedies for sheep and beasts <i>of burden</i> which reinfuse fresh life; and the extent of keeping the sheep, goat, cow, mare, ass, pig <a name="fr_314"></a><a href="#fn_312"><font size="1">1</font></a>, and woman with the male. 55. About beasts <i>of burden</i>, sheep (anûmâânŏ), and women, for whom, on account of contraction of orifice, <i>there is</i> a use of means for making <i>it</i> not painful (atûtakŏ). 56. About the extent of the distance of a male beast from the female when it is necessary to be watchful. 57. About the distance that a man <i>has</i> to remove an ox that <i>has</i> destroyed <i>some</i> concealed hay (barkasag giyah) which is the hay of others, when they quarrel with him; how it is when <i>it is</i> allowable to bring <i>the</i> ox back to <i>his</i> home; <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject.</p>  
 <p>58. About the security of a man from the death (aôsh) of <i>his</i> fathers, and danger <i>having</i> arisen for him from a mouth <i>of</i> bad omen. 59. About the sin of a father owing to a child, when, <i>being</i> given by him to an ill-behaved <i>person</i> <a name="fr_315"></a><a href="#fn_313"><font size="1">2</font></a>, he calls <i>it and</i>, when</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_130"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 130</font></a></p>  
 <p>it comes, <i>there</i> may occur the sin of unlawfully terrifying sheep, and the beast <i>of burden</i> is beaten; <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject. 60. About bringing <a name="fr_316"></a><a href="#fn_314"><font size="1">1</font></a> a plant which is a medicinal herb, <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject.</p>  
 <p>61. About a sociable feast (ham-myâzdîh) with idolators, that is, how it is <i>when held</i> authorisedly, and how <i>it is</i> when it is not; <i>and</i>, when <i>one</i> gives the sociable feast, how it is <i>when they are to be considered</i> unhonoured, and how it is when they are to be considered more honoured even than the Irânians. 62. And about the broken victuals which the idolators <i>have</i> eaten <i>and</i> drunk therein.</p>  
 <p>63. About the proportion of meat with the bread in atonement for deprival of food <a name="fr_317"></a><a href="#fn_315"><font size="1">2</font></a>. 64. About an ordeal which is severe, and <i>one which is</i> not severe; <i>and</i> the evidence of acquittal from the achievement thereof. 65. About the secrets of the religion, and the sin owing to <i>their being</i> disclosed (gushûftŏ). 66. About the sin of speaking evil words to the wives of others. 67. About the extent of the most inferior house, village, community, <i>and</i> province; <i>and</i> that of the most superior. 68. And about what <i>was</i> the mode <i>of</i> residence of Frashô<i>s</i>tar and <i>G</i>âmâsp <a name="fr_318"></a><a href="#fn_316"><font size="1">3</font></a> in a plundering (lâî<i>s</i>kar) army, and their habits.</p>
