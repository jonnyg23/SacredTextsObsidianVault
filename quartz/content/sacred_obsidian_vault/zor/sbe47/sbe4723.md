 <p>1. About the brothers who <i>were</i> a band of opponents of Zaratû<i>s</i>t. 2. The Karaps and Aûsikhshes <a name="fr_439"></a><a href="#fn_435"><font size="1">3</font></a> <i>were</i> brothers’ sons, and have become the devastation (gâstâragîh) of the Irânians; the devastators of the Irânians (Êrânân) <i>were</i> from Kôkharê<i>d</i> <a name="fr_440"></a><a href="#fn_436"><font size="1">4</font></a>, and Kôkharê<i>d</i> <i>was</i> born from Aêshm and Mânû<i>s</i>ak, the sister of Mânû<i>s</i><i>k</i>îhar <a name="fr_441"></a><a href="#fn_437"><font size="1">5</font></a>. 3. At the place where Zaratû<i>s</i>t <i>was</i> born, five brothers have been, whose names were Brâ<i>d</i>-rûkhsh, Brâ<i>d</i>-rôyi<i>s</i>n, Brâ<i>d</i>-rêsh the Tûr, Hazân, and Vadast <a name="fr_442"></a><a href="#fn_438"><font size="1">6</font></a>. 4. Their</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_144"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 144</font></a></p>  
 <p>brotherhood of five—of which the middle <i>one was</i> Brâ<i>d</i>-rêsh the Tûr, who became more of an adversary of Zaratû<i>s</i>t—<i>was</i> a semblance of the brother-hood of five who <i>were</i> sons of Pôrûshaspô, of whom the middle <i>one</i> was Zaratû<i>s</i>t.</p>  
 <p>5. Of the four brothers <i>of</i> Zaratû<i>s</i>t, the names of the two before Zaratû<i>s</i>t <i>were</i> Ratû<i>s</i>tar and Rangû<i>s</i>tar, and of the two after <i>him</i> No<i>d</i>arîgâ and Nîvê<i>d</i>i<i>s</i> <a name="fr_443"></a><a href="#fn_439"><font size="1">1</font></a>. 6. The middle position of Zaratû<i>s</i>t is for the reason that he is so produced at that time, as an inter-medium of the early narrators and the later narrators, that three millenniums came before him and three after. 7. So that he <i>has</i> prescribed to the creatures in what manner he would teach the ancients as to what <i>had</i> occurred, and <i>in</i> what manner <i>it</i> is also to be done as regards what <i>will</i> occur; as is stated in the Gâthas <a name="fr_444"></a><a href="#fn_440"><font size="1">2</font></a> thus: 'Both those I ask of thee, <i>O</i> Aûha<i>r</i>ma<i>z</i><i>d</i>! even whatever <i>has</i> happened till now, <i>and</i> whatever shall happen henceforth?'</p>

 <p><a name="fn_438"></a><a href="sbe4723.htm#fr_442">143:6</a> As these names do not occur in the Avesta, their pronunciation <a name="page_144"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 144</font></a> has to be guessed so as to give a probable meaning to the Pahlavi spelling. The writing of the last name is complicated; it is written <a name="img_14400"></a>
 <img src="img/14400.jpg"> and <a name="img_14401"></a>
 <img src="img/14401.jpg"> in the two MS. authorities, which forms can be most obviously read Vasam and Saman, but Saman can also be read as the Zvâri<i>s</i> yad<i>â</i> = dast, 'a hand;' this leads to the conclusion that the original name underlies the form Vadast, and this is confirmed by the name of the Karap Vaêdvôi<i>s</i>t written at full length in Dk. VII, iv, 21-24. See ibid. ii, 9 n.</p>
