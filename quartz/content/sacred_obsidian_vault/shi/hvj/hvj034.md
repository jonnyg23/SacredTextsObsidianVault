 <img src="img/03300.jpg"></p>  <h3 align="center">33</h3>  <h3 align="center">TOMONORI KINO</h3>  <h3 align="center">KINO TOMONORI</h3>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">  Hisakata no<br> Hikari nodokeki<br>   Haru no hi ni<br> Shizu kokoro, naku<br> Hana no chiruramu.  
 <p>THE spring has come, and once again<br>   The sun shines in the sky;<br> So gently smile the heavens, that<br>   It almost makes me cry,<br>   When blossoms droop and die.</p></div>  
 <p><small>Tomonori Kino was the grandson of Uchisukune Take, a famous warrior, and nephew of Tsura-yuki, who composed verse No. <a href="hvj036.htm#page_35">35</a>; he was one of the compilers of the <i>Kokinshiu</i>, and died at the beginning of the tenth century. He refers in this verse to the fall of the cherry blossoms.</small></p>  
 <p><i>Hisakata</i> is a 'pillow-word' for heaven, without any definite meaning in the present day; it is generally used in poetry in conjunction with such words as sun, moon, sky, or, as in this case, 'the light' (of heaven).</p>  
 <p>The picture shows the poet with his attendant, watching the petals falling from the cherry tree.</p>
