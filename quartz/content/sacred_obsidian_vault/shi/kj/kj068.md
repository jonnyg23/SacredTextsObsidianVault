 <p><a name="page_199"><font size="1" color="green">p. 199</font></a> [162]</p>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. XLI.—EMPEROR KŌ-GEN.]</h2>  
 <p>His Augustness Oho-yamato-ne-ko-hiko -kuni-kuru dwelt in the palace of Sakaki-bara at Karu, <a name="fr_1144"></a><a href="#fn_1140"><font size="1">1</font></a> and ruled the Empire. This heavenly Sovereign wedded Her Augustness Utsu-shiko-me, <a name="fr_1145"></a><a href="#fn_1141"><font size="1">2</font></a> younger sister of His Augustness Utsu-shiko-wo, <a name="fr_1146"></a><a href="#fn_1142"><font size="1">3</font></a> ancestor of the Grandees of Hodzumi, <a name="fr_1147"></a><a href="#fn_1143"><font size="1">4</font></a> and begot august children: His Augustness Oho-biko, <a name="fr_1148"></a><a href="#fn_1144"><font size="1">5</font></a> next His Augustness Sukuna-biko-take-wi-goro; <a name="fr_1149"></a><a href="#fn_1145"><font size="1">6</font></a> next His Augustness Waka-yamato-ne-ko-hiko-oho-bibi <a name="fr_1150"></a><a href="#fn_1146"><font size="1">7</font></a> (<small>three Deities</small>).</p>

 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Again, wedding Her Augustness I-gaka-shiko-me, <a name="fr_1151"></a><a href="#fn_1147"><font size="1">8</font></a> daughter of His Augustness Utsu-shiko-wo, he begot an august child: His Augustness Hiko-futu-oshi-no-makoto. <a name="fr_1152"></a><a href="#fn_1148"><font size="1">9</font></a> Again, wedding Princess Haniyasu, <a name="fr_1153"></a><a href="#fn_1149"><font size="1">10</font></a> daughter of Awotama <a name="fr_1154"></a><a href="#fn_1150"><font size="1">11</font></a> of Kafuchi, he begot an august child: His Augustness Take-hani-yasu-biko <a name="fr_1155"></a><a href="#fn_1151"><font size="1">12</font></a> (<small>one Deity</small>). The august children of this Heavenly Sovereign [numbered in all five Deities]. So His Augustness Waka-yamato-ne-ko-hiko-oho-bibi [was he who afterwards] ruled the Empire. The children of [163] his elder brother, His Augustness Oho-biko, were His Augustness Take-numa-kaha-wake <a name="fr_1156"></a><a href="#fn_1152"><font size="1">13</font></a> (<small>ancestor of the size="1">15</font></a> (<small>This was the ancestor of the Butler Grandees.</small>) <a name="fr_1159"></a><a href="#fn_1155"><font size="1">16</font></a> His Augustness Hiko-futu-oshi-no-mikoto wedded Princess Takachina of Kadzuraki, <a name="fr_1160"></a><a href="#fn_1156"><font size="1">17</font></a> young sister of Cho-nabi, <a name="fr_1161"></a><a href="#fn_1157"><font size="1">18</font></a> ancestor of the Chiefs of Wohari, <a name="fr_1162"></a><a href="#fn_1158"><font size="1">19</font></a> and begot a child: the Noble Umashi Uchi. <a name="fr_1163"></a><a href="#fn_1159"><font size="1">20</font></a> (<small>This was the ancestor of the Grandees of Uchi in Yamashiro.</small>) <a name="fr_1164"></a><a href="#fn_1160"><font size="1">21</font></a> Again, wedding Princess Yama-shita-kage, <a name="fr_1165"></a><a href="#fn_1161"><font size="1">22</font></a> younger sister of Udzu-hiko, <a name="fr_1166"></a><a href="#fn_1162"><font size="1">23</font></a> ancestor of the Rulers of the Lord of Ki, <a name="fr_1167"></a><a href="#fn_1163"><font size="1">24</font></a> he begot a child, the Noble Take-Uchi <a name="fr_1168"></a><a href="#fn_1164"><font size="1">25</font></a> The children of this Noble Take-Uchi [numbered] in all nine (<small>seven males and two females</small>),— namely] the Noble of Hata-no-Yashiro, <a name="fr_1169"></a><a href="#fn_1165"><font size="1">26</font></a> [who] (<small>was the ancestor of the Grandees of Hata, <a name="fr_1170"></a><a href="#fn_1166"><font size="1">27</font></a> of the Grandees of Hayashi, <a name="fr_1171"></a><a href="#fn_1167"><font size="1">28</font></a> of the Grandees of Hami, <a name="fr_1172"></a><a href="#fn_1168"><font size="1">29</font></a> of the Grandees of Hoshikaha, <a name="fr_1173"></a><a href="#fn_1169"><font size="1">30</font></a> of the Grandees of Afumi, <a name="fr_1174"></a><a href="#fn_1170"><font size="1">31</font></a> and of the Dukes of the Hatsuse Tribe</small>); <a name="fr_1175"></a><a href="#fn_1171"><font size="1">32</font></a> next the Noble Kose-no-Wo-Kara <a name="fr_1176"></a><a href="#fn_1172"><font size="1">33</font></a> [who] (<small>was the ancestor of the Grandees of Kose, <a name="fr_1177"></a><a href="#fn_1173"><font size="1">34</font></a> of the Grandees of the Sazaki Tribe, <a name="fr_1178"></a><a href="#fn_1174"><font size="1">35</font></a> and of the Grandees of the Karu Tribe</small>); <a name="fr_1179"></a><a href="#fn_1175"><font size="1">36</font></a> next the Noble Soga no Ishikaha <a name="fr_1180"></a><a href="#fn_1176"><font size="1">37</font></a> [who] (<small>was the ancestor of the Grandees of Soga, <a name="fr_1181"></a><a href="#fn_1177"><font size="1">40</font></a> of the Grandees of Takamuko, <a name="fr_1184"></a><a href="#fn_1180"><font size="1">41</font></a> of the Grandees of Woharida <a name="fr_1185"></a><a href="#fn_1181"><font size="1">42</font></a> of the Grandees of Sakurawi, <a name="fr_1186"></a><a href="#fn_1182"><font size="1">43</font></a> and of the Grandees of Kishida</small>); <a name="fr_1187"></a><a  <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_1140"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1144">199:1</a> <a name="page_201"><font size="1" color="green">p. 201</font></a> In Yamato. For Karu see Sect. LVII, Note I. <i>Sakahi-bara</i> signifies "boundary moor."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1141"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1145">199:2</a> <i>I.e.</i>, perhaps, "the beautiful but alarming female."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1142"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1146">199:3</a> <i>I.e.</i>, perhaps, "the beautiful but alarming male."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1143"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1147">199:4</a> <i>Hodzumi no omi</i>. There are several places called Hodzumi in various provinces. The name appears to signify "piling up rice-ears."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1144"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1148">199:5</a> <i>I.e.</i>, "great prince."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1145"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1149">199:6</a> <i>I.e.</i>, "little prince-fierce-boar-heart," the boar being known for its savage disposition.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1146"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1150">199:7</a> Excluding the last member of the compound, this name signifies "young-Yamato-lord-prince-great" <i>Bibi</i> is identified by Motowori with the word <i>mimi</i>, which so often recurs in proper names (see Sect. XIII, Note 18).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1147"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1151">200:8</a> Motowori explains this name in the sense of "brilliant-alarming-female," but some doubt must attach to it.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1148"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1152">200:9</a> <i>I.e.</i>, perhaps, "prince vast-great-truth."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1149"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1153">200:10</a> <i>Hani-yasu-bime</i>. This name has already been met with in Sect. VII, Note 3. Motowori however supposes that in this place Haniyasu should be considered to be the name of a place in Yamato.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1150"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1154">200:11</a> <i>I.e.</i>, a man called "green jewel" who lived in the province of Kafuchi.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1151"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1155">200:12</a> <i>Take</i> signifies "brave." For the rest of the name see Note 10.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1152"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1156">200:13</a> <i>Take</i> signifies "brave." <i>Wake</i> is either "young" or "lord." For <i>Nuna-kaha</i> see Sect. LI, Note 31.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1153"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1157">200:14</a> <a name="page_202"><font size="1" color="green">p. 202</font></a> <i>Abe no omi</i>. There are several places called Abe, and it is doubtful to which of them the text here refers.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1154"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1158">200:15</a> The signification of <i>inakoshi</i> seems to be "rice-chariot". <i>Hiko</i> is "prince," and <i>wake</i> either "young "or "lord."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1155"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1159">200:16</a> <i>Kashihade no omi</i>, This name is traditionally referred to an incident in the reign of the Emperor Kei-ko, who is said to have bestowed it on one of his attendants who served up to him a particularly savoury dish of shell-fish. "Butlers" (perhaps the word might also be rendered "cooks") have been mentioned towards the end of Sect. XXXII, and again in the legend of Jim-mu's slaughter of the "earth-spiders "related in Sect. XLVIII.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1156"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1160">200:17</a> <i>Katsuraki-no-takachina-bime-no-mikoto</i>. The signification of <i>Takachina</i> is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1157"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1161">200:18</a> By aphaeresis for <i>Oho-inabi</i>, the form of the name given in the "Chronicles of Old Matters of Former Ages." itself perhaps standing by apocope for <i>Oho-ina-biko</i>, which would signify "great rice-prince."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1158"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1162">200:19</a> <i>Wohari no Murazhi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1159"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1163">200:20</a> <i>Umashi Uchi no sukune</i>. Umashi signifies "sweet," and <i>Uchi</i> is the name of a place in Yamashiro.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1160"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1164">200:21</a> <i>Yamashiro no Uchi no omi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1161"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1165">200:22</a> This name may be rendered literally "the shade beneath the mountains," but the signification is "the glow from the autumn leaves on the mountain-side."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1162"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1166">200:23</a> <i>I.e.</i>, probably "wonderful (or precious) prince."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1163"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1167">200:24</a> <i>Ki no kuni no miyatsuko no aya</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1164"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1168">200:25</a> <i>Take-Uchi no sukune</i>. <i>Take</i> signifies "brave," and <i>Uchi</i> is the name of a district in Yamato. The common, but erroneous, reading of this name is <i>Take no Uchi no sukune</i>. The celebrated personage, who may be styled the Methuselah of Japan, is said to have lived during the reigns of five Emperors, who themselves averaged over a hundred years of life each. His own age is variously given as 255, 260, etc. up to 360 years.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1165"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1169">200:26</a> <i>Hata no Yashiro no sukune</i>. <i>Hata</i> and <i>Yashiro</i> are supposed by Motowori to be the names of places in Yamato. <i>Yashiro</i> signifies "shrine." <i>Hata</i> is of uncertain derivation.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1166"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1170">200:27</a> <i>Hata no omi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1167"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1171">200:28</a> <i>Hayashi no omi</i>. Hayashi is the name of a place in Kawachi, and signifies "forest."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1168"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1172">200:29</a> <i>Hami no omi</i>. There is a Hami in Afumi and another in Tamba. The signification of the name is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1169"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1173">200:30</a> <a name="page_203"><font size="1" color="green">p. 203</font></a> <i>Hoshikaha no omi</i>. Hoshikaha is a place in Yamato. The name signifies "star river."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1170"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1174">200:31</a> <i>Afumi no omi</i>. For Afumi see Sect. XXIX, Note 20.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1171"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1175">200:32</a> <i>Hatsuse-be no Kimi</i>. For Hatsuse see Sect. CXLIII, Note 8.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1172"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1176">200:33</a> <i>Kose no Wo-kara no sukune</i>. Kose is the name of a place in Yamato. The meaning of Wo-kara is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1173"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1177">200:34</a> <i>Kose no omi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1174"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1178">200:35</a> <i>Sazakibe no omi</i>. See Sect. LIII, Note 10.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1175"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1179">200:36</a> <i>Kurube no omi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1176"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1180">200:37</a> <i>Soga no Ishikaha no sukune</i>. Soga is a place in Yamato, and Ishikaha a district in Kahachi. In cases like this it must generally be presumed that the family had two seats, or was divided into two branches residing in different places. Sometimes, however, the original seat and that to which the family afterwards removed are meant to be indicated.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1177"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1181">200:38</a> <i>Soga no omi</i>. The signification of Soga is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1178"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1182">200:39</a> <i>Kahanobe no omi</i>. Kahanobe is the name of a district in Settsu, and signifies "river-bank."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1179"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1183">200:40</a> <i>Tanaka no omi</i>. Tanaka is the name of a place in Yamato and signifies "among the rice-fields."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1180"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1184">200:41</a> <i>Takamuko no omi</i>. Takamuko seems to be the name of a place in Echizen. Its signification is uncertain.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1181"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1185">200:42</a> <i>Woharida no omi</i>. Woharida is a place in Yamato. The name seems to mean "little ploughed field."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1182"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1186">200:43</a> <i>Sakurawi no omi</i>. Sakurawi is the name of a place in Kahachi and signifies "cherry-tree well."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1183"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1187">200:44</a> <i>Kishida no omi</i>. Kishida is a place in Yamato. The signification of the name is not clear.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1184"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1188">200:45</a> <i>Heguri no Tsuku no sukune</i>. Heguri is the name of a district in Yamato, and is of uncertain signification. <i>Tsuku</i> (modern <i>dzuku</i>), "owl," is a name which is referred to a tradition that will be found in Motowori's Commentary, Vol. XXII, p. 29.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1185"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1189">201:46</a> <i>Heguri no omi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1186"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1190">201:47</a> <i>Sawara no omi</i>. Sawara was perhaps a district in Chikuzen. The signification of the name is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1187"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1191">201:48</a> <i>Uma mi-kuhi no murazhi</i>. The literal significance of the characters with which <i>Uma-mi-kuhi</i> is written is "horse august post." But whether this name had any relation to horses, or whether it should simply be regarded as the name of a place is quite uncertain.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1188"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1192">201:49</a> <i>Ki no Tsunu no sukune</i>. Ki is the name of a province, and <a name="page_204"><font size="1" color="green">p. 204</font></a> Tsunu that of a district in another province,—the province of Suhau (Suwō). <i>Conf</i>. Note 37.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1189"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1193">201:50</a> <i>Ki no omi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1190"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1194">201:51</a> <i>Tsunu no omi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1191"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1195">201:52</a> <i>Sakamoto no omi</i>. Sakamoto is the name of a place in Idzumi and signifies "base of the hill."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1192"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1196">201:53</a> <i>Kume no Ma-ito-hime</i>. Kume may be, as Motowori says, the name of a place. But see Sect. XXXIV, Note 7. In any case the place, if it existed, was probably called after some person of the name of Kume. The signification of Ma-iro is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1193"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1197">201:54</a> <i>Nu-no-iro-hime</i>. The meaning of this name is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1194"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1198">201:55</a> Kadzuraki is the already frequently mentioned name of a district in Yamato, and Nagaye is likewise the name of a place,—whether in Yamato or in Kahachi is not certain. It signifies "long inlet." Motowori thinks that the syllable <i>so</i> in this place is the same as the of <i>kuma-so</i>, and signifies "valiant "or "fierce."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1195"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1199">201:56</a> <i>Tamade no omi</i>. There is a Tamade in Yamato and another in Kahachi. The signification of the name is uncertain.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1196"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1200">201:57</a> <i>Ikuha no omi</i>. The "Chronicles of Japan" tell us that the original form of this name <i>Ikuha</i> was <i>uki-ha</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, "floating leaf," and give a story to account for it. See Motowori's Commentary, Vol. XXII, pp. 36-37, where the reason traditionally given to explain the fact of the name Ikuha being written with the character <a name="img_20400"></a>
 <img src="img/20400.jpg"> is also mentioned at length.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1197"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1201">201:58</a> <i>Ikuye no omi</i>. Ikuye must have been the name of a place; but nothing is known of it.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1198"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1202">201:59</a> Agina no anti. The same observation applies to this as to the preceding name.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1199"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1203">201:60</a> <i>Waku-go no sukune</i>. <i>Waku-go</i> signifies "young child "or youth," an Honorific designation.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1200"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1204">201:61</a> <i>Yenuma no omi</i>. Yenuma is the name of a district in Kaga, and signifies "inlet-lagoon."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1201"></a><a href="kj068.htm#fr_1205">201:62</a> In Yamato. This pool or lake is often mentioned in the poems of the "Collection of a Myriad Leaves" and was celebrated for its lotus-flowers. We hear of it in the "Chronicles of Japan," as having been dug in the reign of the Emperor Ō-jin, but it was probably, like many others, a natural pool or marsh, which was afterwards improved. The name signifies "sabre."</p>
