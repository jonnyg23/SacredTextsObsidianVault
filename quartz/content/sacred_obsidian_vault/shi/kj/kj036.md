 <p><a name="page_107"><font size="1" color="green">p. 107</font></a></p>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. XXIX.—THE AUGUST CHILDREN OF THE GREAT-HARVEST-DEITY AND OF THE SWIFT-MOUNTAIN-DEITY.]</h2>  
 <p>So the Great-Harvest-Deity wedded the Princess [of?] Inu, <a name="fr_589"></a><a href="#fn_585"><font size="1">1</font></a> daughter of the Divine-Life-Producing-Wondrous-Deity, <a name="fr_590"></a><a href="#fn_586"><font size="1">2</font></a> and begot children: the Deity August-Spirit-of-the-Great-Land; <a name="fr_591"></a><a href="#fn_587"><font size="1">3</font></a> [89] next the Deity of Kara; <a name="fr_592"></a><a href="#fn_588"><font size="1">4</font></a> next the Deity Sohori; <a name="fr_593"></a><a href="#fn_589"><font size="1">5</font></a> next the Deity White-Sun; <a name="fr_594"></a><a href="#fn_590"><font size="1">6</font></a> next the Sage-Deity. <a name="fr_595"></a><a href="#fn_591"><font size="1">7</font></a> (Five Deities <a name="fr_596"></a><a href="#fn_592"><font size="1">8</font></a>). Again he wedded the Refulgent-Princess, <a name="fr_597"></a><a href="#fn_593"><font size="1">9</font></a> and begot children: the Deity Great-Refulgent-Mountain-Dwelling-Grandee, <a name="fr_598"></a><a href="#fn_594"><font size="1">10</font></a> next the August-Harvest-Deity. <a name="fr_599"></a><a href="#fn_595"><font size="1">11</font></a> Again he wedded Princess Ame-shiru-karu-midzu, <a name="fr_600"></a><a href="#fn_596"><font size="1">12</font></a> and begot children: the Deity Oki-tsu-hiko, <a name="fr_601"></a><a href="#fn_597"><font size="1">13</font></a> next Her Augustness Oki-tsu-hime, <a name="fr_602"></a><a href="#fn_598"><font size="1">14</font></a> another name for whom is [90] the Deity Great Furnace-Princess <a name="fr_603"></a><a href="#fn_599"><font size="1">15</font></a>—this is the Deity of the Furnace <a name="fr_604"></a><a href="#fn_600"><font size="1">16</font></a> held in reverence by all people—next the Deity Great-Mountain-Integrator, <a name="fr_605"></a><a href="#fn_601"><font size="1">17</font></a> another name for whom is the Deity-Great-Master-of-the-Mountain-End: <a name="fr_606"></a><a href="#fn_602"><font size="1">18</font></a> this Deity dwells on Mount Hiye <a name="fr_607"></a><a href="#fn_603"><font size="1">19</font></a> in the land of Chika-tsu-Afumi, <a name="fr_608"></a><a href="#fn_604"><font size="1">20</font></a> and is likewise the Deity dwelling at Matsu-no-wo <a name="fr_609"></a><a href="#fn_605"><font size="1">21</font></a> in Kadzunu, <a name="fr_610"></a><a href="#fn_606"><font size="1">22</font></a> who uses the whizzing barb. <a name="fr_611"></a><a href="#fn_607"><font size="1">23</font></a> Next the Deity-of-the-Fire-in-the-Yard; <a name="fr_612"></a><a href="#fn_608"><font size="1">24</font></a> next the Deity Asahi; <a name="fr_613"></a><a href="#fn_609"><font size="1">25</font></a> next the Deity Hahigi; <a name="fr_614"></a><a href="#fn_610"><font size="1">26</font></a> next the Deity Refulgent-Mountain-Dwelling-Grandee; <a name="fr_615"></a><a href="#fn_611"><font size="1">27</font></a> next the Deity [91] Swift-Mountain-Dwelling; <a name="fr_616"></a><a href="#fn_612"><font size="1">28</font></a> next the High Deity-of-the-Fire-in-the-Yard; <a name="fr_617"></a><a href="#fn_613"><font size="1">29</font></a> next the Great-Earth-Deity, <a name="fr_618"></a><a href="#fn_614"><font size="1">30</font></a> another name for whom is the Deity August-Ancestor-of-Earth. <a name="fr_619"></a><a href="#fn_615"><font size="1">31</font></a> (Nine Deities <a name="fr_620"></a><a href="#fn_616"><font size="1">32</font></a>)</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">In the above paragraph the children of the Great-Harvest-Deity, from the Deity August-Spirit-of-the Great-Land down to the Great-Earth-Deity, are altogether sixteen Deities.</div>

 <p>The Deity Swift-Mountain-Dwelling <a name="fr_621"></a><a href="#fn_617"><font size="1">33</font></a> wedded the Deity Princess-of-Great-Food, <a name="fr_622"></a><a href="#fn_618"><font size="1">34</font></a> and begot children: the Deity Young-Mountain-Integrator; <a name="fr_623"></a><a href="#fn_619"><font size="1">35</font></a> next the Young-Harvest-Deity; <a name="fr_624"></a><a href="#fn_620"><font size="1">36</font></a> next his younger sister the Young-Rice-Transplanting-Female-Deity; <a name="fr_625"></a><a href="#fn_621"><font size="1">37</font></a> next the Water-Sprinkling-Deity; <a name="fr_626"></a><a href="#fn_622"><font size="1">38</font></a> next the Deity-of-the-High-Sun-of-Summer, <a name="fr_627"></a><a href="#fn_623"><font size="1">39</font></a> another name for whom is the Female-Deity-of-Summer; <a name="fr_628"></a><a href="#fn_624"><font size="1">40</font></a> next the Autumn-Princess; <a name="fr_629"></a><a href="#fn_625"><font size="1">41</font></a> next the Deity Stem-Harvest; <a name="fr_630"></a><a href="#fn_626"><font size="1">42</font></a> next the Deity Lord-Stem-Tree-Young-House-Rope. <a name="fr_631"></a><a href="#fn_627"><font size="1">43</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">In the above paragraph the children of the Deity Swift-Mountain-Dwelling, from the Deity Young-Mountain-Integrator down to the Deity Lord-Young-House-Rope, <a name="fr_632"></a><a href="#fn_628"><font size="1">44</font></a> are altogether eight Deities.</div>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_585"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_589">107:1</a> <a name="page_108"><font size="1" color="green">p. 108</font></a> <i>Inu-hime</i>. Motowori supposes <i>Inu</i> to be the name of a place. The word properly signifies "dog."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_586"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_590">107:2</a> <i>Kamu-iku-musu-bi-no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_587"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_591">107:3</a> <i>Oho-kuni-mi-tama-no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_588"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_592">107:4</a> <i>Kara-no-kami</i>, <a name="img_10800"></a>
 <img src="img/10800.jpg">. <i>Kara</i> signifies Korea and China, and the Deity thus named appears in the "Chronicle" under the name of <i>I-so-takeru</i> ("Fifty-fold-Valiant"), of whom it is related that he was taken over to Korea by his father <i>Susa-no-wo</i> (the "Impetuous-Male").</p>
 <p><a name="fn_589"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_593">107:5</a> <i>Sohori-no-kami</i>. The etymology is not clear. Hirata derives the name from a Verb <i>soru</i>, "to ride," "to go in a boat," in connection with the story (mentioned in the preceding note) of I-so-takeru having been taken over to Korea. According to this view, <i>Sohori</i>, like <i>Kara-no-kami</i>, would be an alternative name of <i>I-so-takeru</i>. But the derivation is hazardous, to say the least.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_590"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_594">107:6</a> <i>Shira-hi-no-kami</i>. Motowori supposes <i>shira hi</i> (<a name="img_10801"></a>
 <img src="img/10801.jpg">) to be a copyist's error for <i>makahi</i> (<a name="img_10802"></a>
 <img src="img/10802.jpg">). The latter, however, does not make satisfactory sense, and Tomonobu, proposes to invert the characters, thus: <a name="img_10803"></a>
 <img src="img/10803.jpg">, which means "sun-confronting." Motowori suggests that the word <a href="errata.htm#15">may</a>, after all, be but the name of a place.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_591"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_595">107:7</a> <i>Hizhiri-no-kami</i>, written with the characters <a name="img_10804"></a>
 <img src="img/10804.jpg">. The first of <a href="errata.htm#16">these</a> is defined as signifying him who is intuitively wise and good, <i>i.e.</i> <a name="page_109"><font size="1" color="green">p. 109</font></a> the perfect sage. But perhaps we should in Archaic Japanese take the term <i>hizhiri</i> in what is its probable native etymological sense, viz. "sun-governing" (<i>hizhiri</i>, <a name="img_10900"></a>
 <img src="img/10900.jpg">), a title properly applied to the Japanese Emperors as descendants of the Sun-God, and of which the character <a name="img_10901"></a>
 <img src="img/10901.jpg">, which is used of the Chinese Monarchs, is only an equivalent in so far as it, too, is employed as an Honorific title.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_592"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_596">107:8</a> Viz. from the August-Spirit-of-the-Great-Land to the Sage-Deity inclusive.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_593"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_597">107:9</a> <i>Kagaya-hime</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_594"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_598">107:10</a> <i>Oho-kaga-yama-to-omi-no-kami</i>. The translation follows Hirata's interpretation, which nearly agrees with that proposed by Mabuchi.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_595"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_599">107:11</a> <i>Mi-toshi-no-kami</i>. For the meaning of "harvest" attributed to the word <i>toshi</i> see Sect. XX, Note 3.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_596"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_600">107:12</a> <i>Ame-shiru-karu-midzu-hime</i>. The name might tentatively be translated thus: Heaven-Governing-Fresh-Princess-of Karu. Motowori suggests that <i>amerishiru</i> may be but a sort of Pillow-Word for the rest of the name. <i>Ama-tobu</i> is, however, the only Pillow-word for Karu found in the poems. After all, Karu may not here be the name of a place at all.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_597"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_601">107:13</a> <i>Oki-tsu-hiko-no-kami</i>. The translator ventures to think that the names of this deity and the next might simply be rendered (in accordance with the first character, <a name="img_10902"></a>
 <img src="img/10902.jpg"> entering into their composing) "Inner Prince" and "Inner Princess" or "Prince of the Interior" and "Princess of the Interior." Motowori however suggests that <i>Okitsu</i> may be the name of a place, while Hirata derives the names from <i>oki-tsuchi</i>, "laid earth," finding therein a reference to the furnace (made of clay) mentioned immediately below.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_598"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_602">107:14</a> <i>Oki-tsu-hime-no-mikoto</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_599"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_603">107:15</a> <i>Oho-be-hime-no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_600"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_604">107:16</a> <i>Kama-no-kami</i> (<a name="img_10903"></a>
 <img src="img/10903.jpg">). The "furnace" means the "kitchen." Neither Motowori nor Hirata informs us that the immense popularity of this Goddess, as well as her name, can clearly be traced to China.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_601"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_605">107:17</a> <i>Oho-yama-kuni-no-kami</i>. The meaning of <i>kuhi</i>, here (as in the case of <i>Tsumu-guhi</i> and <i>Iku-guhi</i> (see Sect. II, Note 4) rendered by the word "interior," is open to doubt.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_602"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_606">107:18</a> <i>Yama-suwe-no-oko-mushi-no-kami</i>. Motowori supposes the word <i>suwe</i>, "end," to have the signification of "top."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_603"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_607">107:19</a> As it stands, the etymology of this name is not clear. In later times the mountain was called <i>Hiyei</i> (<a name="img_10904"></a>
 <img src="img/10904.jpg">). But whether the, to outward appearance, native <i>Hiye</i> is but a corruption of this Chinese one, or <a name="page_110"><font size="1" color="green">p. 110</font></a> whether it be true that the latter (on this hypothesis bestowed on account of its likeness in sound to the native designation) was not used till the end of the eighth or beginning of the ninth century. as is commonly stated, is difficult to decide.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_604"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_608">107:20</a> <i>I.e.</i> "Close-Fresh-Sea." <i>Afumi</i> (modern pron. <i>Omi</i>, for <i>aha-umi</i>) alone signifies "fresh sea," i e. "lake." This province contains the large take commonly known as Lake Biha, (Biwa), but anciently simply called "the Fresh Sea," as being the lake par excellence of Japan. When one of the eastern provinces received, on account of a large lagoon or inlet which it contains, the name of <i>Toho-tsu-Afumi</i> (in modern pronunciation <i>Tō-tōmi</i>), <i>i.e.</i> "Distant-Fresh-Sea," the epithet Close was prefixed to the name of the province nearer to the ancient centre of government.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_605"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_609">107:21</a> <i>I.e.</i> Pine-tree-Declivity.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_606"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_610">107:22</a> <i>I.e.</i> Pueraria-Moor.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_607"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_611">107:23</a> This passage (<a name="img_11000"></a>
 <img src="img/11000.jpg">) must be corrupt. Mabuchi proposes to insert the character <a name="img_11001"></a>
 <img src="img/11001.jpg"> before <a name="img_11002"></a>
 <img src="img/11002.jpg">, and to understand the author to have meant to tell us that the deity was worshipped with arrows, that is to say, that arrows were offered at his shrine. Motowori's proposal to consider <a name="img_11003"></a>
 <img src="img/11003.jpg"> as an error for <a name="img_11004"></a>
 <img src="img/11004.jpg"> or <a name="img_11005"></a>
 <img src="img/11005.jpg">, and to interpret the clause thus: "the Deity who was changed into an arrow" is also worthy of notice. But a further suggestion of his to read <a name="img_11006"></a>
 <img src="img/11006.jpg"> for <a name="img_11007"></a>
 <img src="img/11007.jpg"> and to interpret thus: "the Deity of the Red Arrow," seems best of all when taken in connection with the tradition, which he quotes from the "Topography of Yamashiro," to the effect that this god took the shape of a red arrow to gain access to his mistress <i>Tama-yori-hime</i>, such a transformation being one of the common-places of Japanese myth.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_608"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_612">107:24</a> <i>Niha-tsu-hi-no-kami</i>. The interpretation of this name here adopted is not Motowori's, who takes <i>hi</i> in the sense of "wondrous," but Hirata's. The latter-author makes it clear that this deity (for whom <i>Niha-taka-tsu-hi-no-kami</i>, <i>i.e.</i> "The High-Deity-of-the-Fire-in-the-Yard," is but a slightly amplified designation) was none other than the above-mentioned Deity of the Kitchen, and his name an inclusive one for the pair of deities <i>Oki-tsu-hiko</i> and <i>Oki-tsu-hime</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_609"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_613">107:25</a> <i>Asuha-ho-kami</i>. The signification of this name is obscure, and Motowori's proposal to derive it from <i>ashi-niha</i>, "foot-place," because the god in question may be supposed to protect the place on which people stand, is not altogether convincing. In fact he himself only advances it with hesitation. It should be added, however, that Hirata stamps it with his special approval, as he does also Motowori's derivation of the following name, <i>Hahigi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_610"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_614">107:26</a> <a name="page_111"><font size="1" color="green">p. 111</font></a> <i>Hahi-gino-kami</i>. Obscure, but ingeniously derived by Motowori from <i>hachi-iri-gimi</i>, <i>i.e.</i> "entering prince," the deity in question being supposed to have been the special <a href="errata.htm#17">protector</a> of the entrances to houses, and to have thence received his name. Mr. Satow has translated it in the Rituals as "Entrance Limit."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_611"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_615">107:27</a> <i>Kaga-yama-to-omi-no-kami</i>. The name is almost identical with that in Note 10.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_612"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_616">107:28</a> <i>Ha-yama-to-no-kami</i>. The interpretation of the name is that proposed by Motowori, and which seems tolerably satisfactory.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_613"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_617">107:29</a> <i>Niha-taka-tsu-hi-no-kami</i>. See note 24.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_614"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_618">107:30</a> <i>Oho-tsuchi-no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_615"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_619">107:31</a> <i>Tsuchi-no-mi-oya-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_616"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_620">107:32</a> This number is obtained if (as is perhaps permissible from a Japanese point of view) we consider <i>Oki-tsu-hiko</i> and <i>Oki-tsu-hime</i> as forming a single deity. Otherwise there are ten. A similar remark applies to the number sixteen mentioned immediately below.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_617"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_621">108:33</a> See Note 28.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_618"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_622">108:34</a> See Sect. V, Note 8. The fact that this goddess is related to have been previously killed (see Sect. XVII) causes Motowori some embarrassment.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_619"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_623">108:35</a> <i>Waka-yama-kuhi-no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_620"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_624">108:36</a> <i>Waka-toshi-no-kami</i>. Motowori proposes (considering this name in connection with the four that follow) to take <i>waka-toshi</i> in this place in the signification of the "the first sprouting" of the young rice. The five deities whose birth is here mentioned seem collectively to represent the natural succession of agricultural operations throughout the year.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_621"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_625">108:37</a> <i>Waka-sa-name-no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_622"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_626">108:38</a> <i>Midzu-maki-no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_623"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_627">108:39</a> <i>Natsu-taka-tsu-hi-no-kami</i>. Motowori's interpretation of <i>hi</i> as "wondrous" is perhaps as good as that here adopted, according to which it signifies "sun." His view would give us in English "the Summer-High-Wondrous-Deity."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_624"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_628">108:40</a> <i>Natsu-no-me-no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_625"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_629">108:41</a> <i>Aki-bime no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_626"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_630">108:42</a> <i>Kuku-toshi-no-kami</i>. The word <i>kuku</i>, "stem," seems to allude to the length of the well-grown rice.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_627"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_631">108:43</a> <a name="page_112"><font size="1" color="green">p. 112</font></a> <i>Kuku-ki-waka-muro-tsunane-no-kami</i>. Motowori supposes this god to have been the protector of houses, and interprets the name to denote the beams, and the ropes with which the beams were bound together. The word here read <i>tsuna</i>, "rope;" is written with the character, and might perhaps be rendered "pueraria." But as in early times the tendrils of such creeping plants formed the only substitute for rope, the two renderings come to have very nearly the same signification.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_628"></a><a href="kj036.htm#fr_632">108:44</a> The name is here abbreviated in the original to <i>Waka-muro-tsuna-ne-no-kami</i>.</p>
