 <p><a name="page_323"><font size="1" color="green">p. 323</font></a></p>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. CXV.—EMPEROR Ō-JIN (PART XII.—DESCENDANTS OF AMA-NO-HI-BOKO, AND TREASURES BROUGHT BY HIM.)]</h2>  
 <p>Forthwith staying in that country, he wedded Saki-tsu-mi, <a name="fr_1961"></a><a href="#fn_1956"><font size="1">1</font></a> daughter of Tajima-no-matawo, <a name="fr_1962"></a><a href="#fn_1957"><font size="1">2</font></a> and begot a child: Tajima-morosuku. <a name="fr_1963"></a><a href="#fn_1958"><font size="1">3</font></a> The latter's child was Tajima-hi-ne. <a name="fr_1964"></a><a href="#fn_1959"><font size="1">4</font></a> The latter's child was Tajima-hinaraki. <a name="fr_1965"></a><a href="#fn_1960"><font size="1">5</font></a> The</p>

 <p>latter's children were Tajima-mori, <a name="fr_1966"></a><a href="#fn_1961"><font size="1">6</font></a> next Tajima-hitaka, <a name="fr_1967"></a><a href="#fn_1962"><font size="1">7</font></a> next Kiyo-hiko <a name="fr_1968"></a><a href="#fn_1963"><font size="1">8</font></a> (<small>three Deities</small>). <a name="fr_1969"></a><a href="#fn_1964"><font size="1">9</font></a> This Kiyo-hiko wedded Tagima-no-mehi, <a name="fr_1970"></a><a href="#fn_1965"><font size="1">10</font></a> and begot children: Suga-no-morowo, <a name="fr_1971"></a><a href="#fn_1966"><font size="1">11</font></a> next his younger sister Suga-kama-yura-domi. <a name="fr_1972"></a><a href="#fn_1967"><font size="1">12</font></a> So the above mentioned Tajima-hitaka wedded his niece Yura-domi, and begot a child: Her Augustness Princess Takanuka of Kadzuraki. <a name="fr_1973"></a><a href="#fn_1968"><font size="1">13</font></a> (<small>This was the august parent <a name="fr_1974"></a><a href="#fn_1969"><font size="1">14</font></a> of Her Augustness Princess Okinaga-tarashi.</small>) So the things which Ama-no-hi-boko brought over here, and which were called the "precious treasures," <a name="fr_1975"></a><a href="#fn_1970"><font size="1">15</font></a> were: two strings of pearls: <a name="fr_1976"></a><a href="#fn_1971"><font size="1">16</font></a> likewise a wave-shaking scarf, a wave-cutting scarf, a wind-shaking scarf, and a wind-cutting scarf; <a name="fr_1977"></a><a href="#fn_1972"><font size="1">17</font></a> likewise a mirror of the offing and a mirror of the shore, <a name="fr_1978"></a><a href="#fn_1973"><font size="1">18</font></a>—eight articles in all. (<small>These are the Eight Great Deities of Idzushi.</small>) <a name="fr_1979"></a><a href="#fn_1974"><font size="1">19</font></a></p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_1956"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1961">323:1</a> <a name="page_324"><font size="1" color="green">p. 324</font></a> This name may mean "lucky ears," or "possessor of luck;" but it is obscure, and is moreover in the ''Chronicles" (where it is given as the name, not of the daughter, but of the father) read <i>Mahe-tsu-mi</i>,—a reading which, will not bear either of these interpretations.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1957"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1962">323:2</a> Matawo seems to signify "complete (<i>i.e.</i>, healthy or vigorous) male." Observe that the word Tajima enters into the designations of most of his descendants.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1958"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1963">323:3</a> In the "Chronicles" <i>Morosuke</i>, and elsewhere <i>Morosugi</i>. The etymology of these names is obscure except that of the last-mentioned, which signifies "many cryptomerias."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1959"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1964">323:4</a> <i>Hi-ne</i> may perhaps signify "wondrous lord."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1960"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1965">323:5</a> The meaning of this name is obscure, but that of <i>Hina-rashi-hime</i> in Sect. XXVI (Note 19) may be compared.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1961"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1966">324:6</a> See Sect. LXXIV, Note 1.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1962"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1967">324:7</a> <i>Hi-taka</i> may <a href="errata.htm#79">signify</a> either "sun-height" or "wondrous height."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1963"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1968">324:8</a> This name signifies "pure prince."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1964"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1969">324:9</a> As usual, it is not the actual word Deity that is used, but the Auxiliary Numeral for Deities.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1965"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1970">324:10</a> <i>Tagima</i> is the name of a place, not to be confounded with the province of Tajima. The signification of <i>mehi</i> is quite obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1966"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1971">324:11</a> <i>Suga</i> may either be the name of place in Tajima, as proposed<a name="page_325"><font size="1" color="green">p. 325</font></a> by Motowori, or identical with the Suga of Sect. XIX. The meaning of Morowo is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1967"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1972">324:12</a> The signification of this name is obscure. But Suga, Kama, and Yura are apparently the names of places.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1968"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1973">324:13</a> <i>Kadzuraki no Taka-nuka-hime</i>. Kadzuraki is the name of a department, and Takanuka that of a place in that department, in the province of Yamato.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1969"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1974">324:14</a> Literally, "ancestress." But see Sect. XXII, Note 4. It will be remembered that <i>Okinaga-tarashi-hime</i> was the Empress Jin-go.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1970"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1975">324:15</a> Literally, "treasures of jewels."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1971"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1976">324:16</a> Or, "beads."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1972"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1977">324:17</a> <i>I.e.</i>, a scarf to raise the waves and a scarf to still the waves, a scarf to raise the wind and a scarf to still the wind. <i>Conf</i>. the magic scarfs mentioned hear the beginning of Sect. XXIII, by waving which the Deity Master-of-the-Great-Land (<i>Oho-kuni-nushi</i>) kept off the snakes, the wasps and the centipedes.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1973"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1978">324:18</a> This seems to be the signification of the original terms <i>oki tsu kagami</i> and <i>hi tsu kagami</i>, but we are not hereby helped to a very clear understanding of the nature of the articles which the author <a href="errata.htm#80">meant</a> to describe. The parallel passage of the "Chronicles" tells us of a "sun, mirror." Indeed it enumerates the "eight precious treasures" in a manner that diverges a great deal from the account given in these "Records."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1974"></a><a href="kj122.htm#fr_1979">324:19</a> Or, the "Eight-fold Great Deity." As has already frequently been remarked, the distinction which we so rigorously draw between Singular and Plural does not occupy the Japanese mind, and "eight "and "eight-fold" are taken to mean much the same thing, In the following sentence we find these eight deities (or this eight-fold deity) spoken of in such a manner as to necessitate the use of the Singular Number in the translation. Motowori supposes that they (or he) took the form of a young man (as in several other legends) to become the father of the Goddess mentioned in the text. . . . <i>Idzushi</i> seems to signify "wonderful stone."</p>
