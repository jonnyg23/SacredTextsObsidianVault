 <p><a name="page_177"><font size="1" color="green">p. 177</font></a></p>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. L.—EMPEROR JIM-MU (PART VII.—THE EMPIRE PACIFIED)]</h2>  
 <p>So then His Augustness Nigi-hayabi <a name="fr_979"></a><a href="#fn_975"><font size="1">1</font></a> waited on and said to the august child of the Heavenly Deity: "As I heard that [thou], the august child of the Heavenly Deity, hadst descended from Heaven, I have followed</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_178"><font size="1" color="green">p. 178</font></a></p>  
 <p>down to wait on thee." Forthwith presenting to him the heavenly symbols, <a name="fr_980"></a><a href="#fn_976"><font size="1">2</font></a> he respectfully served him. So His Augustness Nigi-hayabi wedded the Princess of Tomi, <a name="fr_981"></a><a href="#fn_977"><font size="1">3</font></a> [145] sister of the Prince of Tomi, and begot a child, His Augustness <i>Umashi-ma-ji</i>. <a name="fr_982"></a><a href="#fn_978"><font size="1">4</font></a> (<small>He was the ancestor of the Chiefs of the Warrior-Clan, <a name="fr_983"></a><a href="#fn_979"><font size="1">5</font></a> of the Grandees of Hodzumi; <a name="fr_984"></a><a href="#fn_980"><font size="1">6</font></a> and of the Grandees of the Neck-Clan</small>). <a name="fr_985"></a><a href="#fn_981"><font size="1">7</font></a> So having thus subdued and pacified the savage Deities, and extirpated the unsubmissive people, [His Augustness Kamu-yamato-ihare-biko] dwelt at the palace of Kashibara <a name="fr_986"></a><a href="#fn_982"><font size="1">8</font></a> near Unebi <a name="fr_987"></a><a href="#fn_983"><font size="1">9</font></a> and ruled the Empire. <a name="fr_988"></a><a href="#fn_984"><font size="1">10</font></a></p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_975"></a><a href="kj057.htm#fr_979">177:1</a> The component parts of this name, rendered according to the analogy of that in Sect. XXXIII, Note 5, may be interpreted to signify "Plenty-Swift." The genealogy of this god is not known.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_976"></a><a href="kj057.htm#fr_980">178:2</a> <i>I.e.</i>, the swords, quivers, bow, and arrows mentioned in Sect. XXXIII, as having been brought down from Heaven by the divine attendants of the Emperor Jim-mu's grandfather.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_977"></a><a href="kj057.htm#fr_981">178:3</a> <i>Tomi ya-bime</i>, The syllable <i>ya</i> is inexplicable, but perhaps merely an Expletive.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_978"></a><a href="kj057.htm#fr_982">178:4</a> The signification of this name is by no means clear; but, rendered according to the characters with which it is written in the "Chronicles," it would mean "Savoury-True-Hand."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_979"></a><a href="kj057.htm#fr_983">178:5</a> <i>Mononobe no murazhi</i>. This and the two following are of course "gentile names."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_980"></a><a href="kj057.htm#fr_984">178:6</a> <i>Hodzumi no omi</i>. Hodzumi, which is the name of a place, signifies "rice-ears piled up."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_981"></a><a href="kj057.htm#fr_985">178:7</a> <i>Une-be no omi</i>. The interpretation of this name is given according is Motowori, who explains that the members of this family,—in particular the female members,—waited at the Emperor's table, and wore veils over their necks when so employed. The name is commonly corrupted to <i>uneme</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_982"></a><a href="kj057.htm#fr_986">178:8</a> Better known as Kashihabara. The name signifies "oak-moor," or rather "a place planted with oaks." This is usually, though without sufficient foundation, reckoned the earliest of the historical capitals of Japan. It is in Yamato.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_983"></a><a href="kj057.htm#fr_987">178:9</a> Unebi is the name of a hill in Yamato. The etymology of the word is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_984"></a><a href="kj057.htm#fr_988">178:10</a> <a name="page_179"><font size="1" color="green">p. 179</font></a> <i>I.e.</i>, "ruled the Empire from his palace of Kashibara near Unebi. For the expression <a name="img_17900"></a>
 <img src="img/17900.jpg"> (literally "[all] beneath Heaven"), here rendered "Empire," see Sect. XXVII, Note 13.</p>
