 <p><a name="page_291"><font size="1" color="green">p. 291</font></a></p>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. XCIX.—EMPEROR CHIŪ-AI (PART V.—THE EMPRESS JIN-GŌ FISHES IN TSUKUSHI).]</h2>  
 <p>Again when, having reached the village of Tamashima <a name="fr_1775"></a><a href="#fn_1770"><font size="1">1</font></a> in the Department of Matsura <a name="fr_1776"></a><a href="#fn_1771"><font size="1">2</font></a> in Tsukushi, she partook of an august meal on the bank of the river, it being then the first decade of the fourth moon, she then sat on a shoal <a name="fr_1777"></a><a href="#fn_1772"><font size="1">3</font></a> in the middle of the river, picked out threads from her august skirt, used grains of rice as bait, and hooked the trout <a name="fr_1778"></a><a href="#fn_1773"><font size="1">4</font></a> in the river. (<small>The name by which the river is called is the Wo-gaha; <a name="fr_1779"></a><a href="#fn_1774"><font size="1">5</font></a> again the name by which the shoal is called is Kachi-do-hime. <a name="fr_1780"></a><a href="#fn_1775"><font size="1">6</font></a></small>) So down to the present time it is an uninterrupted [custom] for women in the first decade of the fourth moon to pick out threads from their skirts, use grains as bait, and hook trout.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_1770"></a><a href="kj106.htm#fr_1775">291:1</a> <a name="page_291"><font size="1" color="green">p. 291</font></a> <i>I.e.</i>. "jewel-island."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1771"></a><a href="kj106.htm#fr_1776">291:2</a> <i>Matsura-gata</i>. The "Chronicles" give an absurd derivation of Matsura from the Adjective <i>medzurashi</i>, "astonishing," which the Empress is supposed to have ejaculated on finding a trout hooked to her line! The obvious etymology is <i>matsu-ura</i>, "pine-beach."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1772"></a><a href="kj106.htm#fr_1777">291:3</a> The character in the original is <a name="img_29100"></a>
 <img src="img/29100.jpg"> (for <a name="img_29101"></a>
 <img src="img/29101.jpg">), in Japanese <i>iso</i>, which may or may not be connected with the word <i>ishi</i>, "stone." In any case Motowori is not justified in saying that it must be understood to mean <a name="page_292"><font size="1" color="green">p. 292</font></a> "stone "in this place, as <i>iso</i> means rather a sandy than a stony place, rising above the water level.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1773"></a><a href="kj106.htm#fr_1778">291:4</a> In Japanese <i>ayu</i>, a small species of the salmon family (<i>Plecoglossus altivelis</i>).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1774"></a><a href="kj106.htm#fr_1779">291:5</a> <i>I.e.</i>, "little river."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1775"></a><a href="kj106.htm#fr_1780">291:6</a> <i>I.e.</i>, "princess of the gate of victory." But though the words lend themselves to this interpretation, it can hardly be supposed that such is their real etymology, and indeed the editor of 1687 draws attention in a Note to the difficulty of accepting the statement in the text.</p>
