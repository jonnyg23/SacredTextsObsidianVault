 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>Fictitious and Symbolic Creatures in Art</i>, by John Vinycomb, [1909], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">Sea-lion</h3>  
 <p>or <i>Lion poisson</i>, a mythical sea-creature, frequently used in heraldry as an emblem of bold actions achieved on the ocean in the country's service. It is depicted as the fore part of a lion with webbed feet, the hinder part ending in a fish's tail.</p>  
 <p>Two such animals support the arms of Viscount Falmouth.</p>  
 <p>The Earl of Howth has for supporters <i>a sea-lion</i> </p>  
 <p><a name="page_275"><font size="1" color="green">p. 275</font></a></p>  
 <p><i>argent</i>, and a mermaid, proper. The crest also is a sea-lion.</p>  
 <p>The crest of Duckworth is <i>a tower, the battlements partly demolished, from</i><a name="img_27500"></a>
 </p><table align="RIGHT"><tr><td align="CENTER" valign="CENTER"><img src="img/27500.jpg" alt="Sea-lion erect."></td></tr></table><i> the top flames issuant proper; on the sinister side a sea-lion erect azure, pressing against the tower</i>.  
 <p>Silvestre.—<i>Argent, a sea-lion couchant azure, crowned armed and langued gules</i>.</p>  
 <p>When the sea-lion or other compounded creature of this kind is erect, it should be clearly blazoned as "a sea-lion erect on his tail," to distinguish it from naiant, the swimming position natural to it.</p>
