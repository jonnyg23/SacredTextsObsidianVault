 <h1 align="CENTER">PHONETIC KEY</h1>
 <p>â a longer and shorter forms of the Continental <i>a</i>, like <i>a</i> in <i>far</i></p>
 <u></u><p>â as in <i>fall</i></p>
 <p>A as in <i>final</i>; a close approximation to <i>u</i> in <i>cut</i></p>
 <p>ê e longer and shorter forms of the Continental <i>e</i>, like a in <i>fate</i></p>
 <u></u><p>ê as in <i>bell</i></p>
 <p>î i longer and shorter forms of the Continental <i>i</i>, like <i>ee</i> in <i>street</i></p>
 <u></u><p>î as in <i>hit</i></p>
 <p>ô o longer and shorter forms of English <i>o</i>, as in <i>flow</i></p>
 <p>û as in <i>rule</i></p>
 <p>u as in <i>put</i></p>
 <sup><p>u</p></sup> <sup>o</sup> barely formed <i>o</i> and <i>u</i> sounds; rather qualities of the preceding consonant sounds than independent vowels
 <p>q the velar <i>k</i>, not found in English</p>
 <u></u><p>g the velar <i>g</i> corresponding to the preceding, not found in English</p>
 <u></u><p>y a sound similar to but deeper than the preceding, pronounced by the younger Indians almost like English <i>y</i></p>
 <p>x the velar spirant, pronounced like Spanish <i>j</i> or German <i>ch</i></p>
 <u></u><p>x the palatal spirant, often mistaken for <i>h</i></p>
 <p>c like English <i>sh</i> in <i>short</i></p>
 <p>dz as in <i>adze</i></p>
 <p>ts am in <i>sits</i></p>
 <p>dj like English <i>j</i> and <i>dg</i> in <i>judge</i></p>
 <p>tc like English <i>ch</i> in <i>church</i></p>
 <p>L not found in English, but resembling a rapid pronunciation of <i>t</i> and <i>l</i>, or of <i>k</i> and <i>l</i></p>
 <u></u><p>L not found in English, but resembling a rapid pronunciation of <i>d</i> and <i>l</i></p>
 <strike></strike><p>l a spirant belonging to the same series as the preceding; not found in English though often represented by <i>thl</i> or <i>hl</i></p>
 <p>t, d, n, s, k, g, h, w, y approximate the sounds for which they stand in English though the agreement is by no means absolute</p>
 <p>t!, s!, ts!, tc!, L!, k!, q! are similar to <i>t</i>, <i>s</i>, <i>ts</i>, <i>tc</i>, <i>L</i>, <i>k</i>, <i>q</i>, but are accompanied by a catch in the breath which sometimes gives the impression of a pause, and sometimes sounds like a sharp click</p>
 <p>k*! when <i>k!</i> is pronounced very far forward in the mouth it is sometimes set off in this way, but the distinction between the two sounds is by no means clear</p>
 <p>Labials are found only in a few words of foreign origin</p>
