 <p></p><h2 align="center">II</h2>
 <p></p><h1 align="center">The Perfect Sermon</h1>
 <p><a name="page_306"><font size="1" color="green">p. 306</font></a></p>
 <p><a name="page_307"><font size="1" color="green">p. 307</font></a></p>
 <p></p><h3 align="center">THE PERFECT SERMON</h3>
 <p></p><h4 align="center">OR THE ASCLEPIUS</h4>
 <p><small>(Text: The Greek original is lost, and only a Latin version remains to us. I use the text of Hildebrand (G. F.), <i>L. Apuleii Opera Omnia ex Fide Optimorum Codicum</i> (Leipzig, 1842), Pars II., pp. 279-334; but have very occasionally preferred the text in Patrizzis <i>Nova de Universis Philosophia</i> (Venice, 1593), or of the Bipontine edition of Appuleius, <i>Lucii Apuleji Madaurensis Platonici Philosophi Opera</i> (Biponti, 1788), pp. 285-325.)</small></p>
 <p></p><h4 align="center">I</h4>
 <p>1. <a name="fr_669"></a><a href="#fn_668"><font size="1">1</font></a> [I. M. <a name="fr_670"></a><a href="#fn_669"><font size="1">2</font></a>] [<i>Trismegistus.</i>] God, O Asclepius, hath brought thee unto us that thou mayest hear a Godly sermon, <a name="fr_671"></a><a href="#fn_670"><font size="1">3</font></a> a sermon such as well may seem of all the previous ones weve [either] uttered, or with which weve been inspired by the Divine, more Godly than the piety of [ordinary] faith.</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_308"><font size="1" color="green">p. 308</font></a></p>
 <p>If thou with eye of intellect <a name="fr_672"></a><a href="#fn_671"><font size="1">1</font></a> shalt <i>see</i> this Word <a name="fr_673"></a><a href="#fn_672"><font size="1">2</font></a> thou shalt in thy whole mind be filled quite full of all things good.</p>
 <p>If that, indeed, the “many” be the “good,” and not the “one,” in which are “all.” Indeed the difference between the two is found in their agreement,—“All” is of “One” <a name="fr_674"></a><a href="#fn_673"><font size="1">3</font></a> or “One” is “All.” So closely bound is each to other, that neither can be parted from its mate.</p>
 <p>But this with diligent attention shalt thou learn from out the sermon that shall follow [this].</p>
 <p>But do thou, O Asclepius, go forth a moment and call in the one who is to hear. <a name="fr_675"></a><a href="#fn_674"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>
 <p>(And when he had come in, Asclepius proposed that Ammon too should be allowed to come. Thereon Thrice-greatest said:)</p>
 <p>[<i>Tris.</i>] There is no cause why Ammon should be kept away from us. For we remember how we have ourselves set down in writing many things to his address, <a name="fr_676"></a><a href="#fn_675"><font size="1">5</font></a> as though unto a son most dear and most beloved, of physics many things, of ethics [too] as many as could be.</p>
 <p>It is, however, with <i>thy</i> name I will inscribe this treatise.</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_309"><font size="1" color="green">p. 309</font></a></p>
 <p>But call, I prithee, no one else but Ammon, lest a most pious sermon on a so great theme be spoilt by the admission of the multitude.</p>
 <p>For tis the mark of an unpious mind to publish to the knowledge of the crowd a tractate brimming oer with the full Greatness of Divinity.</p>
 <p>(When Ammon too had come within the holy place, and when the sacred group of four was now complete with piety and with Gods goodly presence—to them, sunk in fit silence reverently, their souls and minds pendent on Hermes lips, thus Love <a name="fr_677"></a><a href="#fn_676"><font size="1">1</font></a> Divine began to speak.)</p>
 <p></p><h4 align="center">II</h4>
 <p>1. [<i>Tris.</i>] The soul of every man, O [my] Asclepius, is deathless; yet not all in like fashion, but some in one way or [one] time, some in another.</p>
 <p><i>Asc.</i> Is not, then, O Thrice-greatest one, each soul of one [and the same] quality?</p>
 <p><i>Tris.</i> How quickly hast thou fallen, O Asclepius, from reasons true sobriety!</p>
 <p>Did not I say that “All” is “One,” and “One” is “All,” <a name="fr_678"></a><a href="#fn_677"><font size="1">2</font></a> in as much as all things have</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_310"><font size="1" color="green">p. 310</font></a></p>
 <p>been in the Creator before they were created. Nor is He called unfitly “All,” in that His members are the “All.”</p>
 <p>Therefore, in all this argument, see that thou keep in mind Him who is “One”-“All,” or who Himself is maker of the “All.”</p>
 <p>2. All things descend from Heaven to Earth, to Water and to Air.</p>
 <p>Tis Fire alone, in that it is borne upwards, giveth life; that which [is carried] downwards [is] subservient to Fire.</p>
 <p>Further, whatever doth descend from the above, begetteth; what floweth upwards, nourisheth.</p>
 <p>Tis Earth alone, in that it resteth on itself, that is Receiver of all things, and [also] the Restorer of all genera that it receives.</p>
 <p>This Whole, <a name="fr_679"></a><a href="#fn_678"><font size="1">1</font></a> therefore, as thou rememberest, <a name="fr_680"></a><a href="#fn_679"><font size="1">2</font></a> in that it is of all,—in other words, all things, embraced by nature under “Soul” and “World,” <a name="fr_681"></a><a href="#fn_680"><font size="1">3</font></a> are in [perpetual] flux, so varied by the multiform equality of all their forms, that countless kinds of well-distinguished qualities may be discerned, yet with this bond of union, that all should seem as One, and from “One” “All.” <a name="fr_682"></a><a href="#fn_681"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_311"><font size="1" color="green">p. 311</font></a></p>
 <p></p><h4 align="center">III</h4>
 <p>1. That, then, from which the whole Cosmos is formed, consisteth of Four Elements—Fire, Water, Earth, and Air; Cosmos [itself is] one, [its] Soul [is] one, and God is one.</p>
 <p>Now lend to me the whole of thee, <a name="fr_683"></a><a href="#fn_682"><font size="1">1</font></a>—all that thou canst in mind, all that thou skillst in penetration.</p>
 <p>For that the Reason <a name="fr_684"></a><a href="#fn_683"><font size="1">2</font></a> of Divinity may not be known except by an intention of the senses like to it. <a name="fr_685"></a><a href="#fn_684"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>
 <p>Tis <a name="fr_686"></a><a href="#fn_685"><font size="1">4</font></a> likest to the torrents flood, down-dashing headlong from above with all-devouring tide; so that it comes about, that by the swiftness of its speed it is too quick for our attention, not only for the hearers, but also for the very teachers. <a name="fr_687"></a><a href="#fn_686"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>

 </p>
