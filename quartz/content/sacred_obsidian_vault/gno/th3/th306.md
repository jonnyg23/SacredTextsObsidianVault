 <p></p><h4 align="center">EXCERPT V</h4>
 <p></p><hr> 
 <p></p><h3 align="center">[OF MATTER]</h3>
 <p><small>(I have added the title, it being the same as that of the main section of Stobæus, Patrizzi (p. 51) giving only the simple heading “From the [Sermons] to Tat.”</small></p>
 <p><small>Text: Stobæus, <i>Phys</i>., xi. 2, under the heading: “Of Hermes from the [Sermons] to Tat”; G. p. 121; M. i. 84, 85; W. i. 131.</small></p>
 <p><small>Ménard, Livre IV., No. viii. of “Fragments from the Books of Hermes to his son Tat,” p. 250.)</small></p>
 <p><i>Her</i>. Matter both has been born, O son, and it has been [before it came into existence]; for Matter is the vase of genesis, <a name="fr_79"></a><a href="#fn_79"><font size="1">1</font></a> and genesis, the mode of energy of God, whos free from all necessity of genesis, and pre-exists.</p>
 <p>[Matter], accordingly, by its reception of the seed of genesis, did come [herself] to birth, and [so] became subject to change, and, being shaped,</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_27"><font size="1" color="green">p. 27</font></a></p>
 <p>took forms; for she, contriving the forms of her [own] changing, presided over her own changing self.</p>
 <p>The unborn state <a name="fr_80"></a><a href="#fn_80"><font size="1">1</font></a> of Matter, then, was formlessness <a name="fr_81"></a><a href="#fn_81"><font size="1">2</font></a>; its genesis is its being brought into activity.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_79"></a><a href="th306.htm#fr_79">26:1</a> Or receptacle or field of genesis, or birth (ἀγγεῖον γενέσεως). The idea of a vessel or vase of birth was a familiar symbol with the Pythagoreans; μεταγγισμός (from the simile of pouring water out of one vessel into another) being one of their synonyms for metempsychosis.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_80"></a><a href="th306.htm#fr_80">27:1</a> ἀγεννησία</p>
 <p><a name="fn_81"></a><a href="th306.htm#fr_81">27:2</a> ἀμορφία. Compare this with the Christian Gnostic commentator of the Naassene Document, quoted by Hippolytus (<i>Philos</i>. v. 7), and the comment of Hippolytus on him: “Their first and Blessed Formless Essence (ἀσχημάτιστος οὐσία), the cause of all forms” (“Myth of Man,” § 7).</p>
