 <p></p><h4 align="center">EXCERPT XII.</h4>
 <p></p><hr> 
 <p></p><h3 align="center">OF PROVIDENCE AND FATE</h3>
 <p><small>(Title from Patrizzi (p. 38); followed by: “From the [Sermons] to Ammon.”</small></p>
 <p><small>Text: Stob., <i>Phys</i>., v. 20, under heading: “Of Hermes from the [Sermons] to Ammon”; G. p. 70; M. i. 48, 49; W. i. 82.</small></p>
 <p><small>Ménard, Livre IV., No. ii. of “Fragments of the Books of Hermes to Ammon,” p. 258.)</small></p>
 <p><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">All</span> things are born by Nature and by Fate, and there is not a [single] space bereft of Providence.</p>
 <p>Now Providence is the Self-perfect <a name="fr_196"></a><a href="#fn_196"><font size="1">1</font></a> Reason.</p>
 <p>And of this [Reason] there are two spontaneous powers,—Necessity and Fate.</p>
 <p>And Fate doth minister to Providence and to Necessity; while unto Fate the Stars <a name="fr_197"></a><a href="#fn_197"><font size="1">2</font></a> do minister.</p>
 <p>For Fate no one is able to escape, nor keep himself from their <a name="fr_198"></a><a href="#fn_198"><font size="1">3</font></a> shrewd scrutiny. <a name="fr_199"></a><a href="#fn_199"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>
 <p>For that the Stars are instruments of Fate; it is at its behest that they effect all things for nature and for men. <a name="fr_200"></a><a href="#fn_200"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>
