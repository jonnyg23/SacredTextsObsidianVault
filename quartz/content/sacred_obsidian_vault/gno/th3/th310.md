 <p></p><h4 align="center">EXCERPT IX.</h4>
 <p></p><hr> 
 <p></p><h3 align="center">OF [THE DECANS AND] THE STARS</h3>
 <p><small>(Patrizzi (p. 38b) does not give the first third of the text (§§ 1-5), and his title, “Of the Stars,” is evidently incomplete; it is followed by “To the Same [<i>i.e.</i> Tat].”</small></p>
 <p><small>Text: Stob., <i>Phys</i>., xxi. 9, under the heading: “Of Hermes from the [Sermon] to Tat,” pp. 184-190; M. i. 129-133; W. i. 189-194.</small></p>
 <p><small>Ménard, Livre IV., No. vi. of “Fragments from the Books of Hermes to his Son Tat,” pp. 242-247, under the sub-heading, “Of the Decans and the Stars.”)</small></p>
 <p>1. <i>Tat</i>. Since in thy former General Sermons (<i>Logoi</i> <a name="fr_121"></a><a href="#fn_121"><font size="1">1</font></a>), [father,] thou didst promise me an explanation of the Six-and-thirty Decans, <a name="fr_122"></a><a href="#fn_122"><font size="1">2</font></a> explain, I prithee, now concerning them and their activity. <a name="fr_123"></a><a href="#fn_123"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>
 <p><i>Her</i>. Theres not the slightest wish in me not to do so, O Tat, and this should prove the</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_46"><font size="1" color="green">p. 46</font></a></p>
 <p>most authoritative sermon (<i>logos</i>) and the chiefest of them all. So ponder on it well.</p>
 <p>We have already spoken unto thee about the Circle of the Animals, or the Life-giving one, <a name="fr_124"></a><a href="#fn_124"><font size="1">1</font></a> of the Five Planets, and of Sun and Moon, and of the Circle <a name="fr_125"></a><a href="#fn_125"><font size="1">2</font></a> of each one of these.</p>
 <p>2. <i>Tat</i>. Thou hast done so, Thrice-greatest one. </p>
 <p><i>Her</i>. Thus would I have thee understand as well about the Six-and-thirty Decans,—calling the former things to mind, in order that the sermon on the latter may also be well understood by thee.</p>
 <p><i>Tat</i>. I have recalled them, father, [to my mind].</p>
 <p><i>Her</i>. We said, [my] son, there is a Body which encompasses all things.</p>
 <p>Conceive it, then, as being in itself a kind of figure of a sphere-like shape; so is the universe conformed.</p>
 <p><i>Tat</i>. Ive thought of such a figure in my mind, just as thou dost describe, O father [mine].</p>
 <p>3. <i>Her</i>. Beneath the Circle of this [all-embracing] frame <a name="fr_126"></a><a href="#fn_126"><font size="1">3</font></a> are ranged the Six-and-thirty Decans, between this Circle of the Universe and that one of the Animals, determining the boundaries of both these Circles, and, as it were,</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_47"><font size="1" color="green">p. 47</font></a></p>
 <p>holding that of the Animals aloft up in the air, and [so] defining it.</p>
 <p>They <a name="fr_127"></a><a href="#fn_127"><font size="1">1</font></a> share the motion of the Planetary Spheres, and [yet] have equal powers with the [main] motion of the Whole, <a name="fr_128"></a><a href="#fn_128"><font size="1">2</font></a> crosswise <a name="fr_129"></a><a href="#fn_129"><font size="1">3</font></a> the Seven.</p>
 <p>Theyre <a name="fr_130"></a><a href="#fn_130"><font size="1">4</font></a> checked by nothing but the All-encircling Body, for this must be the final thing in the [whole grades of] motion,—itself by its own self.</p>
 <p>But they speed on the Seven other Circles, because they <a name="fr_131"></a><a href="#fn_131"><font size="1">5</font></a> move with a less rapid motion than the [Circle] of the All.</p>
 <p>Let us, then, think of them as though of Watchers stationed round [and watching] over both the Seven themselves and oer the Circle of the All,—or rather over all things in the World,</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_48"><font size="1" color="green">p. 48</font></a></p>
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> —holding together all, and keeping the good order of all things.</p>
 <p>4. <i>Tat</i>. Thus do I have it, father, in my mind, from what thou sayst.</p>
 <p><i>Her</i>. Moreover, Tat, thou shouldst have in thy mind that they are also free from the necessities laid on the other Stars.</p>
 <p>They are not checked and settled in their course, nor are they [further] hindered and made to tread in their own steps again <a name="fr_132"></a><a href="#fn_132"><font size="1">1</font></a>; nor are they kept away from <a name="fr_133"></a><a href="#fn_133"><font size="1">2</font></a> the Suns light,—[all of] which things the other Stars endure.</p>
 <p>But free, above them all, as though they were inerrant Guards and Overseers of the whole, they night and day surround the universe.</p>
 <p>5. <i>Tat</i>. Do these, then, also, further exercise an influence <a name="fr_134"></a><a href="#fn_134"><font size="1">3</font></a> upon us?</p>
 <p><i>Her</i>. The greatest, O [my] son. For if they act in <a name="fr_135"></a><a href="#fn_135"><font size="1">4</font></a> them, <a name="fr_136"></a><a href="#fn_136"><font size="1">5</font></a> how should they fail to act on us as well,—both on each one of us and generally? <a name="fr_137"></a><a href="#fn_137"><font size="1">6</font></a></p>
 <p>Thus, O [my] son, of all those things that happen generally, the bringing into action <a name="fr_138"></a><a href="#fn_138"><font size="1">7</font></a> is from these <a name="fr_139"></a><a href="#fn_139"><font size="1">8</font></a>; as for example,—and ponder what I say,—downfalls of kingdoms, states rebellions,</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_49"><font size="1" color="green">p. 49</font></a></p>
 <p>plagues [and] famines, tidal waves [and] quakings of the earth; no one of these, O son, takes place without their action. <a name="fr_140"></a><a href="#fn_140"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>
 <p>Nay, further still, bear this in mind. If they rule over them, and we are in our turn beneath the Seven, dost thou not think that some of their activity extends to us as well,—[who are] assuredly their sons, or [come into existence] by their means?</p>
 <p>6. <i>Tat</i>. What, [then,] may be the type <a name="fr_141"></a><a href="#fn_141"><font size="1">2</font></a> of body that they have, O father [mine]?</p>
 <p><i>Her</i>. The many call them daimones; but they are not some special class of daimones, for they have not some other kind of bodies made of some special kind of matter, nor are they moved by means of soul, as we [are moved], but they are [simple] operations <a name="fr_142"></a><a href="#fn_142"><font size="1">3</font></a> of these Six-and-thirty Gods.</p>
 <p>Nay, further, still, have in thy mind, O Tat, their operations,—that they cast in the earth the seed of those whom [men] call Tanĕs, some playing the part of saviours, others being most destructive. <a name="fr_143"></a><a href="#fn_143"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_50"><font size="1" color="green">p. 50</font></a></p>
 <p>7. Further the Stars <a name="fr_144"></a><a href="#fn_144"><font size="1">1</font></a> in heaven as well do in their several [courses] bear them <a name="fr_145"></a><a href="#fn_145"><font size="1">2</font></a> underworkers <a name="fr_146"></a><a href="#fn_146"><font size="1">3</font></a>; and they <a name="fr_147"></a><a href="#fn_147"><font size="1">4</font></a> have ministers and warriors <a name="fr_148"></a><a href="#fn_148"><font size="1">5</font></a> too.</p>
 <p>And they <a name="fr_149"></a><a href="#fn_149"><font size="1">6</font></a> in [everlasting] congress with them <a name="fr_150"></a><a href="#fn_150"><font size="1">7</font></a> speed on their course in æther floating, fullfilling [all] its <a name="fr_151"></a><a href="#fn_151"><font size="1">8</font></a> space, so that there is no space above empty of stars.</p>
 <p>They are the cosmic engine of the universe, <a name="fr_152"></a><a href="#fn_152"><font size="1">9</font></a> having their own peculiar action, which is subordinate, however, to the action of the Thirty-six,—from whom throughout [all] lands arise the deaths of [all] the other lives <a name="fr_153"></a><a href="#fn_153"><font size="1">10</font></a> with souls, and hosts of [lesser] lives that spoil the fruit.</p>
 <p>8. And under them <a name="fr_154"></a><a href="#fn_154"><font size="1">11</font></a> is what is called the</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_51"><font size="1" color="green">p. 51</font></a></p>
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Bear, <a name="fr_155"></a><a href="#fn_155"><font size="1">1</font></a>—just in the middle of the Circle of the Animals, <a name="fr_156"></a><a href="#fn_156"><font size="1">2</font></a> composed of seven stars, and with another corresponding [Bear] <a name="fr_157"></a><a href="#fn_157"><font size="1">3</font></a> above its head.</p>
 <p>Its energy is as it were an axles, setting nowhere and nowhere rising, but stopping [ever] in the self-same space, and turning round the same, giving its proper motion <a name="fr_158"></a><a href="#fn_158"><font size="1">4</font></a> to the Life-producing Circle, <a name="fr_159"></a><a href="#fn_159"><font size="1">5</font></a> and handing over this whole universe from night to day, from day to night.</p>
 <p>And after this <a name="fr_160"></a><a href="#fn_160"><font size="1">6</font></a> there is another choir of stars, to which we have not thought it proper to give names; but they who will come after us, <a name="fr_161"></a><a href="#fn_161"><font size="1">7</font></a> in imitation, will give them names themselves. <a name="fr_162"></a><a href="#fn_162"><font size="1">8</font></a></p>
 <p>9. Again, below the Moon, are other stars, <a name="fr_163"></a><a href="#fn_163"><font size="1">9</font></a> corruptible, deprived of energy, which hold together for a little while, in that theyve been exhaled out of the earth itself into the air above the earth,—which ever are being broken up, in that they have a nature like unto [that of] useless lives on earth, which come into existence for no other purpose than to die,—such as the tribe of flies, and fleas, and worms, and other things like them.</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_52"><font size="1" color="green">p. 52</font></a></p>
 <p>For these are useful, Tat, neither to us nor to the world; but, on the contrary, they trouble and annoy, being natures by-products, <a name="fr_164"></a><a href="#fn_164"><font size="1">1</font></a> which owe their birth to her extravagance. <a name="fr_165"></a><a href="#fn_165"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>
 <p>Just in the same way, too, the stars exhaled from earth do not attain the upper space.</p>
 <p>They cannot do so, since they are sent forth from below; and, owing to the greatness of their weight, dragged down by their own matter, they quickly are dispersed, and, breaking up, fall back again on earth, affecting nothing but the mere disturbance of the air about the earth.</p>
 <p>10. There is another class, O Tat, that of the so-called long-haired [stars], <a name="fr_166"></a><a href="#fn_166"><font size="1">3</font></a> appearing at their proper times, and after a short time, becoming once again invisible;—they neither rise nor set nor are they broken up.</p>
 <p>These are the brilliant messengers and heralds of the general destinies of things <a name="fr_167"></a><a href="#fn_167"><font size="1">4</font></a> that are to be.</p>
 <p>They occupy the space below the Circle of the Sun.</p>
 <p>When, then, some chance is going to happen to the world, [comets] appear, and, shining for some days, again return behind <a name="fr_168"></a><a href="#fn_168"><font size="1">5</font></a> the Circle of the Sun, and stay invisible,—some showing in</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_53"><font size="1" color="green">p. 53</font></a></p>
 <p>the east, some in the north, some in the west, and others in the south. We call them Prophets. <a name="fr_169"></a><a href="#fn_169"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>
 <p>11. Such is the nature of the stars. The stars, however, differ from the star-groups. <a name="fr_170"></a><a href="#fn_170"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>
 <p>The stars are they which sail <a name="fr_171"></a><a href="#fn_171"><font size="1">3</font></a> in heaven; the star-groups, on the contrary, are fixed in heavens frame, <a name="fr_172"></a><a href="#fn_172"><font size="1">4</font></a> and they are borne along together with the heaven,—Twelve out of which we call the Zōdia. <a name="fr_173"></a><a href="#fn_173"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>
 <p>He who knows these can form some notion clearly of [what] God is; and, if one should dare say so, becoming [thus] a seer for himself, [so] contemplate Him, and, contemplating Him, be blessed.</p>
 <p>12. <i>Tat</i>. Blessèd, in truth, is he, O father [mine], who contemplateth Him.</p>
 <p><i>Her</i>. But tis impossible, O son, that one in body <a name="fr_174"></a><a href="#fn_174"><font size="1">6</font></a> should have this good chance.</p>
 <p>Moreover, he should train his soul beforehand, here and now, that when it reacheth there, [the space] where it is possible for it to contemplate, it may not miss its way.</p>
 <p>But men who love their bodies,—such men will never contemplate the Vision of the Beautiful and Good.</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_54"><font size="1" color="green">p. 54</font></a></p>
 <p>For what, O son, is that [fair] Beauty which hath no form nor any colour, nor any mass? <a name="fr_175"></a><a href="#fn_175"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>
 <p><i>Tat</i>. Can there be aught thats beautiful apart from these?</p>
 <p><i>Her</i>. God only, O [my] son; or rather that which is still greater,—the [proper] name of God.</p>
 <p></p><hr> 
 <p></p><h4 align="center">COMMENTARY</h4>
 <p>The earlier editors of Stobæus (apparently following the mistake of Patrizzi) have Asclepius instead of Tat as the second person of the dialogue, which is clearly wrong according to the text itself (see the first sentence given to Hermes, and §§ 9 and 10). <a name="fr_176"></a><a href="#fn_176"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>
 <p>The excerpt is from a sermon in the Collection to Tat. It belongs to the further explanation of things referred to only generally in the General Sermons; it is, therefore, again probably from one of the Expository Sermons, in which series already a sermon has been given on the Zodiacal Twelve and on the Seven Spheres.</p>
 <p>Seeing also that it is stated that this sermon is “most authoritative and the chiefest of them all,” we must suppose that it came at the end of one of the Books of the Expository Sermons.</p>
 <p>We seem to have the beginning of the sermon, but not the end, for Stobæus breaks off in an aimless and provoking fashion in the midst of a subject.</p>
 <p>For a list of the Egyptian names of the Decans, with their Greek transcriptions and symbols, see Budge, <i>Gods of the Egyptians</i>, ii. 304-308.</p>
