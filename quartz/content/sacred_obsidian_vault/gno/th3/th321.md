 <p></p><h4 align="center">EXCERPT XX.</h4>
 <p></p><hr> 
 <p></p><h3 align="center">[THE POWER OF CHOICE]</h3>
 <p><small>(Patrizzi (p. 42) runs this on to Ex. xix. without a break.</small></p>
 <p><small>Text: Stob., <i>Ethica</i>, vii. 31, under heading: “Of Hermes”; G. (ii.) pp. 654, 655; M. ii. 100, 101; W. ii. 160, 161.</small></p>
 <p><small>Ménard, Livre IV., No. i. of “Fragments Divers,” pp. 271, 272.)</small></p>
 <p><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">There</span> is, then, essence, reason, thought, <a name="fr_277"></a><a href="#fn_277"><font size="1">1</font></a> perception. <a name="fr_278"></a><a href="#fn_278"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>
 <p>Opinion and sensation move towards perception; reason directs itself towards essence; and thought sends itself forth through its own self.</p>
 <p>And thought is interwoven with perception, and entering into one another they become one form,—which is that of the Soul [itself].</p>
 <p>Opinion and sensation move towards the Souls perception; but they do not remain in the same state. Hence is there excess, and falling short, and difference with them.</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_85"><font size="1" color="green">p. 85</font></a></p>
 <p>When they are drawn away from the perception, they deteriorate; but when they follow it and are obedient, they share in the perceptive reason through the sciences. <a name="fr_279"></a><a href="#fn_279"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>
 <p>2. We have the power to choose; it is within our power to choose the better, and in like way [to choose] the worse, according to our will. <a name="fr_280"></a><a href="#fn_280"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>
 <p>And if [our] choice clings to the evil things, it doth consort with the corporeal nature; [and] for this cause Fate rules oer him who makes this choice.</p>
 <p>Since, then, the intellectual essence <a name="fr_281"></a><a href="#fn_281"><font size="1">3</font></a> in us is absolutely free,—[namely] the reason that embraces all in thought,—and that it ever is a law unto itself and self-identical, on this account Fate does not reach it. <a name="fr_282"></a><a href="#fn_282"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>
 <p>Thus furnishing it first from the First God, it <a name="fr_283"></a><a href="#fn_283"><font size="1">5</font></a> sent forth the perceptive reason, and the whole reason which Nature hath appointed unto them that come to birth.</p>
 <p>With these the Soul consorting, consorteth with their fates, though [in herself] she hath no part [or lot] in their fates nature.</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_86"><font size="1" color="green">p. 86</font></a></p>
 <p><small>(Patrizzi (p. 42) adds the following to the preceding; it is not found in Stobæus, and appears to be a scholium.)</small></p>
 <p>What is necessitated by the interwoven harmony <a name="fr_284"></a><a href="#fn_284"><font size="1">1</font></a> of [all] the parts, in no way differs from that which is fated.</p>
 <p></p><hr> 
 <p></p><h4 align="center">COMMENT</h4>
 <p>I have supplied a temporary heading for the sake of uniformity. Our extract, however, seems to be taken from a lengthy treatise, and was probably one of the Sermons to Tat.</p>
