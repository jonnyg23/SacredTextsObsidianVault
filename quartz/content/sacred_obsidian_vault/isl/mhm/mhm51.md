 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">The Maqámát of Badí‘ al-Zamán al-Hamadhání, tr. W.J. Prendergast [1915] at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">XLIII. THE MAQAMA OF THE DINAR</h3>  
 <p>‘ÍSÁ IBN HISHÁM related to us and said: I happened to have made a vow to give a dinar in charity to the greatest mendicant in Baghdad. I enquired for him and I was directed to Abú’l Fatḥ al-Iskanderí. So I went to him to bestow it upon him, and I found him amongst some companions who had gathered in a circle around him. So I said: 'O sons of Sásán, which of you knows his stock-in-trade best and is the sharpest in his art so that I may give him this dinar?' Said al-Iskanderí: 'I am.' Another of the company said: 'Nay, but I am.' Then they wrangled and disputed till I said: 'Let each of you revile his fellow, then whoever gains the mastery carries off the booty, and whoever overcomes takes the spoil.' <a name="fr_980"></a><a href="#fn_980"><font size="1">2</font></a> So al-Iskanderí said: 'O cold of the old woman! <a name="fr_981"></a><a href="#fn_981"><font size="1">3</font></a> O sultriness of Tammuz! <a name="fr_982"></a><a href="#fn_982"><font size="1">4</font></a> O</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_980"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_980">164:2</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>Whoever overcomes takes the spoil:</i> Freytag, <i>Arab Proverbs</i>, i, 677, Cf. Hebrew ‏בָן‎ spoil.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_981"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_981">164:3</a> <i>Cold of the old woman:</i> That is, the four last days of February and three first days of March, thus called because they are the latter part (<font color="GREEN">…</font>) of winter.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_982"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_982">164:4</a> <i>Tammuz:</i> The Syrian month sacred in ancient times to the god of that name, corresponding to July. This god is mentioned in Ezekiel, viii, 14.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_983"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_983">165:1</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>Al-Kúz:</i> A water-bottle, a goglet, arabicized from the Persian <font color="GREEN">…</font>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_984"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_984">165:2</a> <i>Conversation of the singers:</i> Obviously it is the singing of the singers and not their conversation that people want to hear.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_985"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_985">165:3</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>Oppression of the nightmare:</i> Some think this is not an Arabic word and that the proper word is <font color="GREEN">…</font> (Lane, p. 2588). I see no difficulty, however, in evolving this meaning from the root <font color="GREEN">…</font> he pressed or squeezed.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_986"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_986">165:4</a> <i>Ummu Ḥubein:</i> A species of stinking lizard.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_987"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_987">165:5</a> <i>Zaqqum:</i> A certain kind of tree having small leaves, evil-smelling and bitter, found in Tehameh, also the name of the infernal tree whose fruit is the food of the people of hell. See Baiḍáwí, ii, 172.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_988"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_988">165:6</a> <i>O peg of the tent:</i> Cf. more dishevelled than a tent peg. Freytag, <i>Arab Proverbs</i>, i, 706.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_989"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_989">165:7</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>Prop:</i> Literally, the handle of the upper millstone,</p>
 <p><a name="fn_990"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_990">165:8</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>Non-recurring Wednesday:</i> The unlucky Wednesdays of the month, to which this is an allusion, are those which have the number four, e.g. the fourth or the fourteenth of the month; the fourteenth or twenty-fourth, or the fourth before the end of the month. Mas‘údí, <i>Les Prairies D’or</i>, iii, 422. Freytag, <i>Arab Proverbs</i>, i, 276 and Meidaní (Bulak edition), i, 139, when it comes at the end of the month.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_991"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_991">165:9</a> <i>O intercession of the naked:</i> That is, one who is so utterly destitute that he needs to ask for himself, not for others. For the opposite sentiment, see <i>Aghání</i>, viii, 182.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_992"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_992">165:10</a> <i>O Saturday of the children:</i> Succeeding the holiday on Friday, Cf. English school slang, Black Monday.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_993"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_993">165:11</a> <i>O letter of condolence!</i> Because it is supposed to be a very difficult thing <a name="page_166"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 166</font></a> to write if the deceased is not a near relative, or because it is a painful thing for one who is bereaved to read.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_994"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_994">166:1</a> <i>Stinginess of the man of Ahwaz:</i> The people of Ahwaz were notorious for their avarice, stupidity and the vileness of their inclinations. Yaqút, i, 411, 12.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_995"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_995">166:2</a> <i>Garrulousness of the man of Rayy:</i> The word <font color="GREEN">…</font> also means meddlesomeness, or immoderation of any kind, but I have not been able to find any evidence that the people of Rayy were notorious for any of these things.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_996"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_996">166:3</a> <i>Arwand:</i> or Elwand, the Orontes of the ancients, at the foot of which lies the town of Hamadhán.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_997"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_997">166:4</a> <i>Demawand:</i> A mountain north of Teheran. His feet would thus be more than two hundred miles apart!</p>
 <p><a name="fn_998"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_998">166:5</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>Pulse:</i> Arabicized from the Persian <font color="GREEN">…</font> Sanskrit másha, peas.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_999"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_999">166:6</a> <i>O fumes of naphtha!:</i> Apparently a genuine Arabic word from <font color="GREEN">…</font>. It (water) welled or issued forth, and <font color="GREEN">…</font> what oozes or exudes from a mountain as though it were sweat from the sides of the rock. (Lane's Lexicon art. <font color="GREEN">…</font> p. 2759) Cf. Greek ναφθα which is probably a loan word from Arabic.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1000"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_1000">166:7</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>In a state of fasting:</i> Anything eaten or drunk in a state of fasting. The text is wrongly vocalized, for <font color="GREEN">…</font> read <font color="GREEN">…</font>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1001"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_1001">166:8</a> <i>Shaker of the bone:</i> That is, ague.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1002"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_1002">166:9</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>A fals:</i> A small copper coin, the forty-eighth part of a dirhem, i.e. about half a farthing. A loan word from Aramaic.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1003"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_1003">167:1</a> <i>O such and such:</i> Or 'so and so', referring to something too gross to mention. Cf. <i>Ḥarírí</i>, p. 235, line 3., and see Wright's Grammar, i, 268.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1004"></a><a href="mhm51.htm#fr_1004">167:2</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>A weaver:</i> The vocation of the weaver appears to have been regarded by the Arabs as a degrading one. Cf. Letters, p. 273, 'Verily shaving is learnt on the heads of the weavers ', also Yaqút, <i>Geographical Dictionary</i>, iv, 1036. Cf. Ḥarírí, p. 31, on the subject of the dinar, and p. 628 for an example of similar mutual abuse; also see Horace, <i>Satires</i>, Book I, Satire 7.</p>  
 <p>This maqáma contains no poetry.</p>
