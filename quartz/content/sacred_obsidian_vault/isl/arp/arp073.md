 <p><a name="page_146"><font size="1" color="green">p. 146</font></a></p>  <h4 align="center">A SERENADE</h4>  <h5 align="center">TO HIS SLEEPING MISTRESS.</h5>  <h5 align="center">BY ALI BEN ABD ALGANY, OF CORDOVA.</h5>  
 <p>SURE Harut's <a name="fr_23"></a><a href="#fn_23"><font size="1">*</font></a> potent spells were breathed<br>   Upon that magic sword, thine eye;<br> For if it wounds us thus while sheathed,<br>   When drawn ’tis vain its edge to fly.</p>  
 <p>How canst thou doom me, cruel fair,<br>   Plunged in the hell <a name="fr_24"></a><a href="#fn_24"><font size="1">†</font></a> of scorn, to groan?<br> No idol e’er this heart could share<br>   This heart has worshipped thee alone.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_23"></a><a href="arp073.htm#fr_23">146:*</a> A wicked angel, who is permitted to tempt mankind by teaching them magic: see the legend respecting him in Sale's Korān.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_24"></a><a href="arp073.htm#fr_24">146:†</a> The poet here alludes to the punishments denounced in the Koran against those who worship a plurality of gods: "their couch shall be in hell, and over them shall be coverings of fire." Sur. 2.</p><p></p><hr>
