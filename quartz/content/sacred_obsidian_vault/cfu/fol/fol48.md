 <p><a name="page_77"><font size="1" color="green">p. 77</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">LIU TZU-HUI</h3>  <h4 align="center">CIRCA A.D. 1100</h4>  <h3 align="center">LISTENING TO THE HARP</h3>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px"><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Night</span> and the midway moon. Some hidden lute<br> Sounds from a silken alcove. As the wind<br> Swells and recedes the lutany now swells,<br> Now falters. Now commingled with the clouds<br> It throbs betwixt the earth and moon. Almost<br> Like gentle mockery the echoes fall<br> As laughter breaks on tears. The hand that sways  
 <p>The crowded chords I see not, but the heart<br> Made visible by music far away<br> Spells me her dreams. Ah! mourns she not the tryst<br> New made and newly broken, but the old<br> Lost love of long ago. Her melodies<br> Are secret sorrows welling through the lute—<br> Are captive nightingales escaped in song.</p></div>  
 <p><a name="page_78"><font size="1" color="green">p. 78</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">One touch of the chords, and snow-flakes scatter round<br> One, and the flowing brightness of the sun<br> Passes. Perchance she grieves that few may hear<br> And understand. The floating dust collects<br> Beneath her silver nail-tips. Lone is she<br> As orphan phœnix calling, with whose plaint<br> The songs of all birds fail to harmonise.</div>
