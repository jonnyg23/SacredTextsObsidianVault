 <p><a name="page_424"><font size="1" color="green">p. 424</font></a> <a name="page_425"><font size="1" color="green">p. 425</font></a></p>  <h1>Glossary &amp; Index</h1>  <h2>The Pronunciation of Chinese Words</h2>  
 <p>During the course of Chinese history the restriction of intercourse due to mountain-chains or other natural obstacles between various tribes or divisions of the Chinese people led to the birth of a number of families of languages, which again became the parents of numerous local dialects. These dialects have in most cases restricted ranges, so that that of one district may be partially or wholly unintelligible to the natives of another situated at a distance of only a hundred miles or less.</p>  
 <p>The Court or Government language is that spoken in Peking and the metropolitan district, and is the language of official communication throughout the country. Though neither the oldest nor the purest Chinese dialect, it seems destined more than any other to come into universal use in China. The natives of each province or district will of course continue to speak to each other in their own particular dialect, and foreign missionaries or merchants, for example, whose special duties or transactions are connected with special districts will naturally learn and use the dialects of those districts; but as a means of intercommunication generally between natives of different provinces, or between natives and foreigners, the Court language seems likely to continue in use and to spread more and more over the whole country. It is to this that the following remarks apply.</p>  
 <p>The essentials of correct pronunciation of Chinese are accuracy of sound, tone, and rhythm.</p>  <h2>Sound</h2>  
 <p>Vowels and Diphthongs</p>  
 <p><i>a</i> as in <i>father</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ai</i> as in Italian <i>amái</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ao</i>. Italian <i>ao</i> in <i>Aosta</i>: sometimes <i>á-oo,</i> the <i>au</i> in <i>cauto</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>e</i> in <i>eh</i>, <i>en</i>, as in <i>yet</i>, <i>lens</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ei</i>. Nearly <i>ey</i> in <i>grey</i>, but more as in Italian <i>lei</i>, <i>contei</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ê</i>. The vowel-sound in <i>lurk</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>êi</i>. The foregoing <i>ê</i> followed enclitically by <i>y</i>. <i>Money</i> without the <i>n</i> = <i>mêi</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>êrh.</i> The <i>urr</i> in <i>purr</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>i</i>. As a single or final syllable the vowel-sound in <i>ease</i>, <i>tree</i>; in <i>ih</i>, <i>in</i>, <i>ing</i>, as in <i>chick</i>, <i>thing</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ia</i> generally as in the Italian <i>Maria</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>iai</i>. The <i>iai</i> in the Italian <i>vecchiaia</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>iao</i> as in <i>ia</i> and <i>ao</i>, with the terminal peculiarity of the latter.</p>  
 <p><i>ie</i> as in the Italian <i>siesta</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>io</i>. The French <i>io</i> in <i>pioche</i>. <a name="page_426"><font size="1" color="green">p. 426</font></a></p>  
 <p><i>iu</i> as a final, longer than the English <i>ew</i>. In <i>liu, niu</i>, almost <i>leyew, neyew</i>. In <i>chiung, hsiung, iung</i>, is <i>eeyong</i> (<i>ō</i> in <i>roll</i>).</p>  
 <p><i>o.</i> Between vowel-sound in <i>awe</i> and that in <i>roll</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ou.</i> Really <i>êō</i>; <i>ou</i> in <i>round</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ü.</i> The vowel-sound in the French <i>tu, eût</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>üa.</i> Only in <i>üan</i>, which in some tones is <i>üen</i>. The <i>ū</i> as above; the <i>an</i> as in <i>antic</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>üe</i>. The vowel-sounds in the French <i>tu es</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>üo</i>. A disputed sound, used, if at all, interchangeably with <i>io</i> in certain syllables.</p>  
 <p><i>u</i>. The <i>oo</i> in <i>too</i>; in <i>un</i> and <i>ung</i> as in the Italian <i>punto</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ua</i>. Nearly <i>ooa</i>, in many instances contracting to <i>wa</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>uai</i> as in the Italian <i>guai</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>uei.</i> The vowel-sounds in the French <i>jouer</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>uê.</i> Only in final <i>uên</i> = <i>ú-ŭn</i>; frequently <i>wên</i> or <i>wun</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ui.</i> The vowel-sounds in <i>screwy</i>; in some tones <i>uei</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>uo.</i> The Italian <i>uo</i> in <i>fuori</i>; often <i>wo</i>, and at times nearly <i>ŏō</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ŭ.</i> Between the <i>i</i> in <i>bit</i> and the <i>u</i> in <i>shut</i>.</p>  
 <p>Consonants</p>  
 <p><i>ch</i> as in <i>chair</i>; but before <i>ih</i> softened to <i>dj</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ch’</i>. A strong breathing. <i>Mu</i>ch-ha<i>rm</i> without the italicized letters = <i>ch’a</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>f</i> as in farm.</p>  
 <p><i>h</i> as <i>ch</i> in Scotch <i>loch</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>hs</i>. A slight aspirate preceding and modifying the sibilant, which is, however, the stronger of the two consonants; <i>e.g. hsing</i> = <i>hissing</i> without the first <i>i</i>,</p>  
 <p><i>j</i>. Nearly the French <i>j</i> in <i>jaune</i>; the English <i>s</i> in <i>fusion</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>k</i>. <i>c</i> in <i>car</i>, <i>k</i> in <i>king</i>; but when following other sounds often softened to <i>g</i> in <i>go, gate</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>k’</i>. The aspirate as in <i>ch’</i>. <i>Ki</i>ck-ha<i>rd</i> without the italicized letters = <i>k’a</i>; and <i>ki</i>ck-he<i>r</i> == <i>k’ê</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>l</i> as in English.</p>  
 <p><i>m</i> as in English.</p>  
 <p><i>n</i> as in English.</p>  
 <p><i>ng</i>. The italicized letters in the French mo<i>n ga</i>lant = <i>nga</i>; mo<i>n gai</i>llard = <i>ngai</i>; so<i>n go</i>sier = <i>ngo</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>p</i> as in English.</p>  
 <p><i>p’</i> The Irish pronunciation of <i>p</i>arty, <i>p</i>arliament. <i>Sla</i>p-ha<i>rd</i> without the italicized letters = <i>p’a</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>s</i> as in English.</p>  
 <p><i>sh</i> as in English.</p>  
 <p><i>ss</i>. Only in <i>ssŭ</i>. The object of employing <i>ss</i> is to fix attention on the peculiar vowel-sound <i>ŭ</i> (see above).</p>  
 <p><i>t</i> as in English.</p>  
 <p><i>t’</i> The Irish <i>t</i> in <i>t</i>orment. <i>Hi</i>t-ha<i>rd</i> without the italicized letters = <i>t’a</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ts</i> as in <i>jetsam</i>; after another word softened to <i>ds</i> in <i>gladsome</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>ts’.</i> The aspirate intervening, as in <i>ch’</i>, etc. <i>Be</i>ts-ha<i>rd</i> without the italicized letters = <i>ts’a</i>. <a name="page_427"><font size="1" color="green">p. 427</font></a></p>  
 <p><i>tz</i>. Employed to mark the peculiarity of the final <i>ŭ</i>; hardly of greater power than <i>ts</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>tz’</i> like <i>ts’</i>. This, <i>tz</i>, and <i>ss</i> used only before <i>ŭ</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>w</i> as in English; but very faint, or even non-existent, before <i>ü</i>.</p>  
 <p><i>y</i> as in English; but very faint before <i>i</i> or <i>ü</i>.</p>  <h2>Tone</h2>  
 <p>The correct pronunciation of the sound (<i>yin</i>) is not sufficient to make a Chinese spoken word intelligible. Unless the tone (<i>shêng</i>), or musical note, is simultaneously correctly given, either the wrong meaning or no meaning at all will be conveyed. The tone is the key in which the voice is pitched. Accent is a ‘song added to,’ and tone is emphasized accent. The number of these tones differs in the different dialects. In Pekingese there are now four. They are best indicated in transliteration by numbers added to the sound, thus:</p>  
 <p><i>pa</i> (1) <i>pa</i> (2) <i>pa</i> (3) <i>pa</i> (4)</p>  
 <p>To say, for example, <i>pa</i> (3) instead of <i>pa</i> (1) would be as great a mistake as to say ‘grasp’ instead of ‘trumpet.’ Correctness of tone cannot be learnt except by oral instruction.</p>  <h2>Rhythm</h2>  
 <p>What tone is to the individual sound rhythm is to the sentence. This also, together with proper appreciation of the mutual modifications of tone and rhythm, can be correctly acquired only by oral instruction.</p>  <h2>A</h2>
