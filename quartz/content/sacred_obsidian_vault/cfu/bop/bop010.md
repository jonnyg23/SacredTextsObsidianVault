 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">VII</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>T‘u Chüeh;</i> allusive, or narrative. Praise of a rabbit catcher as fit to be a prince's mate.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>Careful he sets his rabbit nets all round;<br> Clang, clang his blows upon the pegs resound.<br> Stalwart the man and bold! his bearing all<br> Shows he might be his prince's shield and wall.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>Careful he is his rabbit nets to place,<br> Where many paths of rabbits’ feet bear trace.<br> Stalwart the man and bold! ‘tis plain to see<br> He to his prince companion good would be.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>Careful he is his rabbit nets to spread,<br> Where in the forest's depth the trees give shade.<br> Stalwart the man and bold! fit his the part<br> Guide to his prince to be, and faithful heart.</p></div>
