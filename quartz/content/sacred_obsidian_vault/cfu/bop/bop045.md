 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">XVII</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Ching Nü;</i> narrative. A gentleman deplores his disappointment in not being met by a lady according to engagement, and celebrates her gifts and beauty.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>O sweet maiden, so fair and retiring,<br>   At the corner I'm waiting for you;<br> And I'm scratching my head, and inquiring<br>   What on earth it were best I should do.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>Oh! the maiden, so handsome and coy,<br>   For a pledge gave a slim rosy reed.<br> Than the reed is she brighter, my joy;<br>   On her loveliness how my thoughts feed!</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>In the pastures a <i>t‘i</i> blade she sought,<br>   And she gave it, so elegant, rare.<br> Oh! the grass does not dwell in my thought,<br>   But the donor, more elegant, fair.</p></div>
