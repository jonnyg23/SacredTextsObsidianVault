 <p><a name="page_275"><font size="1" color="green">p. 275</font></a></p>  <h4 align="center">II</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Wu Chiang Ta Ch‘ê</i>; narrative. Some officer, overloaded in the king's service, thinks it better to try and dismiss his troubles from his mind.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>Push not the cart you stand behind;—<br>   You'll only raise the dust. Nor dwell<br> On your anxieties of mind;—<br>   You'll only make yourself unwell.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>Push not the cart you stand behind;—<br>   The dust will only blind your view.<br> Dwell not on things that vex your mind;—<br>   You never thus can see them true.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>Push not the cart you stand behind;—<br>   The dust will but becloud your eyes.<br> Heed not the troubles of your mind;—<br>   ’Twill weight you as you seek to rise.</p></div>
