 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">IX</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The<i> Ta Ch‘ê;</i> narrative. The influence of a severe and virtuous magistrate in repressing licentiousness.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>He rolls along in carriage grand,<br>   His robes are bright with green,—<br> His robes of rank, as on the bank<br>   The tender sedge is seen.<br> Thinking I always am of thee,<br>   Thinking with fond desire;<br> But dreading his severity,<br>   I must repress the fire.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>With slow and heavy sound his car,—<br>   His car of state, moves on.<br> O’er his dress spread, the colors red<br>   Shine like carnation stone.<br> Thinking I always am of thee;<br>   The fondest thoughts have I.<br> The fear of him alone holds me,<br>   Or to thine arms I'd fly. <a name="page_84"><font size="1" color="green">p. 84</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>Our fate may be, while still alive,<br>   Always apart to dwell;<br> But when we're dead, we shall be laid<br>   In the same earthen cell.<br> If haply thou should’st say that I<br>   Am not in this sincere,<br> I swear its truth by that day's eye,<br>   Whose piercing glance I fear.</p></div>
