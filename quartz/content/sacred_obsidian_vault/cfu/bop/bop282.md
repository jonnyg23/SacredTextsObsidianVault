 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">VIII</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Shih Mai;</i> narrative. Appropriate to King Wu's sacrificing to Heaven, and to the spirits of all the hills and rivers, on a progress through the kingdom, after the overthrow of the Shang dynasty.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;">Now through his many states rides Wu,<br>   A royal progress making.<br> May Heaven its gracious favor show,<br>   Him for its own son taking!<br> Most surely has the house of Chou<br>   Been called to reign by Heaven.<br> To our Wu's slightest nod by all<br>   Trembling response is given. <a name="page_439"><font size="1" color="green">p. 439</font></a><br> All spirits to himself he draws,<br>   O’er sacred rites presiding;<br> Even the spirits of the Ho,<br>   And those on hills abiding.<br> Yes, he our sovereign lord is known<br>   As king supreme and glorious,<br> Our house of Chou distinguished is;<br>   ’Tis brilliant and victorious.<br> Wu to each prince his rank assigns;<br>   And now sweet peace enjoying,<br> He casts the spear and shield aside,<br>   The bow no more employing.<br> I will in ways of virtue walk,<br>   And spread it through our regions.<br> Thus shall the king preserve the throne,<br>   Without the aid of legions.</div>
