   <h3 align="center">PRA<i>S</i>NA I, ADHYÂYA 3, KA<i>N</i><i>D</i>IKÂ 5.</h3>  
 <p>1. Now (follow the duties) of a Snâtaka. <a name="fr_801"></a><a href="#fn_801"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_159"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 159</font></a></p>  
 <p>2. He shall wear a lower garment and upper garment. <a name="fr_802"></a><a href="#fn_802"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>3. Let him carry a staff made of bamboo,</p>  
 <p>4. And a pot filled with water.</p>  
 <p>5. Let him wear two sacrificial threads.</p>  
 <p>6. (He shall possess) a turban, an upper garment (consisting of) a skin, shoes, and a parasol. (He shall keep) a sacred fire and (offer) the new and full moon (Sthâlîpâkas). <a name="fr_803"></a><a href="#fn_803"><font size="1">6</font></a></p>  
 <p>7. He shall cause the hair of his head, of his beard, and of his body, and his nails to be cut on the Parva days. <a name="fr_804"></a><a href="#fn_804"><font size="1">7</font></a></p>  
 <p>8. His livelihood (he shall obtain in the following manner): <a name="fr_805"></a><a href="#fn_805"><font size="1">8</font></a></p>  
 <p>9. Let him beg uncooked (food) from Brâhma<i>n</i>as, Kshatriyas, Vai<i>s</i>yas, or carpenters, <a name="fr_806"></a><a href="#fn_806"><font size="1">9</font></a></p>  
 <p>10. Or (cooked) food (even from many). <a name="fr_807"></a><a href="#fn_807"><font size="1">10</font></a></p>  
 <p>11. Let him remain silent (when he goes to beg).</p>  
 <p>12. Let him perform with that all Pâkaya<i>g</i><i>ñ</i>as, offered to the gods and manes, and the rites, securing welfare. <a name="fr_808"></a><a href="#fn_808"><font size="1">12</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_160"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 160</font></a></p>  
 <p>13. Baudhâyana declares that by (following) this rule the most excellent sages reach the highest abode of Pra<i>g</i>âpati Paramesh<i>th</i>in. <a name="fr_809"></a><a href="#fn_809"><font size="1">13</font></a></p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_801"></a><a href="sbe1438.htm#fr_801">158:1</a> <b>5</b>. Regarding the term Snâtaka, see Âpastamba I, 11, 30, 1-4. Govinda thinks that the following rules are intended to apply In the first instance to a student who has performed the Samâvartana on completion of his studentship and lives unmarried at home. For though the Sm<i>ri</i>ti declares it necessary for a student to enter, on completing his term, at once into one of the remaining three <a name="page_159"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 159</font></a> orders, it may happen; as the commentator observes, that the Snâtaka's marriage cannot take place immediately. The correctness of this view is proved by Âpastamba I, 2, 8, and by the fact that below, II, 3, 5, the rules for a married Snâtaka are given separately.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_802"></a><a href="sbe1438.htm#fr_802">159:2</a>-5. Vasish<i>th</i>a XII, 14.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_803"></a><a href="sbe1438.htm#fr_803">159:6</a> Âpastamba I, 2, 8, 2.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_804"></a><a href="sbe1438.htm#fr_804">159:7</a> Regarding the Parva days, see Vasish<i>th</i>a XII, 21 note.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_805"></a><a href="sbe1438.htm#fr_805">159:8</a> Vasish<i>th</i>a XII, 2-4. 'Though the Snâtaka is the subject of the discussion, the word "his" is used (in this Sûtra) in order to introduce the remaining duties of a householder also.'--Govinda.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_806"></a><a href="sbe1438.htm#fr_806">159:9</a> The carpenter (rathakâra) is a <i>S</i>ûdra, but connected with the Vedic sacrifices.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_807"></a><a href="sbe1438.htm#fr_807">159:10</a> '"Food" (bhaiksham), i.e. a quantity of begged food. The meaning is that in times of distress he may beg from many.'--Govinda.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_808"></a><a href="sbe1438.htm#fr_808">159:12</a> With that, i.e. with the food obtained by begging. Regarding <a name="page_160"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 160</font></a> the Pâkaya<i>g</i><i>ñ</i>as, see Gautama VIII, 18. Govinda gives as an instance of the rites securing welfare (bhûtikarmâ<i>n</i>i) the âyushya<i>k</i>aru, a rice-offering intended to procure long life.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_809"></a><a href="sbe1438.htm#fr_809">160:13</a> Govinda explains Baudhâyana by Kâ<i>n</i>vâyana, and adds that either the author speaks of himself in the third person or a pupil must have compiled the book.</p>
