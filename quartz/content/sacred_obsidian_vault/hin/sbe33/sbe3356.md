 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="../../img/tn_hin.jpg"></a><br><i>The Minor Law Books (SBE33)</i>, by Julius Jolly, [1889], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">VI. ORDEALS.</h3>  
 <p>1. <a name="fr_758"></a><a href="#fn_757"><font size="1">1</font></a> Let (the defendant) touch the heads of his sons, wife, or friends; or else the (ordeal by) sacred libation (may be performed), whatever the nature of the charge may be.</p>  
 <p>2. <a name="fr_759"></a><a href="#fn_758"><font size="1">2</font></a> It is on the claimant that the duty of declaring his readiness to take on himself the penalty (to be awarded to the losing party) devolves in every case. Or the ordeal may be performed by either party at pleasure, the other party consenting to give the penalty (to be awarded in case of defeat).</p>  
 <p>3. <a name="fr_760"></a><a href="#fn_759"><font size="1">3</font></a> To persons suspected by the king, or denounced (as criminals) by (intercourse with) robbers, or intent on their own justification, an ordeal must be administered without binding (an opponent) to give the penalty.</p>  
 <p>4. <a name="fr_761"></a><a href="#fn_760"><font size="1">4</font></a> (The performance of) an ordeal is ordained in</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_248"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 248</font></a></p>  
 <p>important cases, when people are engaged in a controversy; an ordeal must not be administered when there is no one ready to take the punishment on himself.</p>  
 <p>5. <a name="fr_762"></a><a href="#fn_761"><font size="1">5</font></a> Justice is based on truth, and litigation (depends) on witnesses. When a case admits of divine test, human evidence (the testimony of witnesses) or documents must not be resorted to.</p>  
 <p>6. <a name="fr_763"></a><a href="#fn_762"><font size="1">6</font></a> The (ordeal by) sacred libation has been declared by the wise to be applicable to all (castes), and poison (to all castes), excepting the Brahman (caste). (Either the balance is reserved for Brahmans), or the balance may be administered to (members of) every caste.</p>  
 <p>7. <a name="fr_764"></a><a href="#fn_763"><font size="1">7</font></a> The (ordeal by) sacred libation may be administered in every case. The (ordeal by) balance is admissible in every season.</p>  
 <p>8. <a name="fr_765"></a><a href="#fn_764"><font size="1">8</font></a> Eunuchs, distressed or feeble persons, the severely afflicted, infants, old men, women, and the blind should be tested by the balance always.</p>  
 <p>9. <a name="fr_766"></a><a href="#fn_765"><font size="1">9</font></a> (The ordeal by) poison is not destined for</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_249"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 249</font></a></p>  
 <p>women, nor is (the ordeal by) water fit to be administered to them; it is through (the ordeals by) balance, sacred libation, and others that (the judge) must explore the true state of their minds.</p>  
 <p>10. <a name="fr_767"></a><a href="#fn_766"><font size="1">10</font></a> Let (the judge) test strong men by fire, water, or poison, and let him test infants, old or distressed men by the balance.</p>  
 <p>11. Let (the judge) avoid (the ordeal by) fire in the case of lepers, (the ordeal by) water in the case of the asthmatic, and (the ordeal by) poison in the case of bilious or phlegmatic persons.</p>  
 <p>12. <a name="fr_768"></a><a href="#fn_767"><font size="1">12</font></a> In the season of the rains, let the (ordeal by) fire be administered; also in the cold and chilly seasons. In the summer season, the (ordeal by) water is the proper (kind of ordeal). Poison (is destined) for the cold weather.</p>  
 <p>13. <a name="fr_769"></a><a href="#fn_768"><font size="1">13</font></a> The chilly, cold, and rainy seasons are declared to be (the proper seasons) for the (ordeal by) fire; the autumn and summer seasons, for the (ordeal by) water; the (ordeal by) poison, (is fit) for the cold and chilly seasons.</p>  
 <p>14. <a name="fr_770"></a><a href="#fn_769"><font size="1">14</font></a> The months of <i>K</i>aitra (March-April), Mârga<i>s</i>îrsha (November-December), and Vai<i>s</i>âkha (April-May)</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_250"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 250</font></a></p>  
 <p>are proper months for all (ordeals), and not adverse to any kind of ordeal. (Ordeals must) never (be administered) in the afternoon, nor in the twilight, nor at noon.</p>  
 <p>15. <a name="fr_771"></a><a href="#fn_770"><font size="1">15</font></a> Ordeals administered at an improper place, or at an unsuitable time, or performed at a distance from human habitations, constitute a deviation from the proper course of a lawsuit, this is certain.</p>  
 <p>16. <a name="fr_772"></a><a href="#fn_771"><font size="1">16</font></a> The chief judge must superintend the whole of the proceedings at an ordeal, fasting and obeying the king's instructions in the same way as an Adhvaryu (priest officiates) at a sacrifice.</p>  
 <p>17. <a name="fr_773"></a><a href="#fn_772"><font size="1">17</font></a> The chief judge, who must be a Brahman, thoroughly versed in the Vedas and Vedâṅgas, instructed in sacred learning and of religious conduct, tranquil-minded, unambitious,</p>  
 <p>18. Fond of veracity, pure, able, delighting in the welfare of all sentient beings, having kept a fast, clad in his moist garments (after a bath), having cleansed his teeth, should worship all deities according to rule.</p>  
 <p>19. <a name="fr_774"></a><a href="#fn_773"><font size="1">19</font></a> With red perfumes and garlands, as well as</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_251"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 251</font></a></p>  
 <p>with curds, cakes of flour, fried grain and other (offerings), he should first worship the balance, and then show honour to the others.</p>  
 <p>20. <a name="fr_775"></a><a href="#fn_774"><font size="1">20</font></a> The balance and the other ordeals ordained by the sages should be administered by the king by consent of the claimant, but not otherwise.</p>  
 <p>21. When they are performed otherwise (the claimant not giving his consent), he incurs the same guilt as a thief.</p>  
 <p>22. <a name="fr_776"></a><a href="#fn_775"><font size="1">22</font></a> I will state next the excellent rule regarding the (ordeal by) balance, as the king and the chief judge should administer that ordeal to a man (arraigned in a cause).</p>  
 <p>23. <a name="fr_777"></a><a href="#fn_776"><font size="1">23</font></a> The two posts supporting the beam of the balance should measure four Hastas above ground, their (entire) length should be six Hastas.</p>  
 <p>24. <a name="fr_778"></a><a href="#fn_777"><font size="1">24</font></a> The king should cause a wooden beam of the balance to be made, which must be four Hastas long,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_252"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 252</font></a></p>  
 <p>polished, furnished with the. required characteristics, and having the two scales suspended by both extremities.</p>  
 <p>25. After having caused two posts to be erected, on even ground, which must be placed from north to south, and must be arranged both in one line, he shall cause the beam of the balance to be fastened across (the transverse beam which connects) them.</p>  
 <p>26. With an iron cord let a virtuous man surround the beam in the middle and fasten it in an east-western direction, after having carefully connected it (with the transverse beam).</p>  
 <p>27. <a name="fr_779"></a><a href="#fn_778"><font size="1">27</font></a> The (appointed) examiners have to take care always that the two perpendiculars of the balance should be equal in length. Water must be poured out on (the beam of) the balance by skilled persons.</p>  
 <p>28. If the water does not trickle down (from the balance), the balance may be considered as being level.</p>  
 <p>29. <a name="fr_780"></a><a href="#fn_779"><font size="1">29</font></a> With red perfumes and garlands, as well as with curds, cakes of flour, fried grain and other (offerings, the judge) should first worship the balance, and then show honour to the others.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_253"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 253</font></a></p>  
 <p>30. <a name="fr_781"></a><a href="#fn_780"><font size="1">30</font></a> (The person accused being about to be placed in the scale for the second time, the judge should address the balance as follows): 'Thou, O Balance, hast been created by Brahman, to test the wicked. On account of the syllable dha thou art the image of Dharma (Justice), on account of the syllable <i>t</i>a, thou</p>  
 <p>31. Being used for balancing (dh<i>ri</i>ta, in this ordeal) discoverest the vicious. Therefore thou art called dha<i>t</i>a (balance).'</p>  
 <p>32. <a name="fr_782"></a><a href="#fn_781"><font size="1">32</font></a> If (the person) remains level (sama), he is considered to be in a middling position (samatâ). If he comes down, he loses his cause. He who goes down is not innocent; he is innocent who goes up.</p>  
 <p>33. He who remains level is also not acquitted. These are the three possible cases in the (ordeal by) balance. Thus has been declared the never-failing acquittal (obtainable) through the test by balance.</p>  
 <p>34. <a name="fr_783"></a><a href="#fn_782"><font size="1">34</font></a> When (the scales fixed) at the two extremities of the beam have been moved, when the mark which had been made has come off, when (the balance) is going up and down, being agitated by wind,</p>  
 <p>35. Or when (the man appointed to hold it) lets it</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_254"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 254</font></a></p>  
 <p>go all of a sudden: (in all these cases) the matter in dispute must not be decided either way.</p>  
 <p>36. <a name="fr_784"></a><a href="#fn_783"><font size="1">36</font></a> Now then I will proclaim the excellent rule regarding the (ordeal by) fire, as it has been declared (by the sages). The intermediate space between two circles is ordained to measure thirty-two Aṅgulas.</p>  
 <p>37. <a name="fr_785"></a><a href="#fn_784"><font size="1">37</font></a> The seven circles are thus declared by persons thoroughly conversant with the art of computation to cover a space of two hundred and twenty-four Aṅgulas.</p>  
 <p>38. <a name="fr_786"></a><a href="#fn_785"><font size="1">38</font></a> Let the peculiar signs be marked which he has on both hands, both visible and invisible ones, whether caused by a scar or not caused by a scar.</p>  
 <p>39. <a name="fr_787"></a><a href="#fn_786"><font size="1">39</font></a> After having first marked in this way the hands of the person accused, he should offer clarified butter in fire according to rule, as a propitiatory rite, reciting Mantras (at the same time).</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_255"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 255</font></a></p>  
 <p>40. The gods and the guardian deities of the world having been hospitably entertained, let (the person accused) utter the following prayer, facing the sun.</p>  
 <p>41. Thou, O fire, dwellest in the interior of all deities as a flame. Thou conveyest burnt-oblations to the gods, and givest peace of mind.</p>  
 <p>42. Thou, O God, knowest the secret offences and merits of men. Thou, O deity, knowest whatever mortals do not comprehend.</p>  
 <p>43. Arraigned in a cause, I am about to be tested by fire. Therefore deign to deliver me lawfully from the perplexity in which I am involved.</p>  
 <p>44. The man (about to be examined) having made this speech, facing the east, with firmness, his joined hands should be covered with seven equal leaves of the holy fig-tree,</p>  
 <p>45. <a name="fr_788"></a><a href="#fn_787"><font size="1">45</font></a> And both hands should be tied with seven strings of light-coloured thread.</p>  
 <p>46. <a name="fr_789"></a><a href="#fn_788"><font size="1">46</font></a> (Then the man) should take a smooth ball of red-hot iron, fifty Palas in weight, in both hands, and step gradually across the seven circles.</p>  
 <p>47. <a name="fr_790"></a><a href="#fn_789"><font size="1">47</font></a> When a man has carefully stepped through</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_256"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 256</font></a></p>  
 <p>the (seven) circles in this way, without having burnt himself in the least, he is acquitted.</p>  
 <p>48. <a name="fr_791"></a><a href="#fn_790"><font size="1">48</font></a> When he has dropped the ball, his hands should be inspected. If the marks have retained their previous appearance, he should examine (the hands) elsewhere as well.</p>  
 <p>49. When a bloodshot round stain or any other sore caused by fire is seen, the man has to be considered as guilty, because truth and virtue are not found in him.</p>  
 <p>50. <a name="fr_792"></a><a href="#fn_791"><font size="1">50</font></a> I will proclaim next the excellent rule regarding the (ordeal by) water. Let a king who is desirous of establishing perfect truth refrain from administering (this ordeal) in winter.</p>  
 <p>51. <a name="fr_793"></a><a href="#fn_792"><font size="1">51</font></a> With perfumes, garlands, sweet-smelling substances, honey, milk, clarified butter, and the like, let (the judge) perform the worship of Varu<i>n</i>a (the deity of water) first of all.</p>  
 <p>52. <a name="fr_794"></a><a href="#fn_793"><font size="1">52</font></a> Let (the judge) cause this ordeal to be performed</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_257"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 257</font></a></p>  
 <p>in transparent and very cool water, which does not contain aquatic animals or mud, and is abundant and not too shallow.</p>  
 <p>53. <a name="fr_795"></a><a href="#fn_794"><font size="1">53</font></a> Let (a man) go near the bank of the water (in which the accused is to be immersed) and erect an arch as high as the ear (of the person) on the edge (of that water), on level and purified ground.</p>  
 <p>54. <a name="fr_796"></a><a href="#fn_795"><font size="1">54</font></a> A strong bow should be known to have seven hundred; one not particularly strong, six hundred; a weak bow, five hundred. Thus has the rule regarding the bow been declared.</p>  
 <p>55. <a name="fr_797"></a><a href="#fn_796"><font size="1">55</font></a> From a bow of a middling quality let a skilful (archer) discharge three arrows, after having made a target one hundred and fifty Hastas distant.</p>  
 <p>56. <a name="fr_798"></a><a href="#fn_797"><font size="1">56</font></a> (The archer) is blameable if the arrows discharged by him fall short of or go beyond the target. (The person accused) obtains acquittal if his body continues immersed in water after the middling arrow has been (discharged and) brought back.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_258"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 258</font></a></p>  
 <p>57. <a name="fr_799"></a><a href="#fn_798"><font size="1">57</font></a> Among fifty runners, those two who are the quickest runners should be appointed to fetch the arrow.</p>  
 <p>58. <a name="fr_800"></a><a href="#fn_799"><font size="1">58</font></a> Let a strong man, who may be a Brahman, Kshatriya or Vai<i>s</i>ya, and must be free from affection and hatred, be placed in water reaching his navel, (standing erect) like a pillar.</p>  
 <p>59. <a name="fr_801"></a><a href="#fn_800"><font size="1">59</font></a> (The accused), thoroughly controlled in mind, shall seize the thighs of that man under water, and stand in it composed all the time till the (runner) appointed to fetch (the arrow) has returned.</p>  
 <p>60. <a name="fr_802"></a><a href="#fn_801"><font size="1">60</font></a> Then let men strictly devoted to veracity and virtue, acquainted with the application of legal rules, and free from affection and hatred, see that everything is fair.</p>  
 <p>61. <a name="fr_803"></a><a href="#fn_802"><font size="1">61</font></a> An intelligent, pious-minded man should descend into the water and duly address (the deity of water) with the following auspicious texts.</p>  
 <p>61 b. The sacred prayer (runs as follows). Om, adoration to Justice.</p>  
 <p>62. <a name="fr_804"></a><a href="#fn_803"><font size="1">62</font></a> Thou, O lord of waters, who art so pleasantly</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_259"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 259</font></a></p>  
 <p>cool, art the source of (all) beings. Save this man from guilt, thou knowest both good and evil.</p>  
 <p>63. <a name="fr_805"></a><a href="#fn_804"><font size="1">63</font></a> Thou art the first of the gods and the great comforter of the world. Thou, O water, dwellest in the interior of all beings, like a witness.</p>  
 <p>64. <a name="fr_806"></a><a href="#fn_805"><font size="1">64</font></a> Thou, O deity, alone knowest what mortals do not understand. This man, being arraigned in a cause, is going to dive in thee. Therefore deign to deliver him lawfully from this perplexity.</p>  
 <p>65. <a name="fr_807"></a><a href="#fn_806"><font size="1">65</font></a> Then (the accused) should submerge all his limbs in water so as to become invisible.</p>  
 <p>66. <a name="fr_808"></a><a href="#fn_807"><font size="1">66</font></a> A prudent man should leave the water, after having seen the arrow brought back, and should approach the king and all the assessors of the court, after saluting them reverentially.</p>  
 <p>67. <a name="fr_809"></a><a href="#fn_808"><font size="1">67</font></a> Though only his ear, eye, mouth or nose should become visible while he is in water, he cannot be acquitted. If he remains invisible, he obtains acquittal.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_260"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 260</font></a></p>  
 <p>68. <a name="fr_810"></a><a href="#fn_809"><font size="1">68</font></a> Now then I will proclaim the excellent rule regarding the (ordeal by) poison, (stating) how the king should give poison, the best means of purification for a man.</p>  
 <p>69. <a name="fr_811"></a><a href="#fn_810"><font size="1">69</font></a> Let him give the poison in the presence of (images of) the deities and Brahmans, after having kept a fast and worshipped Mahe<i>s</i>vara with incense, offerings, and sacred texts.</p>  
 <p>70. <a name="fr_812"></a><a href="#fn_811"><font size="1">70</font></a> (The judge) should give the poison with fixed attention, facing the north or the east, in the presence of Brahmans, (while the accused) is facing the south.</p>  
 <p>71. <a name="fr_813"></a><a href="#fn_812"><font size="1">71</font></a> Let seven Yavas be given, as a test of innocence, without doubt, of poison from the <i>S</i><i>ri</i>ṅga tree or Vatsanâbha (poison) or Hima<i>g</i>a poison.</p>  
 <p>72. <a name="fr_814"></a><a href="#fn_813"><font size="1">72</font></a> In the morning and in a cool place let the poison be given to all persons, after it has been finely ground, and mixed with clarified butter thirty times the quantity.</p>  
 <p>73. <a name="fr_815"></a><a href="#fn_814"><font size="1">73</font></a> He must not give poison to infants, disabled or superannuated persons, or to those who have committed a very light offence only, or to a madman, to one severely afflicted, to a cripple, or to ascetics.</p>  
 <p>74. <a name="fr_816"></a><a href="#fn_815"><font size="1">74</font></a> If the person (examined) undergoes no change</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_261"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 261</font></a></p>  
 <p>of appearance during the time occupied by clapping the hands five hundred times, he is acquitted, and should be cured (by giving him antidotes of poison).</p>  
 <p>75. <a name="fr_817"></a><a href="#fn_816"><font size="1">75</font></a> On account of thy poisonous and dangerous nature thou art hard on all persons. Thou art appointed to show the difference between good and evil like a witness.</p>  
 <p>76. <a name="fr_818"></a><a href="#fn_817"><font size="1">76</font></a> Thou, O deity, knowest the sacred ordinances and the conduct of men, both good and evil actions, (in short) whatever men do not comprehend.</p>  
 <p>77. <a name="fr_819"></a><a href="#fn_818"><font size="1">77</font></a> This man being arraigned in a cause, wishes to obtain acquittal. Therefore deign to deliver him lawfully from this perplexity.</p>  
 <p>78. <a name="fr_820"></a><a href="#fn_819"><font size="1">78</font></a> This prayer should be pronounced according to rule, while the poison is being swallowed (by the person).</p>  
 <p>79. <a name="fr_821"></a><a href="#fn_820"><font size="1">79</font></a> The king having known the man to be innocent should honourably dismiss him, and proclaim him (innocent) with a loud voice. Thus has the law been laid down.</p>  
 <p>80. <a name="fr_822"></a><a href="#fn_821"><font size="1">80</font></a> (Let the ordeal by sacred libation be administered) to one who consents to it and puts faith (in</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_262"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 262</font></a></p>  
 <p>religion) in the presence of the deities and Brahmans.</p>  
 <p>81. <a name="fr_823"></a><a href="#fn_822"><font size="1">81</font></a> Having called near the person accused, (the judge) should place him inside the circle and cause him to swallow three handsful of water, after having caused him to face the sun.</p>  
 <p>82. <a name="fr_824"></a><a href="#fn_823"><font size="1">82</font></a> After having worshipped that deity (to which the accused is devoted, the judge) should wash (the image of that deity) with water, proclaim his crime, and cause him to swallow the three handsful of water.</p>  
 <p>83. <a name="fr_825"></a><a href="#fn_824"><font size="1">83</font></a> When some one drinks consecrated water of his own accord, after having been charged with a crime, and does not confess his guilt, actuated by avarice, such a wicked man will become a leper (in a future birth).</p>  
 <p>84. <a name="fr_826"></a><a href="#fn_825"><font size="1">84</font></a> When a man tells a lie knowingly and intentionally, after having drunk consecrated water, he is born as a poor, sick, or foolish person in seven (successive) existences.</p>  
 <p>85. When a man administers the (ordeal by) sacred libation by force to procure some advantage to himself, it will destroy himself, nor will his affairs prosper.</p>  
 <p>86. <a name="fr_827"></a><a href="#fn_826"><font size="1">86</font></a> When a man has been convicted (by this</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
