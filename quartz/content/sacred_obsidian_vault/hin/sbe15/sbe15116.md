 <p><a name="page_303"><font size="1" color="green">p. 303</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">FIFTH PRAPÂ<i>TH</i>AKA <a name="fr_1026"></a><a href="#fn_1023"><font size="1">1</font></a>.</h3>  
 <p>1. Next follows Kutsâyana's hymn of praise:</p>  
 <p>'Thou art Brahmâ, and thou art Vish<i>n</i>u, thou art Rudra, thou Pra<i>g</i>âpati <a name="fr_1027"></a><a href="#fn_1024"><font size="1">2</font></a>, thou art Agni, Varu<i>n</i>a, Vâyu, thou art Indra, thou the Moon.</p>  
 <p>Thou art Anna <a name="fr_1028"></a><a href="#fn_1025"><font size="1">3</font></a> (the food or the eater), thou art Yama, thou art the Earth, thou art All, thou art the Imperishable. In thee all things exist in many forms, whether for their natural or for their own (higher) ends.</p>  
 <p>Lord of the Universe, glory to thee! Thou art the Self of All, thou art the maker of All, the enjoyer of All; thou art all life, and the lord of all pleasure and joy <a name="fr_1029"></a><a href="#fn_1026"><font size="1">4</font></a>. Glory to thee, the tranquil, the deeply hidden, the incomprehensible, the immeasurable, without beginning and without end.'</p>  
 <p align="center">__________________</p>  
 <p>2. 'In the beginning <a name="fr_1030"></a><a href="#fn_1027"><font size="1">5</font></a> darkness (tamas) alone was this. It was in the Highest, and, moved by the Highest, it becomes uneven. Thus it becomes obscurity</p>

 <p><a name="page_304"><font size="1" color="green">p. 304</font></a></p>  
 <p>(ra<i>g</i>as) <a name="fr_1031"></a><a href="#fn_1028"><font size="1">1</font></a>. Then this obscurity, being moved, becomes uneven. Thus it becomes goodness (sattva). Then this goodness, being moved, the essence flowed forth <a name="fr_1032"></a><a href="#fn_1029"><font size="1">2</font></a>. This is that part (or state of Self) which is entirely intelligent, reflected in man (as the sun is in different vessels of water) knowing the body (kshetra<i>g</i><i>ñ</i>a), attested by his conceiving, willing, and believing, it is Pra<i>g</i>âpati, called Vi<i>s</i>va. His manifestations have been declared before <a name="fr_1033"></a><a href="#fn_1030"><font size="1">3</font></a>. Now that part of him which belongs to darkness, that, O students <a name="fr_1034"></a><a href="#fn_1031"><font size="1">4</font></a>, is he who is called Rudra. That part of him which belongs to obscurity, that, O students, is he who is called Brahmâ. That part of him which belongs to goodness, that, O students, is he who is called Vish<i>n</i>u. He being one, becomes three, becomes eight <a name="fr_1035"></a><a href="#fn_1032"><font size="1">5</font></a>, becomes eleven <a name="fr_1036"></a><a href="#fn_1033"><font size="1">6</font></a>, becomes twelve, becomes infinite. Because <a name="fr_1037"></a><a href="#fn_1034"><font size="1">7</font></a> he thus came to be, he is the Being (neut.), he moves about, having entered all beings, he has become the Lord of all beings. He is the Self within and without, yes, within and without.'</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_1023"></a><a href="sbe15116.htm#fr_1026">303:1</a> At the beginning of the fifth Prapâ<i>th</i>aka my MS. gives the <i>S</i>lokas which in the printed edition are found in VI, 34, p. 178, Atreme <i>s</i>lokâ bhavanti, yathâ nirindhano vahnir, &amp;c., to nirvishaya<i>m</i> sm<i>ri</i>tam. Then follows as § 2, Atha yatheda<i>m</i> Kautsyâyanistutis, tvam, &amp;c.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1024"></a><a href="sbe15116.htm#fr_1027">303:2</a> The commentator explains Brahmâ by Hira<i>n</i>yagarbha and Pra<i>g</i>âpati by Virâ<i>g</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1025"></a><a href="sbe15116.htm#fr_1028">303:3</a> M. reads tvam Manus, tva<i>m</i> Yama<i>s</i> <i>k</i>a tvam, p<i>ri</i>thivî tvam athâ<i>k</i>yuta<i>h</i>, which is so clearly the right reading that it is difficult to understand how the mistakes arose which are presupposed by the commentary. See Taitt. Up. II, 2.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1026"></a><a href="sbe15116.htm#fr_1029">303:4</a> M. reads vi<i>s</i>vakrî<i>d</i>ârati<i>h</i> prabhu<i>h</i>, which seems better.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1027"></a><a href="sbe15116.htm#fr_1030">303:5</a> M. reads tamo vâ idam ekam âsta tat paro syât tat pare<i>n</i>eritam. It may have been tat pare 'sthât.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1028"></a><a href="sbe15116.htm#fr_1031">304:1</a> M. reads etad vai ra<i>g</i>aso rûpam, which is better, or, at least, more in accordance with what follows.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1029"></a><a href="sbe15116.htm#fr_1032">304:2</a> M. reads sattvam everitarasas sa<i>m</i> prâs<i>ri</i>vat.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1030"></a><a href="sbe15116.htm#fr_1033">304:3</a> A reference to Maitr. Up. II, 5, would have saved the commentator much trouble. M. has a better text. It leaves out vi<i>s</i>veti or vi<i>s</i>vâkhyas after pra<i>g</i>âpati, which may be wrong, but then goes on: tasya proktâ agryâs tanavo brahmâ rudro vish<i>n</i>ur iti. In enumerating the three agryâs tanava<i>h</i>, however, M. is less consistent, for it begins with ra<i>g</i>as or Brahmâ, then goes on to tamas or Rudra, and ends with sattva or Vish<i>n</i>u. The Anubhûtiprakâsa, verse 142, has the right succession.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1031"></a><a href="sbe15116.htm#fr_1034">304:4</a> This vocative, brahma<i>k</i>ârino, is always left out in M.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1032"></a><a href="sbe15116.htm#fr_1035">304:5</a> The five prâ<i>n</i>as, the sun, moon, and asterisms.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1033"></a><a href="sbe15116.htm#fr_1036">304:6</a> The eleven organs of sense and action, which, by dividing manas and buddhi, become twelve.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1034"></a><a href="sbe15116.htm#fr_1037">304:7</a> M. reads aparimitadhâ <i>k</i>odbhûtatvâd bhûteshu <i>k</i>arati pravish<i>t</i>a<i>h</i> sarvabhûtânâm.</p>
