 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="../../img/tn_hin.jpg"></a><br><i>The Upanishads, Part 2 (SBE15)</i>, by Max Müller, [1879], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  
 <p><a name="page_285"><font size="1" color="green">p. 285</font></a> <a name="page_286"><font size="1" color="green">p. 286</font></a> <a name="page_287"><font size="1" color="green">p. 287</font></a></p>  <h1 align="center">Maitrâya<i>n</i>a-Brâhma<i>n</i>a-Upanishad</h1>  <h3 align="center">FIRST PRAPÂTHAKA.</h3>  
 <p>1. The laying of the formerly-described sacrificial fires <a name="fr_926"></a><a href="#fn_923"><font size="1">1</font></a> is indeed the sacrifice of Brahman. Therefore let the sacrificer, after he has laid those fires, meditate on the Self. Thus only does the sacrificer become complete and faultless.</p>  
 <p>But who is to be meditated on? He who is called Prâ<i>n</i>a (breath). Of him there is this story:</p>  
 <p>2. A King, named B<i>ri</i>hadratha, having established his son in his sovereignty <a name="fr_927"></a><a href="#fn_924"><font size="1">2</font></a>, went into the forest, because he considered this body as transient, and had obtained freedom from all desires. Having performed the highest penance, he stands there, with uplifted arms, looking up to the sun. At the end of a thousand (days) <a name="fr_928"></a><a href="#fn_925"><font size="1">3</font></a>, the Saint Sâkâyanya <a name="fr_929"></a><a href="#fn_926"><font size="1">4</font></a>, who knew the Self, came near <a name="fr_930"></a><a href="#fn_927"><font size="1">5</font></a>, burning with splendour,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_288"><font size="1" color="green">p. 288</font></a></p>  
 <p>like a fire without smoke. He said to the King: 'Rise, rise! Choose a boon!'</p>  
 <p>The King, bowing before him, said: 'O Saint, I know not the Self, thou knowest the essence (of the Self). We have heard so. Teach it us.'</p>  
 <p><i>S</i>âkâyanya replied: 'This was achieved of yore; but what thou askest is difficult to obtain <a name="fr_931"></a><a href="#fn_928"><font size="1">1</font></a>. O Aikshvâka, choose other pleasures.'</p>  
 <p>The King, touching the Saint's feet with his head, recited this Gâthâ:</p>  
 <p>3. 'O Saint, What is the use of the enjoyment of pleasures in this offensive, pithless body--a mere mass of bones, skin, sinews, marrow <a name="fr_932"></a><a href="#fn_929"><font size="1">2</font></a>, flesh, seed, blood, mucus, tears, phlegm, ordure, water <a name="fr_933"></a><a href="#fn_930"><font size="1">3</font></a>, bile, and slime! What is the use of the enjoyment of pleasures in this body which is assailed by lust, hatred, greed, delusion, fear, anguish, jealousy, separation from what is loved, union with what is not loved <a name="fr_934"></a><a href="#fn_931"><font size="1">4</font></a>, hunger, thirst, old age, death, illness, grief, and other evils!</p>  
 <p>4. And we see that all this is perishable, as these flies, gnats, and other insects, as herbs and trees <a name="fr_935"></a><a href="#fn_932"><font size="1">5</font></a>,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_289"><font size="1" color="green">p. 289</font></a></p>  
 <p>growing and decaying. And what of these? There are other great ones, mighty wielders of bows, rulers of empires, Sudyumna, Bhûridyumna, Indradyumna, Kuvalayâ<i>s</i>va, Yauvanâ<i>s</i>va, Vadhrya<i>s</i>va, A<i>s</i>vapati <a name="fr_936"></a><a href="#fn_933"><font size="1">1</font></a>, <i>S</i>a<i>s</i>abindu, Hari<i>s</i><i>k</i>andra, Ambarîsha <a name="fr_937"></a><a href="#fn_934"><font size="1">2</font></a>, Nahusha, Anânata, <i>S</i>aryâti, Yayâti, Anara<i>n</i>ya <a name="fr_938"></a><a href="#fn_935"><font size="1">3</font></a>, Ukshasena <a name="fr_939"></a><a href="#fn_936"><font size="1">4</font></a>, &amp;c., and kings such as Marutta, Bharata (Daushyanti), and others, who before the eyes of their whole family surrendered the greatest happiness, and passed on from this world to that. And what of these? There are other great ones. We see the destruction <a name="fr_940"></a><a href="#fn_937"><font size="1">5</font></a> of Gandharvas, Asuras <a name="fr_941"></a><a href="#fn_938"><font size="1">6</font></a>, Yakshas, Râkshasas, Bhûtas, Ga<i>n</i>as, Pi<i>s</i>â<i>k</i>as, snakes, and vampires. And what of these? There is the drying up of other great oceans, the falling of mountains, the moving of the pole-star, the cutting of the wind-ropes (that hold the stars), the submergence of the earth, and the departure of the gods (suras) from their place. In such a world as this, what is the use of the enjoyment of pleasures, if he who has fed <a name="fr_942"></a><a href="#fn_939"><font size="1">7</font></a> on them is seen <a name="fr_943"></a><a href="#fn_940"><font size="1">8</font></a> to return (to this world) again</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_290"><font size="1" color="green">p. 290</font></a></p>  
 <p>and again! Deign therefore to take me out! In this world I am like a frog in a dry well. O Saint, thou art my way, thou art my way.'</p>

 <p><a name="fn_923"></a><a href="sbe15112.htm#fr_926">287:1</a> The performance of all the sacrifices, described in the Maitrâya<i>n</i>a-brâhma<i>n</i>a, is to lead up in the end to a knowledge of Brahman, by rendering a man fit for receiving the highest knowledge.
