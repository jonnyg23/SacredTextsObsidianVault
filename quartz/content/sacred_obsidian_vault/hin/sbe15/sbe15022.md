 <p><a name="page_43"><font size="1" color="green">p. 43</font></a> <a name="page_44"><font size="1" color="green">p. 44</font></a> <a name="page_45"><font size="1" color="green">p. 45</font></a></p>  <h1 align="center">TAITTIRÎYAKA-UPANISHAD.</h1>  <h2 align="center">FIRST VALLÎ,</h2>  <h2 align="center">OR, THE CHAPTER ON <i>S</i>ÎKSHÂ (PRONUNCIATION).</h2>  <h3 align="center">FIRST ANUVÂKA <a name="fr_196"></a><a href="#fn_196"><font size="1">1</font></a>.</h3>  
 <p>1. HARI<i>H</i>, OM! May Mitra be propitious to us, and Varu<i>n</i>a, Aryaman also, Indra, B<i>ri</i>haspati, and the wide-striding Vish<i>n</i>u <a name="fr_197"></a><a href="#fn_197"><font size="1">2</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>Adoration to Brahman! Adoration to thee, O Vâyu (air)! Thou indeed art the visible Brahman. I shall proclaim thee alone as the visible Brahman. I shall proclaim the right. I shall proclaim the true (scil. Brahman).</p>  
 <p>(1-5) <a name="fr_198"></a><a href="#fn_198"><font size="1">3</font></a> May it protect me! May it protect the teacher! yes, may it protect me, and may it protect the teacher! Om! Peace! peace! peace!</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_196"></a><a href="sbe15022.htm#fr_196">45:1</a> This invocation is here counted as an Anuvâka; see Taitt. Âr., ed. Rajendralal Mitra, p. 725.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_197"></a><a href="sbe15022.htm#fr_197">45:2</a> This verse is taken from <i>Ri</i>g-veda-sa<i>m</i>hitâ I, 90, 9. The deities are variously explained by the commentators: Mitra as god of the Prâ<i>n</i>a (forth-breathing) and of the day; Varu<i>n</i>a as god of the Apâna (off-breathing) and of the night. Aryaman is supposed to represent the eye or the sun; Indra, strength; B<i>ri</i>haspati, speech or intellect; Vish<i>n</i>u, the feet. Their favour is invoked, because it is only if they grant health that the study of the highest wisdom can proceed without fail.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_198"></a><a href="sbe15022.htm#fr_198">45:3</a> Five short sentences, in addition to the one paragraph. Such sentences occur at the end of other Anuvâkas also, and are counted separately.</p>
