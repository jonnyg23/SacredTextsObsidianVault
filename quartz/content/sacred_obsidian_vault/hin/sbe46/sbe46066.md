 <p><a name="page_244"><font size="1" color="green">p. 244</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">MA<i>N</i><i>D</i>ALA III, HYMN 6.</h3>  <h4 align="center">ASH<i>T</i>AKA II, ADHYÂYA 8, VARGA 26–27.</h4>  
 <p>1. Bring forward, ye pious singers, stirred in your thoughts <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:1:1">1</a>, (the ladle) which is turned towards the gods. Carrying (the sacrificial butter) from left to right <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:1:2">2</a> it turns eastward, rich in strength, bringing the offering to Agni, full of ghee.</p>  
 <p>2. When born thou hast filled the two worlds, nay thou hast even exceeded them, O friend of sacrifices <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:2:1">1</a>. May, O Agni, thy seven-tongued horses <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:2:2">2</a> move along, by the greatness of heaven and earth <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:2:3">3</a>.</p>  
 <p>3. Heaven and Earth the worshipful <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:3:1">1</a> establish thee as Hot<i>ri</i> for the house, whenever the pious human tribes offering food magnify the bright light.</p>  
 <p>4. (Thou art) seated, the great one, in a firm place <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:4:1">1</a>, between the two mighty Heavens <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:4:2">2</a>, thou who art longed for—(between) the two united <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:4:3">3</a> never-ageing, inviolable wives, the two juice-yielding milch-cows <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:4:4">4</a> of the far-reigning one <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:4:5">5</a>.</p>  
 <p>5. Thy, the great (god's) laws, O Agni, are great. Through the power of thy mind thou hast spread out the two worlds. Thou hast become a messenger at thy birth, thou, O bull, the leader of the tribes.</p>  
 <p>6. Or bind to the pole by means of thy (art of) harnessing the two long-maned, red (horses) of <i>Ri</i>ta, that swim in ghee <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:6:1">1</a>, and carry hither, O god, all gods; perform splendid worship, O <i>G</i>âtavedas!</p>  
 <p>7 <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:7:1">1</a>. Even from heaven thy shining lights have shone; thy splendour follows many resplendent dawns, when the gods, O Agni, praised the cheerful Hot<i>ri</i>'s work <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:7:2">2</a> who eagerly burns in the forests <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:7:3">3</a>.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_245"><font size="1" color="green">p. 245</font></a></p>  
 <p>8. Whether it be the gods who rejoice in the wide air, or those who dwell in the heavenly light, or those who are helpful <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:8:1">1</a>, ready to hear our call, and worshipful; or whether the horses of thy chariot, O Agni, have turned themselves hither—</p>  
 <p>9. Come hither with them, O Agni, on one chariot or on many chariots, for thy horses are powerful. Bring hither, after thy nature, the thirty and the three gods with their wives, and rejoice (in the Soma).</p>  
 <p>10. He is the Hot<i>ri</i> whose sacrifice even the two wide worlds salute over and over again for the sake of prosperity. Turned to the east <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:10:1">1</a>, the two well-established <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:10:2">2</a> (goddesses, Heaven and Earth), the righteous, true ones stand as at the sacrifice <a href="sbe46066.htm#an_3:6:10:3">3</a> of (Agni) the right-born.</p>  
 <p>11 = III, 1, 23.</p>  <h4 align="center">NOTES.</h4>  
 <p>The sane <i>Ri</i>shi and metre.—Verse 1 = TB. II, 8, 2, 5; MS. IV, 14, 3. Verse 9 = AV. XX, 13, 4.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 1.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:1:1">1</a>. The translation of mananâ´ is conjectural, and based only on the etymology. The passage â´t ít râ´<i>g</i>ânam manánâ´<i>h</i> ag<i>ri</i>bh<i>n</i>ata, IX, 70, 3, does not help us much. 'Does not X, 47, 7. stómâ<i>h</i> h<i>ri</i>disp<i>rí</i><i>s</i>a<i>h</i> mánasâ va<i>k</i>yámânâ<i>h</i>, indicate the original reading, mánasâ va<i>k</i>yámânâm?' M. M.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:1:2">2</a>. The srû<i>k</i>a<i>h</i> are called dakshi<i>n</i>âv<i>rí</i>ta<i>h</i>, I, 144, 1. By the word dakshi<i>n</i>âvâ´<i>t</i> the poet probably intended to designate the ladle also as procuring a Dakshi<i>n</i>â (sacrificial fee) to the priest.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_246"><font size="1" color="green">p. 246</font></a></p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 2.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:2:1">1</a>. On práya<i>g</i>yu, see M. M., vol. xxxii, p. 335, and Pischel, Ved. Studien, I, 98.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:2:2">2</a>. The flames of Agni.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:2:3">3</a>. Comp. below, 7, 10. The meaning seems to be: by thy (Agni's) greatness which is equal to that of Heaven and Earth.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 3.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:3:1">1</a>. I refer ya<i>g</i><i>ñ</i>íyâsa<i>h</i>, though it is a plural, to Heaven and Earth. Comp. Delbrück, Altindische Syntax, 103.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 4.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:4:1">1</a>. The Padapâ<i>th</i>a has dhruvá<i>h</i>. I think it should be dhruvé, comp. II, 41, 5. dhruvé sádasi úttame … âsâte; IX, 40, 2. dhruvé sádasi sîdati.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:4:2">2</a>. I. e. Heaven and Earth.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:4:3">3</a>. Â´skra seems derived from â-sa<i>k</i> (Joh. Schmidt, Kuhn's Zeitschrift, XXV, 71).</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:4:4">4</a>. Or 'the two milch-cows which instantly give milk,' if sabar- is to be connected with the Greek ἄφαρ; comp. Bartholomae, Bezzenberger's Beiträge, XV, 17.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:4:5">5</a>. Vish<i>n</i>u is not the only god who is called urugâyá, and there is no reason therefore why the epithet should not be referred here to Agni.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 6.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:6:1">1</a>. Comp. Lanman, Noun-Inflection, pp. 402, 413.—See below, IV, 2, 3.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 7.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:7:1">1</a>. See Geldner, Vedische Studien, I, 114 seq.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:7:2">2</a>. Should the accent be ápa<i>h</i>? Comp. III, 1, 3, note 3.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:7:3">3</a>. It is very probable that u<i>s</i>ádhak (comp. III, 34, 3; VII, 7, 2) is an epithet of Agni. We should expect the genitive; u<i>s</i>ádhak, which violates the construction, seems</p>  
 <p><a name="page_247"><font size="1" color="green">p. 247</font></a></p>  
 <p>to stand metri causa. Or is u<i>s</i>ádhak an accusative singular neuter, so that the literal translation would be: 'When the gods praised the work, burning in the forests, of the Hot<i>ri</i>'?</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 8.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:8:1">1</a>. On û´ma, comp. Pischel, Vedische Studien, I, 223.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 10.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:10:1">1</a>. Comp. above, II, 2, 7.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:10:2">2</a>. See Windisch in the Festgruss an Boehtlingk, p. 114.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_3:6:10:3">3</a>. There is one syllable above the number; the metre and meaning would be all right if we were to read adhvaré (for adhvaréva): '(the two goddesses) stand at the sacrifice,' &amp;c. Prof. Max Müller explains: 'Adhvarâ´-iva, like two sacrifices, like two sacrificial altars, barhis.'</p>
