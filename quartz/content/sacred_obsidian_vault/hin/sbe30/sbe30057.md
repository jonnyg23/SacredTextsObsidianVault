 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>The Grihya Sutras, Part 2 (SBE30)</i>, by Hermann Oldenberg, [1892], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">PA<i>T</i>ALA 4, SECTION 14.</h3>  
 <p>1. And should, after that person has eaten, seize his right hand,</p>  
 <p>2. <a name="fr_581"></a><a href="#fn_581"><font size="1">2</font></a> If he wishes that one of his companions, or a pupil, or a servant should faithfully remain with him and not go away, he should bathe in the morning, should put on clean garments, should show</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_176"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 176</font></a></p>  
 <p>patience (with that servant, &amp;c.) during the day, should speak (only) with Brâhma<i>n</i>as, and by night he should go to the dwelling-place of that person, should make water into a horn of a living animal, and should three times walk round his dwelling-place, sprinkling (his urine) round it, with (the Mantra), 'From the mountain (I sever?) thee, from thy brother, from thy sister, from all thy relations. parishîda<i>h</i> kleshyati (i.e. kvaishyasi?) <i>s</i>a<i>s</i>vat parikupilena sa<i>m</i>krâme<i>n</i>âvi<i>k</i><i>kh</i>idâ, ûlena parimî<i>dh</i>o<font size="-1"><sup>ऽ</sup></font>si parimî<i>dh</i>o<font size="-1"><sup>ऽ</sup></font>sy ûlena.<font size="-1" color="green">'</font></p>  
 <p>3. He puts down the horn of the living animal in a place which is generally accessible.</p>  
 <p>4. <a name="fr_582"></a><a href="#fn_582"><font size="1">4</font></a> One whose companions, pupils, or servants use to run away, should rebuke them with (the Mantra), 'May he who calls hither (?), call you hither! He who brings back, has brought you back (?). May the rebuke of Indra always rebuke you. If you, who worship your own deceit, despise me (?), . . . . may Indra bind you with his bond, and may he drive you back again to me.'</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_177"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 177</font></a></p>  
 <p>5. Then he enters his house, puts a piece of Sidhraka wood on (the fire), and sacrifices with the 'on-drawing verse,' 'Back-bringer, bring them back' (Taitt. Sa<i>m</i>h. III, 3, 10, 1).</p>  
 <p>6. Now (we shall explain) how one should guard his wife.</p>  
 <p>7. <a name="fr_583"></a><a href="#fn_583"><font size="1">7</font></a> One whose wife has a paramour, should grind big centipedes (?) to powder, and should insert (that powder), while his wife is sleeping, into her secret parts, with the Mantra, 'Indra. . . . from other men than me.'</p>  
 <p>8. Now (follows the sacrifice for procuring) prosperity in trade.</p>  
 <p>9. He cuts off (some portion) from (every) article of trade and sacrifices it</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_581"></a><a href="sbe30057.htm#fr_581">175:2</a> 14, 2. Mât<i>ri</i>datta: 'The description of the Samâvartana is finished. <a name="page_176"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 176</font></a> Now some ceremonies connected with special wishes of the person who has performed the Samâvartana and has settled in a house, will be described.' In my opinion, it would be more correct to consider Sûtra 18 of the preceding section as the last of the aphorisms that regard the Samâvartana, With Sûtra 2 compare Pâraskara III, 7; Âpastamba VIII, 23, 6. It seems impossible to attempt to translate the hopelessly corrupt last lines of the Mantra.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_582"></a><a href="sbe30057.htm#fr_582">176:4</a> A part of this Mantra also is most corrupt. In the first line I propose to write, nivarto vo nyavîv<i>ri</i>tat. With the last line comp. Pâraskara III, 7, 3. I think that the text of Pâraskara should be corrected in the following way: pari tvâ hvalano hvalan nivartas tvâ nyavîv<i>ri</i>tat, indra<i>h</i> pâ<i>s</i>ena sitvâ tvâ mahyam . . . (three syllables) ânayet. The Âpastambîya Mantrapâ<i>th</i>a, according to Dr. Winternitz's copy, gives the following text: anupohvad anuhvayo vivartto <a name="page_177"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 177</font></a> vo nyavîv<i>ri</i>dhat. aindra<i>h</i> parikro<i>s</i>o to va<i>h</i> parikro<i>s</i>atu sarvata<i>h</i>. yadi mâm atimanyâdvâ â devâ devavattara indra<i>h</i> pâ<i>s</i>ena <i>s</i>itkvâ vo mahyam id va<i>s</i>am ânayât svâhâ. Comp. Prof. Pischel's remarks, Philologische Abhandlungen, Martin Hertz zum siebzigsten Geburtstage von ehemaligen Schülern dargebracht (Berlin, 1888), p. 69 seq.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_583"></a><a href="sbe30057.htm#fr_583">177:7</a> On sthûrâ dri<i>dh</i>â[<i>h</i>] Mât<i>ri</i>datta says, sthûrâ d<i>ri</i><i>dh</i>â<i>h</i> sthûrâ<i>h</i> <i>s</i>atapadya<i>h</i>. A part of the Mantra is untranslatable on account of the very corrupt condition of the text. The reading given by most of the MSS. is, Indrâya yâsya <i>s</i>epham alikam anyebhya<i>h</i> purushebhyo<font size="-1"><sup>ऽ</sup></font>nyatra mat. The Âpastambîya Mantrapâ<i>th</i>a reads, indrâyâsya phaligam anyebhya<i>h</i> purushebhyonyatra mat. The meaning very probably is that Indra is invoked to keep away from the woman the <i>s</i>epha of all other men except her husband's.</p>
