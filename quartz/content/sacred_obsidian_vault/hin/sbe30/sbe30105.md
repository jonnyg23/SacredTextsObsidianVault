 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>The Grihya Sutras, Part 2 (SBE30)</i>, by Hermann Oldenberg, [1892], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">PA<i>T</i>ALA 5, SECTION 12.</h3>  
 <p>1. Having studied the Veda, when going to take the bath (which signifies the end of his studentship), he enters a cow-shed before sunrise, hangs over its door a skin with the hair inside, and sits there.</p>  
 <p>2. On that day the sun should not shine upon him.</p>  
 <p>3. <a name="fr_763"></a><a href="#fn_763"><font size="1">3</font></a> At noon, after (the ceremonies) from the putting (of wood) on the fire down to the Â<i>g</i>yabhâga oblations (have been performed), he puts a piece of Palâ<i>s</i>a wood on (the fire) with the next (verse; M. II, 7, 1), sits down to the west of the fire on a mat or on erakâ grass, recites the next (verse, II, 7, 2) over a razor, and hands it over to the barber with the next Ya<i>g</i>us (II, 7, 3). (The rites) beginning with the pouring together of (warm and cold) water down to the burying of the hair are the same as above (comp. M. II, 7, 4).</p>  
 <p>4. He sits down behind the cow-shed, takes the girdle off, and hands it over to a Brahma<i>k</i>ârin.</p>  
 <p>5. The (Brahma<i>k</i>ârin) hides it with the next Ya<i>g</i>us (II, 7, 5) at the root of an Udumbara tree or in a tuft of Darbha grass.</p>  
 <p>6. <a name="fr_764"></a><a href="#fn_764"><font size="1">6</font></a> With water of the description stated above he</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_276"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 276</font></a></p>  
 <p>bathes with the six next (verses; II, 7, 6-11), and with the next (II, 7, 12) he cleanses his teeth with a stick of Udumbara wood.</p>  
 <p>7. Having bathed and shampooed his body with such ingredients as are used in bathing, (aromatic powder, &amp;c.),</p>  
 <p>8. He puts on with the next Ya<i>g</i>us (M. II, 7, 13) a fresh under garment, and anoints himself, after having given the salve in charge of the deities with the next (Mantras, II, 7, 14), with the next (verse, II, 7, 15) with sandal salve which is scented with all kinds of perfumes. With the next (verse, II, 7, 16) he moves about a gold pellet with its setting, which is strung on a string, three times from left to right in a water-pot; with the next (verse, II, 7, 17) he ties the (pellet) to his neck; in the same way, without Mantras, he ties a pellet of Bâdara wood to his left hand, and repeats the rites stated above with a fresh upper garment, with the (verses), 'May the rich' (comp. above, IV, 10, 10; M. II, 7, 18).</p>  
 <p>9. To the skirt (of that garment) he ties two earrings, puts them into the (sacrificial spoon called) Darvi, offers the oblations (indicated by the) next (Mantras; M. II, 8, 1-8), pouring the Â<i>g</i>ya over (the ear-rings), and enters upon (the performance of) the <i>G</i>aya and following oblations.</p>  
 <p>10. Having performed (the ceremonies) down to the sprinkling (of water) round (the fire), he should tie (one of the ear-rings) with the same (verses) to his right ear, and with the same (verses one) to his left ear.</p>  
 <p>11. In the same way he should with the following (formulas, M. II, 8, 9-9, 5), according to the characteristics (contained in them), (put) a wreath on his</p>  
 <p><a name="page_277"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 277</font></a></p>  
 <p>head, anoint (his eyes), look into a mirror, (put on) shoes, (and should take) a parasol and a staff.</p>  
 <p>12. He keeps silence until the stars appear.</p>  
 <p>13. When the stars have appeared, he goes away towards the east or north, worships the quarters (of the horizon) with the next hemistich, and the stars and the moon with the next (M. II, 9, 6).</p>  
 <p>14. Having spoken with a friend he may go where he likes.</p>
