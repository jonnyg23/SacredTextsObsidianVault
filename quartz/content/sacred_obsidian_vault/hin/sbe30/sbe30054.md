 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>The Grihya Sutras, Part 2 (SBE30)</i>, by Hermann Oldenberg, [1892], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">PA<i>T</i>ALA 3, SECTION 11.</h3>  
 <p>1. He puts on the two ear-rings, the right one to his right ear, the left one to his left ear, with (the verse which he repeats for each of the two earrings), 'Virâ<i>g</i> and Svarâ<i>g</i>, and the aiding powers that dwell in our house, the prosperity that dwells in the face of royalty: therewith unite me.'</p>  
 <p>2. <a name="fr_554"></a><a href="#fn_554"><font size="1">2</font></a> With (the Mantra), 'With the seasons and the combinations of seasons, for the sake of long life, of</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_168"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 168</font></a></p>  
 <p>splendour, with the sap that dwells in the year: therewith we make them touch the jaws'he clasps the two ear-rings.</p>  
 <p>3. <a name="fr_555"></a><a href="#fn_555"><font size="1">3</font></a> With (the Mantra), 'This herb is protecting, overcoming, and powerful. May it make me shine with golden lustre; (may it make me) beloved among many people; may it make me full of holy lustre. Thou art not a bond'he ties the pellet (of wood, mentioned above, Section 10, Sûtra 6) to his neck.</p>  
 <p>4. <a name="fr_556"></a><a href="#fn_556"><font size="1">4</font></a> He puts on a wreath with the two (verses),</p>  
 <p>'Beautiful one, elevate thyself to beauty, beautifying my face. Beautify my face and make my fortune increase'(and),</p>  
 <p>'(The wreath) which <i>G</i>amadagni has brought to <i>S</i>raddhâ to please her, that I put on (my head) together with fortune and splendour.'</p>  
 <p>5. <a name="fr_557"></a><a href="#fn_557"><font size="1">5</font></a> 'The salve coming from the Trikakud (mountain), born on the Himavat, therewith I anoint you (i.e. the eyes), and with fortune and splendour. (I put?) into myself the demon of the mountain (?)'with (this verse) he anoints himself with Traikakuda salve, (or) if he cannot get that, with some other (salve).</p>  
 <p>6. With (the verse), 'My mind that has fled away' (Taitt. Sa<i>m</i>hitâ VI, 6, 7, 2) he looks into a mirror.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_169"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 169</font></a></p>  
 <p>7. <a name="fr_558"></a><a href="#fn_558"><font size="1">7</font></a> With (the formula), 'On the impulse of the god,' &amp;c., he takes a staff of reed (which somebody hands him), and with (the formula), 'Thou art the thunderbolt of Indra. O A<i>s</i>vins, protect me!'he thrice wipes it off, upwards from below.</p>  
 <p>8. With (the formula), 'Speed! Make speed away from us those who hate us, robbers, creeping things, beasts of prey, Rakshas, Pi<i>s</i>â<i>k</i>as. Protect us, O staff, from danger that comes from men; protect us from every danger; from all sides destroy the robbers'(and with the verse), 'Not naked (i.e. covered with bark) thou art born on all trees, a destroyer of foes. Destroy all hosts of enemies from every side like Maghavan (Indra)'he swings (the staff) three times from left to right over his head.</p>  
 <p>9. <a name="fr_559"></a><a href="#fn_559"><font size="1">9</font></a> With (the formula), 'The divine standing-places are you. Do not pinch me'he steps into the shoes.</p>  
 <p>10. <a name="fr_560"></a><a href="#fn_560"><font size="1">10</font></a> With (the formula), 'Pra<i>g</i>âpati's shelter art thou, the Brahman's covering'he takes the parasol.</p>  
 <p>11. <a name="fr_561"></a><a href="#fn_561"><font size="1">11</font></a> With the verse, 'My staff which fell down in the open air to the ground, that I take up again for the sake of long life, of holiness, of holy lustre'he takes up his staff, if it has fallen from his hand.</p>  
 <p align="center">End of the Third Pa<i>t</i>ala.</p>
