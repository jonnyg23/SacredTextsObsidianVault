 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="../../img/tn_hin.jpg"></a><br><i>The Upanishads, Part 1 (SBE01)</i>, by Max Müller, [1879], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">SEVENTH KHA<i>N</i><i>D</i>A.</h3>  
 <p>1. He begins with the hymn, Tad id âsa, bhuvaneshu <i>g</i>yesh<i>th</i>am (Rv. X, 120). Verily, <i>g</i>yesh<i>th</i>a, the oldest, is mahat, great. Endowed with mahat the form of this day is perfect.</p>  
 <p>2. Then follows the hymn, Tâ<i>m</i> su te kîrtim maghavan mahitvâ (Rv. X, 54), with the auspicious word mahitvâ.</p>  
 <p>3. Then follows the hymn, Bhûya id vav<i>ri</i>dhe vîryâya (Rv. VI, 30), with the auspicious word vîrya.</p>  
 <p>4. Then follows the hymn, N<i>ri</i><i>n</i>âm u tvâ n<i>ri</i>ta<i>m</i>a<i>m</i> gobhir ukthai<i>h</i> (Rv. I, 51, 4), with the auspicious word uktha.</p>  
 <p>5. He extends the first two pâdas, which are too small, by one syllable (Rv. X, 120, 1 a, and Rv. VIII, 69, 2 a) <a name="fr_450"></a><a href="#fn_448"><font size="1">2</font></a>. Into the small heart the vital spirits are placed, into the small stomach food is placed. It</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_184"><font size="1" color="green">p. 184</font></a></p>  
 <p>serves for the attainment of these desires. He who knows this, obtains these desires.</p>  
 <p>6. The two feet, each consisting of ten syllables (Rv. X, 120, 1 a, b), serve for the gaining of both kinds of food <a name="fr_451"></a><a href="#fn_449"><font size="1">1</font></a>, of what has feet (animal food), and what has no feet (vegetable food).</p>  
 <p>7. They come to be of eighteen syllables each <a name="fr_452"></a><a href="#fn_450"><font size="1">2</font></a>. Of those which are ten, nine are the prâ<i>n</i>as (openings of the body) <a name="fr_453"></a><a href="#fn_451"><font size="1">3</font></a>, the tenth is the (vital) self. This is the perfection of the (vital) self; Eight syllables remain in each. He who knows them, obtains whatever he desires.</p>
