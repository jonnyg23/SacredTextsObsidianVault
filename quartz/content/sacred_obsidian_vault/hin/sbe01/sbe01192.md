 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="../../img/tn_hin.jpg"></a><br><i>The Upanishads, Part 1 (SBE01)</i>, by Max Müller, [1879], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">FIFTH KHA<i>N</i><i>D</i>A.</h3>  
 <p>1. He extends these (verses) by (interpolating) the sound <a name="fr_445"></a><a href="#fn_443"><font size="1">1</font></a>. Verily, the sound is purusha, man. Therefore every man when he speaks, sounds loud, as it were.</p>  
 <p>2. At the end of each foot of the first verse of the hymn tad id âsa, he inserts one foot of the second verse of hymn Rv. VIII, 69, nada<i>m</i> va odatînâm, &amp;c. Thus the verse is to be recited as follows:</p>  
 <p>Tad id âsa bhuvaneshu <i>g</i>yesh<i>th</i>am pu<br>     nada<i>m</i> va odatînâm,<br> Yato <i>g</i>a<i>g</i>ña ugras tveshan<i>ri</i>m<i>n</i>o ru<br>     nada<i>m</i> yoyuvatînâm,<br> Sadyo ga<i>g</i>ñâno ni ri<i>n</i>âti <i>s</i>atrûn<br>     pati<i>m</i> vo aghnyânâm,<br> Anu yam vi<i>s</i>ve madanti ûmâ<i>h</i> sho<br>     dhenûnâm ishudhyasi.</p>  
 <p> </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_182"><font size="1" color="green">p. 182</font></a></p>  
 <p>In nada<i>m</i> va odatînâm (Rv. VIII, 69, 2), odati <a name="fr_446"></a><a href="#fn_444"><font size="1">1</font></a> are the waters in heaven, for they water all this; and they are the waters in the mouth, for they water all good food.</p>  
 <p>3. In nada<i>m</i> yoyuvatînâm (Rv. VIII, 69, 2), yoyuvatî are the waters in the sky, for they seem to inundate; and they are the waters of perspiration, for they seem to run continually.</p>  
 <p>4. In pati<i>m</i> vo aghnyânâm (Rv. VIII, 69, 2), aghnyâ are the waters which spring from the smoke of fire, and they are the waters which spring from the organ.</p>  
 <p>5. In dhenûnâm ishudhyasi (Rv. VIII, 69, 2), the dhenu (cows) are the waters, for they delight all this; and ishudhyasi means, thou art food.</p>  
 <p>6. He extends a Trish<i>t</i>ubh and an Anush<i>t</i>ubh <a name="fr_447"></a><a href="#fn_445"><font size="1">2</font></a>. Trish<i>t</i>ubh is the man, Anush<i>t</i>ubh the wife, and they make a couple. Therefore does a man, after having found a wife, consider himself a more perfect man.</p>  
 <p>7. These verses, by repeating the first three times, become twenty-five. The trunk is the twenty-fifth, and Pra<i>g</i>âpati is the twenty-fifth <a name="fr_448"></a><a href="#fn_446"><font size="1">3</font></a>. There are ten fingers on his hands, ten toes on his feet, two legs, two arms, and the trunk the twenty-fifth. He adorns that trunk as the twenty-fifth. Now this day consists of twenty-five, and the Stoma hymn of that day consists of twenty-five: it becomes the same</p>
