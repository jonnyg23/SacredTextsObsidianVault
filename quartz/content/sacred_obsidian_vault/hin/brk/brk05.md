 <p>§ 2. <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">The Four Vedas</span>.—The hymns of the <i>Ṛig-veda,</i> composed by various authors some three thousand years ago, are almost the only monument of the first period of Indian thought; for the collections known as the <i>Yajur-veda</i> and</p>  
 <p><a name="page_12"><font size="1" color="green">p. 12</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> <i>Sāma-veda</i> are for the most part merely adaptations of the Ṛig-veda for special liturgical purposes, while the <i>Atharva-veda,</i> which was not admitted until much later into the Vedic canon, combines Ṛig-vedic hymns selected for ritual objects with a mass of various incantations of little or no philosophic and literary merit. The study of these hymn-collections in their liturgical application by the Brahman schools bore as fruit the bulky volumes known generically as <i>Brāhmaṇas.</i></p>
