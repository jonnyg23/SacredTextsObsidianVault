 <p><a name="page_148"><font size="1" color="green">p. 148</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">XVIII</h3>  
 <p><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">The</span> casual reader may be bored by too many of these drawings, but they are easy to skip, or to take in at a glance, and there may be students who will want to examine them carefully. So I will add a selection of the significant drawings, with only brief remarks. I begin with what I have called partial successes, and then add a few more of those I have called "complete."</p>  
 <p>Let us return to the early drawings, made by my secretary. On the automobile ride to Pasadena, there was an ash-can (<a href="#img_fig100">fig. 100</a>):</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig100"></a>
 <img src="img/fig100.jpg" alt="Fig. 100"></p>  
 <p>For the above my wife wrote: "I see a chain dangling from somethingresembling little chimney pot on top of house."</p>

 <p>was: "These somehow belong together but won't get together" (<a href="#img_fig101">figs. 101, 101a</a>):</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig101"></a>

 <p>Here is a broom, drawn by my secretary (<a href="#img_fig104">fig. 104</a>),</p>  
 <p><a name="page_150"><font size="1" color="green">p. 150</font></a></p>  
 <p>and several efforts to reproduce it (<a href="#img_fig104">figs. 104a, 104b</a>):</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig104"></a>
 <img src="img/fig104.jpg" alt="Fig. 104, Fig. 104a, Fig. 104b"></p>  
 <p>The comments accompanying these drawings read: "All I'm sure of is a straight line with something curved at end of it; once it came" (here is drawing of the flower). "Then it doubled, or reappeared, I don't know which. (Am not sure of curly edges.) Then it was upside down."</p>  
 <p>The next drawing was a heart, and my wife got the upper half with what are apparently blood-drops added (<a href="#img_fig105">figs. 105, 105a</a>):</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig105"></a>
 <img src="img/fig105.jpg" alt="Fig. 105, Fig. 105a"></p>  
 <p><a name="page_151"><font size="1" color="green">p. 151</font></a></p>  
 <p>The above is interesting, as suggesting that whatever agency furnished the information knew more than it was telling. For if Craig's drawing, a pair of curves, constituted a crude letter N, or had no significance, why add the blood-drops, which were not in the original? On the other hand, if her subconscious mind knew it was a heart, why not give her the whole heart, and let her draw it?</p>  
 <p>So much for the drawings of my secretary; and now for my own early drawings. When I was a school boy, we used to represent human figures in this way; and, as you see, Craig got the essentials (<a href="#img_fig106">figs. 106, 106a</a>):</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig106"></a>
 <img src="img/fig106.jpg" alt="Fig. 106, Fig. 106a"></p>  
 <p>Several weeks later, I drew a pair of such figures in action and the comment was: "It's a whirligig of some sort" (<a href="#img_fig107">figs. 107, 107a</a>).</p>  
 <p>After the following drawing, Craig asked me not to do any more hands, for the reason that she "got" this, but thought it was my own hand</p>  
 <p><a name="page_152"><font size="1" color="green">p. 152</font></a></p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig107"></a>
 <img src="img/fig107.jpg" alt="Fig. 107, Fig. 107a"></p>

 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig108"></a>
 <img src="img/fig108.jpg" alt="Fig. 108, Fig. 108a"></p>  
 <p>Next, this bat, with very striking comment.</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig109"></a>
 <img src="img/fig109.jpg" alt="Fig. 109, Fig. 109a"></p>  
 <p><a name="page_153"><font size="1" color="green">p. 153</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> "Looks like ear-shaped something," and again: "Looks like calla lily" (<a href="#img_fig109">figs. 109, 109a</a>):</p>  
 <p>A butterfly net (<a href="#img_fig110">fig. 110, 110a</a>).</p>

 <img src="img/fig112.jpg" alt="Fig. 112, Fig. 112a"></p>  
 <p>A carnation which came after the preceding drawing, and apparently had been anticipated in the "sunrise" (<a href="#img_fig113">figs. 113, 113a</a>).</p>  
 <p>Note that this camp-stool, as I drew it, really</p>  
 <p><a name="page_154"><font size="1" color="green">p. 154</font></a></p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig113"></a>

 <p>The comment on this caterpillar was: "Fork then garden toollawn rake. Leaf." I might add that we have a lawn-rake made of bristly bamboo, which looks very much like my drawing (<a href="#img_fig118">figs. 118, 118a</a>):</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig118"></a>

 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig119"></a>
 <img src="img/fig119.jpg" alt="Fig. 119, Fig. 119a"></p>

 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig120"></a>
 <img src="img/fig120.jpg" alt="Fig. 120, Fig. 120a"></p>  
 <p>In the next one, the curve of the worm is amusingly reproduced by the bird's neck. The comment added: "But it may be a snake." Craig says this is an example of how one part of the drawing comes to her, and then, in haste, her memory-trains and associations supply what they think should be the rest (<a href="#img_fig121">figs. 121, 121a</a>).</p>  
 <p>The umbrella brings up Craig's reptile "complex" again. I assure you that in her garden, she turns sticks into snakes when they are far</p>

 <img src="img/fig121.jpg" alt="Fig. 121, Fig. 121a"></p>

 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig122"></a>
 <img src="img/fig122.jpg" alt="Fig. 122, Fig. 122a"></p>
