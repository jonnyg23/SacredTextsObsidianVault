 <p><a name="page_88"><font size="1" color="green">p. 88</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">GENEALOGY OF RIMMON-NIRARI III</h3>  
 <p>1. The palace of Ramman-nirari, the great king, the mighty king,</p>  
 <p>2. The universal king, <a name="fr_383"></a><a href="#fn_380"><font size="1">1</font></a> King of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assyria</span>, the king whom, as his child, <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assur</span>,</p>  
 <p>3. King of the spirits of heaven (?), <a name="fr_384"></a><a href="#fn_381"><font size="1">2</font></a> appointed, and (with) a kingdom</p>  
 <p>4. without rival has filled</p>  
 <p>5. his hand. From the great sea</p>  
 <p>6. of the rising of the sun to the great</p>  
 <p>7. sea of the setting of the sun</p>  
 <p>8. his hand conquered, and has subdued</p>  
 <p>9. in all entirety. The son of Samsi-Ramman,</p>  
 <p>10. the great king, the mighty king, the universal king, King of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assyria</span>,</p>  
 <p>11. the king without rival, the son of Sulman-asarid, <a name="fr_385"></a><a href="#fn_382"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>12. the king of the four regions, who upon the land of his foes</p>  
 <p>13. has laid (his) yoke, and has overpowered (them) like a flood.</p>  
 <p>14. Grandson of Assur-natsir-pal, <a name="fr_386"></a><a href="#fn_383"><font size="1">4</font></a> the manly warrior, is. who made wide the dwellings of the troops.</p>  
 <p>16. Ramman-nirari, the exalted prince, to whom <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assur</span>, <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Samas</span>,</p>  
 <p>17. <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Ramman</span> <a name="fr_387"></a><a href="#fn_384"><font size="1">5</font></a> and <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Merodach</span> as his helpers</p>  
 <p>18. have gone, and have extended his country,</p>  
 <p>19. descendant of Tukulti-Adar (?), <a name="fr_388"></a><a href="#fn_385"><font size="1">6</font></a> King of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assyria</span>,</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_380"></a><a href="rp20419.htm#fr_383">88:1</a> Literally "king of totality."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_381"></a><a href="rp20419.htm#fr_384">88:2</a> Igigi, perhaps literally "the strong ones," from <i>agâgu</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_382"></a><a href="rp20419.htm#fr_385">88:3</a> Shalmaneser II, B.C. 859–824.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_383"></a><a href="rp20419.htm#fr_386">88:4</a> B.C. 884–859.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_384"></a><a href="rp20419.htm#fr_387">88:5</a> Or Rimmon, the Air-god.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_385"></a><a href="rp20419.htm#fr_388">88:6</a> Or Tiglath-Urns, B.C. 890–884.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_386"></a><a href="rp20419.htm#fr_389">89:1</a> E-kharsak-Kurkurra is here the name of a temple, but it had also, and originally, a cosmical meaning as applied to the world-mountain—that is, to the world conceived as a mountain. And the idea of a world-mountain seems to have passed into that of a mountain in the world, or on the earth, which, as the abode of the gods, recalls the Indian Meru. See Jensen, <i>Kosmologie der Babylonier</i>, pp. 201–205.</p>
