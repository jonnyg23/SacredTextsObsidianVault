 <p><a name="page_83"><font size="1" color="green">p. 83</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">A VOTIVE INSCRIPTION OF ASSUR-NATSIR-PAL</h3>  
 <p>1. Assur-natsir-pal, the great king, the mighty king, king of the world, king of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assyria</span>,</p>  
 <p>2. son of Tukulti-Uras, the great king, the mighty king, king of the world, king of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assyria</span>, son of Rimmon-nirari,</p>  
 <p>3. the great king, the mighty king, king of the world, king of the same <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assyria</span>,</p>  
 <p>4. the warrior chief, who with the help of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assur</span> his lord</p>  
 <p>5. has marched, and among the princes of the four regions <a name="fr_372"></a><a href="#fn_369"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>  
 <p>6. his rival has not had; the king who from</p>  
 <p>7. beyond the <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Tigris</span> to <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Lebanon</span> and the great sea,</p>  
 <p>8. <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Laqi</span> throughout its circuit,</p>  
 <p>9. <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">’Sukhi</span> <a name="fr_373"></a><a href="#fn_370"><font size="1">2</font></a> as far as the city of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Rapiqu</span> to his feet</p>  
 <p>10. subdued; from the source <a name="fr_374"></a><a href="#fn_371"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>11. of the <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">’Supnat</span> <a name="fr_375"></a><a href="#fn_372"><font size="1">4</font></a> to the passes of</p>  
 <p>12. <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Kirruri</span>, to <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Gilzani</span>, <a name="fr_376"></a><a href="#fn_373"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>  
 <p>13. from the other side of the lower <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Zab</span></p>  
 <p>14. to the city of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Til-bari</span>, which (is) above</p>  
 <p>15. <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Zaban</span>, from the city of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Til-sabtani</span></p>  
 <p>16. to the city of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Til-sa-zabdani</span>,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_84"><font size="1" color="green">p. 84</font></a></p>  
 <p>17. the city of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Khirimu</span>, the city of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Kharutu</span>, the fortresses</p>  
 <p>18. of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Kar-Dunias</span> <a name="fr_377"></a><a href="#fn_374"><font size="1">1</font></a> to the territory</p>  
 <p>19. of my country I restored, and the broad</p>  
 <p>20. lands of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Nairi</span> throughout its whole extent</p>  
 <p>21. I conquered. That city I took anew;</p>  
 <p>22. <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Imgur-Bel</span> its name I called;</p>  
 <p>23. this temple with the brick of my palace</p>  
 <p>24. verily I built; an image of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Makhir</span> <a name="fr_378"></a><a href="#fn_375"><font size="1">2</font></a> my lord</p>  
 <p>25. in the midst verily I set up; to <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Lebanon</span></p>  
 <p>26. verily I went; beams of cedar,</p>  
 <p>27. of cypress, of juniper I cut;</p>  
 <p>28. beams of cedar upon this temple</p>  
 <p>29. I fastened; doors of cedar</p>  
 <p>30. I made; with a rim of copper I overlaid (them);</p>  
 <p>31. at its gates I fixed (them); this temple</p>  
 <p>32. I furnished, I made great; <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Makhir</span> the great lord</p>  
 <p>33. in the midst I seated; an inscribed tablet in his temple</p>  
 <p>34. I placed. O later prince of the kings</p>  
 <p>35. my sons, whom <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assur</span> shall call,</p>  
 <p>36. (if) this temple decay, (and) the tablet thou see, and</p>  
 <p>37. read, its ruins do thou restore; thy name with my name write; <a name="fr_379"></a><a href="#fn_376"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>38. to its place do thou bring (it) back! <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assur</span> the lord, the great one, <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Makhir</span>,</p>  
 <p>39. who dwells in this temple by their favourite <a name="fr_380"></a><a href="#fn_377"><font size="1">4</font></a> rightly</p>  
 <p>40. shall triumph; his tablet, his name, his seed in their land may they establish!</p>  
 <p>41. He who the tablet shall see, and offence in plenty</p>  
 <p>42. speak, may <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Ishtar</span>, lady of fight and battle,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_85"><font size="1" color="green">p. 85</font></a></p>  
 <p>43. his weapons break in pieces, his throne</p>  
 <p>44. take from him! <a name="fr_381"></a><a href="#fn_378"><font size="1">1</font></a> He who the tablet shall see, and</p>  
 <p>45. read, (and) anointing the pavement-stones, sacrificing a lamb,</p>  
 <p>46. to its place shall restore (it), <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assur</span> the great lord his prayers</p>  
 <p>47. shall hear, (and) in the battle of kings, the field</p>  
 <p>48. of engagement, his heart's desire <a name="fr_382"></a><a href="#fn_379"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>49. shall cause him to attain.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_369"></a><a href="rp20417.htm#fr_372">83:1</a> The translator in the <i>Transactions of the Society of Biblical Archæology</i>, p. 71, reads <i>ina sar sa kiprat arbata</i>, having evidently mistaken the wedges of the plural-sign for the character for "king."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_370"></a><a href="rp20417.htm#fr_373">83:2</a> The Shuhites of the Old Testament (Job ii. 11), on the west bank of the Euphrates between the Balikh and the Khabour.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_371"></a><a href="rp20417.htm#fr_374">83:3</a> <i>Ris ini</i>, the source: not <i>riseni</i>, as one word, which would be, if anything, an anomalous plural from <i>risu</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_372"></a><a href="rp20417.htm#fr_375">83:4</a> The Sebbeneh Su, which forms one of the sources of the Tigris north of Diarbekir.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_373"></a><a href="rp20417.htm#fr_376">83:5</a> [Or Guzan (Gozan).]</p>
 <p><a name="fn_374"></a><a href="rp20417.htm#fr_377">84:1</a> Babylonia.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_375"></a><a href="rp20417.htm#fr_378">84:2</a> In <i>W. A. I.</i>, ii. 58, 12, Makhir is called "the <i>daughter</i> of Samas"; but the same deity is invoked as a male in one of the penitential psalms (<i>W. A. I.</i>, iv. 66, 2) translated by Zimmern (<i>Busspsalmen</i>, p. 100), and Sayce (<i>Hibbert Lectures</i>, p. 355), "May Makhir, god of dreams, rest upon my head!"</p>
 <p><a name="fn_376"></a><a href="rp20417.htm#fr_379">84:3</a> There is no need here for an amendment of the text, which is plainly as follows: <i>sumi-ka itti sumi-ya sudhur</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_377"></a><a href="rp20417.htm#fr_380">84:4</a> The phrase <i>nisi ini</i>, literally "the raising of the eyes," means "grace" or "favour," hence the object of such grace or favour, a favourite or darling (<i>Liebling</i>, Delitzsch).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_378"></a><a href="rp20417.htm#fr_381">85:1</a> By separating <i>lu</i> from the verb and giving it a temporal meaning the translator in <i>T. S. B. A.</i> (p. 78) has missed the force of this passage, which is clearly precative. See Delitzsch, <i>Assyrian Grammar</i>, p. 260.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_379"></a><a href="rp20417.htm#fr_382">85:2</a> <i>Ammar libbi</i> = <i>mâla libbi</i>, literally "the fulness of the heart;" cp. Esarhaddon, Hexagonal Prism, Col. iv. 41, <i>amtsu mâla libbi</i>, "I attained my heart's desire."</p>
