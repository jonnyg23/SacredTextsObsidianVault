 <p><a name="page_53"><font size="1" color="green">p. 53</font></a></p>  <h4 align="center">THE STELE OF THOTHMES IV</h4>  
 <p>1. The first year, the third month of the inundation (Athyr), the 19th day, under the Majesty of the <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Horus</span>, the strong bull who produces the risings (of the sun), the master of diadems, whose royalty is stable as [that of] <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Tum</span>, the golden hawk, prevailing with the glaive, the vanquisher of the nine bows, <a name="fr_113"></a><a href="#fn_113"><font size="1">1</font></a> king of the South and of the North, Men-khopiru-Ri, the son of the Sun, Thothmos Khakeu, beloved of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Amon-Ra</span>, king of the gods, giver of life serene, like <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Ra</span>, eternally.</p>  
 <p>2. The good god lives, the son of Tuna, who lays claim on <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Harmakhis</span> the sphinx, the life of the universal lord; the omnipotent <a name="fr_114"></a><a href="#fn_114"><font size="1">2</font></a> who creates the beneficent flesh of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Khopri</span>, beautiful of face like the chief his father. As soon as he issues forth, he is furnished with his forms, <a name="fr_115"></a><a href="#fn_115"><font size="1">3</font></a> [and the diadems] of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Horus</span> are on his head; king of the South and of the North, delight of the divine ennead, who purifies <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">On</span>, <a name="fr_116"></a><a href="#fn_116"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>3. who reigns <a name="fr_117"></a><a href="#fn_117"><font size="1">5</font></a> in the abode of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Ptah</span>, offering the truth to <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Tum</span>, presenting <a name="fr_118"></a><a href="#fn_118"><font size="1">6</font></a> it to the master of the southern wall, <a name="fr_119"></a><a href="#fn_119"><font size="1">7</font></a> making endowments of daily offerings <a name="fr_120"></a><a href="#fn_120"><font size="1">8</font></a> to the god, accomplishing all that [now] exists and seeking [new] honours for the gods of the South and of the North, constructing their temples of white stone and confirming all their substance, <a name="fr_121"></a><a href="#fn_121"><font size="1">9</font></a> legitimate <a name="fr_122"></a><a href="#fn_122"><font size="1">10</font></a> son of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Tum</span>, Thothmos Khakeu, like unto <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Ra</span>;</p>

 <p>4. heir of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Horus</span>, <a name="fr_123"></a><a href="#fn_123"><font size="1">1</font></a> master of his throne, Men-khopiru-ri, who gives life. Now, when his Majesty was a child, <a name="fr_124"></a><a href="#fn_124"><font size="1">2</font></a> in the character of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Horus</span> in <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Kheb</span>, <a name="fr_125"></a><a href="#fn_125"><font size="1">3</font></a> his beauty [was] like that of [the god] who avenges his father (<span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Osiris</span>); it was regarded like that of the god himself; the soldiers raised shouts of joy because of him, the Royal sons and all the nobles submitting themselves to his valour <a name="fr_126"></a><a href="#fn_126"><font size="1">4</font></a> because of his exploits;</p>  
 <p>5. for he has renewed the circle of his victories, even as the son of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Nut</span>. <a name="fr_127"></a><a href="#fn_127"><font size="1">5</font></a> At that time he hunted <a name="fr_128"></a><a href="#fn_128"><font size="1">6</font></a> on the mountains of the Memphite nome, taking his pleasure, <a name="fr_129"></a><a href="#fn_129"><font size="1">7</font></a> along the roads of the South and of the North, <a name="fr_130"></a><a href="#fn_130"><font size="1">8</font></a> shooting at the target <a name="fr_131"></a><a href="#fn_131"><font size="1">9</font></a> with darts <a name="fr_132"></a><a href="#fn_132"><font size="1">10</font></a> of bronze, chasing the lions and the gazelles of the desert, advancing on his chariot with horses swifter</p>  
 <p>6. than the wind, together with only one of his servants, <a name="fr_133"></a><a href="#fn_133"><font size="1">11</font></a>" without being recognised by any one. Then came his time for allowing repose to his servants, at the</p>

 <p><i>sopet</i> <a name="fr_134"></a><a href="#fn_134"><font size="1">1</font></a> of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Harmakhis</span> and <a name="fr_135"></a><a href="#fn_135"><font size="1">2</font></a> of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Sokaris</span> In the necropolis, of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Rannuti</span> <a name="fr_136"></a><a href="#fn_136"><font size="1">3</font></a> with the male and female deities, <a name="fr_137"></a><a href="#fn_137"><font size="1">4</font></a> of the mother who engenders the gods of the North, <a name="fr_138"></a><a href="#fn_138"><font size="1">5</font></a> the mistress of the wall of the South,</p>  
 <p>7. <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Sekhet</span> who reigns in <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Xois</span> and in the domain of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Set</span> the great magician; <a name="fr_139"></a><a href="#fn_139"><font size="1">6</font></a>—that sacred place of the creation, <a name="fr_140"></a><a href="#fn_140"><font size="1">7</font></a> [which goes back] to the days <a name="fr_141"></a><a href="#fn_141"><font size="1">8</font></a> of the masters of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Kher</span>, <a name="fr_142"></a><a href="#fn_142"><font size="1">9</font></a> the sacred path of the gods towards the western horizon of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">On</span>; for the sphinx of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Khopri</span>, the very mighty, resides in this place, the greatest of the spirits, the most august of those who are venerated, when the shadow rests upon him. <a name="fr_143"></a><a href="#fn_143"><font size="1">10</font></a></p>  
 <p>The temples of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Memphis</span> and of all the districts which are on both sides [advance] towards him, with the two arms extended to adore his face,</p>  
 <p>8. with magnificent offerings for his double (<i>ka</i>). On one of these days, the royal son, Thothmos, being arrived, while walking at midday and seating himself under the shadow of this mighty god, was overcome by slumber and slept <a name="fr_144"></a><a href="#fn_144"><font size="1">11</font></a> at the very moment when <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Ra</span> is at the summit (of heaven).</p>

 <p><a name="page_56"><font size="1" color="green">p. 56</font></a></p>  
 <p>him with his own mouth, as a father speaks to his son, saying: Look upon me, contemplate me, O my son Thothmos; I am thy father, <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Harmakhis-Khopri-Ra-Tum</span>; I bestow upon thee the sovereignty</p>  
 <p>10. over my domain, the supremacy <a name="fr_145"></a><a href="#fn_145"><font size="1">1</font></a> over the living; thou shalt wear its white crown and its red crown <a name="fr_146"></a><a href="#fn_146"><font size="1">2</font></a> on the throne of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Seb</span> the hereditary chief. <a name="fr_147"></a><a href="#fn_147"><font size="1">3</font></a> May the earth be thine in all its length and breadth; may the splendour of the universal master illumine (thee); may there come unto thee the abundance <a name="fr_148"></a><a href="#fn_148"><font size="1">4</font></a> that is in the double land, the riches brought from every country and the long duration of years. Thine is my face, thine is my heart; thy heart is mine. <a name="fr_149"></a><a href="#fn_149"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>  
 <p>11. Behold my actual condition that thou mayest protect all my perfect limbs. <a name="fr_150"></a><a href="#fn_150"><font size="1">6</font></a> The sand of the desert whereon I am laid has covered me. Save me, <a name="fr_151"></a><a href="#fn_151"><font size="1">7</font></a> causing all that is in my heart <a name="fr_152"></a><a href="#fn_152"><font size="1">8</font></a> to be executed. For I know that thou art my son, my avenger … <i>approach</i> (?), behold I am with thee. I am [thy father] …</p>  
 <p>12. … Afterwards [the prince awakened]; he understood the word of this god and kept silence in his heart … The temples of the district consecrate offerings to this god <a name="fr_153"></a><a href="#fn_153"><font size="1">9</font></a> …</p>  
 <p>13. … Khafri, <a name="fr_154"></a><a href="#fn_154"><font size="1">10</font></a> image made for <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Tum-Harmakhis</span> …</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_113"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_113">53:1</a> That is, of the barbarians.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_114"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_114">53:2</a> <i>Ur sep</i>, properly, "he whose vicissitudes are great."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_115"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_115">53:3</a> This word appears only in Young, <i>Hieroglyphics</i>, pl. 80.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_116"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_116">53:4</a> Or "restores On" (Heliopolis).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_117"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_117">53:5</a> Literally, "who wields the sceptre of the abode of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Ptah</span>," <i>i.e.</i> Memphis.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_118"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_118">53:6</a> Literally, "making it ascend (to the nostrils) of the god," as so often depicted on the monuments.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_119"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_119">53:7</a> Ptah. The southern wall was the part of Memphis where the temple of the god stood.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_120"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_120">53:8</a> <i>Ameni-t-u</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_121"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_121">53:9</a> Or "their existences,'' <i>pat-u</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_122"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_122">53:10</a> Literally "of his loins."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_123"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_123">54:1</a> Or "flesh of Horus."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_124"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_124">54:2</a> <i>Anup</i>, with the determinative of "infant," is used in the sense of "child," "youth," especially when reference is made to the royal family. See Brugsch, <i>Dict</i>. p. 92. Here the word signifies "hereditary prince."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_125"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_125">54:3</a> <i>I.e.</i> in the north of Egypt, where Horus had passed his early years under the charge of his mother Isis. The young prince is likened to Horus.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_126"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_126">54:4</a> Literally, "being under his double solar power" (of North and South).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_127"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_127">54:5</a> Here the god Set.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_128"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_128">54:6</a> The word <i>sam</i>, which is without a determinative, may not signify "to hunt" here. Brugsch (<i>Zeitschrift</i>, 1876, p. 93) thinks that the <i>sokheti-u</i> (or perhaps <i>sam-ti-u</i>), sometimes represented as holding a lance, were warriors or huntsmen. They were more probably shepherds, who when leading their flocks to the "fields" (<i>sokhet</i>, <i>sam</i>) were armed in order to defend their flocks and themselves.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_129"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_129">54:7</a> Literally, "rejoicing his face."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_130"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_130">54:8</a> Going from south to north.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_131"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_131">54:9</a> <i>Heb</i>, with the determinative of a piece of wood on a base and transfixed by featherless arrows.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_132"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_132">54:10</a> <i>Khomt</i> means merely objects of bronze. If the determinative of <i>heb</i> is exactly represented in the copy the objects would be darts.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_133"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_133">54:11</a> <i>Uā</i>, "one," is repeated twice in the copies and hitherto the translation has been "one and one," <i>i.e.</i> "two." I know no other example of such an expression, however, and believe the second <i>uâ</i> to be the result of error. No doubt in the next sentence the servants are spoken of in the plural (<i>shes-u</i>), but the prince was evidently followed by an escort. Here reference is made only to his companion in the chariot.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_134"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_134">55:1</a> <i>Sopt</i> has hitherto been rendered "to make offerings," but the word which has no determinative, denotes, I believe, a locality consecrated to the gods in question. Here perhaps it signifies a quarry or trench running, as is afterwards stated, in the direction of Heliopolis.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_135"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_135">55:2</a> Literally, "by the side of."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_136"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_136">55:3</a> The divine nurse.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_137"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_137">55:4</a> This sense of the words has been suggested by Prof. Maspero.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_138"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_138">55:5</a> Young's copy here contains more characters than that of Lepsius.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_139"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_139">55:6</a> The names of the divinities honoured in the locality mentioned seem to me to be inserted in order to determine the place with more precision; perhaps reference is made to the gorge which leads to the Sphinx. The sentence is continued, not by <i>heka-ur</i> but by <i>as-t zeser ten</i>, in apposition to what precedes. <i>Ur-u</i> appears to be in the plural and thus to refer to Sekhet and Set.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_140"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_140">55:7</a> Literally, "of the first time," an expression generally used of the creation.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_141"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_141">55:8</a> Or perhaps, "which extends to the domains of the masters of Kher."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_142"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_142">55:9</a> An old name of the Egyptian Babylon, now Old Cairo. The road mentioned here appears to be different from that followed by Piankhi when going from Memphis to Babylon.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_143"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_143">55:10</a> Literally, "the time when the shadow rests upon him."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_144"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_144">55:11</a> Or "a dream which sleep produces took him."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_145"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_145">56:1</a> The last words are found only in Young's copy.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_146"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_146">56:2</a> The crowns of Upper and Lower Egypt. The feminine pronoun is suffixed to the words.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_147"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_147">56:3</a> Common title of Seb, indicating the antiquity of his cult. The title (<i>erpa</i>) dates from a period when as yet there was no <i>suten</i> or "king," and recalls an age of primitive feudalism. Amon, who became the supreme deity in the time of the Theban dynasties, is <i>suten</i> or "king" of the gods, as first pointed out by Professor Maspero.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_148"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_148">56:4</a> Literally, "provisions."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_149"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_149">56:5</a> The two copies differ here; I supply <i>ab</i> ("heart") before <i>k-n-a</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_150"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_150">56:6</a> Restored from Young's copy: "behold for thee my destiny, as being in protection of my limbs."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_151"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_151">56:7</a> Literally, "heal me."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_152"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_152">56:8</a> That is, what my heart desires.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_153"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_153">56:9</a> Brugsch conjecturally restores the passage thus: "[Without thinking of freeing from sand the work of king] Khafra, the image he had made for the god Tum-Harmakhis." If we consider the Sphinx as really older than Khafri, the latter part of the proposed translation must be abandoned.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_154"></a><a href="rp20211.htm#fr_154">56:10</a> Khephren of the fourth dynasty.</p>
