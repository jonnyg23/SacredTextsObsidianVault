 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_184"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_184">67:1</a> No. XIV in my forthcoming Paper.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_185"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_185">67:2</a> <i>Maspudh</i>, the Heb. <i>mishpâdh</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_186"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_186">67:3</a> The Simyra of classical writers, the Biblical Zemar (Gen. x. 18), at the foot of Lebanon in Phœnicia.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_187"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_187">67:4</a> Abd-Asirti or Abd-Asirta is also called Abd-Asrâti, and according to Dr. Winckler, in one of the Tel el-Amarna tablets, now at Berlin, the word Asrâti is preceded by the determinative of divinity. <i>Asrâti</i> is the <a name="page_68"><font size="1" color="green">p. 68</font></a> plural of <i>Asirti</i>, which the cuneiform "syllabaries" explain by the words "high place," "oracle," and "sanctuary." It is the <i>ashêrah</i> of the Old Testament, mistranslated "grove" in the Authorised Version. The Ashêrah was properly the upright post often seen upon Assyrian gems which symbolised the goddess of fertility. The latter bore the name of Ashêrah, like her symbol, among the Southern Canaanites, and corresponded to the Ashtoreth or Astartê of Phœnicia. Abd-Asirti would signify "the servant of Ashêrah."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_188"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_188">68:1</a> Or "counsel has been taken," the Assyrian <i>milik</i> signifying both "march" and "counsel."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_189"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_189">68:2</a> <i>Kinanatu</i>, "female slaves" in Assyrian, but here perhaps (like the Hebrew <i>Chenaanî</i>, "a merchant") derived from the name of Canaan.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_190"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_190">68:3</a> Compare the Hebrew Urim and Thummim in the breastplate of the High Priest.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_191"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_191">68:4</a> The reading and translation of this line are extremely doubtful.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_192"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_192">69:1</a> That is, the Egyptian monarch.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_193"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_193">69:2</a> Such seems to be the meaning of the expression <i>istu sani</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_194"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_194">69:3</a> <i>Igur</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_195"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_195">69:4</a> <i>Sipti</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_196"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_196">70:1</a> Mitana or Mitanni lay on the eastern bank of the Euphrates north of the Belikh according to the annals of Tiglath-pileser I. A docket attached to one of the Tel el-Amarna tablets identifies it with the Egyptian Nahrina, the Aram-Naharaim of the Old Testament of which Chushan-rish-athaim was king (Judges iii. 8). What is meant by the suffix <i>nanu</i> I cannot explain.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_197"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_197">70:2</a> <i>Khata</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_198"></a><a href="rp20217.htm#fr_198">70:3</a> Yarimuta is described in another tablet as situated upon the sea, to the north of Phœnicia.</p>
