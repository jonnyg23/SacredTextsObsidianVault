 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_174"></a><a href="rp20215.htm#fr_174">65:2</a> No. III in my Paper on "Babylonian Tablets from Tel el-Amarna" published in the <i>Proceedings of the Society of Biblical Archæology</i>, June 1888.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_175"></a><a href="rp20215.htm#fr_175">65:3</a> The name may also be read Aruki.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_176"></a><a href="rp20215.htm#fr_176">65:4</a> Marratim means the "sea-marshes" in Assyrian, and was specially applied to the marsh-lands in the south of Babylonia (whence the Merathaim of Jer. l. 21). The scribe has transformed the title of the prince "the king (<i>melech</i> or <i>milki</i>) the son of the salt-marshes" into two proper names, Milki and Marratim.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_177"></a><a href="rp20215.htm#fr_177">65:5</a> <i>Gaturri</i>-ki. Gedor (Josh. xv. 58, 1 Chr. xii. 7, 2) is the modern Gedûr north of Hebron.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_178"></a><a href="rp20215.htm#fr_178">65:6</a> "Of the princes." The scribe, however, seems to have meant Rabbah, "the capital," mentioned in Josh. xv. 60.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_179"></a><a href="rp20215.htm#fr_179">65:7</a> The Aramaic <i>marê</i>, "lord." We learn from coins that Marnas was the title of the supreme god of Gaza.</p>
