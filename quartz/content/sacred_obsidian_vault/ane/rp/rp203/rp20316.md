Sayce, [1890], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">V.—<span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Letter from Aziru to his Father Dûdu</span>. <a name="fr_283"></a><a href="#fn_282"><font size="1">1</font></a></h4>  
 <p>1. To Dûdu my lord, my father,<br> 2. thus (speak I) Aziru thy servant;<br> 3. at the feet of my lord I prostrate myself.</p>  
 <p align="center">_______________</p>  
 <p>4. Khatib has gone<br> 5. and has <i>answered</i> (?) the words<br> 6. of the king my lord publicly and well,<br> 7. and I have rejoiced exceedingly;<br> 8. and my country and my brothers,<br> 9. the servants of the king my lord<br> 10. and the servants of Dûdu my lord,<br> 11. have rejoiced exceedingly.<br> 12. Behold, there has gone<br> 13. the prince <a name="fr_284"></a><a href="#fn_283"><font size="1">2</font></a> of the king my lord<br> 14. unto me. From the commands<br> 15. of my lord, my god, my Sun-god,<br> 16. and from the commands of Dûdu<br> 17. my lord, I do not free myself.</p>  
 <p align="center">_______________</p>  
 <p>18. Now, O my lord, Khatib<br> 19. remains with me.<br> 20. I and he <a name="fr_285"></a><a href="#fn_284"><font size="1">3</font></a> will go (together).<br> 21. O my lord, the king of the land of the <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Hittites</span></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_70"><font size="1" color="green">p. 70</font></a></p>  
 <p>22. has marched into the country of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Nukhasse</span><br> 23. but has not prevailed over the cities.<br> 24. May the king of the land of the <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Hittites</span> quit (them)!<br> 25. Therefore now have (we) marched,<br> 26. (even) I and Khatib.</p>  
 <p align="center">_______________</p>  
 <p>27. May the king my lord listen to<br> 28. my words. O my lord, I am afraid<br> 29. of the king my lord<br> 30. and of Dûdu.<br> 31. And now my gods<br> 32. and my messengers also are the men<br> 33. of Dûdu and the nobles<br> 34. of the king my lord, and I march.</p>  
 <p align="center">_______________</p>  
 <p>35. And thus (speak) Dûdu<br> 36. and the king my lord and the nobles:<br> 37. If ever we publish anything<br> 38. about Aziru which is not right<br> 39. (let it be) before my gods and before my god, <a name="fr_286"></a><a href="#fn_285"><font size="1">1</font></a><br> 40. and now I<br> 41. and Khatib (are) servant(s) of the king before (him)</p>  
 <p align="center">_______________</p>  
 <p>42. O Dûdu, verily thou <i>knowest</i> (?),<br> 43. behold, I go to thee.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_282"></a><a href="rp20316.htm#fr_283">69:1</a> Winckler and Abel, <i>Mittheilungen</i>, ii. p. 45.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_283"></a><a href="rp20316.htm#fr_284">69:2</a> <i>Sarru</i>, which must not be interpreted "servant" here, but "prince," like the Heb. <i>sar</i>. In correct Assyrian <i>sarru</i> is "king," <i>malku</i> (<i>melech</i>) "prince," but the writer of the letter follows the Canaanitish usage.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_284"></a><a href="rp20316.htm#fr_285">69:3</a> <i>Sûtu</i>, a curious derivative from <i>sû</i> "he," like <i>yati</i> "myself."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_285"></a><a href="rp20316.htm#fr_286">70:1</a> In place of the anacoluthon we may translate, "(it is) against my gods and against my god." In another letter of Aziru (No. 39), we read, "A second time (I say) to Dûdu my lord; hear the words of the kings of Nukhasse (which) they have spoken against me."</p>
