 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_19"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_19">15:1</a> Mr. Pinches’ copy gives 36 years.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_20"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_20">15:2</a> Is this king merely a duplicate of his predecessor, the different spelling of the name having caused the annalist to divide one king into two?</p>
 <p><a name="fn_21"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_21">16:1</a> Supplied from an inscription of the king himself, who styles himself the son of Tassi-gurumas, the descendant of Ahi … the son of Agum . and the offspring of the god Suqamuna.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_22"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_22">16:2</a> Identified by Dr. Oppert with Kudur-Bel, who, according to Nabonidos, was the father of Sagasalti-buryas, the latter of whom reigned 800 years before himself (B.C. 1340). But the identification is doubtful, since the names do not agree.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_23"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_23">16:3</a> "The servant of Bel" (Kudur-Bel) in Kassite.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_24"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_24">16:4</a> "The man of Merodach" in Kassite.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_25"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_25">16:5</a> Zamama-nadin-sumi was a contemporary of the Assyrian king Assur-dan-an (whose name should probably be read Assur-dan, and be identified with that of Assur-dayan, the great-grandfather of Tiglath-Pileser I.)</p>
 <p><a name="fn_26"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_26">16:6</a> Or Bel-nadin- …</p>
 <p><a name="fn_27"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_27">16:7</a> The Kassites were a rude tribe of the Elamite mountains on the northeast side of Babylonia. Nöldeke has shown that they must be identified with the Kossæans of classical geography.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_28"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_28">17:1</a> Perhaps Merodach-nadin-akhi, the antagonist of the Assyrian king Tiglath-Pileser I., 418 years before the conquest of Babylon by Sennacherib, and consequently B.C. 1106.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_29"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_29">17:2</a> Perhaps the Merodach-sapik-kullat of the Synchronous Tablet, who was a contemporary of Assur-bil-kala, the son of Tiglath-Pileser I.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_30"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_30">17:3</a> Isin (<span style="font-variant:small-caps;">pa-se</span>) was also called Pate’si ("the city of the high-priest" in Babylonia), according to W.A.I., ii. 53, 13.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_31"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_31">17:4</a> That is, the Persian Gulf. Merodach-baladan is described below as also belonging to the dynasty of the country of the Sea, and his ancestral kingdom was that of the Kaldâ or Chaldees in Bit-yagina among the marshes at the mouth of the Euphrates.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_32"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_32">18:1</a> The Nabonassar of Ptolemy's Canon, B.C. 747.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_33"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_33">18:2</a> Called Nadinu in the Babylonian Chronicle.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_34"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_34">18:3</a> Possibly we should supply "years" instead of "kings."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_35"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_35">18:4</a> The annals of Tiglath-Pileser III show that we should read Sapi or Sape. Yukinzira is the Khinziros of Ptolemy's Canon.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_36"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_36">18:5</a> Pulu is the Pul of the Old Testament, the Pôros of Ptolemy's Canon. His name is replaced by that of Tiglath-Pileser in the Babylonian Chronicle, and the two years of his reign correspond with the two years during which Tiglath-Pileser reigned over Babylonia.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_37"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_37">18:6</a> The Shalmaneser of the Babylonian Chronicle and the Assyrian monuments, the Ilulaios of Ptolemy's Canon.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_38"></a><a href="rp20107.htm#fr_38">18:7</a> Does this imply that he was a different person from the famous Merodach-baladan, the contemporary of Sargon and Hezekiah?</p>
