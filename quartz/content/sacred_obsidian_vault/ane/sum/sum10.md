 <p><a name="xref_1"><font color="GREEN">1</font></a>. The extant text of this poem, which we may entitle "The Epic of Enmerkar," is reconstructed from the following tablets and fragments: <i>CBS</i> 29.13.194, 29.16.422; <i>PBS V</i> 8; <i>PBS XIII</i> 8; <i>SEM</i> 14, 16; <i>SRT</i> 34. The following pieces may also belong to this composition: <i>BE XXXI</i> 44 (<i>cf</i>. Kramer, <i>JAOS</i> 60.250); <i>CBS</i> 2291, 7859; <i>HAV</i> 9. "The Epic of Enmerkar" is to be kept distinct from another epic tale concerned with the same Enmerkar, which we may entitle "Enmerkar and Enmushkeshdanna." The extant text of the latter poem is reconstructed from the following tablets and fragments: Ni 2283; <i>PBS V</i> 9, 10; <i>SEM</i> 13, 18, 19. The following pieces also probably belong to it: <i>CBS</i> 29.16.450; <i>HAV</i> 17; <i>SEM</i> 17. In <i>SL</i> 320 I assumed that we had but one epic composition</p>  
 <p><a name="page_107"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 107</font></a></p>  
 <p>dealing with the exploits of Enmerkar in the course of subjugating Aratta to Erech. It now seems more likely that we actually have two such epic tales. The first, described in <i>SL</i> as the "larger portion," corresponds to the poem designated above as "The Epic of Enmerkar"; the second, described in <i>SL</i> as the "smaller portion," corresponds to the one designated "Enmerkar and Enmushkeshdanna." Note also that the number of pieces identified as belonging to these two poems is 20, not 25, as stated in <i>SL</i> 320.</p>

 <p><a name="xref_3"><font color="GREEN">3</font></a>. The term Accadian is now generally applied to the Semitic language spoken in the countries commonly known as Assyria and Babylonia; Assyrian and Babylonian, the terms formerly used to designate this language, are the names of the two best-known dialects of the Accadian language.</p>  
 <p><a name="xref_4"><font color="GREEN">4</font></a>. No satisfactory history of Sumer and the Sumerians has as yet been written. However, the interested reader will obtain a relatively adequate orientation in respect to the fundamental pattern of Sumerian history and its basic problems by examining such works as: L. W. King, <i>A History of Sumer and Akkad</i> (London, 1910); <i>The Cambridge Ancient History</i>, Vol. I (1923; especially chapters X-XII by Stephen Langdon);</p>  
 <p><a name="page_108"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 108</font></a></p>  
 <p><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font> C. L. Wooley, <i>The Sumerians</i> (Oxford, 1929); E. A. Speiser, <i>Mesopotamian Origins: The Basic Population of the Near East</i> (Philadelphia, 1930); Henri Frankfort, <i>Archaeology and the Sumerian Problem</i> (Oriental Institute Studies in Ancient-Oriental Civilization, No. 4; Chicago, 1932); W. F. Albright, <i>From the Stone Age to Christianity</i> (Baltimore, 1940). The reader will find that the statements formulated in these volumes not infrequently show serious divergences, inconsistencies, and contradictions; he is asked to bear in mind that the pertinent source material is highly complex in character and that its study and interpretation are still in a continuous and progressive state of flux.</p>  
 <p><a name="xref_5"><font color="GREEN">5</font></a>. For a more detailed sketch of the decipherment of the cuneiform system of writing, <i>cf</i>. E. A. Wallis Budge, <i>The Rise and Progress of Assyriology</i> (London, 1925); the reader will also find here an excellent pertinent bibliography. For the decipherment of Sumerian in particular, <i>cf</i>. F. H. Weissbach, <i>Zur Lösung der Sumerischen Frage</i> (Leipzig, 1897). As a matter of historical curiosity it is noteworthy to mention that in spite of all evidence to the contrary, the well-known orientalist, J. Halévy, continued to deny the existence of a Sumerian people and language in Mesopotamia, as late as the first decade of the twentieth century. According to his biased and subjectively motivated views, no people other than the Semites had ever been in possession of Babylonia. As for the so-called Sumerian language, it was merely an artificial invention of the Semites, themselves, devised for hieratic and esoteric purposes.</p>  
 <p><a name="xref_6"><font color="GREEN">6</font></a>. The first forty thousand tablets were discovered by the Arab workers while De Sarzec, the excavator, happened to be away from the mound. In the Museum of the Ancient Orient, the tablets excavated at Lagash in the course of the years are stacked high in drawer after drawer; it is difficult to estimate their number but it may be close to 100,000.</p>

 <p><a name="xref_18"><font color="GREEN">18</font></a>. If, proceeding from top to bottom, we examine the first column of the table (<a href="sum05.htm#img_fig01">fig. 1</a>), we note the following:</p>  
 <p>No. 1 is the picture of a star; it represents primarily the Sumerian word <i>an</i>, "heaven." The very same sign, however, is used to represent the word <i>dingir</i>, "god."</p>  
 <p><a name="page_110"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 110</font></a></p>  
 <p>No. 2 represents the word <i>ki</i>, "earth." Obviously it is intended to be a picture of the earth, although the interpretation of the sign is still uncertain.</p>  
 <p>No. 3 is probably a more or less stylized picture of the upper part of a man's body; it represents the word <i>lu</i>, "man."</p>  
 <p>No. 4 is a picture of the pudenda; it represents the word <i>sal</i>, "pudenda." The same sign is used to represent the word <i>munus</i>, "woman."</p>  
 <p>No. 5 is the picture of a mountain; it represents the word <i>kur</i>, whose primary meaning is "mountain."</p>  
 <p>No. 6 illustrates an ingenious device developed early by the inventors of the Sumerian system of writing, whereby they were enabled to represent pictorially words for which the ordinary pictographic representation entailed a certain amount of difficulty. As the reader will note, the sign for the word <i>geme</i>, "slave-girl," is actually a combination of two signs, that for <i>munus</i>, "woman," and that for <i>kur</i>, "mountain"; that is, of signs 4 and 5 on our table. Literally, therefore, this compound sign expresses the idea "mountain-woman." But since the Sumerians obtained their slave-girls largely from the mountainous regions about them, this compound sign adequately represented the Sumerian word for "slave-girl," <i>geme</i>.</p>  
 <p>No. 7 is the picture of a head; it represents the Sumerian word <i>sag</i>, "head."</p>  
 <p>No. 8 is also the picture of a head; the vertical strokes, however, underline the particular part of the head which is intended, that is, the mouth. This sign, therefore, represents the Sumerian word <i>ka</i>, "mouth." The same sign naturally enough represents the word <i>dug</i>, "to speak."</p>  
 <p>No. 9 is probably the picture of a bowl used primarily as a food-container; it represents the word <i>ninda</i>, "food."</p>  
 <p>No. 10 is actually a compound sign consisting of the signs for mouth and food (nos. 8 and 9 on our table); it represents the word <i>ku</i>, "to eat."</p>  
 <p>No. 11 is a picture of a water stream; it represents the word <i>a</i>, "water." This sign furnishes an excellent illustration of the process by which the Sumerian script gradually lost its unwieldy pictographic character and became a phonetic system of writing. As just said, the sign no. 11 was used primarily to represent the Sumerian word a, "water." However, the Sumerians had another word a which was identical in pronunciation with the word <i>a</i>, "water," but which had the entirely different meaning "in." Now this word "in" is a word denoting relationship and stands for a concept which is very difficult to express pictographically. To the originators of the Sumerian script then came the ingenious idea that instead of trying to invent a necessarily highly complicated picture-sign to represent the word "in," they could use the sign for <i>a</i>, "water," since both words sounded exactly alike. In other words, the early Sumerian scribes came to realize that a sign originally belonging to a given word could be used for another word with an altogether unrelated</p>  
 <p><a name="page_111"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 111</font></a></p>  
 <p>meaning, if the sound of the two words were identical. With the gradual spreading of this practice, the Sumerian script lost its pictographic character and tended more and more to become a purely phonetic script.</p>  
 <p>No. 12 is a combination of the signs for "mouth" and "water" (nos. 8 and 11); it represents the word <i>nag</i>, "to drink."</p>  
 <p>No. 13 is a picture of the lower part of the leg and foot in walking position; it represents the word <i>du</i>, "to go," and also the word <i>gub</i>, "to stand."</p>  
 <p>No. 14 is a picture of a bird; it represents the word <i>mushen</i>, "bird."</p>  
 <p>No. 15 is a picture of a fish; it represents the word <i>ha</i>, "fish." This sign furnishes another example of the phonetic development of the Sumerian script. For the Sumerian word <i>ha</i> not only had the meaning "fish" but also "may"; that is, the Sumerians had two words <i>ha</i> which were identical in pronunciation but quite unrelated in meaning. And so, early in the development of the script the Sumerian scribes began to use the sign for <i>ha</i>, "fish," to represent the phonetically identical <i>ha</i>, "may," just as in the case of sign no. 11 they used the sign for <i>a</i>, "water," to represent the word <i>a</i>, "in."</p>  
 <p>No. 16 is a picture of the head and horns of an ox; it represents the word <i>gud</i>, "ox."</p>  
 <p>No. 17 is a picture of the head of a cow; it represents the word <i>ab</i>, "cow."</p>  
 <p>No. 18 is the picture of an ear of barley; it represents the word <i>še</i>, "barley."</p>  
 <p>The signs in the first column which we have examined in detail are from the earliest period in the development of Sumerian writing known to date. Not long after the invention of the pictographic script, however, the Sumerian scribes found it convenient to turn the tablet in such a way that the pictographs lay on their backs. As the writing developed, this practice became standard and the signs were regularly turned 90 degrees. The second column in our tablet gives the pictographic signs in this turned position. To judge from our present data and speaking very roughly, this pictographic script may be dated 3200-2800 B. C. The third column of our table represents what may be termed the "archaic" script, dated roughly 2800-2600 B. C. The fourth column contains the sign-forms of the classical period, 2600-2450 B. C.; the inscriptions of this period contain the purest Sumerian known to date. The Nippur archaic cylinder (<a href="sum05.htm#img_pl03">plate III</a>), inscribed with the oldest myth known, probably belongs to the very end of this period.</p>  
 <p>The fifth column contains the sign-forms of the Sargonid period, roughly 2450-2150 B. C.; it is in this period that the Sumerians met with serious defeats at the hands of the Semites and the Guti. A brief renaissance of Sumerian power followed in the Neo-Sumerian period, roughly 2150-2050 B. C. The sixth column represents the Sumerian</p>  
 <p><a name="page_112"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 112</font></a></p>  
 <p>script of this period. With the destruction of the city of Ur, about 2050 B. C., Sumer practically ceased to exist as a political entity. The period that followed, roughly 2050-1700 B. C., is known as the "early post-Sumerian." During this period Sumerian, though no longer a living language, was retained as the literary and religious language of the Semitic conquerors. It is in this period that by far the greater part of our source material was inscribed, though much of it may have been composed considerably earlier; the seventh column contains the sign-forms then used. The last column illustrates the script as used largely in the first millennium B. C. by the royal scribes of Assyria. It is primarily this late, highly conventionalized script which the European scholars of the nineteenth century first studied and deciphered. And illogically enough, to this very day, this is the script with which students of cuneiform begin their studies.</p>
