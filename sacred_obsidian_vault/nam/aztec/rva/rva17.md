 <h1 align="CENTER">XVII. Hymn to the Gods of Wine.</h1>
 <h3 align="CENTER">XVII. Totochtin incuic Tezcatzoncatl.</h3>
 <p align="CENTER"><a href="#english">English</a></p>
 <center>
 <img src="rva03.jpg">
 <p>
 <font face="arial" size="2">
 TOTOCHTIN, THE RABBITS, GODS OF THE DRUNKARDS
 </font>
 </p>
 </center>
 <p>1. Yyaha, yya yya, yya ayya, ayya ouiya, ayya yya, ayya yya, yyauiyya, ayya ayya, yya ayya, yya yya yye.</p>
 <p>2. Coliuacan mauizpan atlacatl ichana, yya ayya, yyayyo.</p>
 <p>3. Tezcatzonco tecpan teutl, macoc: ye chocaya, auia, macaiui, macayui teutl, macoc yye chocaya.</p>
 <p>4. Auia axalaco, tecpanteutl, macoc yye chocaya, macayui, macayui teutl macoc yye chocaya.</p>
 <i></i><p>Var. 3. Tezcatzoncatl tepan. 4. Axalaca.</p>
 <h3 align="CENTER">Gloss.</h3>
 <p>1. Y tlauelcuic, tlauelcuica.</p>
 <p>2. Coliuacan mauizpa tlacatlichana, <i>q. n.</i>, in tlacatl, <i>id est</i>, octli ompa, ichan ni colhoacan. Mauizpa, <i>q. n.</i>, temamauhtican.</p>
 <p>3. Tezcatzonco, tecpanteutl, <i>q. n.</i>, ye choca in omacoc teutl tezcatzonco tecpan, <i>id est</i>, octli. Quinionacayotia in teutl. Macaiui teutl, <i>q. n.</i>, macamo omatoni in teutl, <i>id est</i>, octli, ye choca cayamo, ynemac.</p>
 <p>4. Aia, axalaco tecpanteutl, <i>q. n.</i>, axala, in tecpanteutl. Ye choca, yn omacoc, <i>id est</i>, octli axalatecpan, ye choca in omacoc, macamo omaco ni ye choca cayamo ynemac.</p>
 <h3 align="CENTER"><a name="english">Hymn to Tezcatzoncatl Totochtin.</a></h3>
 <p>1. Alas! alas! alas! alas! alas! alas!</p>
 <p>2. In the home of our ancestors this creature was a fearful thing.</p>
 <p>{p. 62}</p>
 <p>3. In the temple of Tezcatzoncatl he aids those who cry to him, he gives them to drink; the god gives to drink to those who cry to him.</p>
 <p>4. In the temple by the water-reeds the god aids those who call upon him, he gives them to drink; the god aids those who cry unto him.</p>
 <h3 align="CENTER">Notes.</h3>
 <p>Tezcatzoncatl was one of the chief gods of the native inebriating liquor, the pulque. Its effects were recognized as most disastrous, as is seen from his other names, <i>Tequechmecaniani</i>, "he who hangs people," and <i>Teatlahuiani</i>, "he who drowns people." Sahagun remarks, "They always regarded the pulque as a bad and dangerous article." The word <i>Totochtin</i>, plural of <i>tochtli</i>, rabbit, was applied to drunkards, and also to some of the deities of special forms of drunkenness.</p>
 <p>The first verse is merely a series of lamentations. The second speaks of the sad effects of the pulque in ancient times. (On Colhuacan see Notes to Hymn XIII.)</p></body>
