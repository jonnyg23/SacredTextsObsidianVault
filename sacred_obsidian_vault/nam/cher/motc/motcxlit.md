 <h1 align="CENTER">Cherokee Transcription</h1>
 <p>The Cherokee language has the continental vowel sounds <i>a</i>, <i>e</i>, 
   <i>i</i> and <i>u</i>, but lacks <i>o</i>, which is replaced by a deep <i>â</i>. 
   The obscure or short <i>û</i> lacks is frequently nasalized, but the nasal 
   sound is seldom heard at the end of a word. The only labial is <i>m</i>, which 
   occurs in probably not more than half a dozen words in the Upper and Middle 
   dialects, and is entirely absent from the Lower dialect, in which <i>w</i> takes 
   its place. The characteristic <i>l</i> of the Upper and Middle dialects becomes 
   <i>r</i> in the Lower, but no dialect has both sounds. There is also an aspirated 
   <i>l</i>; <i>k</i> and <i>t</i> have the ordinary sounds of these letters, but 
   <i>g</i> and <i>d</i> are medials, approximating the sounds of <i>k</i> and 
   <i>t</i>, respectively. A frequent double consonant is <i>ts</i>, commonly rendered 
   <i>ch</i> by the old traders.</p>
