 <p><a name="page_ix"><font size="1" color="green">p. ix</font></a></p>  <h1 align="center">AUTHORITIES.</h1>  
 <p>THE authorities consulted in writing this work were as follows:—</p>  
 <p align="center">PERSONS.</p>  
 <p>Tomah Josephs, Passamaquoddy, Indian Governor at Peter Dana's Point, Maine.</p>  
 <p>The Rev. Silas T. Rand, Baptist Missionary among the Micmac Indians at Hantsport, Nova Scotia. This gentleman lent me his manuscript collection of eighty-five stories, all taken down from verbal Indian narration. He also communicated much information in letters, etc.</p>  
 <p>John Gabriel, and his son Peter J. Gabriel, Passamaquoddy Indians, of Point Pleasant, Maine.</p>  
 <p>Noel Josephs, of Peter Dana's Point, alias <i>Che gach goch</i>, the Raven.</p>  
 <p>Joseph Tomah, Passamaquoddy, of Point Pleasant.</p>  
 <p>Louis Mitchell, Indian member of the Legislature of Maine. To this gentleman I am greatly indebted for manuscripts, letters, and oral narrations of great value.</p>  
 <p>Sapiel Selmo, keeper of the Wampum Record, formerly read every four years at the kindling of the great fire at Canawagha.</p>  
 <p>Marie Saksis, of Oldtown, a capital and very accurate narrator of many traditions.</p>  
 <p>Miss Abby Alger, of Boston, by whom I was greatly aided in collecting the Passamaquoddy stories, and who obtained several for me among the St. Francis or Abenaki Indians.</p>  
 <p>Edward Jack, of Fredericton, for several Micmac legends and many letters containing folk-lore, all taken down by him directly from Indians.</p>  
 <p>Mrs. W. Wallace Brown. Mr. Brown was a gent in charge of</p>  
 <p><a name="page_x"><font size="1" color="green">p. x</font></a></p>  
 <p>the Passamaquoddies in Maine. To this lady, who has a great influence over the Indians, and is much interested in their folklore and legends, I am indebted for a large collection of very interesting material of the most varied description.</p>  
 <p>Noel Neptune, Penobscot, Oldtown, Maine.</p>  
 <p align="center">BOOKS, MANUSCRIPTS, ETC.</p>  
 <p><i>The Story of Glooskap</i>. A curious manuscript in Indian-English, obtained for me by Tomah Josephs.</p>  
 <p><i>The Dominion Monthly</i> for 1871. Containing nine Micmac legends by Rev. S. T. Rand.</p>  
 <p><i>Indian Legends</i>. (Manuscript of 900 pp. folio.) Collected among the Micmac Indians, and translated by Silas T. Rand, Missionary to the Micmacs.</p>  
 <p><i>A Manuscript Collection of Passamaquoddy Legends and Folk-Lore</i>. By Mrs. W. Wallace Brown, of Calais, Maine. These are all given with the greatest accuracy as narrated by Indians, some in broken Indian-English. They embrace a very great variety of folk-lore.</p>  
 <p><i>Manuscript Fairy Tales in Indian and English</i>. By Louis Mitchell.</p>  
 <p><i>Manuscript: The Superstitions of the Passamaquoddies</i>. In Indian and English.</p>  
 <p><i>A History of the Passamaquoddy Indians</i>. Manuscript of 80 pages, Indian and English. All of these were written for me by L. Mitchell, M. L.</p>  
 <p><i>Wampum Records</i>. Read for me by Sapiel Selmo, the only living Indian who has the key to them.</p>  
 <p>David Cusick's <i>Sketches of Ancient History of the Six Nations</i>. Lockport, N. Y., 1848. Printed, but written in Indian-English.</p>  
 <p><i>Manuscript: Six Stories of the St. Francis or Abenaki Indians</i>. Taken down by Miss Abby Alger.</p>  
 <p><i>Osgood's Maritime Provinces</i>. In this work there are seven short extracts relative to Glooskap given without reference to any book or author.</p>
