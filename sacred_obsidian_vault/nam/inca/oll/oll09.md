 <p><a name="page_368"><font size="1" color="green">p. 368</font></a></p> <h1 align="CENTER">ACT II</h1> <h3 align="CENTER">SCENE 1</h3> <i>
 </i><p>Ollantay-tampu. Hall of the fortress-palace. Back scene seven immense stone, slabs, resting on them a monolith right across. Above masonry. At sides masonry with recesses; in the R<i>. centre a great doorway. A golden </i>tiana<i> against the central slab</i>.</p><dir> <dir>  
 <p>(<i>Enter</i> OLLANTAY <i>and</i> URCO HUARANCA, <i>both fully armed</i>.)</p></dir> </dir>  
 <p>  <i>Urco Huaranca</i>. Ollantay, thou hast been proclaimed<br> By all the Antis as their Lord.<br> The women weep, as you will see--<br> They lose their husbands and their sons,<br> Ordered to the Chayanta war.<br> When will there be a final stop<br> To distant wars? Year after year<br> They send us all to far-off lands,<br> Where blood is made to flow like rain.<br> The King himself is well supplied<br> With coca and all kinds of food.<br> What cares he that his people starve?<br> Crossing the wilds our llamas die,<br> Our feet are wounded by the thorns,<br> And if we would not die of thirst<br> We carry water on our backs.<br>   <i>Ollantay</i>. Gallant friends! Ye hear those words,<br> Ye listen to the mountain chief.</p> 
 <p><a name="page_369"><font size="1" color="green">p. 369</font></a></p> 
 <p>Filled with compassion for my men,<br> I thus, with sore and heavy heart,<br> Have spoken to the cruel king:<br> 'The Anti-suyu must have rest;<br> All her best men shan't die for thee,<br> By battle, fire, and disease--<br> They die in numbers terrible.<br> How many men have ne'er returned,<br> How many chiefs have met their death<br> For enterprises far away?'<br> For this I left the Inca's court, <a name="fr_36"></a><a href="#fn_36"><font size="1">1</font></a><br> Saying that we must rest in peace;<br> Lot none of us forsake our hearths,<br> And if the Inca still persists,<br> Proclaim with him a mortal feud.</p><dir> <dir>  
 <p>(<i>Enter</i> HANCO HUAYLLU, <i>several chiefs, and a great crowd of soldiers and people</i>.)</p></dir> </dir>  
 <p>  <i>People</i>. Long Eve our king, Ollantay<br> Bring forth the standard and the fringe,<br> Invest him with the crimson fringe<br> In Tampu now the Inca reigns,<br> He rises like the star of day.</p><dir> <dir>  
 <p>(<i>The chiefs, soldiers, and people range them selves round. </i>Ollantay<i> is seated on the tiana by </i>Hanco Huayllu<i>, an aged </i>Auqui<i> or Prince</i>.)</p></dir> </dir>  
 <p>  <i>Hanco Huayllu</i>. Receive from me the royal fringe,<br> 'Tis given by the people's will.</p> 
 <p>
 </p> 
 <p><a name="page_370"><font size="1" color="green">p. 370</font></a></p> 
 <p>Uilcañota <a name="fr_37"></a><a href="#fn_37"><font size="1">1</font></a> is a distant land,<br> Yet, even now, her people come<br> To range themselves beneath thy law.</p><dir> <dir>  
 <p>(Ollantay <i>is invested with the fringe. He rises</i>.)</p></dir> </dir>  
 <p>  <i>Ollantay</i>. Urco Huaranca, thee I name<br> Of Anti-suyu Chief and Lord;<br> Receive the arrows and the plume,</p> 
 <p>(Gives them.)</p> 
 <p>Henceforth thou art our general.<br>   <i>People</i>. Long life to the Mountain Chief.<br>   <i>Ollantay</i>. Hanco Huayllu, <a name="fr_38"></a><a href="#fn_38"><font size="1">2</font></a> of all my lords<br> Thou art most venerable and wise,<br> Being kin to the august High Priest,<br> It is my wish that thou shouldst give<br> The ring unto the Mountain Chief.</p><dir> <dir>  
 <p>(Urco Huaranca <i>kneels, and</i> Hanco Huayllu <i>addresses him</i>.)</p></dir> </dir>  
 <p>  <i>Hanco Huayllu</i>. This ring around thy finger's placed<br> That thou mayst feel, and ne'er forget,<br> That when in fight thou art engaged,<br> Clemency becomes a hero chief.<br>   <i>Urco Huaranca</i>. A thousand times, illustrious king,<br> I bless thee for thy trust in me.<br>   <i>Hanco Huayllu</i>. Behold the valiant Mountain Chief,<br> Now fully armed from head to foot,<br> And bristling like the <i>quiscahuan</i>, <a name="fr_39"></a><a href="#fn_39"><font size="1">3</font></a><br> Accoutred as becomes a knight.</p> 
 <p>
 </p> 
 <p>
 </p> 
 <p>
 </p> 
 <p><a name="page_371"><font size="1" color="green">p. 371</font></a></p><dir> <dir>  
 <p>(<i>Turning to</i> Urco Huaranca.)</p></dir> </dir>  
 <p>Ne'er let thine enemies take thee in rear<br> Man of the Puna, <a name="fr_40"></a><a href="#fn_40"><font size="1">1</font></a> it ne'er can be said<br> You fled or trembled as a reed.<br>   <i>Urco Huaranca</i>. Hear me, warriors of the Andes!<br> Already we have a valiant king,<br> It might be he will be attacked;<br> 'Tis said th' old Inca sends a force,<br> The men of Cuzco now advance.<br> We have not a single day to lose;<br> Call from the heights our Puna men,<br> Prepare their arms without delay,<br> Make Tampu strong with rampart walls,<br> No outlet leave without a guard;<br> On hill slopes gather pois'nous herbs<br> To shoot our arrows, carrying death.<br>   <i>Ollantay</i> (<i>to</i> Urco Huaranca). Select the chiefs!<br> Fix all the posts for different tribes;<br> Our foes keep marching without sleep--<br> Contrive to check them by surprise.<br> The <i>compi</i> <a name="fr_41"></a><a href="#fn_41"><font size="1">2</font></a> ruse may cause their flight.<br>   <i>Urco Huaranca</i>. Thirty thousand brave Antis are here,<br> Amongst them no weakling is found;<br> Apu Maruti, <a name="fr_42"></a><a href="#fn_42"><font size="1">3</font></a> the mighty in war,<br> From high Uilcapampa <a name="fr_43"></a><a href="#fn_43"><font size="1">4</font></a> will come,<br> On steep Tinquiqueru <a name="fr_44"></a><a href="#fn_44"><font size="1">5</font></a> he'll stand</p> 
 <p>
 </p> 
 <p>
 </p> 
 <p>
 </p> 
 <p>
 </p> 
 <p>
 </p> 
 <p><a name="page_372"><font size="1" color="green">p. 372</font></a></p> 
 <p>To march when the signal appears;<br> On the opposite side of the stream<br> Prince Chara <a name="fr_45"></a><a href="#fn_45"><font size="1">1</font></a> has mustered his force;<br> In the gorge Charamuni <a name="fr_46"></a><a href="#fn_46"><font size="1">2</font></a> I post<br> Ten thousand armed Antis on watch;<br> Another such force is in wait<br> On the left, in the vale of Pachar. <a name="fr_47"></a><a href="#fn_47"><font size="1">3</font></a><br> We are ready to meet our foes,<br> We await them with resolute calm;<br> They will march in their confident pride<br> Until their retreat is out off,<br> Then the trumpet of war shall resound,<br> From the mountains the stones shall pour down,<br> Great blocks will be hurled from above.<br> The Huancas <a name="fr_48"></a><a href="#fn_48"><font size="1">4</font></a> are crushed or dispersed,<br> Then the knife shall do its fell work,<br> All will perish by blows from our hands,<br> Our arrows will follow their flight.<br>   <i>People and soldiers</i>. It is well! It is very well!</p><dir> <dir>  
 <p>(<i>Cheers and martial music</i>.)</p></dir> </dir>  
 <p align="RIGHT">[<i>Exeunt</i>.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_36"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_36">369:1</a> This, as we have seen, was not the reason why Ollantay fled from Cuzco; but, from a leader's point of view, it was an excellent reason to give to the people of Anti-suyu. The great wars of the Incas were, to some extent, a heavy drain upon the people, but the recruiting was managed with such skill, and was so equally divided among a number of provinces, that it was not much felt.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_37"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_37">370:1</a> The snowy mountain far to the south, in sight from Cuzco. <i>Uilca</i>, sacred; <i>unuta</i>, water. Here is the source of the river <i>Uilcamayu</i>, which flows by Ollantay-tampu.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_38"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_38">370:2</a> The aged <i>Hanco Huayllu</i> as <i>Auqui</i>, or Prince of the Blood, and relation of the High Priest, gave éclat to these ceremonies.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_39"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_39">370:3</a> <i>Quiscahuan</i>. anything full of thorns.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_40"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_40">371:1</a> <i>Puna</i>, the loftier parts of the Andes.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_41"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_41">371:2</a> <i>Compi</i>, cloth or a cloak. This was an expression of the ancient Peruvians, perhaps equivalent to our 'hoodwinking.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_42"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_42">371:3</a> Apu Maruti was the head of the <i>ayllu</i> of the Inca Yahuar Huaccac, grandfather of Pachacuti. It was called the <i>ayllu</i> Aucaylli Panaca.--Mesa, <i>Anales del Cuzco</i>, quoted by Zegarra.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_43"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_43">371:4</a> Uilcapampa, mass of mountains between the Uilcamayu and Apurimac.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_44"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_44">371:5</a> Tinqui Queru, between Urupampa and Tampu. The word means 'two vases coupled.' Here are two rounded hills connected by a saddle, three and a half miles from Tampu.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_45"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_45">372:1</a> Chara, was another descendant of Yahuar Huaccac.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_46"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_46">372:2</a> A ravine on the right bank of the Vilcamayu.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_47"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_47">372:3</a> Pachar is on the left bank of the Vilcamayu opposite Ollantay-tampu, with which it is connected by a rope bridge.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_48"></a><a href="oll09.htm#fr_48">372:4</a> Huancas, natives of the valley of Jauja--Inca recruits.</p>
