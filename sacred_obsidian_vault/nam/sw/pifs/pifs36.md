 <h1 align="CENTER">XXXIII</h1>
 <h3 align="CENTER">A PUEBLO FAIRY TALE AND THE WAY IT WAS TOLD</h3>
 <i></i><p>ONE of Mark Twain's most amusing whims was to take a story of his own, turn it into French, and then translate it literally back to English. The result of transferring these strange idioms bodily was very grotesque, and a remarkable object-lesson as to the difference in "habits," so to speak, between two languages. This is one of the first things one notices in learning Latin--an inversion of the order of words in a sentence, which seems very awkward to us.</p>
 <p>The Indian languages have not so many characteristic idioms; but the order of the sentence, and the fashion of compounding words, make an absolutely literal translation almost unintelligible. It ma interest you to see exactly how one of these folk-stories is told--original Indian and all; so here is an interlined translation of such a story, each Indian word having printed under it the English word (or words) for which it stands. <a name="fr_114"></a><a href="#fn_113"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>

 <p><a name="fn_113"></a><a href="pifs36.htm#fr_114">240:1</a> In pronouncing the Tigua, <i>A</i> is like <i>ah</i>, and <i>U</i> like <i>oo</i> in "boo"; <i>I</i> and <i>ee</i> sound like <i>ee</i> in "deed"; <i>E</i> like <i>ay</i> in "day"; <i>Eh</i> like <i>e</i> in "bed"; <i>Ü</i> as in the German; <i>Hui</i> like "wee"--as which it is often spelled here; <i>Hue</i> like <i>we</i> in "wed"--also spelled here <i>weh</i>; (<i>n</i>) indicates that the vowel is to be pronounced "through the nose." The other letters have their ordinary English sounds. The apostrophe means a little holding of the breath after the consonant, before making the vowel sounds at all.</p>
