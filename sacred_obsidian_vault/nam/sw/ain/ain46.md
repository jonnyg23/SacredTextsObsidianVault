 <h3 align="CENTER">A SONG OF TCHEUNASSAT SEEVEN</h3>
 <p>There stands a dead vahahkkee<br>
 On top of it there runs back and forth the Seeven<br>
 And he has a robe with yellow hand prints on it.</p>
 <h3 align="CENTER">THE LARK'S SONG ABOUT HIS LOST WIFE <a name="fr_8"></a><a href="#fn_8"><font size="1">1</font></a></h3>
 <p>My poor wife!<br>
 In the West she seems to be bound by the song of the Bamboo.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_8"></a><a href="ain46.htm#fr_8">240:1</a> This is a Pima flute-song, a record of which I obtained for my phonograph while in Arizona. It has no direct connection with the legends; but illustrates the Story of Tcheunassat Seeven a little, as it is about a woman, the wife of an Indian named the Lark, who is led away by the seductive singing of another Indian named the Bamboo; the Indians having an idea that women were most easily seduced by music. The Pimas, when they speak English, calling the wild cane bamboo.</p><p></p><hr>
