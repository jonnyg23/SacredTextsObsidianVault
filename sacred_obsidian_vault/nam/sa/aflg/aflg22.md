 <h2 align="center">GLOSSARY</h2>
 
 <p>A<small>COURI</small> <i>or</i> A<small>GOUTI</small>, A<small>DOURI</small>. Species of <i>Dasyprocta</i>; small game animals of great food value.</p>
 
 <p>B<small>ABOON</small>. The almost invariable Creole term applied to the Howling Monkey (<i>Mycetes seniculus</i>).</p>
 
 <p>B<small>ABRACOTE</small> (a corrupted form of <i>Barbecue</i>). A small three-legged or four-legged wooden staging upon which meat is laid to be smoke-dried.</p>
 
 <p>B<small>ANAB</small> <i>or</i> B<small>ENAB</small>. A temporary shed built of sticks and leaves for shelter from sun, rain, or dew.</p>
 
 <p>B<small>ARBECUE</small>. To smoke-dry.</p>
 
 <p>B<small>ELTIRI</small>. A fermented drink, made from cassava, essentially for home consumption.</p>
 
 <p>B<small>UCK</small> (feminine, B<small>UCKEEN</small>). A term originally applied by the Dutch to any aboriginal Indian.</p>
 
 <p>B<small>ULLET</small> T<small>REE</small>. The <i>Mimusops globosa</i>, a timber tree with excellent fruit and valuable "milk."</p>
 
 <p>B<small>UNIA</small>. The "stinking-bird" (<i>Ostinops</i> spp.).</p>
 
 <p>B<small>USH-COW</small>. Another name for the tapir.</p>
 
 <p>B<small>USH-HOG</small>. <i>Dicotyles</i> sp.</p>
 
 <p>B<small>USH-MASTER</small> snake. <i>Lachesis mutus</i>.</p>
 
 <p>B<small>USH-RAT</small>. The name applied in the Pomeroon District to the opossum. There are, however, several bush-rats proper (<i>Muridæ</i>).</p>
 
 <p>C<small>AMUDI,</small> C<small>AMOODIE</small>. The land (<i>Boa constrictor</i>) or water (<i>Eunectes murinus</i>) boas.</p>
 
 <p>C<small>ASSAVA</small> bread. The Indian's "staff of life," made from the <i>Manihot</i>.</p>
 
 <p>C<small>ASSIRI,</small> C<small>AXIRI</small>. A fermented drink, similar to beer, made from cassava; drunk in large quantities to intoxicate.</p>
 
 <p>C<small>HIGOE</small>. A species of flea which, penetrating the flesh, may cause great irritation.</p>
 
 <p>C<small>ORIAL</small>. A dug-out canoe.</p>
 
 <p>C<small>REOLE</small>. A British Guiana term applied to any person born in the colony, of parents other than aboriginal Indians.</p>
 
 <p>C<small>URARE</small> (known also as U<small>RALI,</small> W<small>URALI</small>). The well-known deadly poison of which the chief ingredient is derived from <i>Strychnos toxifera</i>.</p>
 
 <p>G<small>ILBACKER</small>. A food-fish (<i>Sciadeicthys parkeri</i>).</p>
 
 <p>H<small>AIAMARA</small>. A food-fish (<i>Hoplias macrophthalmus</i>).</p>
 
 <p>H<small>AIAWA,</small> H<small>AIOWA</small>. The Incense tree, from which a very fragrant resin is derived.</p>
 
 <p>H<small>ARRI-HARRI</small>. <i>See</i> Y<small>ARRI-YARRI</small>.</p>
 
 <p>H<small>OBU</small>. The Hog-plum (<i>Spondias lutea</i>).</p>
 
 <p>H<small>OWLING MONKEY</small>. See B<small>ABOON</small>.</p>
 
 <p>H<small>URI</small>. A food-fish (<i>Hoplias malabaricus</i>).</p>
 
 <p>I<small>GARIPE</small>. A water-channel.</p>
 
 <p>I<small>TABO.</small> A water-channel.</p>
 
 <p>I<small>TE</small> (written also I<small>TA</small> <i>and</i> E<small>TA</small>). The <i>Mauritia</i> palm; of immense economic value to the Indians.</p>
 
 <p>I<small>TIRITI</small>. A plant (<i>Ischnosiphon</i>) with a reedlike stem, which is split down to form strands for plaiting.</p>
 
 <p>J<small>IGGER</small>. <i>See</i> C<small>HIGOE</small>.</p>
 
 <p>K<small>ESKEDEE</small>. The onomatopœic term for a certain bird (a species of <i>Lanius</i>).</p>
 
 <p>K<small>OKERITE</small>. The <i>Maximiliana regia</i> palm.</p>
 
 <p>K<small>URAUA</small> (written also C<small>ROWA</small>). A species of Agave from the fibers of which very strong cords are made.</p>
 
 <p>K<small>USHI</small>. A leaf-cutting ant; very destructive on the plantations.</p>
 
 <p>L<small>ABARIA</small>. An exceedingly poisonous snake (<i>Lachesis lanceolatus</i>).</p>
 
 <p>L<small>ABBA</small>. One of the most savory of the rodents (<i>Cœlogenys paca</i>).</p>

 <p>L<small>UKUNANNI</small>. A food fish (<i>Cichla ocellaris</i>). The Pomeroon Indians sometimes speak of it as "sunfish," owing to its brilliant coloration.</p>
 
 <p>M<small>AAM</small>. <i>See</i> S<small>CRUB-TURKEY</small>.</p>
 
 <p>M<small>AIPURI</small>. The Indian name for the tapir.</p>
 
 <p>M<small>ALOKA,</small> M<small>ALOCCA</small>. A large house in which several families live a more or less communal life.</p>
 
 <p>M<small>ANICOLE</small>. The <i>Euterpe edulis</i> palm.</p>
 
 <p>M<small>ARABUNTA</small>. The Creole term for several species of wasp.</p>
 
 <p>M<small>ARAKA</small>. The Calabash-gourd rattle.</p>
 
 <p>M<small>ARUDI</small>. A black fowl (<i>Penelope</i> sp.) with a scarlet neck.</p>
 
 <p>M<small>ATAPI</small>. The plaited snakelike press for squeezing the poisonous juice out of the cassava.</p>
 
 <p>M<small>ORA</small>. One of the giants of the forest trees the wood of which is very hard.</p>
 
 <p>M<small>OROKOT</small>. A food fish, something like the pacu (<i>Myletes</i>).</p>
 
 <p>"N<small>ANCY</small>"-<small>STORY</small>. One of the many African stories having the mythical spider, or <i>anansi</i>, as hero; hence applied by the Creoles to any legend, myth, or fairy tale</p>
 
 <p>N<small>EGROCOP</small>. "Crane" or "stork" (<i>Mycteria americana</i>).</p>
 
 <p>O<small>POSSUM</small>. See B<small>USH-RAT</small>; Y<small>AWARRI</small>.</p>
 
 <p>O<small>ÜICOU</small>. A fermented drink, or paste, made from cassava and potatoes.</p>
 
 <p>P<small>AIWARRI</small>. A blackish fermented drink made from cassava used on occasions of feasting and sport.</p>
 
 <p>P<small>ARIPI</small> <i>or</i> P<small>EACH-PALM</small>. <i>Guilielma</i> sp.</p>
 
 <p>P<small>ECCARY</small>. The kairuni or abuya, two of the bush-hogs.</p>
 
 <p>P<small>EGALL</small>. A basket having a plaited body and cover, much like a European woman's traveling dress basket.</p>
 
 <p>P<small>EPPER-POT</small>. A meat stew containing capsicums and cassareep, kept good by daily sterilizing (boiling).</p>
 
 <p>P<small>IMPLER</small> P<small>ALM</small>. From "pimple," meaning a thorn; species of <i>Bactris</i>.</p>
 
 <p>P<small>IRAI</small>. A very voracious carnivorous fish (<i>Pygocentris</i> sp.) with exceedingly strong and sharp incisors.</p>
 
 <p>P<small>OWIS</small>. An excellent bird (<i>Crax</i> sp.) for the table.</p>
 
 <p>P<small>URPLE-HEART</small>. A strong timber (<i>Copaifera</i> sp.) used in making dug-out canoes house posts, etc.</p>
 
 <p>Q<small>UAKE</small>. A term applied indiscriminately to all open-work Indian baskets.</p>
 
 <p>Q<small>UATTA</small>. A monkey (<i>Ateles paniscus</i>).</p>
 
 <p>Q<small>UERRIMAN</small>. A food fish (<i>Mugil brasilianus</i>).</p>
 
 <p>S<small>ALAPENTA</small>. The Creole term for the larger lizards.</p>
 
 <p>S<small>AWYER</small> B<small>EETLE</small>. A beetle (<i>Macrodontia dejani</i>) which "rings" branches of trees.</p>
 
 <p>S<small>CRUB-TURKEY</small> <i>or</i> M<small>AAM</small>. <i>Tinamus subcristatus</i>.</p>
 
 <p>S<small>EA-COW</small>. A Creole term for the <i>Manatus</i>.</p>
 
 <p>S<small>ILK-COTTON</small> T<small>REE</small>. A large deciduous tree (<i>Eriodendron, Bombax</i>).</p>
 
 <p>T<small>IGER</small>. The Creole term applied to practically all the members of the feline tribe—jaguar, puma, wildcat, and others.</p>
 
 <p>T<small>RULI</small> <i>or</i> T<small>ROOLIE</small>. The <i>Manicaria</i> palm, the leaves of which are used for thatch.</p>
 
 <p>T<small>RUMPETER</small>. The Warracabba bird (<i>Psophia</i> sp.).</p>
 
 <p>U<small>RALI</small>. <i>See</i> C<small>URARE</small>.</p>
 
 <p>V<small>ULTURE</small>. The carrion-crow "governor," or "Boss" (<i>Sarcoramphus papa</i>).</p>
 
 <p>W<small>ATER-DOG</small>. The Creole term for otter.</p>
 
 <p>W<small>OODSKIN</small>. A canoe made of a single sheet of bark.</p>
 
 <p>W<small>URALI,</small> U<small>RALI</small>. Other forms of the word C<small>URARE</small>.</p>
 
 <p>Y<small>ACKMAN</small>. The Jagd-man's, or Huntsman's, Ant, which comes at regular intervals and clears out all the other ants.</p>
 
 <p>Y<small>ARRI-YARRI</small>. The Indian flute.</p>
 
 <p>Y<small>AWARRI</small>. Species of opossum (<i>Didelphys</i>).</p>
