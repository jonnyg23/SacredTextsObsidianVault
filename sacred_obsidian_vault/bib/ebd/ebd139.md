 <p><a name="000"><b>Fruit</b></a>     A word as used in Scripture denoting produce in general, whether vegetable or animal. The Hebrews divided the fruits of the land into three classes:     (1.) The fruit of the field, "corn-fruit" (Heb.  dagan ); all kinds of grain and pulse. (2.) The fruit of the vine, "vintage-fruit" (Heb.  tirosh ); grapes, whether moist or dried. (3.) "Orchard-fruits" (Heb.  yitshar ), as dates, figs, citrons, etc.    Injunctions concerning offerings and tithes were expressed by these Hebrew terms alone (<a href="../kjv/num018.htm#012">Num 18:12</a>; <a href="../kjv/deu014.htm#023">Deu 14:23</a>). This word "fruit" is also used of children or offspring (<a href="../kjv/gen030.htm#002">Gen 30:2</a>; <a href="../kjv/deu007.htm#013">Deu 7:13</a>; <a href="../kjv/luk001.htm#042">Luk 1:42</a>; <a href="../kjv/psa021.htm#010">Psa 21:10</a>; <a href="../kjv/psa132.htm#011">Psa 132:11</a>); also of the progeny of beasts (<a href="../kjv/deu028.htm#051">Deu 28:51</a>; <a href="../kjv/isa014.htm#029">Isa 14:29</a>). It is used metaphorically in a variety of forms (<a href="../kjv/psa104.htm#013">Psa 104:13</a>; <a href="../kjv/pro001.htm#031">Pro 1:31</a>; <a href="../kjv/pro011.htm#030">Pro 11:30</a>; <a href="../kjv/pro031.htm#016">Pro 31:16</a>; <a href="../kjv/isa003.htm#010">Isa 3:10</a>; <a href="../kjv/isa010.htm#012">Isa 10:12</a>; <a href="../kjv/mat003.htm#008">Mat 3:8</a>; <a href="../kjv/mat021.htm#041">Mat 21:41</a>; <a href="../kjv/mat026.htm#029">Mat 26:29</a>; <a href="../kjv/heb013.htm#015">Heb 13:15</a>; <a href="../kjv/rom007.htm#004">Rom 7:4</a>, <a href="../kjv/rom007.htm#005">Rom 7:5</a>; <a href="../kjv/rom015.htm#028">Rom 15:28</a>). The fruits of the Spirit (<a href="../kjv/gal005.htm#022">Gal 5:22</a>, <a href="../kjv/gal005.htm#023">Gal 5:23</a>; <a href="../kjv/eph005.htm#009">Eph 5:9</a>; <a href="../kjv/jam003.htm#017">Jam 3:17</a>, <a href="../kjv/jam003.htm#018">Jam 3:18</a>) are those gracious dispositions and habits which the Spirit produces in those in whom he dwells and works. 
 </p>
 <p><a name="001"><b>Frying-pan</b></a>     (Heb.  marhesheth , a "boiler"), a pot for boiling meat (<a href="../kjv/lev002.htm#007">Lev 2:7</a>; <a href="../kjv/lev007.htm#009">Lev 7:9</a>). 
 </p>
 <p><a name="002"><b>Fuel</b></a>     Almost every kind of combustible matter was used for fuel, such as the withered stalks of herbs (<a href="../kjv/mat006.htm#030">Mat 6:30</a>), thorns (<a href="../kjv/psa058.htm#009">Psa 58:9</a>; <a href="../kjv/ecc007.htm#006">Ecc 7:6</a>), animal excrements (<a href="../kjv/eze004.htm#012">Eze 4:12</a>; <a href="../kjv/eze015.htm#004">Eze 15:4</a>, <a href="../kjv/eze015.htm#006">Eze 15:6</a>; <a href="../kjv/eze021.htm#032">Eze 21:32</a>). Wood or charcoal is much used still in all the towns of Syria and Egypt. It is largely brought from the region of Hebron to Jerusalem. (See COAL.) 
 </p>
 <p><a name="003"><b>Fugitive</b></a>     <a href="../kjv/gen004.htm#012">Gen 4:12</a>, <a href="../kjv/gen004.htm#014">Gen 4:14</a>, a rover or wanderer (Heb.  n'a ); <a href="../kjv/jdg012.htm#004">Jdg 12:4</a>, a refugee, one who has escaped (Heb.  palit ); <a href="../kjv/kg2025.htm#011">Kg2 25:11</a>, a deserter, one who has fallen away to the enemy (Heb.  nophel ); <a href="../kjv/eze017.htm#021">Eze 17:21</a>, one who has broken away in flight (Heb.  mibrah ); <a href="../kjv/isa015.htm#005">Isa 15:5</a>; <a href="../kjv/isa043.htm#014">Isa 43:14</a>, a breaker away, a fugitive (Heb.  beriah ), one who flees away. 
 </p>
 <p><a name="004"><b>Fuller</b></a>     The word "full" is from the Anglo-Saxon fullian, meaning "to whiten." To full is to press or scour cloth in a mill. This art is one of great antiquity. Mention is made of "fuller's soap" (<a href="../kjv/mal003.htm#002">Mal 3:2</a>), and of "the fuller's field" (<a href="../kjv/kg2018.htm#017">Kg2 18:17</a>). At his transfiguration our Lord's raiment is said to have been white "so as no fuller on earth could white them" (<a href="../kjv/mar009.htm#003">Mar 9:3</a>). En-rogel (q.v.), meaning literally "foot-fountain," has been interpreted as the "fuller's fountain," because there the fullers trod the cloth with their feet. 
 </p>
 <p><a name="005"><b>Fuller's Soap</b></a>     (Heb.  borith mekabbeshim  i.e., "alkali of those treading cloth"). Mention is made (<a href="../kjv/pro025.htm#020">Pro 25:20</a>; <a href="../kjv/jer002.htm#022">Jer 2:22</a>) of nitre and also (<a href="../kjv/mal003.htm#002">Mal 3:2</a>) of soap (Heb.  borith ) used by the fuller in his operations. Nitre is found in Syria, and vegetable alkali was obtained from the ashes of certain plants. (See SOAP.) 
 </p>
 <p><a name="006"><b>Fuller's Field</b></a>     A spot near Jerusalem (<a href="../kjv/kg2018.htm#017">Kg2 18:17</a>; <a href="../kjv/isa036.htm#002">Isa 36:2</a>; <a href="../kjv/isa007.htm#003">Isa 7:3</a>), on the side of the highway west of the city, not far distant from the "upper pool" at the head of the valley of Hinnom. Here the fullers pursued their occupation. 
 </p>
 <p><a name="007"><b>Fulness</b></a>      (1.) Of time (<a href="../kjv/gal004.htm#004">Gal 4:4</a>), the time appointed by God, and foretold by the prophets, when Messiah should appear. (2.) Of Christ (<a href="../kjv/joh001.htm#016">Joh 1:16</a>), the superabundance of grace with which he was filled. (3.) Of the Godhead bodily dwelling in Christ (<a href="../kjv/col002.htm#009">Col 2:9</a>), i.e., the whole nature and attributes of God are in Christ. (4.) <a href="../kjv/eph001.htm#023">Eph 1:23</a>, the church as the fulness of Christ, i.e., the church makes Christ a complete and perfect head. 
 </p>
 <p><a name="008"><b>Funeral</b></a>     Burying was among the Jews the only mode of disposing of corpses (<a href="../kjv/gen023.htm#019">Gen 23:19</a>; <a href="../kjv/gen025.htm#009">Gen 25:9</a>; <a href="../kjv/gen035.htm#008">Gen 35:8</a>, <a href="../kjv/gen035.htm#009">Gen 35:9</a>, etc.). The first traces of burning the dead are found in <a href="../kjv/sa1031.htm#012">Sa1 31:12</a>. The burning of the body was affixed by the law of Moses as a penalty to certain crimes (<a href="../kjv/lev020.htm#014">Lev 20:14</a>; <a href="../kjv/lev021.htm#009">Lev 21:9</a>). To leave the dead unburied was regarded with horror (<a href="../kjv/kg1013.htm#022">Kg1 13:22</a>; <a href="../kjv/kg1014.htm#011">Kg1 14:11</a>; <a href="../kjv/kg1016.htm#004">Kg1 16:4</a>; <a href="../kjv/kg1021.htm#024">Kg1 21:24</a>, etc.). In the earliest times of which we have record kinsmen carried their dead to the grave (<a href="../kjv/gen025.htm#009">Gen 25:9</a>; <a href="../kjv/gen035.htm#029">Gen 35:29</a>; <a href="../kjv/jdg016.htm#031">Jdg 16:31</a>), but in later times this was done by others (<a href="../kjv/amo006.htm#010">Amo 6:10</a>). Immediately after decease the body was washed, and then wrapped in a large cloth (<a href="../kjv/act009.htm#037">Act 9:37</a>; <a href="../kjv/mat027.htm#059">Mat 27:59</a>; <a href="../kjv/mar015.htm#046">Mar 15:46</a>). In the case of persons of distinction, aromatics were laid on the folds of the cloth (<a href="../kjv/joh019.htm#039">Joh 19:39</a>; compare <a href="../kjv/joh012.htm#007">Joh 12:7</a>). As a rule the burial (q.v.) took place on the very day of the death (<a href="../kjv/act005.htm#006">Act 5:6</a>, <a href="../kjv/act005.htm#010">Act 5:10</a>), and the body was removed to the grave in an open coffin or on a bier (<a href="../kjv/luk007.htm#014">Luk 7:14</a>). After the burial a funeral meal was usually given (<a href="../kjv/sa2003.htm#035">Sa2 3:35</a>; <a href="../kjv/jer016.htm#005">Jer 16:5</a>, <a href="../kjv/jer016.htm#007">Jer 16:7</a>; <a href="../kjv/hos009.htm#004">Hos 9:4</a>). 
 </p>
 <p><a name="009"><b>Furlong</b></a>     A stadium, a Greek measure of distance equal to 606 feet and 9 inches (<a href="../kjv/luk024.htm#013">Luk 24:13</a>; <a href="../kjv/joh006.htm#019">Joh 6:19</a>; <a href="../kjv/joh011.htm#018">Joh 11:18</a>; <a href="../kjv/rev014.htm#020">Rev 14:20</a>; <a href="../kjv/rev021.htm#016">Rev 21:16</a>). 
 </p>
