 <p><a name="000"><b>Beeves</b></a>     (An old English plural of the word beef), a name applicable to all ruminating animals except camels, and especially to the Bovidce, or horned cattle (<a href="../kjv/lev022.htm#019">Lev 22:19</a>, <a href="../kjv/lev022.htm#021">Lev 22:21</a>; <a href="../kjv/num031.htm#028">Num 31:28</a>, <a href="../kjv/num031.htm#030">Num 31:30</a>, <a href="../kjv/num031.htm#033">Num 31:33</a>, <a href="../kjv/num031.htm#038">Num 31:38</a>, <a href="../kjv/num031.htm#044">Num 31:44</a>). 
 </p>
 <p><a name="001"><b>Beg</b></a>     That the poor existed among the Hebrews we have abundant evidence (<a href="../kjv/exo023.htm#011">Exo 23:11</a>; <a href="../kjv/deu015.htm#011">Deu 15:11</a>), but there is no mention of beggars properly so called in the Old Testament. The poor were provided for by the law of Moses (<a href="../kjv/lev019.htm#010">Lev 19:10</a>; <a href="../kjv/deu012.htm#012">Deu 12:12</a>; <a href="../kjv/deu014.htm#029">Deu 14:29</a>). It is predicted of the seed of the wicked that they shall be beggars (<a href="../kjv/psa037.htm#025">Psa 37:25</a>; <a href="../kjv/psa109.htm#010">Psa 109:10</a>). In the New Testament we find not seldom mention made of beggars (<a href="../kjv/mar010.htm#046">Mar 10:46</a>; <a href="../kjv/luk016.htm#020">Luk 16:20</a>, <a href="../kjv/luk016.htm#021">Luk 16:21</a>; <a href="../kjv/act003.htm#002">Act 3:2</a>), yet there is no mention of such a class as vagrant beggars, so numerous in the East. "Beggarly," in <a href="../kjv/gal004.htm#009">Gal 4:9</a>, means worthless. 
 </p>
 <p><a name="002"><b>Behead</b></a>     A method of taking away life practiced among the Egyptians (<a href="../kjv/gen040.htm#017">Gen 40:17</a>). There are instances of this mode of punishment also among the Hebrews (<a href="../kjv/sa2004.htm#008">Sa2 4:8</a>; <a href="../kjv/sa2020.htm#021">Sa2 20:21</a>, <a href="../kjv/sa2020.htm#022">Sa2 20:22</a>; <a href="../kjv/kg2010.htm#006">Kg2 10:6</a>). It is also mentioned in the New Testament (<a href="../kjv/mat014.htm#008">Mat 14:8</a>; <a href="../kjv/act012.htm#002">Act 12:2</a>). 
 </p>
 <p><a name="003"><b>Behemoth</b></a>     (<a href="../kjv/job040.htm#015">Job 40:15</a>). Some have supposed this to be an Egyptian word meaning a "water-ox." The Revised Version has here in the margin "hippopotamus," which is probably the correct rendering of the word. The word occurs frequently in Scripture, but, except here, always as a common name, and translated "beast" or "cattle." 
 </p>
 <p><a name="004"><b>Bekah</b></a>     Both the name and its explanation, "a half shekel," are given in <a href="../kjv/exo038.htm#026">Exo 38:26</a>. The word properly means a "division," a "part." (R.V., "beka.") 
 </p>
 <p><a name="005"><b>Bel</b></a>     The Aramaic form of Baal, the national god of the Babylonians (<a href="../kjv/isa046.htm#001">Isa 46:1</a>; <a href="../kjv/jer050.htm#002">Jer 50:2</a>; <a href="../kjv/jer051.htm#044">Jer 51:44</a>). It signifies "lord." (See BAAL.) 
 </p>
 <p><a name="006"><b>Bela</b></a>    A thing swallowed.     (1.) A city on the shore of the Dead Sea, not far from Sodom, called also Zoar. It was the only one of the five cities that was spared at Lot's intercession (<a href="../kjv/gen019.htm#020">Gen 19:20</a>, <a href="../kjv/gen019.htm#023">Gen 19:23</a>). It is first mentioned in <a href="../kjv/gen014.htm#002">Gen 14:2</a>, <a href="../kjv/gen014.htm#008">Gen 14:8</a>. (2.) The eldest son of Benjamin (<a href="../kjv/num026.htm#038">Num 26:38</a>; "Belah," <a href="../kjv/gen046.htm#021">Gen 46:21</a>). (3.) The son of Beor, and a king of Edom (<a href="../kjv/gen036.htm#032">Gen 36:32</a>, <a href="../kjv/gen036.htm#033">Gen 36:33</a>; <a href="../kjv/ch1001.htm#043">Ch1 1:43</a>) (4.) A son of Azaz (<a href="../kjv/ch1005.htm#008">Ch1 5:8</a>). 
 </p>
 <p><a name="007"><b>Belial</b></a>     Worthlessness, frequently used in the Old Testament as a proper name. It is first used in <a href="../kjv/deu013.htm#013">Deu 13:13</a>. In the New Testament it is found only in <a href="../kjv/co2006.htm#015">Co2 6:15</a>, where it is used as a name of Satan, the personification of all that is evil. It is translated "wicked" in <a href="../kjv/deu015.htm#009">Deu 15:9</a>; <a href="../kjv/psa041.htm#008">Psa 41:8</a> (R.V. marg.); <a href="../kjv/psa101.htm#003">Psa 101:3</a>; <a href="../kjv/pro006.htm#012">Pro 6:12</a>, etc. The expression "son" or "man of Belial" means simply a worthless, lawless person (<a href="../kjv/jdg019.htm#022">Jdg 19:22</a>; <a href="../kjv/jdg020.htm#013">Jdg 20:13</a>; <a href="../kjv/sa1001.htm#016">Sa1 1:16</a>; <a href="../kjv/sa1002.htm#012">Sa1 2:12</a>). 
 </p>
 <p><a name="008"><b>Bell</b></a>     The bells first mentioned in Scripture are the small golden bells attached to the hem of the high priest's ephod (<a href="../kjv/exo028.htm#033">Exo 28:33</a>, <a href="../kjv/exo028.htm#034">Exo 28:34</a>, <a href="../kjv/exo028.htm#035">Exo 28:35</a>). The "bells of the horses" mentioned by Zechariah (<a href="../kjv/zac014.htm#020">Zac 14:20</a>) were attached to the bridles or belts round the necks of horses trained for war, so as to accustom them to noise and tumult. 
 </p>
 <p><a name="009"><b>Bellows</b></a>     Occurs only in <a href="../kjv/jer006.htm#029">Jer 6:29</a>, in relation to the casting of metal. Probably they consisted of leather bags similar to those common in Egypt. 
 </p>
