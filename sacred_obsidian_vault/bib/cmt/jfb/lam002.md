Fausset and David Brown [1882] at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <h3 align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#001">Lamentations Chapter 2</a></h3> 
 <p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#001">Lamentations 2:1</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_001">lam 2:1</a></font></span></p>(Lam. 2:1-22)
 <p>
 </p><p>How--The title of the collection repeated here, and in <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam004.htm#001">Lam 4:1</a>. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>covered . . . with a cloud--that is, with the darkness of ignominy. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>cast down from heaven unto . . . earth-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat011.htm#023">Mat 11:23</a>); dashed down from the highest prosperity to the lowest misery. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>beauty of Israel--the beautiful temple (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa029.htm#002">Psa 29:2</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa074.htm#007">Psa 74:7</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa096.htm#009">Psa 96:9</a>, Margin; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa060.htm#007">Isa 60:7</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa064.htm#011">Isa 64:11</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>his footstool--the ark (compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/ch1028.htm#002">Ch1 28:2</a>, with <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa099.htm#005">Psa 99:5</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa132.htm#007">Psa 132:7</a>). They once had gloried more in the ark than in the God whose symbol it was; they now feel it was but His "footstool," yet that it had been a great glory to them that God deigned to use it as such.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#002">Lamentations 2:2</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_002">lam 2:2</a></font></span></p>polluted--by delivering it into the hands of the profane foe. Compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa089.htm#039">Psa 89:39</a>, "profaned . . . crown."
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#003">Lamentations 2:3</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_003">lam 2:3</a></font></span></p>horn--worn in the East as an ornament on the forehead, and an emblem of power and majesty (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa1002.htm#010">Sa1 2:10</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa132.htm#017">Psa 132:17</a>; see on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jer048.htm#025">Jer 48:25</a>). 
 <p>
 </p><p>drawn back . . . fight hand-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa074.htm#011">Psa 74:11</a>). God has withdrawn the help which He before gave them. Not as HENDERSON, "He has turned back his (Israel's) right hand" (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa089.htm#043">Psa 89:43</a>).
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#004">Lamentations 2:4</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_004">lam 2:4</a></font></span></p>(<a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa063.htm#010">Isa 63:10</a>). 
 <p>
 </p><p>stood with . . . right hand--He took His stand so as to use His right hand as an adversary. HENDERSON makes the image to be that of an archer steadying his right hand to take aim. Not only did He withdraw His help, but also took arms against Israel. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>all . . . pleasant to . . . eye-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/eze024.htm#025">Eze 24:25</a>). All that were conspicuous for youth, beauty, and rank. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>in . . . tabernacle--the dwellings of Jerusalem.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#005">Lamentations 2:5</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_005">lam 2:5</a></font></span></p>an enemy-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/jer030.htm#014">Jer 30:14</a>). 
 <p>
 </p><p>mourning and lamentation--There is a play of similar sounds in the original, "sorrow and sadness," to heighten the effect (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/job030.htm#003">Job 30:3</a>, Hebrew; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/eze035.htm#003">Eze 35:3</a>, Margin).
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#006">Lamentations 2:6</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_006">lam 2:6</a></font></span></p>tabernacle--rather, "He hath violently taken away His hedge (the hedge of the place sacred to Him, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa080.htm#012">Psa 80:12</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa089.htm#040">Psa 89:40</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa005.htm#005">Isa 5:5</a>), as that of a garden" [MAURER]. CALVIN supports English Version, "His tabernacle (that is, temple) as (one would take away the temporary cottage or booth) of a garden." <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa001.htm#008">Isa 1:8</a> accords with this (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/job027.htm#018">Job 27:18</a>). 
 <p>
 </p><p>places of . . . assembly--the temple and synagogues (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa074.htm#007">Psa 74:7-8</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>solemn feasts-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam001.htm#004">Lam 1:4</a>).
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#007">Lamentations 2:7</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_007">lam 2:7</a></font></span></p>they . . . made a noise in . . . house of . . . Lord, as in . . . feast--The foe's shout of triumph in the captured temple bore a resemblance (but oh, how sad a contrast as to the occasion of it!) to the joyous thanksgivings we used to offer in the same place at our "solemn feasts" (compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#022">Lam 2:22</a>).
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#008">Lamentations 2:8</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_008">lam 2:8</a></font></span></p>stretched . . . a line--The Easterns used a measuring-line not merely in building, but in destroying edifices (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/kg2021.htm#013">Kg2 21:13</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa034.htm#011">Isa 34:11</a>); implying here the unsparing rigidness with which He would exact punishment.
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#009">Lamentations 2:9</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_009">lam 2:9</a></font></span></p>Her gates cannot oppose the entrance of the foe into the city, for they are sunk under a mass of rubbish and earth. 
 <p>
 </p><p>broken . . . bars-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/jer051.htm#030">Jer 51:30</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>her king . . . among . . . Gentiles-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/deu028.htm#036">Deu 28:36</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>law . . . no more-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/ch2015.htm#003">Ch2 15:3</a>). The civil and religious laws were one under the theocracy. "All the legal ordinances (prophetical as well as priestly) of the theocracy, are no more" (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa074.htm#009">Psa 74:9</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/eze007.htm#026">Eze 7:26</a>).
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#010">Lamentations 2:10</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_010">lam 2:10</a></font></span></p>(<a href="../../../bib/kjv/job002.htm#012">Job 2:12-13</a>). The "elders," by their example, would draw the others to violent grief. 
 <p>
 </p><p>the virgins--who usually are so anxious to set off their personal appearances to advantage.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#011">Lamentations 2:11</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_011">lam 2:11</a></font></span></p>liver is poured, &amp;c.--that is, as the liver was thought to be the seat of the passions, "all my feelings are poured out and prostrated for," &amp;c. The "liver," is here put for the bile ("gall," <a href="../../../bib/kjv/job016.htm#013">Job 16:13</a>; "bowels," <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa022.htm#014">Psa 22:14</a>) in a bladder on the surface of the liver, copiously discharged when the passions are agitated. 
 <p>
 </p><p>swoon--through faintness from the effects of hunger.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#012">Lamentations 2:12</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_012">lam 2:12</a></font></span></p>as the wounded--famine being as deadly as the sword (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/jer052.htm#006">Jer 52:6</a>). 
 <p>
 </p><p>soul . . . poured . . . into . . . mothers bosom--Instinctively turning to their mother's bosom, but finding no milk there, they breathe out their life as it were "into her bosom."
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#013">Lamentations 2:13</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_013">lam 2:13</a></font></span></p>What thing shall I take to witness--What can I bring forward as a witness, or instance, to prove that others have sustained as grievous ills as thou? I cannot console thee as mourners are often consoled by showing that thy lot is only what others, too, suffer. The "sea" affords the only suitable emblem of thy woes, by its boundless extent and depth (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam001.htm#012">Lam 1:12</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/dan009.htm#012">Dan 9:12</a>).
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#014">Lamentations 2:14</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_014">lam 2:14</a></font></span></p>Thy prophets--not God's (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/jer023.htm#026">Jer 23:26</a>). 
 <p>
 </p><p>vain . . . for thee--to gratify thy appetite, not for truth, but for false things. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>not discovered thine iniquity--in opposition to God's command to the true prophets (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa058.htm#001">Isa 58:1</a>). Literally, "They have not taken off (the veil) which was on thine iniquity, so as to set it before thee." 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>burdens--Their prophecies were soothing and flattering; but the result of them was heavy calamities to the people, worse than even what the prophecies of Jeremiah, which they in derision called "burdens," threatened. Hence he terms their pretended prophecies "false burdens," which proved to the Jews "causes of their banishment" [CALVIN].
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#015">Lamentations 2:15</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_015">lam 2:15</a></font></span></p>clap . . . hands--in derision (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/job027.htm#023">Job 27:23</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/job034.htm#037">Job 34:37</a>). 
 <p>
 </p><p>wag . . . head-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/kg2019.htm#021">Kg2 19:21</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa044.htm#014">Psa 44:14</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>perfection of beauty . . . joy of . . . earth-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa048.htm#002">Psa 48:2</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa050.htm#002">Psa 50:2</a>). The Jews' enemies quote their very words in scorn.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#016">Lamentations 2:16</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_016">lam 2:16</a></font></span></p>For the transposition of Hebrew letters (Pe and Ain, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#016">Lam 2:16-17</a>) in the order of verses, see Introduction. 
 <p>
 </p><p>opened . . . mouth--as ravening, roaring wild beasts (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/job016.htm#009">Job 16:9-10</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa022.htm#013">Psa 22:13</a>). Herein Jerusalem was a type of Messiah. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>gnash . . . teeth--in vindictive malice. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>we have seen it-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa035.htm#021">Psa 35:21</a>).
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#017">Lamentations 2:17</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_017">lam 2:17</a></font></span></p>Lord--Let not the foe exult as if it was their doing. It was "the Lord" who thus fulfilled the threats uttered by His prophets for the guilt of Judea (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev026.htm#016">Lev 26:16-25</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/deu028.htm#036">Deu 28:36-48</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/deu028.htm#053">Deu 28:53</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jer019.htm#009">Jer 19:9</a>).
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#018">Lamentations 2:18</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_018">lam 2:18</a></font></span></p>wall-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#008">Lam 2:8</a>). Personified. "Their heart," that is, the Jews'; while their heart is lifted up to the Lord in prayer, their speech is addressed to the "wall" (the part being put for the whole city). 
 <p>
 </p><p>let tears, &amp;c.-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/jer014.htm#017">Jer 14:17</a>). The wall is called on to weep for its own ruin and that of the city. Compare the similar personification (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam001.htm#004">Lam 1:4</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>apple--the pupil of the eye (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa017.htm#008">Psa 17:8</a>).
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#019">Lamentations 2:19</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_019">lam 2:19</a></font></span></p>cry . . . in . . . night-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa119.htm#147">Psa 119:147</a>). 
 <p>
 </p><p>beginning of . . . watches--that is, the first of the three equal divisions (four hours each) into which the ancient Jews divided the night; namely, from sunset to ten o'clock. The second was called "the middle watch" (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/jdg007.htm#019">Jdg 7:19</a>), from ten till two o'clock. The third, "the morning watch," from two to sunrise (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/exo014.htm#024">Exo 14:24</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa1011.htm#011">Sa1 11:11</a>). Afterwards, under the Romans, they had four watches (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat014.htm#025">Mat 14:25</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk012.htm#038">Luk 12:38</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>for . . . thy . . . children--that God, if He will not spare thee, may at least preserve "thy young children." 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>top of . . . street-- (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa051.htm#020">Isa 51:20</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/nah003.htm#010">Nah 3:10</a>).
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#020">Lamentations 2:20</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_020">lam 2:20</a></font></span></p>women eat . . . fruit--as threatened (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev026.htm#029">Lev 26:29</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/deu028.htm#053">Deu 28:53</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/deu028.htm#056">Deu 28:56-57</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jer019.htm#009">Jer 19:9</a>). 
 <p>
 </p><p>children . . . span long--or else, "children whom they carry in their arms" [MAURER].
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#021">Lamentations 2:21</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_021">lam 2:21</a></font></span></p>(<a href="../../../bib/kjv/ch2036.htm#017">Ch2 36:17</a>).
 <p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam002.htm#022">Lamentations 2:22</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_022">lam 2:22</a></font></span></p>Thou hast called as in . . . solemn day . . . terrors--Thou hast summoned my enemies against me from all quarters, just as multitudes used to be convened to Jerusalem, on the solemn feast days. The objects, for which the enemies and the festal multitude respectively met, formed a sad contrast. Compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam001.htm#015">Lam 1:15</a> : "called an assembly against me."
 <p>
 </p><p>Jeremiah proposes his own experience under afflictions, as an example as to how the Jews should behave under theirs, so as to have hope of a restoration; hence the change from singular to plural (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam003.htm#022">Lam 3:22</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lam003.htm#040">Lam 3:40-47</a>). The stanzas consist of three lines, each of which begins with the same Hebrew letter.
 </p><p>
