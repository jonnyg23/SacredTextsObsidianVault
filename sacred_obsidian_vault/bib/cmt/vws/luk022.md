 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>Vincent's Word Studies</i>, by Marvin R. Vincent, [1886], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <h3 align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#001">Luke Chapter 22</a></h3> 
 <p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#001">Luke 22:1</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_001">luk 22:1</a></font></span></p>

 </p><p>Properly festival. See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar014.htm#001">Mar 14:1</a>.
 </p><p>Drew nigh
 </p><p>Imperfect: "was drawing nigh."

 </p><p>Kill (ἀνέλωσιν)
 </p><p>Lit., to take up and carry off, and so to make way with.

 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#019">Luke 22:19</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_019">luk 22:19</a></font></span></p>

 </p><p>Better, a loaf. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#020">Luke 22:20</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_020">luk 22:20</a></font></span></p>
 <p>The cup
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar014.htm#023">Mar 14:23</a>.
 </p><p>Testament (διαθήκη) - shed
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat026.htm#028">Mat 26:28</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#021">Luke 22:21</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_021">luk 22:21</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Betrayeth (παραδιδόντος)
 </p><p>The present participle: is now engaged in betraying.
 </p><p>With me
 </p><p>"He does not say with you: thus separating the traitor from the rest of the disciples, and showing that now he alone has to do with that wretch, as with an enemy" (Bengel).

 </p><p>Greatest

 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#026">Luke 22:26</a></p>

 <p>Doth serve
 </p><p>See on minister, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat020.htm#026">Mat 20:26</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#028">Luke 22:28</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_028">luk 22:28</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Continued (διαμεμενηκότες)
 </p><p>Lit., "have remained through" (διά). 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#029">Luke 22:29</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_029">luk 22:29</a></font></span></p>
 <p>I appoint (διατίθεμαι)
 </p><p>Implying allotment: assigning in the course of distribution (διά). Wyc., dispose. Luke is especially fond of compounds with διά. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#031">Luke 22:31</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_031">luk 22:31</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Hath desired (ἐξῃτήσατο)
 </p><p>Only here in New Testament. It sometimes means to obtain by asking, or to beg off. So Xenophon, "Anabasis," i., 1, 3. The mother of Cyrus, who is charged with an attempt to kill his brother, begged him off (ἐξαιτησαμένη). Rev., in margin, obtained you by asking. The result proved that Satan had obtained him for the time.
 </p><p>Sift (σινιάσαι)
 </p><p>Only here in New Testament.
 </p><p>Wheat (σῖτον)
 </p><p>A general term, grain. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#032">Luke 22:32</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_032">luk 22:32</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Prayed (ἐδεήθην)
 </p><p>See on prayers, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk005.htm#033">Luk 5:33</a>.
 </p><p>Art converted (ἐπιστρέψας)
 </p><p>Converted is simply the Latinized rendering of the word to turn round (convertere). Rev. renders the aorist participle, denoting a definite act, by once: "when once thou hast turned again."
 </p><p>Strengthen (στήρισον)
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk016.htm#025">Luk 16:25</a>, and <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pe1005.htm#010">Pe1 5:10</a>. Rev., stablish, which is much better. Strengthen may denote only a temporary effect. The word implies fixedness. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#034">Luke 22:34</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_034">luk 22:34</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Peter
 </p><p>The only instance of Christ's directly addressing him as Peter. He refers to him by that name, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar016.htm#007">Mar 16:7</a>.
 </p><p>The cock
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat026.htm#034">Mat 26:34</a>
 </p><p>Deny
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar014.htm#030">Mar 14:30</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#036">Luke 22:36</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_036">luk 22:36</a></font></span></p>
 <p>He that hath no sword, etc
 </p><p>But sword is not governed by hath. It is too far off in the sentence. The meaning is, he that hath not purse or scrip (and is therefore penniless), let him sell his garment and buy a sword. So Wyc. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#037">Luke 22:37</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_037">luk 22:37</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Have an end (τέλος ἔχει)
 </p><p>The phrase is synonymous with be accomplished (τελεσθῆναι, Rev., fulfilled). In classical Greek this latter word is often used of the fulfilment of an oracle: also of things which are settled beyond controversy. The two expressions here give the two meanings. The prophecy is fulfilled; the things concerning me are finally settled.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#040">Luke 22:40</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_040">luk 22:40</a></font></span></p>
 <p>The place
 </p><p>See on Gethsemane, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat026.htm#036">Mat 26:36</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#041">Luke 22:41</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_041">luk 22:41</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Was withdrawn (ἀπεσπάσθη)
 </p><p>The Vulgate has avulsus est, "he was torn away," as by an inward urgency. Godet adopts this view, and so, apparently, Wyc., he was taken away. Meyer inclines to it; De Wette decidedly rejects it. Compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/act021.htm#001">Act 21:1</a>.
 </p><p>Prayed
 </p><p>Imperfect, began to pray. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#043">Luke 22:43</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_043">luk 22:43</a></font></span></p>
 <p>There appeared (ὤφθη)
 </p><p>The word most commonly used in the New Testament of seeing visions. See <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat017.htm#003">Mat 17:3</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar009.htm#004">Mar 9:4</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk001.htm#011">Luk 1:11</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#043">Luk 22:43</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/act002.htm#017">Act 2:17</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/act007.htm#035">Act 7:35</a>. The kindred noun ὀπτασία, wherever it occurs in the New Testament, means a vision. See <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk001.htm#002">Luk 1:2</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk024.htm#023">Luk 24:23</a>, etc.
 </p><p>Strengthening (ἐνισχύων)
 </p><p>Only here and <a href="../../../bib/kjv/act009.htm#019">Act 9:19</a>. See on was not able, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk014.htm#030">Luk 14:30</a>; and cannot, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk016.htm#003">Luk 16:3</a>. Commonly intransitive; to prevail in or among. Used transitively only by Hippocrates and Luke. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#044">Luke 22:44</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_044">luk 22:44</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Being in an agony (γενόμενος ἐν ἀγωνίᾳ)
 </p><p>There is in the aorist participle a suggestion of a growing intensity in the struggle, which is not conveyed by the simple being. Literally, though very awkwardly, it is, having become in an agony: having progressed from the first prayer (began to pray, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#041">Luk 22:41</a>) into an intense struggle of prayer and sorrow. Wycliffe's rendering hints at this: and he, made in agony, prayed. Agony occurs only here. It is used by medical writers, and the fact of a sweat accompanying an agony is also mentioned by them.
 </p><p>More earnestly (ἐκτενέστερον)
 </p><p>See on fervently, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pe1001.htm#022">Pe1 1:22</a>.
 </p><p>Was (ἐγένετο)
 </p><p>More correctly, as Rev., became. See on γενόμενος, being, above.
 </p><p>Great drops (θρόμβοι)
 </p><p>Only here in New Testament: gouts or clots. Very common in medical language. Aristotle mentions a bloody sweat arising from the blood being in poor condition; and Theophrastus mentions a physician who compared a species of sweat to blood. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#045">Luke 22:45</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_045">luk 22:45</a></font></span></p>
 <p>For sorrow
 </p><p>The mention of the cause of the drowsiness is characteristic.

 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#051">Luke 22:51</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_051">luk 22:51</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Suffer ye thus far
 </p><p>This is variously interpreted. I think the text requires that the words should be addressed to the disciples, and taken as the answer to the question, shall we smite, etc. The meaning then is, permit them to go so far as to seize me. The expression thus corresponds with <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat026.htm#052">Mat 26:52</a>,
 </p><p>Ear (ὠτίου)
 </p><p>This time Luke uses the diminutive. Wyc., little ear.
 </p><p>Healed
 </p><p>Only Luke records the healing. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#052">Luke 22:52</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_052">luk 22:52</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Thief (λῃστὴν)
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat026.htm#055">Mat 26:55</a>; and <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk010.htm#030">Luk 10:30</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar011.htm#017">Mar 11:17</a>.
 </p><p> 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#055">Luke 22:55</a></p>

 </p><p>Hall
 </p><p>Or court. See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar014.htm#054">Mar 14:54</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#056">Luke 22:56</a></p>

 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar014.htm#054">Mar 14:54</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#063">Luke 22:63</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_063">luk 22:63</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Smote (δέροντες)
 </p><p>Originally to flay; thence to cudgel. Compare our vulgarism, to tan or hide. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk022.htm#066">Luke 22:66</a></p>
