 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#004">Luke 21:4</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_004">luk 21:4</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Offerings of God
 </p><p>The best texts omit of God. Rev., more simply, unto the gifts.
 </p><p>Penury (ὑστερήματος)
 </p><p>Lit., lack. Rev., neatly, of her want.

 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#005">Luke 21:5</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_005">luk 21:5</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Stones
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar013.htm#001">Mar 13:1</a>.
 </p><p>Offerings (ἀναθήμασιν)
 </p><p>Only here in New Testament. From ἀνατίθημι, to set up. Hence of something set up in the temple as a votive offering. Such were the golden vines presented by Herod the Great, with bunches of grapes as large as a man, and mounted above the entrance to the holy place. The magnificent porch of the temple was adorned with many such dedicated gifts, such as a golden wreath which Sosius offered after he had taken Jerusalem in conjunction with Herod; and rich flagons which Augustus and his wife had given to the sanctuary. Gifts were bestowed by princes friendly to Israel, both on the temple and on provincial synagogues. The word ἀνάθεμθ (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/gal001.htm#008">Gal 1:8</a>, Rev.), is the same word, something devoted, and so devoted to evil and accursed. Luke uses the classical form. The other is the common or Hellenistic form. The two forms develop gradually a divergence in meaning; the one signifying devoted in a good, the other in a bad sense. The same process may be observed in other languages. Thus knave, lad, becomes a rascal: villain, a farmer, becomes a scoundrel: cunning, skilful, becomes crafty.

 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#009">Luke 21:9</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_009">luk 21:9</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Commotions (ἀκαταστασίας)
 </p><p>From ἀ, not, and καθίστημι, to establish. Hence disestablishments; unsettlements. Rev., tumults.
 </p><p>Be not terrified (μὴ πτοηθῆτε)
 </p><p>Only here and <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk024.htm#037">Luk 24:37</a>.
 </p><p>By and by (εὐθέως)
 </p><p>Better as Rev., immediately. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#011">Luke 21:11</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_011">luk 21:11</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Earthquakes
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar013.htm#007">Mar 13:7</a>.
 </p><p>Famines and pestilences (λιμοὶ καὶ λοιμοὶ)
 </p><p>Some texts reverse the order of the words. A paronomasia or combination of like-sounding words: limoi, loimoi. Especially common in Paul's epistles.
 </p><p>Fearful sights (φοβητρά)
 </p><p>Only here in New Testament, and rare in classical Greek. In Septuagint, Isaiah 19:17. Not confined to sights, but fearful things. Rev., better, terrors. Used in medical language by Hippocrates, of fearful objects imagined by the sick.

 </p><p>Lit., turn out; issue.

 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#023">Luke 21:23</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_023">luk 21:23</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Distress (ἀνάγκη)
 </p><p>Originally constraint, necessity; thence force or violence, and in the classical poets, distress, anguish. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#024">Luke 21:24</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_024">luk 21:24</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Edge (στόματι)
 </p><p>Lit., the mouth. So Wyc. Either in the sense of the foremost part, or picturing the sword as a devouring monster. In <a href="../../../bib/kjv/heb011.htm#033">Heb 11:33</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/heb011.htm#034">Heb 11:34</a>, the word is used in both senses: "the mouths of lions;" "the edge of the sword."
 </p><p>Led away captive
 </p><p>See on captives, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk004.htm#018">Luk 4:18</a>.
 </p><p>Trodden down
 </p><p>Denoting the oppression and contempt which shall follow conquest. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#025">Luke 21:25</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_025">luk 21:25</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Signs (σημεῖα)
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat024.htm#024">Mat 24:24</a>.
 </p><p>Distress (συνοχὴ)
 </p><p>Only here and <a href="../../../bib/kjv/co2002.htm#004">Co2 2:4</a>. Kindred with συνεχομένη, taken (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk004.htm#038">Luk 4:38</a>), on which see note. The original idea of the word is being held in a tight grasp.
 </p><p>With perplexity, the sea and the waves roaring
 </p><p>The A. V. follows the reading ἠχούσης, the participle, roaring. The proper reading is ἠχοῦς the noun, the roaring. Render perplexity for the roaring of the sea, etc. Ἠχώ, roaring, is properly a returned sound, an echo. Generally a ringing sound, as of the blows on an anvil.
 </p><p>Waves (σάλου)
 </p><p>Only here in New Testament. The radical notion of the word is unsteady motion, especially the rolling swell of the sea. Rev., better, billows. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#026">Luke 21:26</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_026">luk 21:26</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Failing (ἀποψυχόντων)
 </p><p>Only here in New Testament. The word originally means to leave off breathing; to swoon. Thus Homer, when Laertes recognizes Ulysses:
 </p><p>"He threw
 </p><p>Round his dear son his arms. The hardy chief,
 </p><p>Ulysses, drew him fainting (ἀποψύχοντα) to his heart."
 </p><p>Odyssey, xxiv., 846.
 </p><p>So also Sophocles, of Hector dragged behind Achilles' chariot:
 </p><p>"He breathed out his life (ἀπέψυξεν βίον).
 </p><p>Ajax, 1031.
 </p><p>Matthew alone uses the simple verb, ψύχω, to breathe or blow. See on wax cold, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat024.htm#012">Mat 24:12</a>. Luke uses four compounds of this simple verb, all of which are peculiar to him. Compare cool, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk016.htm#024">Luk 16:24</a>; refreshing, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/act003.htm#019">Act 3:19</a>; gave up the ghost, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/act005.htm#005">Act 5:5</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/act005.htm#010">Act 5:10</a>.
 </p><p>Expectation (προσδοκίας)
 </p><p>Only here and <a href="../../../bib/kjv/act012.htm#011">Act 12:11</a>.
 </p><p>The world
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk002.htm#001">Luk 2:1</a>.
 </p><p>Shall be shaken (σαλευθήσονται)
 </p><p>Compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat011.htm#007">Mat 11:7</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk006.htm#038">Luk 6:38</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/act004.htm#031">Act 4:31</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/heb012.htm#026">Heb 12:26</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/heb012.htm#027">Heb 12:27</a>. The root of the verb is the same as that of billows, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#025">Luk 21:25</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#028">Luke 21:28</a></p>

 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#030">Luke 21:30</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_030">luk 21:30</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Ye see (βλέποντες)
 </p><p>Lit., "looking, ye know," etc. Implying careful observation, with a view to determine the progress of the season.
 </p><p>Know (γινώσκετε)
 </p><p>Perceive would be better. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#031">Luke 21:31</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_031">luk 21:31</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Come to pass (γινόμενα)
 </p><p>The present participle. Rev., more correctly, "coming to pass'" in process of fulfilment. Compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar013.htm#029">Mar 13:29</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#034">Luke 21:34</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_034">luk 21:34</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Overcharged (βαρηζῶσιν)
 </p><p>Weighed down. Compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk009.htm#032">Luk 9:32</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/co2005.htm#004">Co2 5:4</a>.
 </p><p>Surfeiting (κραιπάλῃ)
 </p><p>Only here in New Testament. Derivation uncertain: akin to the Latin crapula, intoxication. Trench finds an equivalent in fulsomeness, in its original sense of fulness. In the medical writings it is used of drunken nausea or headache.
 </p><p>Drunkenness (μέθῃ)
 </p><p>Compare are well drunk, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/joh002.htm#010">Joh 2:10</a>. This and kindred words in the New Testament always refer to intoxication, or that which intoxicates. See note on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/joh002.htm#010">Joh 2:10</a>.
 </p><p>Cares (μερίμναις)
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat006.htm#025">Mat 6:25</a>.
 </p><p>Of this life (βιωτικαῖς)
 </p><p>The rendering is too general; though it might be difficult to give a better. Βίος, life, means life considered either as to its duration (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/pe1004.htm#003">Pe1 4:3</a>); the means of support (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar012.htm#044">Mar 12:44</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk008.htm#043">Luk 8:43</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#004">Luk 21:4</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jo1003.htm#017">Jo1 3:17</a>); or the manner of leading it (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/ti1002.htm#002">Ti1 2:2</a>). The meaning here is pertaining to the support or luxury of life; and so in the only other passages where it occurs, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/co1006.htm#003">Co1 6:3</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/co1006.htm#004">Co1 6:4</a>. The parallel is <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat006.htm#031">Mat 6:31</a>. Wyc., business of this life.
 </p><p>Suddenly (αἰφνίδιος)
 </p><p>Only here and <a href="../../../bib/kjv/th1005.htm#003">Th1 5:3</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk021.htm#035">Luke 21:35</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_035">luk 21:35</a></font></span></p>
 <p>As a snare

 </p><p>Only here and <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat021.htm#017">Mat 21:17</a>.
