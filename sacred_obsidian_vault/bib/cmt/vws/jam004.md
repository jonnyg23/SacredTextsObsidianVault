 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>Vincent's Word Studies</i>, by Marvin R. Vincent, [1886], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <h3 align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#001">James Chapter 4</a></h3> 
 <p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#001">James 4:1</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_001">jam 4:1</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Lusts (ἡδονῶν)
 </p><p>Lit., pleasures, as Rev. Properly, sensual pleasures. The sinful pleasures are the outgrowths of the lusts, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#002">Jam 4:2</a>.
 </p><p>That war (στρατευομένων)
 </p><p>The thought of wars and rightings is carried into the figurative description of the sensuality which arrays its forces and carries on its campaign in the members. The verb does not imply mere fighting, but all that is included in military service. A remarkable parallel occurs in Plato, "Phaedo," 66: "For whence come wars and rightings and factions? Whence but from the body and the lusts of the body?" Compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pe1002.htm#011">Pe1 2:11</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/rom007.htm#023">Rom 7:23</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#002">James 4:2</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_002">jam 4:2</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Ye lust
 </p><p>See on desire, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pe1001.htm#012">Pe1 1:12</a>; and <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar004.htm#019">Mar 4:19</a>.
 </p><p>Desire to have (ζηλοῦτε)
 </p><p>Rev., covet, and are jealous, in margin. See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam003.htm#014">Jam 3:14</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#003">James 4:3</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_003">jam 4:3</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Ye ask (αἰτεῖτε)
 </p><p>See on ἠρώτων, besought, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat015.htm#023">Mat 15:23</a>.
 </p><p>Amiss (κακῶς)
 </p><p>Lit., evilly: with evil intent, as explained by the following sentence.
 </p><p>Consume it upon (δαπανησήτε ἐν)
 </p><p>More correctly, as Rev., spend it in. The sense is not lay out expense upon your pleasures, but spend in the exercise of; under the dominion of. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#004">James 4:4</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_004">jam 4:4</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Ye adulterers (μοιχοὶ)
 </p><p>All the best texts omit.
 </p><p>Adulteresses (μοιχαλίδες)
 </p><p>The feminine term is the general designation of all whom James here rebukes. The apostate members of the church are figuratively regarded as unfaithful spouses; according to the common Old-Testament figure, in which God is the bridegroom or husband to whom his people are wedded. See Jeremiah 3; Hosea 2, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/hos003.htm#001">Hos 3:1-5</a>, 4; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa054.htm#005">Isa 54:5</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa062.htm#004">Isa 62:4</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa062.htm#005">Isa 62:5</a>. Also, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat012.htm#039">Mat 12:39</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/co2011.htm#002">Co2 11:2</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/rev019.htm#007">Rev 19:7</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/rev021.htm#009">Rev 21:9</a>.
 </p><p>Will be (βουληθῇ εἶναι)
 </p><p>More correctly, as Rev., would be. Lit., may have been minded to be.
 </p><p>Is the enemy (καθίσταται)
 </p><p>Thereby constitutes himself. Rev., maketh himself. See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam003.htm#006">Jam 3:6</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#005">James 4:5</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_005">jam 4:5</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Do ye think (δοκεῖτε)
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam001.htm#026">Jam 1:26</a>.
 </p><p>The scripture (ἡ γραφὴ)
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar012.htm#010">Mar 12:10</a>. Properly, a passage of scripture.
 </p><p>In vain (κενῶς)
 </p><p>Only here in New Testament. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#006">James 4:6</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_006">jam 4:6</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Resisteth
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pe1005.htm#005">Pe1 5:5</a>.
 </p><p>Proud
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar007.htm#022">Mar 7:22</a>.
 </p><p>Humble
 </p><p>See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat007.htm#029">Mat 7:29</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#007">James 4:7</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_007">jam 4:7</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Submit yourselves (ὑποτάγητε)
 </p><p>Rev., be subject. The verb means to place or arrange under; as resist (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#006">Jam 4:6</a>) is to array against. God sets himself in array against the proud; therefore, array yourselves under God, that ye may withstand the devil. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#008">James 4:8</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_008">jam 4:8</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Purify (ἁγνίσατε)
 </p><p>One of the three instances in the New Testament in which the word is not used of ceremonial purification. The others are <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pe1001.htm#022">Pe1 1:22</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jo1003.htm#003">Jo1 3:3</a>.
 </p><p>Double-minded (δίψυχοι)
 </p><p>Compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam001.htm#008">Jam 1:8</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#009">James 4:9</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_009">jam 4:9</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Be afflicted (ταλαιπώρησατε)
 </p><p>Only here in New Testament. The kindred noun ταλαιπωρία, misery, occurs <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam005.htm#001">Jam 5:1</a>.
 </p><p>Mourn (πενθήσατε)
 </p><p>Used of grief that is manifested. So mostly in New Testament, and very commonly joined, as here, with weep. So <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mar016.htm#010">Mar 16:10</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk006.htm#025">Luk 6:25</a>, etc. In the next sentence occurs the kindred noun πένθος, mourning, into which laughter, also something manifest, is to be changed.
 </p><p>Heaviness (κατήφειαν)
 </p><p>Properly, a casting down of the eyes. Compare <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk018.htm#013">Luk 18:13</a>. Only here in New Testament. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#012">James 4:12</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_012">jam 4:12</a></font></span></p>
 <p>There is one lawgiver (εἶς ἐστὶν ὁ νομοθέτης)
 </p><p>The A. V. fails to note the emphatic position of one. Better, Rev., one only is the lawgiver. Νομοθέτης, lawgiver, only here in New Testament.
 </p><p>But who art thou? (σὺ δὲ τίς εἶ)
 </p><p>According to the Greek order: but thou, who art thou? 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#013">James 4:13</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_013">jam 4:13</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Go to now (ἄγε νῦν)
 </p><p>Go to is an obsolete phrase, though retained in Rev. It is a formula for calling attention: come now.
 </p><p>Such a city (τήνδε τὴν πόλιν)
 </p><p>More accurately, as Rev., this city.
 </p><p>Continue there a year (ποιήσομεν ἐκεῖ ἐνιαυτὸν)
 </p><p>Lit., we will make a year. See, for the same form of expression, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/act015.htm#033">Act 15:33</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/act018.htm#023">Act 18:23</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/co2011.htm#025">Co2 11:25</a>. Better, as Rev., spend a year there. (Compare the A. V., <a href="../../../bib/kjv/act018.htm#023">Act 18:23</a>, rightly retained by Rev.) The word ποιήσομεν implies more than mere continuance; rather, a doing something with the year.
 </p><p>And
 </p><p>The frequent use of the copulative gives a lively tone to the passage, expressive of the lightness and thoughtlessness of a careless spirit.
 </p><p>Buy and sell (ἐμπορευσόμεθα)
 </p><p>Rev., more concisely, trade. Only here and <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pe2002.htm#003">Pe2 2:3</a>. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#014">James 4:14</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_014">jam 4:14</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Whereas ye know not (οἵτινες οὐκ ἐπίστασθε)
 </p><p>The pronoun marking a class, as being of those who know not.
 </p><p>What shall be on the morrow (τὸ τῆς αὔριον)
 </p><p>Lit., the thing of the morrow. The texts vary. Westcott and Hort read, Ye know not what your life shall be on the morrow, for ye are a vapor: thus throwing out the question.
 </p><p>What is your life? (ποία)
 </p><p>Lit., of what kind or nature.
 </p><p>It is even a vapor (ἀτμὶς γάρ ἐστιν)
 </p><p>But all the best texts read ἐστε, ye are. So Rev., which, however, retains the question, what is your life ?
 </p><p>Appeareth - vanisheth
 </p><p>Both participles, appearing, vanishing.
 </p><p>And then (ἔπειτα καὶ)
 </p><p>The καὶ placed after the adverb then is not copulative, but expresses that the vapor vanishes even as it appeared. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#015">James 4:15</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_015">jam 4:15</a></font></span></p>
 <p>For that ye ought to say (ἀντὶ τοῦ λέγειν ὑμᾶς)
 </p><p><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#014">Jam 4:14</a> was parenthetical, so that at this point the thought is taken up from <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#013">Jam 4:13</a> : Ye who say we will go, etc. - for that ye ought to say. The rendering in margin of Rev. is simpler: instead of your saying. 
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam004.htm#016">James 4:16</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_016">jam 4:16</a></font></span></p>
 <p>Ye rejoice (καυχᾶσθε)
 </p><p>Rev., glory. See on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jam002.htm#013">Jam 2:13</a>.
 </p><p>Boastings (ἀλαζονείαις)
 </p><p>Only here and <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jo1002.htm#016">Jo1 2:16</a>. The kindred word ἀλαζών, a boaster, is derived from ἄλη, a wandering or roaming; hence, primarily, a vagabond, a quack, a mountebank. From the empty boasts of such concerning the cures and wonders they could perform, the word passed into the sense of boaster. One may boast truthfully; but ἀλαζονεία is false and swaggering boasting. Rev. renders vauntings, and rightly, since vaunt is from the Latin vanus, empty, and therefore expresses idle or vain boasting.
