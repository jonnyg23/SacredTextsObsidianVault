 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>The Scofield Bible Commentary</i>, by Cyrus Ingerson Scofield, [1917], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <h3 align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa045.htm#001">Psalms Chapter 45</a></h3> 
 <p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa045.htm#001">Psalms 45:1</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_001">psa 45:1</a></font></span></p>
 <p>
 </p><p>Shoshannim 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>Shoshannim, "lilies," and so, the spring; the Shoshannim Psalms were probably connected with the Passover season, and hence reminders of redemption out of bondage, and of the origins of Israel. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>
 </p><p>king 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>This great psalm of the King, with Psalms 46-47, obviously looks forward to the advent in glory. The reference in (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/heb001.htm#008">Heb 1:8</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/heb001.htm#009">Heb 1:9</a>) is not so much to the anointing as an event (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat003.htm#016">Mat 3:16</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat003.htm#017">Mat 3:17</a>) as to the permanent state of the King. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>Compare (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa011.htm#001">Isa 11:1-2</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>The divisions are: 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(1) The supreme beauty of the King (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa045.htm#001">Psa 45:1</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa045.htm#002">Psa 45:2</a>); 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(2) the coming of the King in glory (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa045.htm#003">Psa 45:3-5</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/rev019.htm#011">Rev 19:11-21</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(3) the deity of the King and character of His reign (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa045.htm#006">Psa 45:6</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa045.htm#007">Psa 45:7</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/heb001.htm#008">Heb 1:8</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/heb001.htm#009">Heb 1:9</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa011.htm#001">Isa 11:1-5</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(4) as associated with Him in earthly rule, the queen is presented, (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa045.htm#009">Psa 45:9-13</a>) and in that relation the King is not called Elohim 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(See Scofield) - (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen001.htm#001">Gen 1:1</a>) 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>as in verse 6 (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa045.htm#006">Psa 45:6</a>), but Adonai, the husband name of Deity 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(See Scofield) - (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen015.htm#002">Gen 15:2</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(5) the virgin companions of the queen, who would seem to be the Jewish remnant. (See Scofield) - (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/rom011.htm#005">Rom 11:5</a>). (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/rev014.htm#001">Rev 14:1-4</a>) are next seen (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa045.htm#014">Psa 45:14</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa045.htm#015">Psa 45:15</a>); and 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(6) the Psalm closes with a reference to the earthly fame of the King. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>See Psalm 68, next in order of the Messianic Psalms.
