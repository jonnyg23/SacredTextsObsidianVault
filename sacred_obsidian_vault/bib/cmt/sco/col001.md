 <p>
 </p><p>reconciled 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>Reconciliation. The Greek word signifies "to change thoroughly from," and occurs in (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/rom005.htm#010">Rom 5:10</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/rom011.htm#015">Rom 11:15</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/co1007.htm#011">Co1 7:11</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/co2005.htm#018">Co2 5:18</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/co2005.htm#019">Co2 5:19</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/co2005.htm#020">Co2 5:20</a>). Reconciliation looks toward the effect of the death of christ upon man, as propitiation, 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(See Scofield) - (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/rom003.htm#025">Rom 3:25</a>), 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>is the Godward aspect, and is that effect of the death of Christ upon the believing sinner which, through divine power, works in him a "thorough change" toward God from enmity and aversion to love and trust. It is never said that God is reconciled. God is propitiated, the sinner reconciled. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>Compare (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/co2005.htm#018">Co2 5:18-21</a>).
