 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>The Scofield Bible Commentary</i>, by Cyrus Ingerson Scofield, [1917], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <h3 align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen017.htm#001">Genesis Chapter 17</a></h3> 
 <p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen017.htm#001">Genesis 17:1</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_001">gen 17:1</a></font></span></p>
 <p>
 </p><p>Almighty God 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>"Almighty God" (Hebrew, El Shaddai) 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(1) The etymological signification of Almighty God (El Shaddai) is both interesting and touching. God (El) signifies the "Strong One" (See Scofield) - (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen001.htm#001">Gen 1:1</a>). The qualifying word Shaddai is formed from the Hebrew word "shad," the breast, invariably used in Scripture for a woman's breast; for example (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen049.htm#025">Gen 49:25</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/job003.htm#012">Job 3:12</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa022.htm#009">Psa 22:9</a>); Song of (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/sol001.htm#013">Sol 1:13</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/sol004.htm#005">Sol 4:5</a>); Song of (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/sol007.htm#003">Sol 7:3</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/sol007.htm#007">Sol 7:7</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/sol007.htm#008">Sol 7:8</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/sol008.htm#001">Sol 8:1</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/sol008.htm#008">Sol 8:8</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/sol008.htm#010">Sol 8:10</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa028.htm#009">Isa 28:9</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/eze016.htm#007">Eze 16:7</a>). Shaddai therefore means primarily "the breasted." God is "Shaddai," because He is the Nourisher, the Strength-giver, and so, in a secondary sense, the Satisfier, who pours himself into believing lives. As a fretful, unsatisfied babe is not only strengthened and nourished from the mother's breast, but also is quieted, rested, satisfied, so El Shaddai is that name of God which sets Him forth as the Strength-giver and Satisfier of His people. It is on every account to be regretted that "Shaddai" was translated "Almighty." The primary name El or Elohim sufficiently signifies almightiness. "All-sufficient" would far better express both the Hebrew meaning and the characteristic use of the name in Scripture. 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(2) Almighty God (El Shaddai) not only enriches, but makes fruitful. This is nowhere better illustrated than in the first occurrence of the name (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen017.htm#001">Gen 17:1-8</a>). To a man ninety-nine years of age, and "as good as dead" (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/heb011.htm#012">Heb 11:12</a>). He said: "I am the Almighty God El Shaddai . . . I will . . . multiply thee exceedingly." To the same purport is the use of the name in (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen028.htm#003">Gen 28:3</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen028.htm#004">Gen 28:4</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(3) As Giver of fruitfulness, Almighty God (El Shaddai) chastens His people. For the moral connection of chastening with fruit bearing, see (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/joh015.htm#002">Joh 15:2</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/heb012.htm#010">Heb 12:10</a>); (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/rut001.htm#020">Rut 1:20</a>). Hence, Almighty is the characteristic name of God in Job, occurring thirty-one times in that book. The hand of El Shaddai falls upon Job, the best man of his time, not in judgment, but in purifying unto greater fruitfulness (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/job005.htm#017">Job 5:17-25</a>).
