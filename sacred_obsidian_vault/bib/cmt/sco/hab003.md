 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>The Scofield Bible Commentary</i>, by Cyrus Ingerson Scofield, [1917], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <h3 align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/hab003.htm#001">Habakkuk Chapter 3</a></h3> 
 <p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/hab003.htm#001">Habakkuk 3:1</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_001">hab 3:1</a></font></span></p>
 <p>
 </p><p>Prayer 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>Prayer in the Old Testament is in contrast with prayer in the New Testament in two respects: 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(1) In the former the basis of prayer is a covenant of God, or an appeal to his revealed character as merciful, gracious, etc. In the latter the basis is relationship: "When ye pray, say, Our Father" (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat006.htm#009">Mat 6:9</a>). 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>(2) A comparison, for example of the prayers of Moses and Paul, will show that one was praying for an earthly people whose dangers and blessings were earthly; the other for a heavenly people whose dangers and blessings were spiritual.  
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/hab003.htm#007">Habakkuk 3:7</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_007">hab 3:7</a></font></span></p>
 <p>
 </p><p>Cushan 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>Or, Ethiopia.  
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/hab003.htm#011">Habakkuk 3:11</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_011">hab 3:11</a></font></span></p>
 <p>
 </p><p>at the light 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>Or, thine arrows walked in the light, etc.  
 </p><p align="center"><a href="../../../bib/kjv/hab003.htm#017">Habakkuk 3:17</a></p> 
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_017">hab 3:17</a></font></span></p>
 <p>
 </p><p>Although 
 </p><p>
 </p><p>That is, despite the afflictions of Israel in dispersion, the prophet will rejoice because of the Lord, as yet to return to His temple.
