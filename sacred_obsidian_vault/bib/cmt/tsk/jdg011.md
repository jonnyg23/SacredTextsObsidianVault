 </p><p>an harlot:  Heb. a woman
 </p><p>an harlot:  Probably zonah should be rendered as in <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jos002.htm#001">Jos 2:1</a>, a hostess, or inn-keeper, so Targum of Jonathan, wehoo bar ittetha pundekeetha, "and he was the son of a woman, a tavern-keeper."  She was very probably a Canaanite, as she is called, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jdg011.htm#002">Jdg 11:2</a>, a strange woman, ishah achereth, "a woman of another race;" and on this account his brethren drove him from the family, as not having a full right to the inheritance.

 </p><p>Jephthah uttered:  That is, upon his elevation, he immediately retired to his devotion, and in prayer spread the whole matter before God, both his choice to the office, and his execution of the office, as one that had his eye ever toward the Lord, and would do nothing without him; that leaned not to his own understanding or courage, but depended on the Almighty God, and his favour.  This is an ensample worthy of universal imitation; in All Our Ways, whether great or apparently subordinate, let us acknowledge God and seek his direction. So shall we make our way prosperous, and obtain that peace which passeth all understanding.  Jephthah opened his campaign with prayer.

 <p>Wilt not thou possess:  This is simply an argumentum ad hominem; in which Jephthah argues on this principles recognized by the king of Ammon. Now we know that Jehovah, our God, has given us the land of the Israelites; and therefore we will not give it up."

 <p>did with:  That Jephthah did not sacrifice his daughter, but consecrated her to the service of God in the tabernacle, in a state of celibacy, will we imagine be evident from the following consideration  -  
 </p><p>1. Human sacrifices were ever an abomination to Jehovah, of which Jephthah could not be ignorant; and consequently he would neither have made such a vow, nor carried it into execution.  
 </p><p>2. We are expressly told (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/jdg011.htm#029">Jdg 11:29</a>) that Jephthah was under the influence of the Spirit of God, which would effectually prevent him from embruing his hands in the blood of his own child.  
 </p><p>3. He had it in his power to redeem his daughter (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev027.htm#004">Lev 27:4</a>); and surely his only child must have been of more value than thirty shekles.  
 </p><p>4. Besides, who was to perform the horrid rite?  Not Jephthah himself, who was no priest, and in whom it would have been most unnatural and inhuman; and the priests would certainly have dissuaded him from it.  
 </p><p>5. The sacred historian informs us, that she bewailed her virginity, that she knew no man, and that the Israelitish women went yearly to comfort or lament with her. <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jdg011.htm#031">Jdg 11:31</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev027.htm#028">Lev 27:28</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev027.htm#029">Lev 27:29</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/deu012.htm#031">Deu 12:31</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa066.htm#003">Isa 66:3</a>
