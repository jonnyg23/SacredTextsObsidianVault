 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_005">lev 24:5</a></font></span></p>
 <p>The loaves of bread which the officiating priest placed every sabbath day upon the golden table in the Sanctum, before the Lord, were twelve in number, representing the twelve tribes of Israel.  The loaves must have been large, since two tenth deals (about six pints) of flour were used for each, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev024.htm#003">Lev 24:3</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev024.htm#006">Lev 24:6</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev024.htm#007">Lev 24:7</a>.  They were served up hot on the sabbath day in the Sanctum, when the stale ones, which had been exposed the whole week, were taken away, and none but the priests were allowed to eat them.  In an extraordinary extremity, David and his men partook of the shew-bread (see note on <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa1021.htm#006">Sa1 21:6</a>), the urgent necessity alone justifying the act.  The Hebrew signifies bread of faces, or, of the face.

 <p>blasphemeth:  As the word nakav not only signifies to curse, or blaspheme, but also to express, or distinguish by name (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/num001.htm#017">Num 1:17</a>.  <a href="../../../bib/kjv/ch1012.htm#031">Ch1 12:31</a>.  <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa062.htm#002">Isa 62:2</a>), hence the Jews, at a very early period, understood this law as prohibiting them from uttering the name Jehovah, on any other than sacred occasions.
