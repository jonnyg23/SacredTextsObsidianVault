 <p>his head:  Riding furiously under the thick boughs of a great oak, which hung low and had never been cropped, either the twisted branches, or some low forked bough of the tree, caught him by the neck, or, as some think, by the loops into which his long hair had been pinned, which had been so much his pride, and was now justly made a halter for him.  He may have hung so low from the bough, in consequence of the length of his hair, that he could not use his hands to help himself, or so entangled that his hands were bound, so that the more he struggled the more he was embarrassed.  This set him up as a fair mark to the servants of David; and although David would have spared his rebellious son, if his orders had been executed, yet he could not turn the sword of Divine justice, in executing the just, righteous sentence of death on this traitorous son. <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2018.htm#014">Sa2 18:14</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2014.htm#026">Sa2 14:26</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2017.htm#023">Sa2 17:23</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat027.htm#005">Mat 27:5</a>

 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_017">sa2 18:17</a></font></span></p>
 <p>laid:  This was the ancient method of burying, whether heroes or traitors; the heap of stones being designed to perpetuate the memory of the event, whether good or bad.  The Arabs in general make use of no other monument than a heap of stones over a grave.  Thus, in an Arabic poem, it is related, that Hatim the father, and Adi the grandfather of Kais, having been murdered, at a time before Kais was capable of reflection, his mother kept it a profound secret; and in order to guard him against having any suspicion, she collected a parcel of stone on two hillocks in the neighbourhood, and told her son that the one was the grave of his father, and the other of his grandfather.  The ancient cairns in Ireland and Scotland, and the tumuli in England, are of this kind. <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jos007.htm#026">Jos 7:26</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jos008.htm#029">Jos 8:29</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jos010.htm#027">Jos 10:27</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/pro010.htm#007">Pro 10:7</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jer022.htm#018">Jer 22:18</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jer022.htm#019">Jer 22:19</a>

 </p><p>the Lord:  <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2018.htm#019">Sa2 18:19</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2018.htm#028">Sa2 18:28</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2022.htm#048">Sa2 22:48</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2022.htm#049">Sa2 22:49</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/deu032.htm#035">Deu 32:35</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/deu032.htm#036">Deu 32:36</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa058.htm#010">Psa 58:10</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa094.htm#001">Psa 94:1-4</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa124.htm#002">Psa 124:2</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa124.htm#003">Psa 124:3</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk018.htm#007">Luk 18:7</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/luk018.htm#008">Luk 18:8</a>; Cushi was the man Joab ordered to carry the tidings to David. He was an Ethiopian, as his name signifies, and some think he was so by birth  -  a black, who waited on Joab, probably one of the ten who had helped to dispatch Absalom; though it was dangerous for one of those to bring the news to David, lest his fate should be the same with theirs that reported the death of Saul and Ishbosheth to him.
