 <p>not make baldness:  This custom is also called rounding the corners of the head (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev019.htm#027">Lev 19:27</a>), and seems to have been performed in honour of some idol. <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev010.htm#006">Lev 10:6</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev019.htm#027">Lev 19:27</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev019.htm#028">Lev 19:28</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/deu014.htm#001">Deu 14:1</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa015.htm#002">Isa 15:2</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/isa022.htm#012">Isa 22:12</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jer016.htm#006">Jer 16:6</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/jer048.htm#037">Jer 48:37</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/eze044.htm#020">Eze 44:20</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/amo008.htm#010">Amo 8:10</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mic001.htm#016">Mic 1:16</a> 
 </p><p>the corner:  The Hebrew peath zakon, may denote the whiskers; as the Syriac phatho signifies.  These are by the Arabs, according to Niebuhr, still cut entirely off, or worn quite short; and hence they are called by Jeremiah, קצוצי  פאה, those with cropped whiskers.  Perhaps some superstition, of which we are ignorant, was connected with this; but whether or not, it was the object of Moses to keep the Israelites distinct from other nations.

 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_010">lev 21:10</a></font></span></p>
 <p>upon:  <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev008.htm#012">Lev 8:12</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev010.htm#007">Lev 10:7</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev016.htm#032">Lev 16:32</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/exo029.htm#029">Exo 29:29</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/exo029.htm#030">Exo 29:30</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/num035.htm#025">Num 35:25</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa133.htm#002">Psa 133:2</a> 
 </p><p>consecrated:  <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev008.htm#007">Lev 8:7-9</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/exo028.htm#002">Exo 28:2-4</a> 
 </p><p>uncover:  <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev010.htm#006">Lev 10:6</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev010.htm#007">Lev 10:7</a>, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/lev013.htm#045">Lev 13:45</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/sa2015.htm#030">Sa2 15:30</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/est006.htm#012">Est 6:12</a>, not rend, <a href="../../../bib/kjv/gen037.htm#034">Gen 37:34</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/job001.htm#020">Job 1:20</a>; <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat026.htm#065">Mat 26:65</a>; All human corpses were considered as unclean.  Whoever touched one was unclean for seven days, and was obliged on the third and seventh day to purify himself according to the Mosaic instructions.  In the case of the priest it went still farther; insomuch, that even mourning for the dead by any external sign, such as tearing their clothes, defiled them. Hence such mournings were absolutely forbidden to be used in any case, and by the other priests also, except in the case of their very nearest relations, for whom they were allowed to mourn.  This statue is founded on the importance of sustaining the decency and purity of Divine worship.  The servants of the Deity were to keep themselves at a distance from every thing that in the least degree savoured of uncleanness.
