 <p></p><h3 align="center">CYPRIAN <a name="fr_531"></a><a href="#fn_531"><font size="1">1</font></a></h3>
 <p><small>i. <i>De Idolorum Vanitate</i>, vi.; Baluze, p. 220 (Paris, 1726).</small></p>
 <p></p><h5 align="center">GOD IS BEYOND ALL UNDERSTANDING</h5>
 <p><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Thrice-Greatest</span> Hermes speaks of the One God, and confesses Him beyond all understanding and all appraisement.</p>
 <p></p><hr> 
 <p>This is evidently a reference to the most quoted sentence of Hermes. See Justin Martyr i. below, and other references.</p>
 <p>Chambers (p. 140), after this notice in Cyprian, inserts a passage from Eusebius (<i>c</i>. 325 A.D.), which he says is “a clear quotation from the Pœmandres of Hermes, whom, however, he [Eusebius] probably confounds with the Shepherd of Hermas.”</p>
 <p>Eusebius (<i>Hist. Ecc</i>., v. 8), however, quotes Irenæus (iv. 20, 2), who quotes literally <i>The Shepherd of Hermas</i> (Mand., i.). Indeed, it is the most famous sentence in that early document. See the list of its quotations by the Fathers in the note to Gebhardt and Harnacks text (Leipzig, 1897), p. 70. Such verbal exactitude is not to be found in the remaining Trismegistic literature; the idea, however, is the basis of the whole Trismegistic theology.</p>
