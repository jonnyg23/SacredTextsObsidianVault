 <p></p><h4 align="center">EXCERPT XXIV.</h4>
 <p></p><hr> 
 <p></p><h3 align="center">[A HYMN OF THE GODS]</h3>
 <p><small>(Text: Stob., <i>Phys</i>., v. 14, under the simple heading: “Of Hermes”; G. p. 65 M. i. 45; W. i. 77. The same verses are read in the appendix to the <i>Anthologia Palatina</i>, p. 768, n. 40.)</small></p>
 <p><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Seven</span> Stars far varied in their course revolved upon the [wide] Olympian plain; with them for ever will Eternity <a name="fr_291"></a><a href="#fn_291"><font size="1">1</font></a> spin [fate] <a name="fr_292"></a><a href="#fn_292"><font size="1">2</font></a>:—Mēnē that shines by night, [and] gloomy Kronos, [and] sweet Hēlios, and Paphiē whos carried in the shrine, <a name="fr_293"></a><a href="#fn_293"><font size="1">3</font></a> courageous Arēs, fair-wingèd Hermēs, and Zeus the primal source <a name="fr_294"></a><a href="#fn_294"><font size="1">4</font></a> from whom Nature doth come.</p>
 <p>Now they themselves have had the race of</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_92"><font size="1" color="green">p. 92</font></a></p>
 <p>men entrusted to their care; so that in us there is a Mēnē, Zeus, an Arēs, Paphiē, a Kronos, Hēlios and Hermēs.</p>
 <p>Wherefore we are divided up [so as] to draw from the ætherial spirit, <a name="fr_295"></a><a href="#fn_295"><font size="1">1</font></a> tears, laughter, anger, birth, reason, sleep, desire.</p>
 <p>Tears are Kronos, birth Zeus, reason [is] Hermēs, courage Mars, and Mēnē sleep, in sooth, and Cytherēa desire, and Hēlios [is] laughter—for tis because of him that justly every mortal thinking thing doth laugh and the immortal world.</p>
 <p></p><hr> 
 <p></p><h4 align="center">COMMENT</h4>
 <p>This is the only known specimen of verses attributed to the Trismegistic tradition. Liddell and Scott, however, under “νυκτιφανής,” do not question this attribution, while Clement of Alexandria (<i>Strom</i>., vi. p. 633 [this is a reference of Wachsmuths which I cannot verify]) praises the “Hymns of the Gods” of Hermes. On the contrary, in <i>Anthol. Palat</i>., p. 442, n. 491, the seventh verse is ascribed to Theon of Alexandria.</p>
