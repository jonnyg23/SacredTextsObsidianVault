 <a href="img/pl_j.jpg"><img src="tn/pl_j.jpg" alt="Plate J"><br><font size="-3">Click to enlarge</font></a><br>Plate J<br></span></p>  <h3 align="center">PLATE J.</h3>  <h3 align="center">SUBJECTS CONNECTED WITH THE MITHRAIC MYSTERIES.</h3>  
 <p>1. This pretty design may be tersely described in a line of Manilius--</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"Quadrijugis et Phœbus equis et Delia bigis."</font></dir>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Sol and Luna in their appropriate equipages: a heathenish device enough, but the same hand has backed their influence by the invocation, "Iao</p>  
 <p><a name="page_443"><font size="1" color="green">p. 443</font></a></p>  
 <p>Sabaoth, Abraxas;" He, <i>i.e.</i> the living God, protect Aparastathe, the bearer of this talisman. Similar formulæ, always attached to women's names, very frequently occur.</p>  
 <p>2. The Zoroastrian <i>Dog</i>, of whom and whose office so much has been said in the text. The ill-cut Bird is intended for the Raven, the usual Mithraic attribute.</p>  
 <p>3. The Lion of Baal, the Syrian Sun-god; below, the Scarabæus typifying the Creative Power. The Phœnician inscription <i>Osan ēl</i>," God gives strength," is the name of the owner of the signet.. Lévy quotes an agate scarabæus at the British Museum exactly similar to this gem (a fine sard), a proof of the popularity of both type and name.</p>  
 <p>4. A unique talismanic device, converting a male Sphinx into a novel bird, by the addition of the legs of a crane and the tail of a scorpion. It is engraved in the Persian style: my motive for admitting it into the present class.</p>  
 <p>5. The Zodiacal Lion, guided by its astral Genius in its course through the seven planets.</p>  
 <p>6. The Sun-god, with radiated head, mounted upon a camel, typifying the East. Below is set the fire-altar of Mithraic worship. He is followed by Chanticleer, attribute of the god of Day, preceded by the Horse--his peculiar sacrifice, as Ovid tells us:--</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"Placat <i>equo</i> Persis radiis Hyperiona cinctum,<br>  Ne detur Celeri victima tarda deo." (Fasti, I. 305-86.)</font></dir>
