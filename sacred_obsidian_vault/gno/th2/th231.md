 <p></p><h4 align="center">CORPUS HERMETICUM (XVI.)</h4>
 <p></p><hr> 
 <p></p><h3 align="center">THE DEFINITIONS OF ASCLEPIUS UNTO KING AMMON</h3>
 <p></p><h5 align="center">THE PERFECT SERMON OF ASCLEPIUS UNTO THE KING</h5>
 <p align="center"><small>(Text: R. 348-354; Pat. at end. <a name="fr_558"></a><a href="#fn_557"><font size="1">1</font></a>)</small></p>
 <p>1. <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Great</span> is the sermon (<i>logos</i>) which I send to thee, O King—the summing up and digest, as it were, of all the rest.</p>
 <p>For it is not composed to suit the manys prejudice, since it contains much that refuteth them.</p>
 <p>Nay, it will seem to thee as well to contradict sometimes my sermons too.</p>
 <p>Hermes, my master, in many a conversation, both when alone, and sometimes, too, when Tat was there, has said, that unto those who come across my books, their composition will seem most simple and [most] clear; but, on the contrary, as tis unclear, and has the [inner]</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_267"><font size="1" color="green">p. 267</font></a></p>
 <p>meaning of its words concealed, it will be still unclearer, when, afterwards, the Greeks will want to turn our tongue into their own,—for this will be a very great distorting and obscuring of [even] what has been [already] written.</p>
 <p>2. Turned into our own native tongue, <a name="fr_559"></a><a href="#fn_558"><font size="1">1</font></a> the sermon (<i>logos</i>) keepeth clear the meaning <a name="fr_560"></a><a href="#fn_559"><font size="1">2</font></a> of the words (<i>logoi</i>) [at any rate].</p>
 <p>For that its very quality of sound, the [very] power of the Egyptian names, have in themselves the bringing into act of what is said.</p>
 <p>As far as, then, thou canst, O King—(and thou canst [do] all things)—keep [this] our sermon from translation; in order that such mighty mysteries may not come to the Greeks, and the disdainful speech of Greece, with [all] its looseness, and its surface beauty, <a name="fr_561"></a><a href="#fn_560"><font size="1">3</font></a> so to speak, take all the strength out of <a name="fr_562"></a><a href="#fn_561"><font size="1">4</font></a> the solemn and the strong—the energetic <a name="fr_563"></a><a href="#fn_562"><font size="1">5</font></a> speech of Names.</p>
 <p>The Greeks, O King, have novel words, energic of “argumentation” [only]; and thus is the philosophizing of the Greeks—the noise of words.</p>
 <p>But we do not use words; but we use sounds full-filled with deeds.</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_268"><font size="1" color="green">p. 268</font></a></p>
 <p>3. Thus, then, will I begin the sermon by invocation unto God, the universals Lord and Maker, [their] Sire, and [their] Encompasser; who though being All is One, <a name="fr_564"></a><a href="#fn_563"><font size="1">1</font></a> and though being One is All; for that the Fullness of all things is One, and [is] in One, this latter One not coming as a second [One], but both being One.</p>
 <p>And this is the idea <a name="fr_565"></a><a href="#fn_564"><font size="1">2</font></a> that I would have thee keep, through the whole study of our sermon, Sire!</p>
 <p>For should one try to separate what <i>seems</i> to be both All <i>and</i> One <i>and</i> Same from One,—he will be found to take <a name="fr_566"></a><a href="#fn_565"><font size="1">3</font></a> his epithet of “All” from [the idea of] multitude, and not from [that of) fullness <a name="fr_567"></a><a href="#fn_566"><font size="1">4</font></a>—which is impossible; for if he part All from the One, he will destroy the All. <a name="fr_568"></a><a href="#fn_567"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>
 <p>For all things <i>must</i> be One—if they indeed <i>are</i> One. Yea, they are One; and they shall never cease being One—in order that the Fullness may not be destroyed.</p>
 <p align="center">*          *          *          *          *</p>
 <p>4. See then in Earth a host of founts of Water and of Fire forth-spirting in its midmost parts; in one and the same [space all] the three</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_269"><font size="1" color="green">p. 269</font></a></p>
 <p>natures visible—of Fire, and Water, and of Earth, depending from one Root. <a name="fr_569"></a><a href="#fn_568"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>
 <p>Whence, too, it <a name="fr_570"></a><a href="#fn_569"><font size="1">2</font></a> is believed to be the Treasury <a name="fr_571"></a><a href="#fn_570"><font size="1">3</font></a> of every matter. It sendeth forth of its <a name="fr_572"></a><a href="#fn_571"><font size="1">4</font></a> abundance, and in the place [of what it sendeth forth] receiveth the subsistence from above. <a name="fr_573"></a><a href="#fn_572"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>
 <p>For thus the Demiurge <a name="fr_574"></a><a href="#fn_573"><font size="1">6</font></a>—I mean the Sun—eternally doth order Heaven and Earth, pouring down Essence, <a name="fr_575"></a><a href="#fn_574"><font size="1">7</font></a> and taking Matter up, drawing both round Himself and to Himself all things, and from Himself giving all things to all.</p>
 <p>For He it is whose goodly energies extend not only through the Heaven and the Air, but also onto Earth, right down unto the lowest Depth and the Abyss.</p>
 <p>6. And if there be an Essence which the mind alone can grasp, <a name="fr_576"></a><a href="#fn_575"><font size="1">8</font></a> this is his Substance, <a name="fr_577"></a><a href="#fn_576"><font size="1">9</font></a> the reservoir <a name="fr_578"></a><a href="#fn_577"><font size="1">10</font></a> of which would be His Light.</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_270"><font size="1" color="green">p. 270</font></a></p>
 <p>But whence this [Substance] doth arise, or floweth forth, He, [and He] only, knows.</p>
 <p align="center">*          *          *          *          *</p>
 <p>Or rather, in space and nature, He is near unto Himself . . . though as He is not seen by us, . . . understand [Him] by conjecture. <a name="fr_579"></a><a href="#fn_578"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>
 <p>7. The spectacle of Him, however, is not left unto conjecture; nay [for] His very rays, <a name="fr_580"></a><a href="#fn_579"><font size="1">2</font></a> in greatest splendour, shine all round on all the Cosmos that doth lie above and lie below.</p>
 <p>For He is stablished in the midst, wreathed with the Cosmos, <a name="fr_581"></a><a href="#fn_580"><font size="1">3</font></a> and just as a good charioteer, He safely drives the cosmic team, <a name="fr_582"></a><a href="#fn_581"><font size="1">4</font></a> and holds them in unto Himself, <a name="fr_583"></a><a href="#fn_582"><font size="1">5</font></a> lest they should run away in dire disorder.</p>
 <p>The reins are Life, and Soul, and Spirit, Deathlessness, and Genesis.</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_271"><font size="1" color="green">p. 271</font></a></p>
 <p>He lets it, then, drive [round] not far off from Himself—nay, if the truth be said, together with Himself.</p>
 <p>8. And in this way He operates <a name="fr_584"></a><a href="#fn_583"><font size="1">1</font></a> all things. To the immortals He distributeth perpetual permanence; and with the upper hemisphere of His own Light—all that he sends above from out His other side, <a name="fr_585"></a><a href="#fn_584"><font size="1">2</font></a> [the side of him] which looks to Heaven—He nourisheth the deathless parts of Cosmos.</p>
 <p>But with that side that sendeth down [its Light], and shineth round all of the hemisphere <a name="fr_586"></a><a href="#fn_585"><font size="1">3</font></a> of Water, and of Earth, and Air, He vivifieth, and by births and changes keepeth in movement to and fro the animals <a name="fr_587"></a><a href="#fn_586"><font size="1">4</font></a> in these [the lower] parts of Cosmos. . . .</p>
 <p>9. He changes them in spiral fashion, and doth transform them into one another, genus to genus, species into species, their mutual changes into one another being balanced—just as He does when He doth deal with the Great Bodies.</p>
 <p>For in the case of every body, [its] permanence [consists in] transformation.</p>
 <p>In case of an immortal one, there is no</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_272"><font size="1" color="green">p. 272</font></a></p>
 <p>dissolution; but when it is a mortal one, it is accompanied with dissolution. <a name="fr_588"></a><a href="#fn_587"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>
 <p>And this is how the deathless body doth differ from the mortal, and how the mortal one doth differ from the deathless.</p>
 <p>10. Moreover, as His Lights continuous, so is His Power of giving Life to lives continuous, and not to be brought to an end in space or in abundance.</p>
 <p>For there are many choirs of daimons round Him, like unto hosts of very various kinds; who though they dwell with mortals, yet are not far from the immortals; but having as their lot from here unto the spaces of the Gods, <a name="fr_589"></a><a href="#fn_588"><font size="1">2</font></a> they watch oer the affairs of men, and work out things appointed by the Gods—by means of storms, whirlwinds and hurricanes, by transmutations wrought by fire and shakings of the earth, <a name="fr_590"></a><a href="#fn_589"><font size="1">3</font></a> with famines also and with wars requiting [mans] impiety,—for this is in mans case the greatest ill against the Gods.</p>
 <p>11. For that the duty of the Gods is to give benefits; the duty of mankind is to give worship <a name="fr_591"></a><a href="#fn_590"><font size="1">4</font></a>; the duty of the daimons is to give requital.</p>
 <p>For as to all the other things men do, through</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_273"><font size="1" color="green">p. 273</font></a></p>
 <p>error, or foolhardiness, or by necessity, which they call Fate, <a name="fr_592"></a><a href="#fn_591"><font size="1">1</font></a> or ignorance—these are not held requitable among the Gods; impiety alone is guilty at their bar.</p>
 <p>12. The Sun is the preserver <a name="fr_593"></a><a href="#fn_592"><font size="1">2</font></a> and the nurse of every class. <a name="fr_594"></a><a href="#fn_593"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>
 <p>And just as the Intelligible World, <a name="fr_595"></a><a href="#fn_594"><font size="1">4</font></a> holding the Sensible in its embrace, fills it [all] full, distending it with forms of every kind and every shape—so, too, the Sun distendeth all in Cosmos, affording births to all, and strengtheneth them.</p>
 <p>When they are weary or they fail, He takes them in His arms again.</p>
 <p>13. And under Him is ranged the choir of daimons—or, rather, choirs; for these are multitudinous and very varied, ranked underneath the groups of Stars, <a name="fr_596"></a><a href="#fn_595"><font size="1">5</font></a> in equal number with each one of them.</p>
 <p>So, marshalled in their ranks, they are the ministers of each one of the Stars, being in their natures good, and bad, that is, in their activities (for that a daimons essence is activity); while</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_274"><font size="1" color="green">p. 274</font></a></p>
 <p>some of them are [of] mixed [natures], good and bad.</p>
 <p>14. To all of these has been allotted the authority oer things upon the Earth; and it is they who bring about the multifold confusion of the turmoils on the Earth—for states and nations generally, and for each individual separately.</p>
 <p>For they do shape our souls like to themselves, and set them moving with them,—obsessing nerves, and marrow, veins and arteries, the brain itself, down to the very heart. <a name="fr_597"></a><a href="#fn_596"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>
 <p>15. For on each one of us being born and made alive, the daimons take hold on us—those [daimones] who are in service at that moment [of the wheel] of Genesis, who are ranged under each one of the Stars. <a name="fr_598"></a><a href="#fn_597"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>
 <p>For that these change at every moment; they do not stay the same, but circle back again.</p>
 <p>These, then, descending through the body <a name="fr_599"></a><a href="#fn_598"><font size="1">3</font></a> to the two parts <a name="fr_600"></a><a href="#fn_599"><font size="1">4</font></a> of the soul, set it <a name="fr_601"></a><a href="#fn_600"><font size="1">5</font></a> awhirling, each one towards its own activity.</p>
 <p>But the souls rational part is set above the lordship of the daimons—designed to be receptacle of God.</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_275"><font size="1" color="green">p. 275</font></a></p>
 <p>16. Who then doth have a Ray shining upon him through the Sun within his rational part—and these in all are few on them the daimons do not act; for no one of the daimons or of Gods has any power against one Ray of God.</p>
 <p>As for the rest, they are all led and driven, soul and body, by the daimons—loving and hating the activities of these.</p>
 <p>The reason (<i>logos</i>), [then,] is not the love that is deceived and that deceives. <a name="fr_602"></a><a href="#fn_601"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>
 <p>The daimons, therefore, exercise the whole of this terrene economy, <a name="fr_603"></a><a href="#fn_602"><font size="1">2</font></a> using our bodies as [their] instruments.</p>
 <p>And this economy Hermes has called Heimarmenē. <a name="fr_604"></a><a href="#fn_603"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>
 <p>17. The World Intelligible, <a name="fr_605"></a><a href="#fn_604"><font size="1">4</font></a> then, depends from God; the Sensible from the Intelligible [World].</p>
 <p>The Sun, through the Intelligible and the Sensible Cosmos, pours forth abundantly the stream from God of Good,—that is, the demiurgic operation.</p>
 <p>And round the Sun are the Eight Spheres, dependent from Him—the [Sphere] of the</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_276"><font size="1" color="green">p. 276</font></a></p>
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Non-wandering Ones, the Six [Spheres] of the Wanderers, and one Circumterrene.</p>
 <p>And from the Spheres depend the daimones; and from these, men.</p>
 <p>And thus all things and all [of them] depend from God. <a name="fr_606"></a><a href="#fn_605"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>
 <p>18. Wherefore God is the Sire of all; the Suns [their] Demiurge; the Cosmos is the instrument of demiurgic operation.</p>
 <p>Intelligible Essence regulateth Heaven; and Heaven, the Gods; the daimones, ranked underneath the Gods, regulate men.</p>
 <p>This is the host <a name="fr_607"></a><a href="#fn_606"><font size="1">2</font></a> of Gods and daimones. <a name="fr_608"></a><a href="#fn_607"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>
 <p>Through these God makes all things for His own self.</p>
 <p>And all [of them] are parts of God; and if they all [are] parts—then, God is all.</p>
 <p>Thus, making all, He makes Himself; nor ever can He cease [His making], for He Himself is ceaseless. <a name="fr_609"></a><a href="#fn_608"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>
 <p>Just, then, as God doth have no end and no beginning, so doth His making have no end and no beginning. <a name="fr_610"></a><a href="#fn_609"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>
 <p align="center">*          *          *          *          *</p>

 <p><a name="fn_593"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_594">273:3</a> Or genus.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_594"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_595">273:4</a> Or Cosmos.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_595"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_596">273:5</a> ὑπὸ τὰς τῶν ἀστέρων πλινθίδας. πλινθίς = πλινθίον, and is used of any rectangular figure, and also of groups of stars as in Eratosth. <i>apud</i> Strab., II. i. 35; v. 36 (<i>Lex.</i> Sophocles); compare αἱ τῶν πλινθίων ὑπογραφαί, the fields, or spaces, into which the Augurs divided the heavens, <i>templa</i>, or <i>regiones coeli</i> (<i>Lex.</i> Liddell and Scott).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_596"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_597">274:1</a> Lit. viscera.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_597"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_598">274:2</a> <i>Cf.</i> <i>P. S. A.</i>, xxxv. 2.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_598"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_599">274:3</a> <i>Cf.</i> <i>C. H.</i>, xiii. (xiv.) 7.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_599"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_600">274:4</a> The two irrational parts, “passion” and “desire” (θυμὸς and ἐπιθυμία).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_600"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_601">274:5</a> The soul.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_601"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_602">275:1</a> This Erōs is the lower love (<i>cf.</i> <i>C. H.</i>, i. 18: “love, the cause of death”), not the Divine Love who inspires Hermes in “The Perfect Sermon” and who is mentioned <i>C. H.</i>, xviii. 14.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_602"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_603">275:2</a> διοίκησιν.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_603"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_604">275:3</a> Or, Fate; <i>cf.</i> <i>C. H.</i>, i. 9; and <i>P. S. A.</i>, xix.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_604"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_605">275:4</a> Or, Intelligible Cosmos.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_605"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_606">276:1</a> <i>Cf.</i> <i>P. S. A.</i>, iv. 1 n.; and xix.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_606"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_607">276:2</a> Or army, or hierarchy. Compare the “soldier” degree of the Mithriaca.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_607"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_608">276:3</a> <i>Cf.</i> <i>C. H.</i>, x. (xi.) 22; and <i>P. S. A.</i>, v. 1.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_608"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_609">276:4</a> <i>Cf.</i> <i>C. H.</i>, xiv. (xv.), 7 and 5.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_609"></a><a href="th231.htm#fr_610">276:5</a> See Commentary to Frag. iv. (Lact., <i>D. I.</i>, ii. 15).</p>
