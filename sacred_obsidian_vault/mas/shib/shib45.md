 <p><a name="page_150"><font size="1" color="green">p. 150</font></a></p>  <h4 align="center">TO A SKULL.</h4>  
 <p>Part of a poem found beside a skeleton in the Museum of the London Royal College of Surgeons, author unknown. It was published in the Morning Chronicle early in the present century.</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px"><small>Behold this ruin! Twas a skull<br> Once of ethereal spirit full.<br> This narrow cell was life's retreat;<br> This space was thought's mysterious seat.<br> What beauteous visions filled this spot!<br> What dreams of pleasure long forgot!<br> Nor hope, nor joy, nor love, nor fear<br> Has left one trace of record here.  
 </small><p>Beneath this mouldering canopy<br> Once shone the bright and busy eye:<br> But start not at the dismal void<br> If social love that eye employed,<br> If with no lawless fire it gleamed,<br> But through the dews of kindness beamed,<br> That eye shall be forever bright<br> When stars and sun are sunk in night.</p>  
 <p>Within this hollow cavern hung<br> The ready, swift and tuneful tongue:<br> If falsehood's honey it disdained,<br> And when it could not praise was chained;<br> If bold in virtue's cause it spoke<br> Yet gentle concord never broke<br> This silent tongue shall plead for thee<br> When Time reveals Eternity!</p></div>
