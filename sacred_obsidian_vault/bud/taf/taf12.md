   <h3 align="center">WORKS IN CHINESE TRANSLATIONS.</h3>  
 <p>The works ascribed to Açvaghosha and still existing in Chinese translations are eight in number. They are: (1) The <a name="img_03600"></a>
 <img src="img/03600.jpg"> <i>Tai shêng ch‘i hsin lun</i> (<i>Mahâyânaçraddhotpâdaçâstra</i>): <i>discourse on the awakening of</i> </p>  
 <p><a name="page_37"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 37</font></a></p>  
 <p><i>faith in the Mahâyâna</i>. It is the principal work of Açvaghosha, and through this we are able to recognise what an important position he occupies in the development of the Mahayanistic world-conception and theory of final emancipation. Its outlines and the accounts of its Chinese translation will be given below. (2) The <a name="img_03700"></a>
 <img src="img/03700.jpg"> <i>Ta sung ti hsüan wên pên lun</i>, a fundamental treatise on the spiritual stages for reaching final deliverance. The book has a decided tendency to mysticism, explaining a gradual development of religious consciousness through fifty-one different spiritual stages. It may be considered a precursory work out of which Vajrabodhi's Mantrism finally made a full manifestation. It was translated by Paramârtha between A. D. 557-569. Twenty <i>fasciculi</i>, forty chapters. (3) The <a name="img_03701"></a>
 <img src="img/03701.jpg"><i>Ta chuang yen lun ching</i> (<i>Mahâlamkârasûtraçâstra</i>), the <i>Book of Great Glory</i>, or a compilation of stories illustrating the retribution of karma. The stories relate mostly to the events that occurred in Western India. Beal translated some of them in his <i>Buddhist Literature in China</i>. The Chinese translator is Kumârajîva, circa A. D. 405. Fifteen fasciculi.. (4) The <a name="img_03702"></a>
 <img src="img/03702.jpg"> <i>Fo shu hing tsan</i> (<i>Buddha-caritakâvya</i>), a well known poem on the life of Buddha. The Chinese translation is by Dharmaraksha between A. D. 414-421. Five <i>fasciculi</i>, twenty eight chapters, Beal's English translation forms Vol. XIX. of <i>The Sacred Books of the East</i>; and Cowell's translation from Sanskrit, Vol. XLIX of the same. (5) The <a name="img_03703"></a>
 <img src="img/03703.jpg"></p>  
 <p><a name="page_38"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 38</font></a></p>  
 <p><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font> <a name="img_03800"></a>
 <img src="img/03800.jpg"> <i>Ni kan tzŭ wên wu wu i ching</i>, <i>a sûtra on a Nirgrantha's asking about the theory of non-ego</i>. The book foreshadows the Mâdhyamika philosophy of Nâgârjuna, for the two forms of truth are distinguished there, Pure Truth (<i>Parmârtha-satya</i>) and Practical Truth (<i>Samvṛtti-satya</i>), <a name="fr_54"></a><a href="taf13.htm#fn_54"><font size="1">1</font></a> and the <i>Çûnyatâ</i> theory also is proclaimed. (6) The <a name="img_03801"></a>
 <img src="img/03801.jpg"> <i>Shih pu shan yeh tao ching</i>, <i>a sûtra on the ten no-good deeds</i>. (7) The <a name="img_03802"></a>
 <img src="img/03802.jpg"> <i>Shih shih fa wu shih sung</i>, fifty verses on the rules of serving a master or teacher. (8) The <a name="img_03803"></a>
 <img src="img/03803.jpg"> <i>Lu tao lun ‘hui ching</i>, <i>a sûtra on transmigration through the six states of existence</i>. These last four works are very short, all translated by Jih-ch‘êng (Divayaças?), between A. D. 1004-1058.</p>
