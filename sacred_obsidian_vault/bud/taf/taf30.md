 <p><a name="page_151"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 151</font></a></p>  <h1 align="center">GLOSSARY.</h1>  
 <p>Activity-consciousness <a name="img_15100"></a>
 <img src="img/15100.jpg"> <i>yeh shih</i>, <i>karmavijñâna</i>? the assertion of the "Will to Live."</p>  
 <p>Affectional hindrance <a name="img_15101"></a>
 <img src="img/15101.jpg"> <i>fan nao chang</i>, <i>kleçâvarana</i>, hindrance to the attainment of Nirvâna, arising from the assertion of the "Will to Live."</p>  
 <p>Affirmation, or Non-emptiness, <a name="img_15102"></a>
 <img src="img/15102.jpg"> <i>pu k‘ung</i>, <i>açûnyatâ</i>, suchness as constituting the basis of reality; it is equivalent to the Tathâgata's Womb.</p>  
 <p>All-conserving mind, The, <a name="img_15103"></a>
 <img src="img/15103.jpg">, <i>a lai ya shih</i>, or <i>tsang shih</i>, or <a name="img_15104"></a>
 <img src="img/15104.jpg"> <i>a li ya</i>, <a name="img_15105"></a>
 <img src="img/15105.jpg"> <i>mu mo shih</i>, <i>âlaya-vijñâna</i>, a stage in the evolution of suchness, in which consciousness is awakened to recognise a distinction between suchness and birth-and-death.</p>  
 <p>Aspiration <a name="img_15106"></a>
 <img src="img/15106.jpg"> <i>fa hsin</i>, <i>cittotpâda</i>, desire to attain the most perfect knowledge.</p>  
 <p>Âtman <a name="img_15107"></a>
 <img src="img/15107.jpg"> <i>wu</i>, (1) ego-soul; (2) noumenon or thing-in-itself. Anâtman is a negative form of the same.</p>  
 <p>Birth-and-death <a name="img_15108"></a>
 <img src="img/15108.jpg"> <i>shêng mieh</i>, <i>samsâra</i>, the material principle in contradistinction to the formal principle, suchness.</p>  
 <p>Consciousness <a name="img_15109"></a>
 <img src="img/15109.jpg"> <i>shih</i>, <i>vijñâna</i>, mentation in general.</p>  
 <p>Defilement <a name="img_15110"></a>
 <img src="img/15110.jpg">, <i>jan</i>, a cognisance of dual aspect of suchness; not necessarily moral or intellectual fault.</p>  
 <p>Dharma <a name="img_15111"></a>
 <img src="img/15111.jpg"> <i>fa</i>, (1) that which subsists, or substance; (2) law, doctrine, or regulative principle.</p>  
 <p>Dharmakâya <a name="img_15112"></a>
 <img src="img/15112.jpg"> <i>fa shên</i>, absolute being, or absolute knowledge when considered from the idealistic point of view.</p>  
 <p>Ego <a name="img_15113"></a>
 <img src="img/15113.jpg"> <i>i</i>, <i>manas</i>, the subjective mind which believes consciously or unconsciously in the existence of the ego-soul.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_152"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 152</font></a></p>  
 <p>Ego-consciousness <a name="img_15200"></a>
 <img src="img/15200.jpg"> <i>i shih</i>, <i>manovijñâna</i>, egocentric thoughts in general; the mind that makes a deliberate assumption of a dualistic existence of the ego and the non-ego,</p>  
 <p>Enlightenment <a name="img_15201"></a>
 <img src="img/15201.jpg"> <i>chiao</i> (<i>buddhi</i>?), another name for suchness, psychologically considered.</p>  
 <p>Evolving-consciousness <a name="img_15202"></a>
 <img src="img/15202.jpg"> <i>chuan shih</i>, <i>pravrtti-vijñâna</i>, a state of suchness out of which mentation in general evolves.</p>  
 <p>Ignorance <a name="img_15203"></a>
 <img src="img/15203.jpg"> <i>wu ming</i>, <i>avidya</i>, a state of suchness in its evolution; practically the same as birth-and-death.</p>  
 <p>Intellectual hindrance <a name="img_15204"></a>
 <img src="img/15204.jpg"> <i>so chih chang</i>, <a href="errata.htm#25"><i>jñeyâvarana</i></a>, the hindrance to the attainment of Nirvâna, which arises from intellectual prejudices.</p>  
 <p>Interrelated defilement <a name="img_15205"></a>
 <img src="img/15205.jpg"> <i>hsiang ying jan</i>, a conscious assertion of dualism.</p>  
 <p>Karma-hindrance, <i>yeh chang</i>, <i>karmâvarana</i>, the hindrance in the way to Nirvâna, that is brought forth by evil deeds done in previous lives.</p>  
 <p>Mahâyâna <a name="img_15206"></a>
 <img src="img/15206.jpg"> <i>tai chang</i>, literally, great conveyance, another name for suchness.</p>  
 <p>Means. or expediency <a name="img_15207"></a>
 <img src="img/15207.jpg"> <i>fang pien</i>, <i>upâya</i>, when philosophically considered, the process of evolution, whereby the unconditional suchness becomes conditional.</p>  
 <p>Mind <a name="img_15208"></a>
 <img src="img/15208.jpg"> <i>hsin</i>, <i>citta</i>, relative aspect of suchness. Soul, mind, and suchness are to a certain extent synonymous, but in this translation the following distinction is made: Suchness, when unqualified, signifies its absolute aspect and is practically the same with the soul, while the term mind is used to denote a state of suchness in its operation or evolution.</p>  
 <p>Negation, or emptiness <a name="img_15209"></a>
 <img src="img/15209.jpg"> <i>k‘ung</i>, <i>çûnyatâ</i>, an aspect of suchness as transcending all forms of <a href="errata.htm#26">relativity</a>.</p>  
 <p>Nirvâna <a name="img_15210"></a>
 <img src="img/15210.jpg"> <i>nieh p‘an</i>, the recognition of the truth or suchness.</p>  
 <p>Non-enlightenment <a name="img_15211"></a>
 <img src="img/15211.jpg"> <i>pu chiao</i> (<i>nirbuddhi</i>?), another name for ignorance, psychologically considered. Non-enlightenment, defilement, birth-and-death, and ignorance, are more or. less synonymous and interchangeable.</p>  
 <p>Non-particularisation <a name="img_15212"></a>
 <img src="img/15212.jpg"> <i>wu fên pieh</i>, the subjective attitude that is free from a deliberate assertion of dualism; i is similar in a sense to Lao-Tze's "Non-assertion."</p>  
 <p><a name="page_153"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 153</font></a></p>  
 <p>Not-interrelated defilement <a name="img_15300"></a>
 <img src="img/15300.jpg"> <i>pu hsiang ying jan</i>, an unconscious assertion of dualism.</p>  
 <p>Particularisation-consciousness <a name="img_15301"></a>
 <img src="img/15301.jpg"> <i>fên pieh shih</i>, the consciousness that adheres to the dual aspect of existence; a synonym of phenomena-particularising-consciousness.</p>  
 <p>Prejudice <a name="img_15302"></a>
 <img src="img/15302.jpg"> <i>fan nao</i>, <i>âçrava</i> or <i>kleça</i>, the subjectivity that averts the due exercise of will and intellect.</p>  
 <p>Samâdhi <a name="img_15303"></a>
 <img src="img/15303.jpg"> <i>san mei</i>, or <a name="img_15304"></a>
 <img src="img/15304.jpg"> <i>ting</i>, literally equilibrium, a state of consciousness in which all modes of mental activity are in equilibrium.</p>  
 <p>Soul <a name="img_15305"></a>
 <img src="img/15305.jpg"> <i>hsin</i>, <i>hrdaya</i> or <i>citta</i>, that which constitutes the kernel of things, but not in the Christian conception of the word; a synonym of absolute suchness.</p>  
 <p>Soul as birth-and-death, <a name="img_15306"></a>
 <img src="img/15306.jpg"> <i>hsin shêng mieh</i>, relative aspect of suchness as material principle; a synonym of ignorance.</p>  
 <p>Soul as suchness <a name="img_15307"></a>
 <img src="img/15307.jpg"> <i>hsin chen ju</i>, absolute aspect of suchness as purely formal.</p>  
 <p>Subjectivity <a name="img_15308"></a>
 <img src="img/15308.jpg"> <i>wang nien</i>, or <a name="img_15309"></a>
 <img src="img/15309.jpg"> <i>wang nien hsin</i>, or <a name="img_15310"></a>
 <img src="img/15310.jpg"> <i>hsin nien</i>, or simply <a name="img_15311"></a>
 <img src="img/15311.jpg"> <i>nien</i>, <i>smrti</i>, literally, recollection or memory, or <a name="img_15312"></a>
 <img src="img/15312.jpg"> <i>fên piéh</i>, particularisation; the mentation that is not in accordance with the conception of suchness.</p>  
 <p>Suchness <a name="img_15313"></a>
 <img src="img/15313.jpg"> <i>chên ju</i>, <i>bhûtatathatâ</i>, the highest reality, or the "purely formal" aspect of existence.</p>  
 <p>Tathâgata's womb <a name="img_15314"></a>
 <img src="img/15314.jpg"> <i>ju lai tsang</i>, <i>tathâgata-garbha</i>, a state of suchness as containing every possible merit.</p>  
 <p>Totality of things <a name="img_15315"></a>
 <img src="img/15315.jpg"> <i>fah chieh</i>, <i>dharmadhâtu</i>, literally, the basis of things, that is, the universe as a whole.</p>  
 <p>Vow <a name="img_15316"></a>
 <img src="img/15316.jpg"> <i>yüan</i>, or <a name="img_15317"></a>
 <img src="img/15317.jpg"> <i>shih yüan</i>, <i>pranidhâna</i>, commonly translated prayer, but not in the Christian sense, for Buddhists think that a vow or vehement desire has power enough to achieve what is desired, according to their idealistic conception of the world.</p>
