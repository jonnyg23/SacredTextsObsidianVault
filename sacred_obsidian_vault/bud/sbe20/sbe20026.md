   <h3 align="center">11.</h3>  
 <p>1. Now at that time the Bhikkhus sewed their robes together after tearing the cloth with their hands <a name="fr_199"></a><a href="#fn_194"><font size="1">2</font></a>; and the robes became jagged.</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a blade and of a sheath (for the blade) made of felt <a name="fr_200"></a><a href="#fn_195"><font size="1">3</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>Now at that time a blade with a haft to it <a name="fr_201"></a><a href="#fn_196"><font size="1">4</font></a> had come into the possession of the Sa<i>m</i>gha.</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_91"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 91</font></a></p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a blade with a haft to it.'</p>  
 <p>Now at that time the <i>Kh</i>abbaggiya Bhikkhus used various kinds of long handles to their blades, made of silver, and made of gold.</p>  
 <p>People murmured (&amp;c., as usual, down to) They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'You are not, O Bhikkhus, to use various kinds of handles to your blades. Whosoever does so, shall be guilty of a dukka<i>t</i>a. I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of handles to your blades made of bone, or ivory. or horn, or of the na la reed, or of bamboo, or hard wood, or of lac, or of the shells of fruit, or of bronze, or of the centre of the chank-shell <a name="fr_202"></a><a href="#fn_197"><font size="1">1</font></a>:</p>  
 <p>2. Now at that time the Bhikkhus sewed their robes with quills or bits of bamboo rind, and the robes were badly sewn.</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of needles.'</p>  
 <p>The needles got blunted <a name="fr_203"></a><a href="#fn_198"><font size="1">2</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a needle-case made of bamboo <a name="fr_204"></a><a href="#fn_199"><font size="1">3</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>Even in the needle-cases the needles became blunt.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_92"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 92</font></a></p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to fill the cases with chunam <a name="fr_205"></a><a href="#fn_200"><font size="1">1</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>Even in the chunam the needles became blunt.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to fill the cases with barley-meal <a name="fr_206"></a><a href="#fn_201"><font size="1">2</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>Even in the barley-meal the needles became blunt.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of powdered stone <a name="fr_207"></a><a href="#fn_202"><font size="1">3</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>Even in the powdered stone the needles became blunt.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to mix (the powder) with beeswax <a name="fr_208"></a><a href="#fn_203"><font size="1">4</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>The powder still did not cohere.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to mix sipâ<i>t</i>ika gum <a name="fr_209"></a><a href="#fn_204"><font size="1">5</font></a> with the powder <a name="fr_210"></a><a href="#fn_205"><font size="1">6</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>3. Now at that time the Bhikkhus sewed their robes together by planting stakes here and there, and uniting them (with strings). The robes became out of shape <a name="fr_211"></a><a href="#fn_206"><font size="1">7</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a Ka<i>th</i>ina <a name="fr_212"></a><a href="#fn_207"><font size="1">8</font></a>,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_93"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 93</font></a></p>  
 <p>and that you are to sew the robes together after tying down Ka<i>th</i>ina-strings here and there.'</p>  
 <p>They spread out the Ka<i>th</i>ina on uneven (ground), and the Ka<i>th</i>ina fell to pieces <a name="fr_213"></a><a href="#fn_208"><font size="1">1</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>'You are not, O Bhikkhus, to spread out the Ka<i>th</i>ina on uneven (ground). Whosoever does so, shall be guilty of a dukka<i>t</i>a.'</p>  
 <p>They spread out the Ka<i>th</i>ina on the ground, and the Ka<i>th</i>ina became dirty.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a grass-mat.'</p>  
 <p>The edge of the Ka<i>th</i>ina decayed through age.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to strengthen it by a doubling or a binding along the edge <a name="fr_214"></a><a href="#fn_209"><font size="1">2</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>The Ka<i>th</i>ina was not large enough <a name="fr_215"></a><a href="#fn_210"><font size="1">3</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_94"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 94</font></a></p>  
 <p>da<i>n</i><i>d</i>a-ka<i>th</i>ina <a name="fr_216"></a><a href="#fn_211"><font size="1">1</font></a>, of a pidalaka <a name="fr_217"></a><a href="#fn_211"><font size="1">1</font></a>, of a ticket, of binding strings, and of binding threads <a name="fr_218"></a><a href="#fn_212"><font size="1">2</font></a>; and that you sew your robes together after binding them therewith.'</p>  
 <p>The interstices between the threads became irregular in length <a name="fr_219"></a><a href="#fn_213"><font size="1">3</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of little marks (of the leaf of the talipot palm, or such-like things) <a name="fr_220"></a><a href="#fn_214"><font size="1">4</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>The threads became crooked.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of false threads (laid along the cloth to show where it is to be cut or sewn) <a name="fr_221"></a><a href="#fn_215"><font size="1">5</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>4. Now at that time the Bhikkhus got on to the Ka<i>th</i>ina with unwashen feet, or wet feet, or with their shoes on <a name="fr_222"></a><a href="#fn_216"><font size="1">6</font></a>, and the Ka<i>th</i>ina was soiled.</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'You are not, O Bhikkhus, to get on to the Ka<i>th</i>ina with unwashen feet, or with wet feet, or with your shoes on. Whosoever does so, shall be guilty of a dukka<i>t</i>a.'</p>  
 <p>5. Now at that time the Bhikkhus, when sewing</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_95"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 95</font></a></p>  
 <p>their robes, held the stuff with their fingers, and their fingers were hurt.</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a thimble <a name="fr_223"></a><a href="#fn_217"><font size="1">1</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>Now at that time the <i>Kh</i>abbaggiya Bhikkhus used various kinds of thimbles; gold ones, and silver ones.</p>  
 <p>People murmured, &amp;c. The Bhikkhus heard, &amp;c. They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'You are not, O Bhikkhus, to use various kinds of thimbles. Whosoever does so, shall be guilty of a dukka<i>t</i>a. I allow you, O Bhikkhus, thimbles made of bone, or ivory, or horn, or of the na la reed, or of bamboo, or of hard wood, or of lac, or of the shells of fruit, or of bronze, or of the centre of the chank-shell <a name="fr_224"></a><a href="#fn_218"><font size="1">2</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>Now at that time the needles, and scissors, and thimbles got lost.</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a box or drawer <a name="fr_225"></a><a href="#fn_219"><font size="1">3</font></a> in the workshop.'</p>  
 <p>They got crowded together in the workshop box. They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a thimble bag (to carry the thimbles about in).'</p>  
 <p>They had no shoulder-strap.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_96"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 96</font></a></p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a shoulder-strap, or of a piece of string, to tie the bags on with <a name="fr_226"></a><a href="#fn_220"><font size="1">1</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>6 <a name="fr_227"></a><a href="#fn_221"><font size="1">2</font></a>. Now at that time the Bhikkhus, when sewing their robes in the open air, were distressed by heat and by cold.</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a hall or of a shed for the Ka<i>th</i>ina:</p>  
 <p>The Ka<i>th</i>ina hall had too low a basement, and it was inundated with water.</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to make it with a high basement <a name="fr_228"></a><a href="#fn_222"><font size="1">3</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>The facing (of the basement) fell in.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to line the basement with facing of three kinds <a name="fr_229"></a><a href="#fn_223"><font size="1">4</font></a>--brick facing, stone facing, or wooden facing.'</p>  
 <p>They found difficulty in getting up into it.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of stairs of three kinds--brick stairs, stone stairs, or wooden stairs.'</p>  
 <p>As they were going up them they fell off.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a balustrade <a name="fr_230"></a><a href="#fn_224"><font size="1">5</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_97"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 97</font></a></p>  
 <p>Straw and plaster fell (from the walls and roof) into the Ka<i>th</i>ina-hall.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to first cover over (the walls and roof with skins <a name="fr_231"></a><a href="#fn_225"><font size="1">1</font></a>), and then plaster them within and without. (And I allow the use of) whitewash, and blacking, and red colouring <a name="fr_232"></a><a href="#fn_226"><font size="1">2</font></a>, and wreath-work, and creeper-work, and bone hooks, and cupboards <a name="fr_233"></a><a href="#fn_227"><font size="1">3</font></a>, and bamboos to hang robes on, and strings to hang robes on.'</p>  
 <p>7. Now at that time the Bhikkhus, when they had sewn the robes together, left the Ka<i>th</i>ina as it was, and went away; and the robes were eaten by rats and white ants.</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to fold up the Ka<i>th</i>ina.</p>  
 <p>The Ka<i>th</i>ina came to pieces.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_98"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 98</font></a></p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to fold up the Ka<i>th</i>ina in a cow-hide (?) <a name="fr_234"></a><a href="#fn_228"><font size="1">1</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>The Ka<i>th</i>ina got uncovered.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of strings to tie it up with.'</p>  
 <p>Now at that time the Bhikkhus went away, putting the Ka<i>th</i>ina up against the wall or a pillar; and the Ka<i>th</i>ina, falling over, was broken.</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to hang it on a stake of the wall,. or on a hook <a name="fr_235"></a><a href="#fn_229"><font size="1">2</font></a>.'</p>

 <p><a name="fn_221"></a><a href="sbe20026.htm#fr_227">96:2</a> The whole of this paragraph is repeated below, though not in the same order, of the <i>K</i>aṅkama or cloister, and of the <i>G</i>antâghara, or bath-house. (See V, 14, 2, 3.)</p>
 <p><a name="fn_222"></a><a href="sbe20026.htm#fr_228">96:3</a> That is, to build it on a raised platform, the technical term for which is <i>k</i>aya.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_223"></a><a href="sbe20026.htm#fr_229">96:4</a> See our note below on V, 14, 3. The whole passage recurs of the lining of a well at V, 16, 2, and of Vihâras themselves at VI, 3, 3.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_224"></a><a href="sbe20026.htm#fr_230">96:5</a> Âlambana-bâha<i>m</i>. At Mahâ-sudassana Sutta I, 59, there is <a name="page_97"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 97</font></a> a description of flights of stairs (sopânâ), each of which had thambhâ, evidently posts or banisters; sû<i>k</i>iyo, apparently cross-bars let in to these banisters; and u<i>n</i>hîsa<i>m</i>, either a head-line running along the top of the banisters, or a figure-head at the lower end of such a head-line. (See Rh. D.'s 'Buddhist Suttas,' p. 262.) This and the previous paragraphs are repeated below, V, 14, 2, of the <i>K</i>aṅkama.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_225"></a><a href="sbe20026.htm#fr_231">97:1</a> See Mahâvagga V, 11, where the same technical term (ogumpheti) is used. Buddhaghosa's note is given at p. 317 of the text. See also V, 14, 3, below.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_226"></a><a href="sbe20026.htm#fr_232">97:2</a> Geruka-parikamma<i>m</i>. This reading, and not gerika, is confirmed by VI, 3, 1, VI, 17, 1, where the two previous words also occur. On this mode of preparing walls and floors, see our note below on VI, 20.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_227"></a><a href="sbe20026.htm#fr_233">97:3</a> Pa<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>a-pa<i>t</i>ika<i>m</i> or -pa<i>t</i><i>th</i>ika<i>m</i>, a term of doubtful signification which recurs, together with all the previous words, in the Old Commentary on the 19th Pâ<i>k</i>ittiya. Compare pa<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>a-prastha in B<i>R</i>. The word is perhaps however connected with Sanskrit pa<i>t</i><i>t</i>ikâ, as <i>k</i>ela-pattikam at V, 21, 2 undoubtedly is. It occurs below, in a similar connection, at VI, 3, 1.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_228"></a><a href="sbe20026.htm#fr_234">98:1</a> Go-gha<i>m</i>sikâya. Compare pâda-gha<i>m</i>sani at 22. 1; and on the use of sa<i>m</i>harati in a similar connection, see VI, 2, 7.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_229"></a><a href="sbe20026.htm#fr_235">98:2</a> Nâga-dante. See the note on Mallaka at <i>K</i>ullavagga V, I, 4.</p>
