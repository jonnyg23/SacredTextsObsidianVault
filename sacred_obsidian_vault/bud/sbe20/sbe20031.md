   <h3 align="center">16.</h3>  
 <p>1. Now at that time the Bhikkhus in the bath-room put the robes down on the ground, and the robes became dirty.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_111"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 111</font></a></p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a bamboo to hang your robes on, and of a string to hang your robes on <a name="fr_285"></a><a href="#fn_279"><font size="1">1</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>When rain fell, it fell over the robes.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to have a hall to the bath-room.'</p>  
 <p>The basement of the bath-room hall was too low [&amp;c., as in 11. 6; 14. 2 as to basement, roof-facing, stairs, and balustrade, followed by the closing words of 11. 6 and 14. 2, down to the end].</p>  
 <p>2. Now at that time the Bhikkhus were afraid <a name="fr_286"></a><a href="#fn_280"><font size="1">2</font></a> to do service to one another, both when in the bath-room and in the water.</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, three kinds of coverings--the covering of the bath-room, the covering of the water, and the covering by clothes <a name="fr_287"></a><a href="#fn_281"><font size="1">3</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>Now at that time there was no water in the bath-room.</p>  
 <p>They told this matter to the Blessed One. I allow, O Bhikkhus, a well.'</p>  
 <p>The facing of the well fell in <a name="fr_288"></a><a href="#fn_282"><font size="1">4</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to line the well with</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_112"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 112</font></a></p>  
 <p>facings of three kinds <a name="fr_289"></a><a href="#fn_283"><font size="1">1</font></a>--brick facing, stone facing, and wooden facing.'</p>  
 <p>[Then follow the paragraphs as to the high basement, the facing of the roof, the stairs, and the balustrade, as in 11. 6; 14. 2; and above, § 1 <a name="fr_290"></a><a href="#fn_284"><font size="1">2</font></a>.]</p>  
 <p>Now at that time the Bhikkhus drew water with jungle-rope <a name="fr_291"></a><a href="#fn_285"><font size="1">3</font></a>, or with their waistbands.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a string rope to draw water with.'</p>  
 <p>Their hands were hurt (by the rope).</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of a long pole balanced as a lever <a name="fr_292"></a><a href="#fn_286"><font size="1">4</font></a>, of a bullock machine <a name="fr_293"></a><a href="#fn_287"><font size="1">5</font></a>, or of a wheel and axle <a name="fr_294"></a><a href="#fn_288"><font size="1">6</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_113"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 113</font></a></p>  
 <p>A number of pots were broken.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, water-vessels <a name="fr_295"></a><a href="#fn_289"><font size="1">1</font></a> of three kinds--brass pots, wooden pots, and skins <a name="fr_296"></a><a href="#fn_290"><font size="1">2</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>Now at that time the Bhikkhus, when drawing water in the open air, suffered from heat and cold.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to build a shed over the well <a name="fr_297"></a><a href="#fn_291"><font size="1">3</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>Straw and plaster fell into the building over the well.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to cover the shed with skins, and to plaster it within and without; and I allow the use of whitewash, blacking, red-colouring, wreath work, creeper work, cupboards, bamboos to hang robes on, and strings to hang robes on.'</p>  
 <p>The well was uncovered, and it was littered over with grass, and plaster, and dirt.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, a lid <a name="fr_298"></a><a href="#fn_292"><font size="1">4</font></a> to the well.'</p>  
 <p>Water-vessels were found wanting.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, the use of troughs <a name="fr_299"></a><a href="#fn_293"><font size="1">5</font></a> and basons.'</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_279"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_285">111:1</a> So also above, 11. 6 (at the end), and 14. 2 (at the end).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_280"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_286">111:2</a> On account of the rule laid down in chapter 15.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_281"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_287">111:3</a> This rule abrogates that laid down in chapter 15, so far as regards bathing and shampooing. Buddhaghosa says accordingly, Tisso pa<i>t</i>i<i>k</i><i>kh</i>âdayo ti. Ettha <i>g</i>antâghara-pa<i>t</i>i<i>k</i><i>kh</i>âdi <i>k</i>a udakapa<i>t</i>i<i>k</i><i>kh</i>âdi <i>k</i>a parikamma<i>m</i> karontass’ eva va<i>t</i><i>t</i>ati, sesesu abhivâdanâdisu na va<i>t</i><i>t</i>ati. Vattha-pa<i>t</i>i<i>k</i><i>kh</i>âdi sabba-kammesu va<i>t</i><i>t</i>ati.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_282"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_288">111:4</a> Kûla<i>m</i> lu<i>g</i><i>g</i>ati. (The reading is not without doubt.) Compare Palu<i>g</i><i>g</i>ati. The same expression occurs below, V, 17, 2; and lu<i>g</i><i>g</i>ati at Mahâvagga VIII, 21, 1.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_283"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_289">112:1</a> <i>K</i>initu<i>m</i> tayo <i>k</i>aye. See our note 4 above on V, 14, 3, and the passages there quoted. The whole passage occurs V, 11, 6.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_284"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_290">112:2</a> All this refers doubtless to the kind of shed or portico to be erected over the well. (See below.) One would expect that the formal licence for such a ma<i>n</i><i>d</i>apa would have been inserted here in due course as above, 11. 6, for the Ka<i>th</i>ina-sâlâ.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_285"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_291">112:3</a> This is the usual Anglo-Indian term for the creepers so commonly used for such purposes. The Pâli word is vallikâ, which occurs in a different sense at V, 2, I.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_286"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_292">112:4</a> Tulâ. This is the ordinary and simple machine, so common in all countries where irrigation is carried on, for raising water from canals or from shallow wells. Buddhaghosa says here: Tulan ti pa<i>n</i><i>n</i>ikâna<i>m</i> viya udaka abbhâhana-tulâ. Pa<i>n</i><i>n</i>ika is 'florist' (see <i>G</i>âtaka I, 411, II, 180). Abbhâhana must be wrong (see Sutta Nipâta III, 8, 8); possibly abbhâvâhana is the correct reading.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_287"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_293">112:5</a> The name of this machine is spelt differently in the MSS. (karaka<i>t</i>aṅka the Sinhalese MS., and karaka<i>d</i>aka the Burmese MSS.), and the reading is doubtful. Buddhaghosa says: Dakadaka<i>t</i>ako (sic! In the next note but two the same MS. reads ka<i>t</i>adaka<i>t</i>ake) vu<i>k</i><i>k</i>ati go<i>n</i>e vâ yo<i>g</i>etvâ hatthehi vâ gahetvâ dîgha-varattâdîhi âka<i>d</i><i>dh</i>ana-yanta<i>m</i>. We can only say negatively that the word can have nothing to do either with karka<i>t</i>aka, a hook in the form of a crab's claw; or with kara-ka<i>n</i><i>t</i>aka, finger-nail.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_288"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_294">112:6</a> <i>K</i>akkava<i>t</i><i>t</i>aka<i>m</i>, on which Buddhaghosa has the unintelligible note arahatta(!)-gha<i>t</i>i-yanta<i>m</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_289"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_295">113:1</a> Vârake. The spelling of this word in Childers's Dictionary (varâko) is a misprint. Both the passages he quotes read vârako. Other water-vessels, besides these three, are allowed by the closing rule of this chapter.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_290"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_296">113:2</a> <i>K</i>amma-kha<i>n</i><i>d</i>a<i>m</i> nâma tûlâya vâ ka<i>t</i>adaka<i>t</i>ake vâ yo<i>g</i>etabba<i>m</i> <i>k</i>amma-bhâ<i>g</i>ana<i>m</i> (B.). The rendering adopted by Childers from Turnour (Mahâva<i>m</i>sa, p. 3) is therefore incorrect.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_291"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_297">113:3</a> The following passage has already occurred above, V, 11, 6 and V, 14, 3.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_292"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_298">113:4</a> Apidhâna<i>m</i>. See Mahâvagga VI, 12, 2.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_293"></a><a href="sbe20031.htm#fr_299">113:5</a> Udaka-do<i>n</i>i<i>m</i>. At <i>G</i>âtaka I, 450, such a do<i>n</i>i is said to have been made out of the trunk of a tree. Compare the use of mattikâ-do<i>n</i>ika<i>m</i> at V, 14, 3.</p>
