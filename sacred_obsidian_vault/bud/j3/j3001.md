 <p>30I. CULLAKĀLIṄGA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3002.htm#page_1">1</a></p>  
 <p>A king, being eager to fight, finds occasion to quarrel with another king. Misled by a prophecy of victory and neglecting the omens, he is defeated by his adversary.</p>  
 <p>302. MAHĀASSĀROHA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3002.htm#page_1">1</a></p>  
 <p>A king, being defeated by rebels, finds a hospitable shelter with a poor countryman, and rewards his benefactor with the half of his kingdom.</p>  
 <p>303. EKARAJA-JATAKA <a href="j3004.htm#page_9">9</a></p>  
 <p>A king is taken prisoner and tortured, and by his patience under suffering wins his enemy to repentance.</p>  
 <p>304. DADDARA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3004.htm#page_10">10</a></p>  
 <p>How two brothers were driven from their father's kingdom, and how their pride was humbled by the contumely they suffered in their exile.</p>  
 <p>305. SĪLAVĪMAṀSANA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3005.htm#page_12">12</a></p>  
 <p>A teacher tests the virtue of his pupils by tempting them to steal. The only youth, that stands the test, is rewarded by marrying his master's daughter.</p>  
 <p>306. SUJĀTA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3006.htm#page_13">13</a></p>  
 <p>How the daughter of a fruiterer became a queen, and by her pride nearly lost her position.</p>  
 <p>307. PALĀSA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3007.htm#page_15">15</a></p>  
 <p>A brahmin pays honour to a tree-spirit and is rewarded by the discovery of a buried treasure.</p>  
 <p>308. JAVASAKUṆA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3009.htm#page_17">17</a></p>  
 <p>The story of the woodpecker and the ungrateful lion.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_x"><font size="1" color="green">p. x</font></a></p>  
 <p>309. CHAVAKA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3009.htm#page_18">18</a></p>  
 <p>How a pariah, who stole mangoes, ventured to reprove a king for allowing a priest to teach him from a lower seat.</p>  
 <p>310. SAYHA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3010.htm#page_20">20</a></p>  
 <p>How a brahmin refused to give up the ascetic life in order to become family priest to a king.</p>  
 <p>311. PUCIMANDA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3011.htm#page_22">22</a></p>  
 <p>How a nimb-tree spirit frightened away a robber whose presence endangered the safety of the tree.</p>  
 <p>312. KASSAPAMANDIYA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3013.htm#page_24">24</a></p>  
 <p>A father and son in journeying together fall out by the way, and the old man is reproved for his want of self-restraint.</p>  
 <p>313. KHANTIVĀDI-JĀTAKA <a href="j3014.htm#page_26">26</a></p>  
 <p>How a wicked king cruelly maltreated an ascetic, and how the patience of the holy man endured to the end, and the king was cast into Hell.</p>  
 <p>314. LOHAKUMBHI-JĀTAKA <a href="j3014.htm#page_29">29</a></p>  
 <p>A king is terrified by hearing awful cries in the night and is urged by his family priest to avert the evil omen by the sacrifice of living creatures. A young brahmin interprets the sounds to be the cries uttered by lost souls in Hell, and the king takes comfort and forbids the sacrifice.</p>  
 <p>315. MAṀMMSA-JĀTAKA<a href="j3015.htm#page_32">32</a></p>  
 <p>How four young merchants tried to wheedle a hunter out of his venison, and how one alone by his cunning address succeeded.</p>  
 <p>316. SASA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3016.htm#page_34">34</a></p>  
 <p>How a hare, in default of other food, offered its own flesh to be eaten, and was rewarded by having its form supernaturally impressed on the face of the moon.</p>  
 <p>317. MATARODANA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3018.htm#page_38">38</a></p>  
 <p>How a youth, when his brother died, demonstrated the folly of grieving for the dead.</p>  
 <p>318. KANAVERA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3018.htm#page_39">39</a></p>  
 <p>How a courtezan rescued a robber by betraying her lover to death, and how she was afterwards punished for her treachery.</p>  
 <p>319. TITTIRA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3020.htm#page_43">43</a></p>  
 <p>A decoy-partridge is troubled with scruples of conscience.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_xi"><font size="1" color="green">p. xi</font></a></p>  
 <p>320. SUCCAJA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3020.htm#page_44">44</a></p>  
 <p>How a prince requited his wife's devotion with base ingratitude, until he was brought to a better mind by the admonition of his minister.</p>  
 <p>321 KUṬIDŪSAKA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3021.htm#page_47">47</a></p>  
 <p>How a monkey, through envy, destroyed a bird's nest.</p>

 <p>Of a foolish mendicant who met his death by mistaking the butting of a ram for a respectful salutation.</p>  
 <p>325. GODHA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3025.htm#page_56">56</a></p>

 <p>The story of the holy man who found a wife by means of a golden image, and how on her death he neither fasted nor wept.</p>  
 <p>329. KĀLABĀHU-JĀTAKA <a href="j3030.htm#page_65">65</a></p>  
 <p>The story of the parrots and the black monkey, and how the monkey fell into disgrace and the parrots regained the king's favour.</p>

 <p>Of the man who tested the power of virtue and of the moral lessons he learned from the hawk and the piece of meat and from the slave-girl to whom loss of hope alone brought peace.</p>

 <p>How a talkative king was admonished by the fate of the young bird that cried "cuckoo" too soon.</p>  
 <p>332. RATHALAṬṬHI-JĀTAKA <a href="j3032.htm#page_69">69</a></p>

 <p><a name="page_xii"><font size="1" color="green">p. xii</font></a></p>

 <p>334. RĀJOVĀDA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3035.htm#page_73">73</a></p>

 <p>How a king became an ascetic on being shewn a grey hair by his chief queen.</p>  
 <p>412. KOṬISIMBALI-JĀTAKA <a href="j3112.htm#page_239">239</a></p>  
 <p>How a tree-spirit was frightened by a bird and comforted by a roc-king.</p>  
 <p>413. DHŪMAKĀRI-JĀTAKA <a href="j3113.htm#page_241">241</a></p>  
 <p>How a king neglected old friends for new ones: his case illustrated by a story of a brahmin goatherd and some deer.</p>  
 <p>414. JĀGARA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3114.htm#page_243">243</a></p>  
 <p>How an ascetic kept vigil at nights.</p>  
 <p>415. KUMMĀSAPIṆḌA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3115.htm#page_244">244</a></p>  
 <p>How a king and queen declared the merits in former births that brought about their birth in royal rank.</p>  
 <p>416. PARANTAPA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3113.htm#page_240">240</a></p>  
 <p>How a prince understood the speech of jackals: and how a king's son discovered and avenged his father's murder after many years.</p>  
 <p>417. KACCĀNI-JĀTAKA <a href="j3118.htm#page_253">253</a></p>  
 <p>How an old woman, expelled from her son's house owing to her daughter-in-law, thought that Right was dead: and how the whole family became reconciled.</p>  
 <p>418. AṬṬHASADDA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3118.htm#page_256">256</a></p>  
 <p>How eight sounds that had frightened a king were explained to him harmlessly.</p>  
 <p>419. SULASĀ-JĀTAKA <a href="j3119.htm#page_260">260</a></p>  
 <p>How a man who would have killed his wife was killed by her.</p>  
 <p>420. SUMAṄGALA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3120.htm#page_263">263</a></p>  
 <p>How a king would not decide a case till his anger was over.</p>  
 <p>421. GAṄGAMĀLA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3122.htm#page_266">266</a></p>  
 <p>How a willing servant was reborn as a king: how he shared his kingdom for a time with a poor water-carrier who had shown himself an honest fellow: how a barber got from the king the explanation of his birth in the kingly rank, and became a paccekabuddha, honoured by the king.</p>  
 <p>422. CETIYA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3122.htm#page_271">271</a></p>  
 <p>How a king, who told a lie in the golden age, sank into the earth and so down to Hell.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_xix"><font size="1" color="green">p. xix</font></a></p>  
 <p>423. INDRIYA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3123.htm#page_276">276</a></p>  
 <p>How a tempted ascetic was warned by the story of a miserable hunter.</p>  
 <p>424. ĀDITTA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3125.htm#page_280">280</a></p>  
 <p>How seven paccekabuddhas came and received gifts from a king.</p>  
 <p>425. AṬṬHĀNA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3125.htm#page_282">282</a></p>  
 <p>How an ascetic repulsed a woman who had once behaved harshly to him.</p>  
 <p>426. DĪPI-JĀTAKA <a href="j3127.htm#page_285">285</a></p>  
 <p>How a panther ate a she-goat for all her politeness.</p>  
 <p>427. GIJJHA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3128.htm#page_287">287</a></p>  
 <p>How a vulture perished, through attempting too bold a flight.</p>  
 <p>428. KOSAMBĪ-JĀTAKA <a href="j3129.htm#page_289">289</a></p>  
 <p>(Imperfect—with a reference to the story in No. 371.)</p>  
 <p>429. MAHĀSUKA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3129.htm#page_291">291</a></p>  
 <p>How a grateful parrot refused to leave a barren fig-tree.</p>  
 <p>430. CULLASUKA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3131.htm#page_294">294</a></p>  
 <p>The same story as the preceding one.</p>  
 <p>431. HĀRITA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3132.htm#page_295">295</a></p>  
 <p>Of an ascetic who would not tell a lie to conceal his sin.</p>  
 <p>432. PADAKUSALAMĀṆAVA-JATAKA <a href="j3133.htm#page_298">298</a></p>  
 <p>A boy receives, as a gift from a goblin mother, the power of recognizing footsteps even in the air, and a king, to test the boy's skill, steals his own jewels and then sets the boy to catch the thief. When the boy by a number of pointed stories convicts him of theft, the king is put to death by his own subjects and the boy becomes king.</p>  
 <p>433. LOMASAKASSAPA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3133.htm#page_306">306</a></p>  
 <p>How a king promised his daughter in marriage to an ascetic, if he would offer a living sacrifice, and how the ascetic resisted the temptation.</p>  
 <p>434. CAKKAVĀKA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3134.htm#page_309">309</a></p>  
 <p>How a crow, through his greediness, could not attain to the beauty of the ruddy goose.</p>  
 <p>435. HALIDDIRĀGA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3135.htm#page_311">311</a></p>  
 <p>A youth, who was being led astray by female seductions, is rescued by the sage counsels of his father.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_xx"><font size="1" color="green">p. xx</font></a></p>  
 <p>436. SAMUGGA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3136.htm#page_313">313</a></p>  
 <p>How a demon, who swallowed his wife and carried her about in his belly, even so failed to keep her virtuous.</p>  
 <p>437. PŪTIMAṀSA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3137.htm#page_316">316</a></p>  
 <p>How a wise she-goat outwitted the jackal that was plotting to kill her.</p>  
 <p>438. TITTIRA-JĀTAKA <a href="j3138.htm#page_319">319</a></p>  
 <p>How a wicked ascetic killed a learned partridge, and how a lion and a tiger avenged the death of the partridge.</p>
