   <h3 align="center">12.</h3>  
 <p>1. Now when the Blessed One had remained at Râ<i>g</i>agaha as long as he thought fit, he set forth on his journey towards Dakkhi<i>n</i>â-giri (the Southern Hills <a name="fr_363"></a><a href="#fn_358"><font size="1">2</font></a>). And the Blessed One beheld how the Magadha rice fields were divided into short pieces <a name="fr_364"></a><a href="#fn_359"><font size="1">3</font></a>,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_208"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 208</font></a></p>  
 <p>and in rows <a name="fr_365"></a><a href="#fn_360"><font size="1">1</font></a>, and by outside boundaries <a name="fr_366"></a><a href="#fn_361"><font size="1">2</font></a> (or ridges), and by cross boundaries <a name="fr_367"></a><a href="#fn_362"><font size="1">3</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>On seeing this the Blessed One spake thus to the venerable Ânanda: 'Dost thou perceive, Ânanda, how the Magadha rice fields are divided into short pieces, and in rows, and by outside boundaries, and by cross boundaries?'</p>  
 <p>'Even so, Lord.'</p>  
 <p>'Could you, Ânanda, provide <a name="fr_368"></a><a href="#fn_363"><font size="1">4</font></a> robes of a like kind for the Bhikkhus?'</p>  
 <p>'I could, Lord.'</p>  
 <p>Now when the Blessed One had remained in the Southern Hills as long as he thought fit, he returned again to Râ<i>g</i>agaha.</p>  
 <p>Then Ânanda provided robes of a like kind for many Bhikkhus; and going up to the place where the Blessed One was, he spake thus to the Blessed One: 'May the Blessed One be pleased to look at the robes which I have provided.'</p>  
 <p>2. Then the Blessed One on that occasion addressed the Bhikkhus and said: 'An able man, O Bhikkhus, is Ânanda; of great understanding, O Bhikkhus, is Ânanda, inasmuch as what has been spoken by me in short that can he understand in full, and can make the cross seams <a name="fr_369"></a><a href="#fn_364"><font size="1">5</font></a>, and the</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_209"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 209</font></a></p>  
 <p>intermediate cross seams <a name="fr_370"></a><a href="#fn_365"><font size="1">1</font></a>, and the greater circles <a name="fr_371"></a><a href="#fn_366"><font size="1">2</font></a>, and the lesser circles <a name="fr_372"></a><a href="#fn_367"><font size="1">3</font></a>, and the turning in <a name="fr_373"></a><a href="#fn_368"><font size="1">4</font></a>, and the lining of the turning in <a name="fr_374"></a><a href="#fn_369"><font size="1">5</font></a>, and the collar piece <a name="fr_375"></a><a href="#fn_370"><font size="1">6</font></a>, and the knee piece <a name="fr_376"></a><a href="#fn_371"><font size="1">7</font></a>, and the elbow piece <a name="fr_377"></a><a href="#fn_372"><font size="1">8</font></a>. And it shall be of torn pieces <a name="fr_378"></a><a href="#fn_373"><font size="1">9</font></a>, roughly sewn together <a name="fr_379"></a><a href="#fn_374"><font size="1">10</font></a>, suitable for a Sama<i>n</i>a, a thing which his enemies cannot covet <a name="fr_380"></a><a href="#fn_375"><font size="1">11</font></a>. I enjoin upon you, O Bhikkhus, the use of an under robe of torn pieces, and of an upper robe of torn pieces, and of a waist cloth of torn pieces <a name="fr_381"></a><a href="#fn_376"><font size="1">12</font></a>.'</p>

 <p><a name="fn_373"></a><a href="sbe17079.htm#fr_378">209:9</a> See the end of the last chapter.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_374"></a><a href="sbe17079.htm#fr_379">209:10</a> Satta-lûkha; in which compound the signification of satta is by no means clear. Buddhaghosa has no note upon it. Now it is curious that in chapter 21, below, it is laid down that the robe is to be sutta-lûkha, the meaning of which would fit this passage excellently. We have accordingly adopted that reading here.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_375"></a><a href="sbe17079.htm#fr_380">209:11</a> Compare the similar expressions at <i>G</i>âtaka I, 8 and 9.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_376"></a><a href="sbe17079.htm#fr_381">209:12</a> The general sense of this chapter is clear enough. As an Indian field, the common property of the village community, was <a name="page_210"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 210</font></a> divided, for the purposes of cultivation, across and across, so must also the Bhikkhu's robe be divided. That some, both of the agricultural and of the tailoring terms, should now be unintelligible to us is not surprising. Buddhaghosa himself, as the extracts from his commentary show, was not certain of the meaning of them all.</p>
