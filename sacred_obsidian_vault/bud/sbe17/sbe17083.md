 <p><a name="page_226"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 226</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">16.</h3>  
 <p>1, 2. Now at that time Bhikkhus who had eaten sweet foods went to sleep unmindful and unthoughtful. And they who had thus gone to sleep, dreamed <a name="fr_417"></a><a href="#fn_409"><font size="1">1</font></a> . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .</p>  
 <p>3 'I allow, O Bhikkhus, for the protection of the body, and of the robe, and of the sleeping-place, the use of a mat.'</p>  
 <p>4. Now at that time the mat, being too short <a name="fr_418"></a><a href="#fn_410"><font size="1">2</font></a>, did not protect the whole of the sleeping-place.</p>  
 <p>'I allow you, O Bhikkhus, to have a covering made as large as you like.'</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_409"></a><a href="sbe17083.htm#fr_417">226:1</a> The remainder of this introductory story scarcely bears translation. The first sentences recur in the Sutta-vibhaṅga, Sa<i>m</i>ghâdisesa I, 2, I, and Pâ<i>k</i>ittiya V, 1, I.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_410"></a><a href="sbe17083.htm#fr_418">226:2</a> The length of a mat (nisîdana<i>m</i>) was limited by the 89th Pâ<i>k</i>ittiya to two spans by one.</p>
