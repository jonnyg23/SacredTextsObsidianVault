 <h3 align="CENTER">547.</h3>
 <p>
 </p><h3 align="CENTER">WHERE A SPECTATOR SHOULD STAND TO LOOK AT A PICTURE.</h3>
 <p>
 Supposing <i>a b</i> to be the picture and <i>d</i> to be the light, I say
 that if you place yourself between <i>c</i> and <i>e</i> you will not
 </p><p><a name="page_275"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 275</font></a></p>
 <p></p><p align="CENTER"><a name="img_v127500"></a>
 <img src="img/v127500.jpg"></p>
 understand the picture well and particularly if it is done in oils,
 or still more if it is varnished, because it will be lustrous and
 somewhat of the nature of a mirror. And for this reason the nearer
 you go towards the point <i>c</i>, the less you will see, because the
 rays of light falling from the window on the picture are reflected
 to that point. But if you place yourself between <i>e</i> and <i>d</i> you
 will get a good view of it, and the more so as you approach the
 point <i>d</i>, because that spot is least exposed to these reflected
 rays of light.
