 </p><h3 align="CENTER">THE FARTHER THE DERIVED SHADOW IS PROLONGED THE LIGHTER IT BECOMES.</h3>
 <p>
 You will find that the proportion of the diameter of the derived
 shadow to that of the primary shadow will be the same as that
 between the darkness of the primary shadow and that of the derived
 shadow.
 </p><p>
  <a name="fr_80"></a><a href="#fn_80"><font size="1">78</font></a>
  Let <i>a b</i> be the diameter of the
 primary shadow and <i>c d</i> that of the derived shadow, I say that <i>a
 b</i> going, as you see, three times into <i>d c</i>, the shadow <i>d c</i> will
 be three times as light as the shadow <i>a b</i>.  <a name="fr_81"></a><a href="#fn_81"><font size="1">79</font></a>
 
 </p><p>
 If the size of the illuminating body is larger than that of
 </p><p><a name="page_99"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 99</font></a></p>
 <p>the illuminated body an intersection of shadow will occur, beyond which
 the shadows will run off in two opposite directions as if they were
 caused by two separate lights.
 </p><p>
