 <p align="CENTER"><a name="img_v113100"></a>
 <img src="img/v113100.jpg"></p>
 <p>
 <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"> The importance of light and shade in the perspective of disappearance (235-239).</font></span>An opaque body seen in a line in which the light falls will reveal
 no prominences to the eye. For instance, let <i>a</i> be the solid body
 and <i>c</i> the light; <i>c m</i> and <i>c n</i> will be the lines of incidence of
 the light, that is to say the lines which transmit the light to the
 object <i>a</i>. The eye being at the point <i>b</i>, I say
 </p><p><a name="page_132"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 132</font></a></p>
 <p>that since the
 light <i>c</i> falls on the whole part <i>m n</i> the portions in relief on
 that side will all be illuminated. Hence the eye placed at <i>c</i>
 cannot see any light and shade and, not seeing it, every portion
 will appear of the same tone, therefore the relief in the prominent
 or rounded parts will not be visible.
 </p><p>
