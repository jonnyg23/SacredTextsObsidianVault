 <p><a name="page_98"><font size="1" color="green">p. 98</font></a> [82]</p>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. XXVI.—THE DEITIES THE AUGUST DESCENDANTS OF THE DEITY MASTER-OF-THE-GREAT-LAND.]</h2>  
 <p>So this Deity Master-of-the-Great-Land wedded Her Augustness Torrent-Mist-Princess, the Deity dwelling in the inner temple of Munakata. <a name="fr_538"></a><a href="#fn_534"><font size="1">1</font></a> and begot children: the Deity Aji-shiki-taka-hiko-ne, <a name="fr_539"></a><a href="#fn_535"><font size="1">2</font></a> next his younger sister Her Augustness High-Princess, <a name="fr_540"></a><a href="#fn_536"><font size="1">3</font></a> another name for whom is Her Augustness Princess Under-Shining. <a name="fr_541"></a><a href="#fn_537"><font size="1">4</font></a> This Deity Aji-shiki-taka-hiko-ne is he who is now called the Great August Deity of Kamo. <a name="fr_542"></a><a href="#fn_538"><font size="1">5</font></a> Again the Deity Master-of-the-Great-Land wedded Her Augustness Princess Divine-House-Shield <a name="fr_543"></a><a href="#fn_539"><font size="1">6</font></a> and begot a child: the Deity Thing-Sign-Master. <a name="fr_544"></a><a href="#fn_540"><font size="1">7</font></a> Again he wedded the Deity Bird-Ears, <a name="fr_545"></a><a href="#fn_541"><font size="1">8</font></a> daughter of the Deity Eight-Island-Possessor, <a name="fr_546"></a><a href="#fn_542"><font size="1">9</font></a> and begot</p>

href="#fn_544"><font size="1">11</font></a> and begot a child: the Deity Land-Great-Wealth. <a name="fr_549"></a><a href="#fn_545"><font size="1">12</font></a> This Deity wedded the Deity Ashi-nadaka, <a name="fr_550"></a><a href="#fn_546"><font size="1">13</font></a> another name for whom is Princess-Eight-Rivers-and-Inlets, <a name="fr_551"></a><a href="#fn_547"><font size="1">14</font></a> and begot a child: the Deity Swift-Awful-Brave-Sahaya-Land-Ruler. <a name="fr_552"></a><a href="#fn_548"><font size="1">15</font></a> This Deity wedded Princess Luck-Spirit, <a name="fr_553"></a><a href="#fn_549"><font size="1">16</font></a> daughter of the [84] Deity Heavenly-Awful-Master, <a name="fr_554"></a><a href="#fn_550"><font size="1">17</font></a> and begot a child: the Deity Awful-Master-Prince. <a name="fr_555"></a><a href="#fn_551"><font size="1">18</font></a> This Deity wedded Princess Hina-rashi, <a name="fr_556"></a><a href="#fn_552"><font size="1">19</font></a> daughter of the Deity Okami, <a name="fr_557"></a><a href="#fn_553"><font size="1">20</font></a> and begot a child: the Deity Tahiri-kishi-marumi. <a name="fr_558"></a><a href="#fn_554"><font size="1">21</font></a> This Deity wedded the Deity Princess-Life-Spirit-Luck-Spirit, <a name="fr_559"></a><a href="#fn_555"><font size="1">22</font></a> daughter of the Deity Waiting-to-See-the-Flowers-of-the-Holly, <a name="fr_560"></a><a href="#fn_556"><font size="1">23</font></a> and begot a child: the Deity Mira-na-mi. <a name="fr_561"></a><a href="#fn_557"><font size="1">24</font></a> This Deity wedded Princess Awo-numa-oshi, <a name="fr_562"></a><a href="#fn_558"><font size="1">25</font></a> daughter of the Master-of-Shiki-yama, <a name="fr_563"></a><a href="#fn_559"><font size="1">26</font></a> and begot a child: the Deity Nunoshi-tomi-tori-naru-mi. <a name="fr_564"></a><a href="#fn_560"><font size="1">27</font></a> This Deity wedded the Young-Day-Female-Deity, <a name="fr_565"></a><a href="#fn_561"><font size="1">28</font></a> and begot a child: the Deity Heavenly-Hibara-Great-Long-Wind-Wealth. <a name="fr_566"></a><a href="#fn_562"><font size="1">29</font></a> This Deity wedded the Deity Toho-tsu-ma-chi-ne, <a name="fr_567"></a><a href="#fn_563"><font size="1">30</font></a> daughter of the Deity Heavenly-Pass Boundary, <a name="fr_568"></a><a href="#fn_564"><font size="1">31</font></a> and begot a child: the Deity Toho-tsu-yama-zaki-tarashi. <a name="fr_569"></a><a href="#fn_565"><font size="1">32</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">From the above-mentioned Deity Eight-Island-Ruler down to the Deity Toho-tsu-yama-zaki-tarashi are called the Deities of seventeen generations. <a name="fr_570"></a><a href="#fn_566"><font size="1">33</font></a></div>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_534"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_538">98:1</a> <a name="page_99"><font size="1" color="green">p. 99</font></a> See Sect. XIII, Note 15 and Sect. XIV. Note 2.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_535"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_539">98:2</a> <i>Aji-shiki taka-hiko-ne no-kami</i>. The meaning of the first two members of this compound name is altogether obscure. <i>Taka-hiko-ne</i> signifies "high-prince lord."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_536"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_540">98:3</a> <i>Taka-hime-no-mikoto</i>. <i>Taka-hime</i> is supposed by Hirata to be a mutilated form of <i>Taka-teru-hime</i>, "High-Shining-Princess," which would make the two names of this personage properly complementary.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_537"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_541">98:4</a> <a name="page_100"><font size="1" color="green">p. 100</font></a> <i>Shita-teru-hime-no-mikoto</i>. This goddess is popularly supposed to have been extremely beautiful, whence perhaps the name, which might be taken to imply that her beauty shone forth from under her garments as in the case of <i>So-towori-hime</i> (see Sect. CXXXVII, Note 9).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_538"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_542">98:5</a> Because there worshipped. The etymology of Kamo is not clear.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_539"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_543">98:6</a> <i>Kamu-ya-tate-hime-no-mikoto</i>. The translation here follows the Chinese characters. Another proposal of Motowori's is to regard the syllables ya-tate as a corruption of <i>iya-taka-teri</i>, "more and more high shining," which would give us for the whole name in English "Divine-More-and-More-High-Shining-Princess."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_540"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_544">98:7</a> <i>I.e.</i>, "the Deity who gave a sign of the thing he did." The Japanese original is <i>Koto-shire-nushi-no-kami</i>. The translation of the name here given follows Motowori's interpretation, which takes it to contain an allusion to the act by which its bearer symbolized his surrender of the sovereignty of the land to the descendant of the Sun-Goddess. Lengthened forms of the name are <i>Ya-he-koto-shiro-nushi-no-kami</i> ("the Deity Eight-Fold-Thing-Sign-Master") and <i>Tsumi-ba-ya-he-koto-shiro-nushi-no-kami</i>, the first three syllables of which latter are obscure. Both of the lengthened forms are supposed to contain a reference to the manifold "green branches" mentioned in the legend referred to,—that. viz., which forms the subject-matter of Sect. XXXII.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_541"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_545">98:8</a> <i>Tori-mimi-no-kami</i>. Motowori suggests that tori, "bird," may be but the name of a place in Yamato.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_542"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_546">98:9</a> <i>Ya-shima-muji-no-kami</i>. "Possessor" is the probable meaning of <i>muji</i>, regarded here and elsewhere as an alternative form of <i>mochi</i>. Motowori suggests that Yashima may be meant for the name of a district in Yamato, in which case both this god and his daughter would have been named from the places of their birth or residence, which are near each other in the same province.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_543"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_547">99:10</a> <i>Tori-nara-mi-no-kami</i>. The above interpretation, which is proposed by Motowori, seems more acceptable than "Bird-Sounding-Sea," which the Chinese characters yield. <i>Tori</i> "bird," if taken above to be the name of a place, must be likewise so considered here.—Motowori reasonably conjectures that a clause to the following effect is here omitted: "He wedded such and such a princess, daughter of such and such a Deity, and begot a child: the Deity <i>Take-mina-gata</i>" [<i>i.e.</i> probably Brave-August-Name-Firm] (See Sect. XXXII, Note 21). Hirata's text in his "Exposition of the Ancient Histories" is <a name="img_10000"></a>
 <img src="img/10000.jpg">.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_544"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_548">99:11</a> <a name="page_101"><font size="1" color="green">p. 101</font></a> The text is here evidently corrupt, and Motowori proposes to read either <i>Hina-teri-nukata-bichi-wo-no-kami no musume Iko-chi-ni-no-kami</i> which would give us in English "the Deity Ikochini, daughter of the male Deity Hina-teri-nukata-bichi," or else to take the whole as the father's name, and to suppose that the name of the daughter has been accidentally omitted. <i>Hina-teri</i> means "Rustic Illuminator," and the name resembles that of a deity mentioned in Sect. XIV, Note 6. <i>Nukata</i> and <i>Bichi</i> (or <i>Hiji</i>, reversing the position of the <i>nigori</i>) are supposed to be names of places. <i>Ikochini</i> is altogether obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_545"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_549">99:12</a> <i>Kuni-oshi-tomi-no-kami</i>, <i>oshi</i>, as in other instances, being considered a contraction of <i>ohoshi</i>, "great."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_546"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_550">99:13</a> <i>Ashi-nakada-no-kami</i>. It is not clear whether this is a personal name or, as Motowori supposes, the name of the place where the goddess resided. He quotes places named Ashidaka and Ashida; but this hardly seems satisfactory. In any case the name remains obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_547"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_551">99:14</a> <i>Ya-kaha-ye-hime</i>. The translation follows the meaning of the Chinese characters with which the name is written. It is, however, also open to us to consider <i>Yaka-ha-ye</i> as a corruption of <i>iya-ko-haye</i>, "more flourishing."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_548"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_552">99:15</a> <i>Haya-mika-no-take sahaya-ji-nu-mi-no-kami</i>. The syllables <i>sahaya</i> are obscure, and Motowori's proposal to consider them as the name of a place has only been followed in the translation for want of something more satisfactory.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_549"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_553">99:16</a> <i>Saki-tama-bime</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_550"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_554">99:17</a> <i>Ame-no-mika-a-nushi-no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_551"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_555">99:18</a> <i>wika-nushi-hike-no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_552"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_556">99:19</a> <i>Hina-rashi-bime</i>. Motowori takes Hina to be the name of a place, and <i>rashi</i>, to be an apocopated form of <i>tarashi</i> or some such word. But this is mere guess-work.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_553"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_557">99:20</a> <i>Okami-no-kami</i>. See Sect. VIII, Note 9.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_554"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_558">99:21</a> <i>Tahiri-kishi-marumi-no-kami</i>. The meaning of this name is quite obscure. Motowori throws out the suggestion that <i>Tahiri</i> may stand for <i>Tari-hiri</i> and <i>Kishi-marumi</i> for <i>Kizhima-tsu-mi</i>,—<i>Tarihi</i> and <i>Kizhiwa</i> being names of places, and <i>tsu-mi</i>, as usual, being credited with the signification of "possessor."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_555"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_559">99:22</a> <i>Iku-tama-saki-tama-hime</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_556"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_560">99:23</a> <i>Hihira-gi-no-sono-hana-madzu-mi-no-kami</i>. The interpretation of the name here given is conjectural as far as the words "waiting to see" (taken on Tominobu's authority to be the most likely meaning of <i>madzu-mi</i>) <a name="page_102"><font size="1" color="green">p. 102</font></a> are concerned. Motowori suggests that <i>hihira-gi-no</i> may be but a sort of Pillow-Word, and not part of the actual name at all, and the remaining characters corrupted. <i>Hihira-gi</i> rendered "holly," is properly the <i>Olea Aquifolia</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_557"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_561">99:24</a> <i>Miro-nami-no-kami</i>. Meaning obscure. <i>Miro</i> is supposed by Motowori to be the name of a place, and <i>na</i> and <i>mi</i> to be Honorific appellations.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_558"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_562">99:25</a> <i>Awo-numa-nu-oshi-hime</i>. Meaning obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_559"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_563">99:26</a> <i>Shiki-yama-nushi-no-kami</i>. <i>Shiki-yama</i> is supposed to be the name of a place in Echizen.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_560"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_564">99:27</a> <i>Nunoshi-tomi-tori-nara-mi-no-kami</i>. <i>Nunoshi</i> is supposed to be the name of a place, and identical with <i>Nunoshi</i>, which forms part of the mother's name. Motowori takes <i>tomi</i> to be an Honorific, and <i>Tori</i> (as previously in the case of the deities <i>Tori-mimi</i> and <i>Tori-naru-mi</i> (See Notes 8 and 10) to be the name of another place. The translator would prefer to take both words in their common signification, and (leaving <i>nunoshi</i> aside as incomprehensible) to render the rest of the name thus: "Wealth-Bird-Growing-Ears."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_561"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_565">99:28</a> <i>Waka-hiru-me-na-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_562"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_566">99:29</a> <i>Ame-no-hibara-oho-shi-na-domi-no-kami</i>. Motowori supposes Hibara to be the name of a place, a view which the translator has adopted for want of a better.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_563"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_567">99:30</a> <i>Toho-tsu-ma-chi-ne-no-kami</i>. Motowori supposes Tohotsu to be the name of a place, and the remaining syllables to be Honorific.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_564"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_568">99:31</a> There is no footnote 31—<i>JBH</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_565"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_569">99:32</a> <i>Toho-tsu-yama-zaki-tarashi-no-kami</i>. <i>Toho-tsu</i> (lit. "distant") and <i>yamazaki</i> ("mountain-cape") are both considered by Motowori to be names of places. Tarashi signifies "perfect" or "perfection." We might perhaps render the name thus: "Perfection-of-the-Distant-Mountain- Cape."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_566"></a><a href="kj033.htm#fr_570">99:33</a> <i>I.e.</i> "seventeen generations of Deities." But the construction is curious. Motowori points out that there is here an error in the computation, as the text enumerates but fifteen generations. The names of the gods and goddess mentioned in this section offer unusual difficulties Motowori says that it is with hesitation that he proposes many of his interpretations, and it is with still greater hesitation that the translator has accepted them.</p>
