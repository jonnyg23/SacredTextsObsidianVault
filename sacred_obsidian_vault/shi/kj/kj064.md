 <p><a name="page_192"><font size="1" color="green">p. 192</font></a> [156]</p>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. LVII.—EMPEROR I-TOKU.]</h2>  
 <p>His Augustness Oho-yamato-hiko-suki-tomo dwelt in the palace of Sakahiwo at Karu, <a name="fr_1077"></a><a href="#fn_1073"><font size="1">1</font></a> and ruled the Empire. This Heavenly Sovereign wedded her Augustness Princess Futo-ma-waka, <a name="fr_1078"></a><a href="#fn_1074"><font size="1">2</font></a> another name for whom was Her Augustness Princess Ihi-bi, <a name="fr_1079"></a><a href="#fn_1075"><font size="1">3</font></a> ancestress of the Departmental Lords of Shiki, <a name="fr_1080"></a><a href="#fn_1076"><font size="1">4</font></a> and begot august children: His Augustness Mi-ma-tsu-hiko-wake-shine, <a name="fr_1081"></a><a href="#fn_1077"><font size="1">5</font></a> next His Augustness Tagishi-hiko <a name="fr_1082"></a><a href="#fn_1078"><font size="1">6</font></a> (<small>two Deities</small>). So His Augustness Mi-ma-tsu-hiko-kawe-shine [was he who afterwards] ruled the Empire. The next His Augustness Tagishi-hiko (<small>was the ancestor of the Lords of Chinu, <a name="fr_1083"></a><a href="#fn_1079"><font size="1">7</font></a> of the Lords of Take in Tajima, <a name="fr_1084"></a><a href="#fn_1080"><font size="1">8</font></a> and of the Territorial Lords of Ashiwi</small>). <a name="fr_1085"></a><a href="#fn_1081"><font size="1">9</font></a> The Heavenly Sovereign's august years were forty-five. His august mausoleum is above the Vale of Manago by Mount Unebi. <a name="fr_1086"></a><a href="#fn_1082"><font size="1">10</font></a></p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_1073"></a><a href="kj064.htm#fr_1077">192:1</a> <a name="page_192"><font size="1" color="green">p. 192</font></a> Karu, which still remains as a village in the province of Yamato, was famous down to the early historical days of Japan, being often mentioned by the poets. The derivation of the name is quite uncertain. <i>Sakahi-wo</i> probably signifies "boundary mound."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1074"></a><a href="kj064.htm#fr_1078">192:2</a> <i>Futo-ma-wakes-hime-no-mikoto</i>. The compound signifies "vast, true, and young princess."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1075"></a><a href="kj064.htm#fr_1079">192:3</a> <i>Ihibi-hime-no-mikoto</i>. The name seems to signify "rice-sun-princess."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1076"></a><a href="kj064.htm#fr_1080">192:4</a> See Sect. LV, Note 3.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1077"></a><a href="kj064.htm#fr_1081">192:5</a> The import of this name is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1078"></a><a href="kj064.htm#fr_1082">192:6</a> <i>i.e.</i>, perhaps "rudder prince."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1079"></a><a href="kj064.htm#fr_1083">192:7</a> <i>Chinu no wake</i>. For Chinu see Sect. XLIV, Notes 35 and 36.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1080"></a><a href="kj064.htm#fr_1084">192:8</a> <i>Tajima no Take no wake</i>. Motowori tells us that no mention of any place called Take in the province of Tajima is to be found in any other book.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1081"></a><a href="kj064.htm#fr_1085">192:9</a> <i>Ashiwi no inaki</i>. The same remark applies to this name as to the last. The two "gentile names" here mentioned are equally unknown except from this passage.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1082"></a><a href="kj064.htm#fr_1086">192:10</a> <i>Unebi-yama no Manago-dani</i>. It is in Yamato, and is now called Masago. The name signifies "a sandy place, or desert."</p>
