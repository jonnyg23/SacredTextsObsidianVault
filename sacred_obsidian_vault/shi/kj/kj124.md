 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_1994"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_1999">329:1</a> <a name="page_330"><font size="1" color="green">p. 330</font></a> <i>I.e.</i>, the Emperor Ō-jin's.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1995"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2000">329:2</a> The import of this compound is not clear.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1996"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2001">329:3</a> <i>I.e.</i>, "the  younger princess, the truly young princess."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1997"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2002">329:4</a> <i>i.e.</i>, "the great lord."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1998"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2003">329:5</a> <i>Oho-hodo no miko</i>. The signification of <i>Oho-hodo</i> is obscure. Motowori surmises it to have been originally the name of a place.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1999"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2004">329:6</a> <i>I.e.</i>, "the great middle princess of Osaka. Osaka is the name of a place in Yamato. The word "middle "should by the analogy of other such genealogies indicate the fact that this princess was the fourth child out of seven. Here however she is mentioned second, and the same designation is applied to the two next daughters. There is evidently some confusion in the tradition.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2000"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2005">330:7</a> <i>I.e.</i>, "the middle princess of Tawi,"—a place in Kahachi.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2001"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2006">330:8</a> <i>I.e.</i>, the middle princess of Tamiya.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2002"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2007">330:9</a> <i>I.e.</i>, "the lady Koto-fushi of Fuji-hara." But the meaning of Kato fushi is obscure, and Motowori surmises it to be an alternative or corrupt form of <i>Sotohoshi</i>. (For the celebrated princess of the latter name see Sect. CXXXVII, Note 9.) Fujihara is the name of a place in Yamato, and signifies "wistaria-moor."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2003"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2008">330:10</a> <i>Torime no miko</i>. This name is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2004"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2009">330:11</a> <i>Sane no miko</i>. Motowori believes <i>sane</i> to stand erroneously for <i>sami</i>; but both forms are obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2005"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2010">330:12</a> The Japanese word includes both genders.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2006"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2011">330:13</a> <a name="page_331"><font size="1" color="green">p. 331</font></a> <i>Mikuni no kimi</i>. <i>Mikuni</i> is the name of a well-known place in the province of Echizen. It signifies "three countries."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2007"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2012">330:14</a> <i>Hata no kimi</i>. There are several places called Hata, and it is not known which of them is here intended. The signification of the name is also uncertain.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2008"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2013">330:15</a> <i>Okinaga no kimi</i>. See Sect. LXII, Note 31.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2009"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2014">330:16</a> <i>Sakata no Saka-bito no kimi</i>. This is Motowori's restoration of an apparently corrupt text. Sakata and Sakabito are both taken to be names of places, the first of a district in Afumi, the second of a place in Settsu. Sakabito (<a name="img_33100"></a>
 <img src="img/33100.jpg">) seems a very curious compound for the name of a place. Moreover the double title is unusual, and it may be thought that the word "Dukes" has fallen out of the text, and that in reality two families were intended to be spoken of.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2010"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2015">330:17</a> <i>Yamaji no kimi</i>. <i>Yamaji</i> ("mountain road") is supposed by Motowori to be the name of a place,—perhaps in the province of Higo.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2011"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2016">330:18</a> <i>Tsukushi no Meta no kimi</i>. Tsukushi is the old name of the whole of the South-Western island of the Japanese archipelago and Meta the name of a place in the province of Hizen in that island. The etymology of Meta is uncertain.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2012"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2017">330:19</a> <i>Fuse no kimi</i>. Fuse is the name of uncertain import found in several provinces. It is not known which is meant to be here designated.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2013"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2018">330:20</a> <i>Naka-tsu-hiko no miko</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, "the middle prince," a designation which would lead one to expect to find mention of an elder brother.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2014"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2019">330:21</a> <i>Iwashima no miko</i>. Iwashima seems to be the name of a place, but the signification of <i>Iha</i> (not to be confounded with <i>iha</i> "stone" or "rock") is altogether obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2015"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2020">330:22</a> <i>Katashiha no miko</i>. This prince has not been mentioned in the previous genealogies, which is curious. Katashiha is the name of a place in Chikuzen, and signifies "hard rock."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2016"></a><a href="kj124.htm#fr_2021">330:23</a> <i>Kunu no miko</i>. <i>Kunu</i> is altogether obscure.</p>
