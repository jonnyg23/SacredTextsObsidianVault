 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Kojiki</i>, translated by Basil Hall Chamberlain, [1919], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. CXXVI.—EMPEROR NIN-TOKU (PART VIII.—DEATH OF KING HAYABUSA-WAKE AND QUEEN MEDORI).]</h2>  
 <p>Again the Heavenly Sovereign begged for his younger half-sister Queen Medori, using as middle-man his younger brother King Haya-busa-wake. Then Queen Medori [281] spoke to King Hayabusa-wake, saying: "Owing to the</p>  
 <p><a name="page_350"><font size="1" color="green">p. 350</font></a></p>  
 <p>violence of the Empress, [the Heavenly Sovereign] has not deigned to take Yata-no-waki-iratsume [into the Palace.] So I will not respectfully serve him. I will become the wife of Thine Augustness." Forthwith they wedded each other, wherefore King Hayabusa-wake made no report [to the Heavenly Sovereign. <a name="fr_2107"></a><a href="#fn_2102"><font size="1">1</font></a>] Then the Heavenly Sovereign, going straight to the place where Queen Medori dwelt, stood on the door-sill of the palace. Hereupon, Queen Medori being at her loom, was weaving garments. Then the Heavenly Sovereign sang saying:</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">"Oh! for whom may be the garments that my Great Lady Medori weaves?" <a name="fr_2108"></a><a href="#fn_2103"><font size="1">2</font></a></div>  
 <p>Queen Medori replied in a Song saying:</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">"For an august veil for the high-going Falcon-Lord." <a name="fr_2109"></a><a href="#fn_2104"><font size="1">3</font></a></div>  
 <p>So the Heavenly Sovereign, perceiving her feelings, returned into the palace. At this time <a name="fr_2110"></a><a href="#fn_2105"><font size="1">4</font></a> when her husband King Hayabusa-wake came, his wife Queen Medori sang, saying:</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">"The lark flies to heaven. Oh! high-going Falcon-Lord, catch the wren." <a name="fr_2111"></a><a href="#fn_2106"><font size="1">5</font></a></div>  
 <p>[282] The Heavenly Sovereign, hearing this Song, <a name="fr_2112"></a><a href="#fn_2107"><font size="1">6</font></a> forthwith raised an army, wishing to slay King Hayabusa and Queen Medori, who then fled away together, and ascended Mount Kurahashi. <a name="fr_2113"></a><a href="#fn_2108"><font size="1">7</font></a> Thereupon King Hayabusa-wake sang, saying:</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">"Owing to the steepness of ladder-like Mount Kurahashi, being unable to clamber [up] the rocks, oh! she takes my hand! <a name="fr_2114"></a><a href="#fn_2109"><font size="1">8</font></a></div>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>Again he sang, saying:</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">"Though ladder-like Mount Kurahashi be <a name="page_351"><font size="1" color="green">p. 351</font></a> steep, it is not steep when I ascend it with my younger sister."</div>  
 <p>So when they fled thence, and reached Soni in Uda, <a name="fr_2115"></a><a href="#fn_2110"><font size="1">9</font></a> the Imperials <a name="fr_2116"></a><a href="#fn_2111"><font size="1">10</font></a> army pursued, overtook, and slew them.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_2102"></a><a href="kj133.htm#fr_2107">350:1</a> <a name="page_351"><font size="1" color="green">p. 351</font></a> <i>Scil</i>. of the success of his mediation.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2103"></a><a href="kj133.htm#fr_2108">350:2</a> Or, "for whom is the loom [employed], with which my Great Lady Medori weaves?"—The word <i>hata</i> in Archaic Japanese signifies both "garment" and the instrument which is used to weave a garment, <i>i.e.</i> a "loom" (<a name="img_35100"></a>
 <img src="img/35100.jpg"> and <a name="img_35101"></a>
 <img src="img/35101.jpg">). In later times the second meaning has prevailed to the exclusion of the first.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2104"></a><a href="kj133.htm#fr_2109">350:3</a> There is here a play on the name of the Queen's paramour Hayabusa-wake, which signifies "Falcon-Lord" as in the translation—The parallel passage of the "Chronicles" gives these two Songs as a single one which is put into the mouth of Queen Medori's handmaidens,—is a more acceptable version of the incident.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2105"></a><a href="kj133.htm#fr_2110">350:4</a> Motowori suspects that there is here an error in the text, which should, according to him, read: "After this."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2106"></a><a href="kj133.htm#fr_2111">350:5</a> The gist of this Song is an instigation to murder the Emperor (whose name was <i>Oho-sazaki</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, "Great Wren." Conf. Sect. CIV, Note 18), addressed to the singer's husband (whose name was <i>Hayabusa-wake</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, "Falcon Lord"). But the allusion to the lake remains obscure. Keichiū suggests that it is simply mentioned as a term of comparison for the falcon's power of flight, while Motowori opines that the meaning rather is: "The lark flies so high up to heaven that it would be hard to catch it; but the wren is an easy prey."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2107"></a><a href="kj133.htm#fr_2112">350:6</a> Viz., as may be supposed, repeated by some fourth person.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2108"></a><a href="kj133.htm#fr_2113">350:7</a> <i>Kurahashi-yama</i>, in Yamato.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2109"></a><a href="kj133.htm#fr_2114">350:8</a> The Song, like the next, is too clear to stand in need of explanation. "Ladder-like" is an attempt to render the force of the Pillow-Word <i>hashi-tate</i>. See Mabuchi's "Dictionary of Pillow-Words," s.v., for the exact force attributed to it by Mabuchi.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2110"></a><a href="kj133.htm#fr_2115">351:9</a> For Uda see Sect. XLVI, Note, 14. The etymology of Soni is equally obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_2111"></a><a href="kj133.htm#fr_2116">351:10</a> The character <a name="img_35102"></a>
 <img src="img/35102.jpg">, though read by the commentators with the usual Japanese Honorific <i>mi</i>, "august," has here its proper Chinese signification of "Imperial."</p>
