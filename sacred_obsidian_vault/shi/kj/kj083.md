 <p><a name="page_248"><font size="1" color="green">p. 248</font></a> [201]</p>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. LXXVI.—EMPRESS KEI-KO (PART I,—GENEALOGIES)]</h2>  
 <p>The Heavenly Sovereign Oho-tarashi-hiko-oshiro-wake dwelt in the palace of Hishiro at Makimuku, <a name="fr_1513"></a><a href="#fn_1509"><font size="1">1</font></a> and ruled the Empire. This Heavenly Sovereign wedded the Elder Lady of Inabi in Harima, <a name="fr_1514"></a><a href="#fn_1510"><font size="1">2</font></a> daughter of Waka-take, Prince of Kibi <a name="fr_1515"></a><a href="#fn_1511"><font size="1">3</font></a> ancestor of the Grandees of Kibi, <a name="fr_1516"></a><a href="#fn_1512"><font size="1">4</font></a> and begot</p>

 <p>kings and queens beside these <a name="fr_1546"></a><a href="#fn_1542"><font size="1">34</font></a> were all granted Rulerships in the various lands, or else [posts as] Lords, Territorial Lords or Departmental Chiefs. <a name="fr_1547"></a><a href="#fn_1543"><font size="1">35</font></a> So His Augustness Waka-tarashi-hiko [was he who afterward] ruled the Empire. His Augustness Wo-usu subdued the savage deities and likewise the unsubmissive people in the East [204] and West. The next, King Kushi-tsunu-wake (<small>was the ancestor of the chiefs of Mamuta</small>). <a name="fr_1548"></a><a href="#fn_1544"><font size="1">36</font></a> The next, His Augustness Oho-Usu, (<small>was the ancestor of the Dukes of Mori, <a name="fr_1549"></a><a href="#fn_1545"><font size="1">37</font></a> of the Dukes of Ohota <a name="fr_1550"></a><a href="#fn_1546"><font size="1">38</font></a> and of the Dukes of Shimada.</small>) <a name="fr_1551"></a><a href="#fn_1547"><font size="1">39</font></a> The next, King Kamukushi, (<small>was the ancestor of the Sakabe Abiko in the Land of Ki, <a name="fr_1552"></a><a href="#fn_1548"><font size="1">40</font></a> and of the Sakabe of Uda</small>). <a name="fr_1553"></a><a href="#fn_1549"><font size="1">41</font></a> The next, King Toyo-kuni-wake (<small>was the ancestor of the Rulers of the Land of Himuka.</small>) <a name="fr_1554"></a><a href="#fn_1550"><font size="1">42</font></a></p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_1509"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1513">248:1</a> <a name="page_250"><font size="1" color="green">p. 250</font></a> In the province of Yamato. The etymology of <i>Makimuku</i> is obscure. <i>Hishiro</i> is tentatively derived by Motowori from <i>hi</i>, the <i>Chamaecyparis obtusa</i> (a kind of conifer), and <i>shiro</i>, "an enclosure."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1510"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1514">248:2</a> <i>Harima no inabi no oho-iratsume</i>. Inabi is also known under the alternative form of <i>Inami</i>: etymology uncertain.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1511"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1515">248:3</a> <i>Waka-take Kibi tsu biko</i>, <i>Waka-take</i> signifies "young brave."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1512"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1516">248:4</a> <i>Kibi no omi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1513"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1517">249:5</a> <i>Kushi</i> signifies "wondrous," and wake either "young," or "lord." The meaning of <i>tsunu</i> is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1514"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1518">249:6</a> The names of this prince and the next signify respectively "great-foot-pestle" and "little foot-pestle," the origin of the bestowal of which singular designations is thus related in the parallel passage of the "Chronicles:" "The Imperial child Oho-usu and His Augustness Wo-usu were born together the same day as twins. The Heavenly Sovereign, astonished, informed the foot-pestle. So the two Kings were called Great Foot-pestle and <a href="errata.htm#50">Little</a> Foot-pestle." What the import of this passage may be is, however, a mystery both to Tanigaha Shisei and to Motowori.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1515"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1519">249:7</a> Motowori supposes <i>wo-guna</i> to have been an archaic word for "boy," "me-guna" signifying "girl." <i>Yamato wo-guna</i> would thus signify <i>the</i> boy of Japan." a not inappropriate designation for this prince, who under his later name of Yamato-take (Japan Brave," <i>i.e.</i> <i>the</i> brave man of Japan") has remained as the chief legendary type of the martial prowess of his native land.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1516"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1520">249:8</a> <a name="page_251"><font size="1" color="green">p. 251</font></a> <i>I.e.</i>. Yamato Prince.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1517"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1521">249:9</a> <i>Komu-kushi no miko</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, "divine wondrous."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1518"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1522">249:10</a> <i>Yamasaka no iri-bime no mikoto</i>. The signification of this name and of the next (<i>Ya-saka no iri-biko no mikoto</i>) is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1519"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1523">249:11</a> <i>Waka-tarashi-hiko no mikoto</i>, i.e., "young and perfect prince."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1520"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1524">249:12</a> <i>I-ho-ki no iri-biko no mikoto</i>. Signification obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1521"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1525">249:13</a> Or, <i>Oshi-wake</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, perhaps "Great Lord."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1522"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1526">249:14</a> <i>I-ho-ki no iri-bime no mikoto</i>. Signification obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1523"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1527">249:15</a> <i>Toyo-to-wake no mikoto</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, perhaps "luxuriant swift prince."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1524"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1528">249:16</a> <i>Nunoshiro no iratsumo</i>. Signification obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1525"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1529">249:17</a> <i>Nunaki no iratsume</i>. Signification obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1526"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1530">249:18</a> <i>Kago yori-hime no mikoto</i>. <i>Yori ime</i> probably means "good princess." The sense of <i>kago</i> is very doubtful, for it may either be the name of a place, or else identical with the Verb <i>kagayaku</i> "to shine," or with <i>kago</i>, "a stag."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1527"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1531">249:19</a> <i>Waka-ki no iri-biko no mikoto</i>. The signification of this name is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1528"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1532">249:20</a> <i>Kibi no ye-hike no mikoto</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1529"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1533">249:21</a> <i>Takaki-hime no mikoto</i>. The meaning of <i>takaki</i> in this place is not certain.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1530"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1534">249:22</a> <i>Oto-hime no mikoto</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, "the younger princes."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1531"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1535">249:23</a> <i>Himuka no Mi-hakashi-bime</i>. <i>Mi-hakashi</i> signifies "august sabre." See Motowori's Commentary, Vol. XXVI, p. 11, for a gloss on this curious name.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1532"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1536">249:24</a> <i>Toyo-kuni-wake no miko</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, perhaps "lord of the luxuriant land," or else "lord of the land of Toyo," the Emperor Kei-kō having, according to the account in the "Chronicles," spent some years fighting in South-Western Japan, where the province of Toyo is situated.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1533"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1537">249:25</a> <i>Inabi no waki-iratsume</i>. See Note 2 to this Section.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1534"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1538">249:26</a> <i>Ma-waka no miko</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, "truly young prince."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1535"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1539">249:27</a> <i>Hiko-hito no oho-ye no miko</i>. <i>Hiko</i> signifies "prince," <i>hito</i> is "person" (or here, according to Motowori, "headman"), and <i>oho ye</i> is "great elder brother."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1536"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1540">249:28</a> <i>Ka-guro-hime</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, probably "the black-haired princess."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1537"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1541">249:29</a> <i>Sume-iro-oho-naka-tsu-biko-no-mikoto</i>. The signification of this name is not clear. Motowori identifies <i>sume</i> with the like-sounding Verb signifying "to be supreme." <i>Oha-naka-tsu-hiko</i> may signify "great middle prince," referring to the comparative ages of this prince and his brethren.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1538"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1542">249:30</a> <a name="page_252"><font size="1" color="green">p. 252</font></a> There is here an evident error in the genealogy, as it would make the emperor marry his own great-great-grand daughter! A guess of the editor of 1687 that for Yamato-take we should read Waka-take (a son of the Emperor Kō-rei) is approved by Motowori, and may be adopted as probably correct,—<i>i.e.</i> (what is but little likely) if this portion of the "Records" should eventually be proved to be historically trustworthy. The question is discussed by Motowori in Vol. XXVI, pp. 12-14. of his Commentary.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1539"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1543">249:31</a> <i>Oho-ye no miko</i>. This name would, as Motowori remarks, appear to have erroneously crept in here through the influence of the name mentioned in Note 27, the whole account of this union with Princess Ka-guro being corrupt.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1540"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1544">249:32</a> The Japanese term (<a name="img_25200"></a>
 <img src="img/25200.jpg"> <i>miko</i>) includes both males and females.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1541"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1545">249:33</a> <a name="img_25201"></a>
 <img src="img/25201.jpg">.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1542"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1546">250:34</a> As above remarked, the Japanese term includes both males and females, and moreover some of the female children are specially mentioned. The difficulty as to how females could have been appointed to the offices here mentioned is not solved by Motowori, whose note on this passage is evasive.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1543"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1547">250:35</a> The four names of offices (also used as "gentile names") here mentioned are in the original Japanese <i>Kuni no Miyatsuko</i>, <i>Wake</i>, <i>Inaki</i>, and <i>Agata-mushi</i>. (See Introduction, <a href="kj002.htm#page_xvi">p. xvi</a>.)</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1544"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1548">250:36</a> <i>Mamuta no murazhi</i>. (See Sect. LIII, Note 1.)</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1545"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1549">250:37</a> <i>Mori no kimi</i>. <i>Mori</i> seems to be the name of a place (perhaps in Mino); but nothing is known of this family.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1546"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1550">250:38</a> <i>Ohota no kimi</i>. <i>Ohota</i> is the name of a place in Mino, and signifies "great rice-field."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1547"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1551">250:39</a> <i>Shimada no kimi</i>. <i>Shimada</i> is perhaps the name of a place in Wohari. It signifies "island rice-fields."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1548"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1552">250:40</a> <i>Ki no kuni no sakabe no abiko</i>. For also see Sect. LXXII, Note 85. <i>Sakabe</i> seems to signify "liquor tribe," this family and the next having been entrusted with the management of the Imperial feasts.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1549"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1553">250:41</a> <i>Uda no sakabe</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, the "Liquor Tribe of Uda." (in Yamato).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1550"></a><a href="kj083.htm#fr_1554">250:42</a> <i>Himuka no kuni no miyatsuko</i>.</p>
