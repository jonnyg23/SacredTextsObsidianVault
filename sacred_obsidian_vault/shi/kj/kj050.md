 <p><a name="page_158"><font size="1" color="green">p. 158</font></a> [129]</p>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. XLIII.—THE AUGUST CHILDREN OF HIS AUGUSTNESS CORMORANT-THATCH-MEETING-INCOMPLETELY]</h2>  
 <p>His Augustness Heaven's-Sun-Height-Prince-Wave-limit-Brave-Cormorant-Thatch-Meeting-Incompletely wedded his maternal aunt Her Augustness Jewel-Good-Princess, and begot august children named: His Augustness Five-Reaches; <a name="fr_867"></a><a href="#fn_863"><font size="1">1</font></a> next His Augustness Boiled-Rice; <a name="fr_868"></a><a href="#fn_864"><font size="1">2</font></a> next His Augustness August-Food-Master; <a name="fr_869"></a><a href="#fn_865"><font size="1">3</font></a> next His Augustness Young-August-Food-Master, <a name="fr_870"></a><a href="#fn_866"><font size="1">4</font></a> another name for whom is His Augustness Luxuriant-August-Food-Master, <a name="fr_871"></a><a href="#fn_867"><font size="1">5</font></a> and another name is His Augustness Divine-Yamato-Ihare-Prince. <a name="fr_872"></a><a href="#fn_868"><font size="1">6</font></a> So His Augustness August-Food-Master, treading on the crest of the waves, crossed over to the Eternal Land. <a name="fr_873"></a><a href="#fn_869"><font size="1">7</font></a> His Augustness Boiled-Rice went into the Sea-Plain, it being his deceased mother's <a name="fr_874"></a><a href="#fn_870"><font size="1">8</font></a> land.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_863"></a><a href="kj050.htm#fr_867">158:1</a> <a name="page_158"><font size="1" color="green">p. 158</font></a> <i>Itsu-se-no-mikoto</i>. The "reaches" are the reaches of a river: at least this seems the most natural view to take of the meaning of the name. Motowori and Moribe, however, consider it to be a corruption of <i>idzu-shine</i>, to which they assign the signification of "powerful rice." <a name="page_159"><font size="1" color="green">p. 159</font></a> Excepting for the fact of its bringing this name into harmony with the three that follow, and which all relate to food, there seems little to recommend so far-fetched a derivation.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_864"></a><a href="kj050.htm#fr_868">158:2</a> <i>Inu-hi-na-mikoto</i>. If Motowori's <a href="errata.htm#24">derivation</a> of the name from <i>ina-ihi</i> is correct, it might be rendered with greater exactness "Rice-boiled-rice," ine denoting rice in the husk and ihi the same when boiled.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_865"></a><a href="kj050.htm#fr_869">158:3</a> <i>Mi-ke-nu-no-mikoto</i>. This name has been translated in accordance with Motowori's interpretation of the syllables that compose it.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_866"></a><a href="kj050.htm#fr_870">158:4</a> <i>Waka-nu-ke-nu-no-mikoto</i>. <i>Conf</i>. preceding Note.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_867"></a><a href="kj050.htm#fr_871">158:5</a> Toyo-nu-ke-nu-no-mikoto. <i>Conf</i>. Note 3.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_868"></a><a href="kj050.htm#fr_872">158:6</a> <i>Kamu-yamato-ihare-biko-no-mikoto</i>. <i>Yamato</i> being the name of the province where this prince, the first Emperor of the so-called "human age," fixed his capital, it appropriately forms part of his name. For a discussion of the etymology of the word Yamato, see Sect. V, Note 26. <i>Ihare</i>, a word which is said to signify "assembling," is the name of a village in that province.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_869"></a><a href="kj050.htm#fr_873">158:7</a> See Sect. XXVII, Note 12.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_870"></a><a href="kj050.htm#fr_874">158:8</a> See Sect. XII, Note 8.</p>
