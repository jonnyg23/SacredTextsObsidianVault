 <p><a name="page_193"><font size="1" color="green">p. 193</font></a></p>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. LVIII.—EMPEROR KO-SHO.]</h2>  
 <p>His Augustness Mi-ma-tsu-hiko-kawe-shine dwelt at the palace of Waki-no-kami in Kadzuraki, <a name="fr_1087"></a><a href="#fn_1083"><font size="1">1</font></a> and ruled the Empire. This Heavenly Sovereign wedded Her Augustness [157] Princess Yoso-taho, <a name="fr_1088"></a><a href="#fn_1084"><font size="1">2</font></a> younger sister of Oki-tsu-yoso <a name="fr_1089"></a><a href="#fn_1085"><font size="1">3</font></a> ancestor of the Chiefs of Wohari, <a name="fr_1090"></a><a href="#fn_1086"><font size="1">4</font></a> and begot august children: His Augustness Ame-oshi-tarashi-hiko, <a name="fr_1091"></a><a href="#fn_1087"><font size="1">5</font></a> and next His Augustness Oho-yamato-tarashi-hiko-kuni-oshi-bito <a name="fr_1092"></a><a href="#fn_1088"><font size="1">6</font></a> (<small>two Deities</small>). Now the younger brother, His Augustness Tarashi-hiko-kuni-oshi-bito [was he who afterwards] ruled the Empire. The elder brother His <a href="errata.htm#42">Augustness</a> Ame-oshi-tarashi-hiko (<small>was the ancestor of the Grandees of Kasuga, <a name="fr_1093"></a><a href="#fn_1089"><font size="1">7</font></a> the Grandees of Ohoyake, <a name="fr_1094"></a><a href="#fn_1090"><font size="1">8</font></a> the Grandees of Ahata, <a name="fr_1095"></a><a href="#fn_1091"><font size="1">9</font></a> the Grandees of Wonu; <a name="fr_1096"></a><a href="#fn_1092"><font size="1">10</font></a> the Grandees of Kaki-no-moto, <a name="fr_1097"></a><a href="#fn_1093"><font size="1">11</font></a> the Grandees of Ichihiwi; <a name="fr_1098"></a><a href="#fn_1094"><font size="1">12</font></a> the Grandees of Ohosaka, <a name="fr_1099"></a><a href="#fn_1095"><font size="1">13</font></a> the Grandees of Ana, <a name="fr_1100"></a><a href="#fn_1096"><font size="1">14</font></a> the Grandees of Taki: <a name="fr_1101"></a><a href="#fn_1097"><font size="1">15</font></a> [158] the Grandees of Haguri, <a name="fr_1102"></a><a href="#fn_1098"><font size="1">16</font></a> the Grandees of Chita, <a name="fr_1103"></a><a href="#fn_1099"><font size="1">17</font></a> the Grandees of Muza; <a name="fr_1104"></a><a href="#fn_1100"><font size="1">18</font></a> the Grandees of Tsunuyama, <a name="fr_1105"></a><a href="#fn_1101"><font size="1">19</font></a> the Dukes of Ihitaka in Ise, <a name="fr_1106"></a><a href="#fn_1102"><font size="1">20</font></a> the Dukes of Ichishi, <a name="fr_1107"></a><a href="#fn_1103"><font size="1">21</font></a> and the Rulers of the Land of Chika-tsu-Afumi</small>). <a name="fr_1108"></a><a href="#fn_1104"><font size="1">22</font></a></p>  
 <p>The Heavenly Sovereign's august years were ninety-three. His August mausoleum is on Mount Hakata <a name="fr_1109"></a><a href="#fn_1105"><font size="1">23</font></a> at Waki-no-kami.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_1083"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1087">193:1</a> <a name="page_193"><font size="1" color="green">p. 193</font></a> In Yamato. The name of Waki-no-kami is of uncertain derivation. In the "Chronicles" the Emperor Kō-shō is said to have "dwelt at the palace of Ikegokoro at Waki-no-kami." For Kadzuraki see Sect. LV, Note 1.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1084"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1088">193:2</a> <i>Yoso-taho-bime-no-mikoto</i>. Here written phonetically, this name appears in the "Chronicles "written with characters to which the signification of "perfectly ornamented princess" should be attributed.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1085"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1089">193:3</a> The signification of this name is obscure, but it seems to be connected in some way with that of the other sister.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1086"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1090">193:4</a> <i>Wohari no murazhi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1087"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1091">193:5</a> <i>I.e.</i>, heavenly great perfect prince."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1088"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1092">193:6</a> <i>I.e.</i>, "great Yamato perfect prince, country great man." This name appears in an abbreviated form in the next sentence.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1089"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1093">193:7</a> <a name="page_194"><font size="1" color="green">p. 194</font></a><i>Kasuga na omi</i>. Kasuga is a celebrated place in the province of Yamato. The name is of uncertain origin, though the "Catalogue of Family Names" gives a story referring it to <i>Kasu gaki</i> (<a name="img_19400"></a>
 <img src="img/19400.jpg">), <i>i.e.</i>, "lees fence." The curious combination of characters with which Kasuga is written,—<a name="img_19401"></a>
 <img src="img/19401.jpg">—may be traced to the Pillow-Word <i>haru-hi no</i> (<a name="img_19402"></a>
 <img src="img/19402.jpg">) which was not unnaturally prefixed to a name which so much resembled the Verb <i>kasumu</i>, "to be misty."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1090"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1094">193:8</a> <i>Ohoyake no omi</i>. Ohoyake is a place in Yamato. The name signifies "great granary."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1091"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1095">193:9</a> <i>Ahata no omi</i>. Ahata is a place in Yamashiro. The name signifies "millet-field."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1092"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1096">193:10</a> <i>Wonu no omi</i>. Wonu is a place in Afumi. The name signifies "little moor."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1093"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1097">193:11</a> <i>Kakinnomoto-uo-omi</i>. <i>Kaki-no-moto</i> signifies "at the bottom of the persimmon-tree," and the name is said by the compiler of the "Catalogue of Family Names" to have been granted to this family in allusion to a persimmon-tree which grew near their gate. This name was rendered illustrious in the eighth century by the poet Kakinomoto Hitomaro (See the present writer's "Classical Poetry of the Japanese," p. 217 of seq.).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1094"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1098">193:12</a> <i>Ichihiwi no omi</i>. Ichihiwi is in Yamato. The name may signify "oak-well."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1095"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1099">193:13</a> <i>Ohosaka no omi</i>. Ohosaka is a place in Bingo. The name signifies "great hill, or pass."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1096"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1100">193:14</a> <i>Ana no omi</i>. Ana is a department in Bingo. The name signifies "hole" or "cave."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1097"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1101">193:15</a> <i>Taki no omi</i>. Taki is a district in Tamba. The name, which is a common one in Japan, means "water-falls."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1098"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1102">193:16</a> <i>Haguri no omi</i>. Haguri is a district in Wohari. The signification of the name is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1099"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1103">193:17</a> <i>Chita no omi</i>. Chita is a district in Wohari. The signification of the name is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1100"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1104">193:18</a> <i>Musa no omi</i>. Mudza is a district in Kadzusa. The name seems to be a corruption of the Chinese words <a name="img_19403"></a>
 <img src="img/19403.jpg">, "warlike archer."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1101"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1105">193:19</a> <i>Tsunuyama no omi</i>. The family, the place, and the signification of the name are alike obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1102"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1106">193:20</a> <i>Ise no Ihitaka no kimi</i>. Ihitaka is the name of a district, and is traced to the signification of "abundant rice."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1103"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1107">193:21</a> <i>Ichishi no kimi</i>. Ichishi is a district in Ise. The signification of the name is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1104"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1108">193:22</a> <a name="page_195"><font size="1" color="green">p. 195</font></a> <i>Chika tsu Afumi no kuni-no-miyatsuko</i>. For Chika-tsu-Afumi see Sect. XXIX, Note 20.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1105"></a><a href="kj065.htm#fr_1109">193:23</a> In Yamato. The signification of the name is obscure.</p>
