 <p><a name="page_212"><font size="1" color="green">p. 212</font></a> [173]</p>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. LXIII.—EMPEROR SŪ-JIN (PART I.—GENEALOGIES).]</h2>  
 <p>His Augustness Mima-ki-iri-biko-iniwe dwelt at the Palace of Midzu-gaki at Shiki, <a name="fr_1292"></a><a href="#fn_1288"><font size="1">1</font></a> and ruled the Empire. This Heavenly Sovereign wedded Tohotsu-no-ayu-me-me-kuhashi, <a name="fr_1293"></a><a href="#fn_1289"><font size="1">2</font></a> daughter of Arakaha-to-be, <a name="fr_1294"></a><a href="#fn_1290"><font size="1">3</font></a> Ruler of the land of Ki, <a name="fr_1295"></a><a href="#fn_1291"><font size="1">4</font></a> and begot august children: His Augustness Toyo-ki-iri-biko, <a name="fr_1296"></a><a href="#fn_1292"><font size="1">5</font></a> and next Her Augustness Toyo-suki-iri-bime <a name="fr_1297"></a><a href="#fn_1293"><font size="1">6</font></a> (<small>two Deities</small>). Again, wedding the Great Princess of Ama, <a name="fr_1298"></a><a href="#fn_1294"><font size="1">7</font></a> ancestress of the Chiefs of Wohori, <a name="fr_1299"></a><a href="#fn_1295"><font size="1">8</font></a> he begot august children: His Augustness Oho-iri-ki; <a name="fr_1300"></a><a href="#fn_1296"><font size="1">9</font></a> next His Augustness</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_1288"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1292">212:1</a> <a name="page_213"><font size="1" color="green">p. 213</font></a> In Yamato. <i>Shiki</i> probably signifies "stone castle" (<i>ishi-ki</i>). <i>Midzugaki</i> signifies "fresh young hedge,"—an Honorific designation of the hedge round the Emperor's abode, which passed into a Proper Name.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1289"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1293">212:2</a> According to Motowori's exegesis, this name is a rather remarkable instance of verbal usage, <i>ayu-me</i> ("trout-eyes") being not properly part of the name at all, but only a Pillow-Word for what follows, viz., <i>Me-kakushi-hime</i>, <i>i.e.</i> "Princess Beautiful Eyes." <i>Tohotsu</i> is the name of a place in the province of Ki, signifying "far harbour."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1290"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1294">212:3</a> <i>Arakaha</i> is the name of a place in Ki, and signifies "rough (<i>i.e.</i> impetuous and dangerous) river." The syllables <i>to-be</i> are of uncertain import.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1291"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1295">212:4</a> <i>Ki no kuni no miyatsuko</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1292"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1296">212:5</a> <i>Toyo</i> signifies "luxuriant" and <i>biko</i> (<i>hiko</i>) "prince." The other elements of the compound are obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1293"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1297">212:6</a> <a name="page_214"><font size="1" color="green">p. 214</font></a> <i>Bime</i> (<i>hime</i>) signifies "princess," and <i>suki</i> is identified by Motowori with Shiki, the name of the residence of the monarch whose daughter this princess was.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1294"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1298">212:7</a> <i>Oho-ama-hime</i>. Ama is the name of a district in Wohari, and probably signifies "fisherman."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1295"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1299">212:8</a> <i>Wohari no murazhi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1296"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1300">212:9</a> The signification of this and the two following names is uncertain.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1297"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1301">213:10</a> <i>Towochi</i> is the name of a district in Yamato.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1298"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1302">213:11</a> <i>Mimatsu-hime</i>. Signification uncertain.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1299"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1303">213:12</a> Signification uncertain.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1300"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1304">213:13</a> The syllables <i>ma</i> and <i>waka</i>, literally "true and young" or "truly young "are honorific. <i>Iza</i> is of doubtful signification.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1301"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1305">213:14</a> <i>Kuni-kata-hime</i>. <i>Kuni</i> means "country," and <i>kata</i> here probably signifies "hard" or "firm."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1302"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1306">213:15</a> <i>Ohiji-tsuku-yamato-hime</i>. <i>Ohiji</i> probably means "a thousand," and <i>Yamato</i> is the name of a province. <i>Tsuku</i> is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1303"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1307">213:16</a> <i>Iga-hime</i>. <i>Iga</i> is the name of a district and of a province (see Sect. LVI, Note 9).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1304"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1308">213:17</a> <i>I.e.</i>, "Yamato Prince."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1305"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1309">213:18</a> <i>Kami-tsu-ke-nu</i> [<i>no kimi</i>]. The character <a name="img_21400"></a>
 <img src="img/21400.jpg"> (Dukes) is supplied by Motowori. This is the ancient form of the name now pronounced <i>Kōdzuke</i> (<i>conf</i>. <i>Shimo-tsu-ke-nu</i> corrupted to <i>Shimotsuke</i>). The author of the "Inquiry into the Meaning of the Names of All the Provinces" draws attention to the curious fact that, whereas the final syllable <i>nu</i> of the original word is dropped in speaking, it is the <i>ke</i> which is dropped in writing—the original form of the name, as written, being <a name="img_21401"></a>
 <img src="img/21401.jpg">, whereas now only the first two of these three characters are used. Its signification is supposed to be "upper vegetation-moor," <i>ke</i> (<a name="img_21402"></a>
 <img src="img/21402.jpg">) being regarded as the Archaic general term for trees and grasses, identical with <i>ke</i> that means "hairs," vegetation having struck the early speakers of the language as being similar to the hairs on the bodies of men and beasts.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1306"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1310">213:19</a> <i>Shimo-tsu-ke-nu no kimi</i>. <i>Shimo</i> means "lower." For the rest of the name see preceding Note. Both names are those of provinces in Eastern Japan.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1307"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1311">213:20</a> Or more literally, "worshipped and celebrated the festivals at," etc.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1308"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1312">213:21</a> <i>Viz</i>., of the Sun-Goddess (<i>Ama-terasu</i>).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1309"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1313">213:22</a> <i>Noto no omi</i>. <i>Noto</i>, formerly a part of the province of Echizen, <a name="page_215"><font size="1" color="green">p. 215</font></a> is the name of the peninsula that juts out into the Sea of Japan on the western coast of the Main Island. The etymology of the name is obscure.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1310"></a><a href="kj070.htm#fr_1314">213:23</a> The meaning of this imperfectly formed sentence is: On the occasion of "the interment of the next prince, His Augustness Yamato-hiko, the custom of setting a row of the deceased prince's retainers round his grave and burying them alive was first introduced." For a further notice of this custom see Sect. LXXV, Note 4. According to the "Chronicles," the "ancient" custom of burying retainers up to their necks in the neighbourhood of their lord's grave was abolished after this very same interment. Motowori endeavours to reconcile the two statements by supposing that the custom was really an old one, but that on the occasion here mentioned the number of victims was increased to an unprecedented degree, so that, as related in the "Chronicles," their cries, while their heads were being pulled to pieces by the crows and dogs, filled the Emperor with commiseration.</p>
