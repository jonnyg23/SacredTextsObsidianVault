 <p><a name="page_78"><font size="1" color="green">p. 78</font></a></p>  <h2 align="center">[SECT. XX.—THE AUGUST ANCESTORS OF THE DEITY-MASTER-OF-THE-GREAT LAND.]</h2>  
 <p>Quare, quum incepit in thalamo [opus procreationis] cum Mirâ-Herâ-Inadâ, procreavit Deum nomine Eight-Island Ruler. <a name="fr_454"></a><a href="#fn_450"><font size="1">1</font></a> And again, having wedded the Divine-Princess-of-Great-Majesty, <a name="fr_455"></a><a href="#fn_451"><font size="1">2</font></a> daughter of the Deity Great-Mountain-Possessor, he begot children: the Great-Harvest Deity <a name="fr_456"></a><a href="#fn_452"><font size="1">3</font></a> and the August-Spirit-of-Food. <a name="fr_457"></a><a href="#fn_453"><font size="1">4</font></a> The elder brother the Deity Eight-Island-Ruler wedded Princess-Falling-Like-the-Flowers-of-the-Trees, <a name="fr_458"></a><a href="#fn_454"><font size="1">5</font></a> daughter of the Deity Great-Mountain-Possessor, and begot a child: the Deity Fuha-no-moji-Ku-nu-su-nu. <a name="fr_459"></a><a href="#fn_455"><font size="1">6</font></a> This Deity wedded Princess Hikaha, <a name="fr_460"></a><a href="#fn_456"><font size="1">7</font></a> daughter of the Deity Okami, <a name="fr_461"></a><a href="#fn_457"><font size="1">8</font></a> and begot a child: Water-Spoilt-Blossom-of-Fuka-buchi. <a name="fr_462"></a><a href="#fn_458"><font size="1">9</font></a> This Deity [67] wedded the Deity Ame-no-tsudohe-chi-ne, <a name="fr_463"></a><a href="#fn_459"><font size="1">10</font></a> and begot a child: the Deity Great-Water-Master. <a name="fr_464"></a><a href="#fn_460"><font size="1">11</font></a> This Deity wedded the Deity Grand-Ears <a name="fr_465"></a><a href="#fn_461"><font size="1">12</font></a> daughter of the Deity Funu-dzu-nu, <a name="fr_466"></a><a href="#fn_462"><font size="1">13</font></a> and begot a child: the Deity Heavenly-Brandishing-Prince-Lord. <a name="fr_467"></a><a href="#fn_463"><font size="1">14</font></a> This Deity wedded the Young-Princess-of-the-Small-Country, <a name="fr_468"></a><a href="#fn_464"><font size="1">15</font></a> daughter of the Great-Deity-of-the-Small-Country, <a name="fr_469"></a><a href="#fn_465"><font size="1">16</font></a> and begot a child: the</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_450"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_454">78:1</a> <a name="page_79"><font size="1" color="green">p. 79</font></a> <i>Ya-shima-zhi-nu-mi</i>. Ya-shima means "eight islands." The syllables <i>zhi-nu-mi</i> are obscure, but the translator has little doubt "ruler" fairly represents their import. Motowori takes <i>zhi</i> to be an apocopated and <i>nigori’ed</i> form of <i>shiru</i>, "to rule," nu to be an apocopated form of <i>nushi</i>, "master," and <i>mi</i> to be an apocopated form of the Honorific termination <i>mimi</i>. Tanigaha Shisei considers <i>zhimu</i> to stand for <i>shidzumuru</i>, "to govern," which comes to the same thing so far as the sense is concerned.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_451"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_455">78:2</a> <i>Kamu-oho-ichi-hime</i>. The rendering of <i>Oho-ichi</i> as "Great Majesty" rests on a plausible conjecture of Hirata's, who proposes to, identify <i>ichi</i> with <i>idzu</i> (<a name="img_07900"></a>
 <img src="img/07900.jpg">). Motowori thinks that <i>Oho-ichi</i> should be taken as the name of a place; but this seems less good.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_452"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_456">78:3</a> <i>Oho-toshi-no-kami</i>, written <a name="img_07901"></a>
 <img src="img/07901.jpg">, the obvious rendering of which would be "great year." But the Japanese term <i>toshi</i> is believed to have originally signified, not "year" in the abstract, but that which was produced each year, viz., the harvest (conf. <i>toru</i>, "to take").</p>
 <p><a name="fn_453"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_457">78:4</a> <i>Uka-no-mi-tama</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_454"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_458">78:5</a> <i>Ko-no-hana-chiru-hime</i>, so called, says Motowori, because she probably died young, as a blossom that falls from the tree. We might however perhaps take the Verb <i>chiru</i> in a Causative sense, and consider the name to signify "the Princess-Who-Causes-the-Flowers-of-the-Trees-to- Fall. A sister of this goddess appears in the pretty legend narrated in Sect. XXXVII under the parallel name of the Princess-Blossoming-Brilliantly-Like-the-Flowers-of-the-Trees. See Note 3 to that Sect.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_455"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_459">78:6</a> <i>Fuha-no-moji-ku-nu-su-nu-no-kami</i>. The import of this name is quite uncertain. <i>Fuha</i> however seems to be the name of a place.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_456"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_460">78:7</a> "<i>Hi-kaha-mime</i>. <i>Hi-kaha</i> (lit. "sun-river") is supposed to stand for the name of a place in Musashi, which is however written "ice-river" (<a name="img_07902"></a>
 <img src="img/07902.jpg"> and not <a name="img_07903"></a>
 <img src="img/07903.jpg">), the old Japanese words for "ice" and "sun" being homonymous.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_457"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_461">78:8</a> <a name="page_80"><font size="1" color="green">p. 80</font></a> See Sect. VIII. (Note 9), where the name is given as <i>Kura okami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_458"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_462">78:9</a> <i>Fuka-buchi-no-midzu-yare-hana</i>. If Fuka-buchi were ascertained to be not, as is supposed, the name of a place, we should have to render it "deep pool," and the whole would mean in English "Water-Spoilt-Blossom-of-the-Deep-Pool."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_459"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_463">78:10</a> <i>Ame-no-tsudohe-chi-ne-no-kami</i>. In this name nothing is clear but the first three syllables, which signify "heavenly." But if Mabuchi's conjecture as to the meaning of the rest were accepted, we should have to translate the whole by "Heavenly-Assembling-Town-Lady."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_460"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_464">78:11</a> This is the meaning plausibly assigned by Motowori to the original <i>O-midzu-nu-no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_461"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_465">78:12</a> <i>Fute-mimi-no-kami</i>, plausibly conjectured by Tominobu to stand for <i>Futo-mimi</i>, etc., which gives the sense here adopted.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_462"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_466">78:13</a> <i>Funu-dzu-nu-no-kami</i>. Motowori believes Funu to be the name of a place, and interprets the name to signify "Master of Funu." But this seems highly uncertain.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_463"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_467">78:14</a> <i>Ame-no-fuyu-kinu-no-kami</i> (Motowori's reading) or <i>Ama-no</i>, etc. (Hirata's reading). The translation of the name follows Hirata's explanation, which is based on Motowori's, and according to which the characters <a name="img_08000"></a>
 <img src="img/08000.jpg"> ("winter garments") in this text, and <a name="img_08001"></a>
 <img src="img/08001.jpg"> read <i>Fuki-ne</i> in the "Chronicles," are merely phonetic, while the meaning is derived from a comparison of the sounds given by each. Though himself believing in the soundness of Hirata's conclusion, the translator must admit that it is not indisputable.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_464"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_468">78:15</a> <i>Sasu-kuni-waka-hime</i>, or <i>Sashi-kuni</i>, etc. The former reading, which Hirata's adopts, seems best. The meaning of <i>sasu</i>, here rendered "small," is open to doubt.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_465"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_469">78:16</a> <i>Sasu-kuni-oho</i>-[<i>no</i>-]<i>kami</i>, or <i>Sashi</i>, etc. The syllable <i>no</i> in the Japanese reading seems to be a superfluous addition of the modern commentators.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_466"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_470">79:17</a> <i>Oho-kuni-nushi-ne-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_467"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_471">79:18</a> <i>Oho-na-muji-no-kami</i>, to which Tominobu proposes to give the sense of "Great Hole-Possessor," in connection with the story of the mouse-hole in which he took refuge from the fire lit by the Impetuous-Male-Deity (<i>Susa-no-wo</i>) for his destruction (see Sect. XXIII). But the interpretation followed in the translation is the most likely as well as the orthodox one, this Deity being entitled the possessor of a Great Name or of Great Names on account of his renown in Japanese mythic story.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_468"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_472">79:19</a> <a name="page_81"><font size="1" color="green">p. 81</font></a> <i>Ashi-hara-shiko-wo-no-kami</i>. The "reed-plains" are doubtless put by metonymy for Japan.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_469"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_473">79:20</a> <i>Yachi-hoko-no-kami</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_470"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_474">79:21</a> <i>Utsushi-kuni-tama-no-kami</i>. The name must be understood to mean "Spirit of the Land of the Living," and to be antithetical to that of one of his fathers-in-law, the Impetuous Male-Deity (<i>Susa-no-wo</i>) who became the god of Hades.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_471"></a><a href="kj027.htm#fr_475">79:22</a> Or "he had five names."</p>
