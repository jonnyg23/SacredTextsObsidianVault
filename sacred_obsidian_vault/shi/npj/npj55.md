 <h1 align="CENTER">SHORT BIBLIOGRAPHY</h1>
 <h3 align="CENTER">EUROPEAN</h3>
 <p>B. H. Chamberlain: <i>The Classical Poetry of the Japanese</i>, 1880 (Rhymed paraphrases of <i>Sesshōseki</i>, <i>Kantan</i>, <i>Nakamitsu</i> and part of <i>Hagoromo</i>; translations of the farces <i>Honekawa</i> and <i>Zazen</i>).</p>
 <i></i><p>The Chrysanthemum, 1882, Translation of <i>Hachi no Ki</i>.</p>
 <p>F. W. K. Müller in <i>Festschrift f. Adolf Bastian</i>, pp. 513-537, <i>Ikkaku Sennin, eine mittelaterliche-Oper</i>, 1896.</p>
 <p>Aston, <i>History of Japanese Literature</i>, 1899. Osman Edwards: <i>Japanese Plays and Playfellows</i>, 1901. (Refers to performances of <i>Shunkwan</i>, <i>Koi no Omoni</i>, <i>Aoi no Uye</i>, <i>Benkei in the Boat</i> and <i>Tsuchigumo</i>.)</p>
 <p>F. Brinkley, Japan, III. 21-60, 1901-2. (Translates <i>Ataka</i> and the farce <i>Sannin Katawa</i>.)</p>
 <p>F. Victor Dickins, <i>Japanese Texts</i>, 1906. (Text and Translation of <i>Takasago</i>).</p>
 <p>K. Florenz, <i>Geschichte d. Japanischen Literatur</i>, 1906. (Translations of <i>Takasago</i> and <i>Benkei in the Boat</i>; summaries of <i>Ataka</i>, <i>Mochizuki</i> and <i>Hanjo</i>. Translation of the farce <i>Hagi-Daimyō</i>.)</p>
 <p>N. Péri: <i>Etudes sur le drame lyrique japonais</i>, in <i>Bulletin de l'Ecole d'Extrême-Orient</i>, 1909-1913. (Includes translations of <i>Oimatsu</i>, <i>Atsumori</i>, <i>Ohara Gokū</i>, <i>Sotoba Komachi</i> and <i>The Damask Drum</i>.)</p>
 <p>G. B. Sansom: Translations of <i>Ataka</i>, <i>Benkei in the Boat</i> and <i>Sakuragawa</i>.</p>
 <p>H. L. Joly: Notes on masks, dances, etc., in <i>Transactions of Japan Society</i>, 1912.</p>
 <p>M. Stopes: <i>Plays of Old Japan</i>, 1913. (Translations of <i>Motomezuka</i>, <i>Kagekiyo</i> and <i>Sumidagawa</i>; summary of <i>Tamura</i>.)</p>
 <p>E. Fenollosa and Ezra Pound: <i>Noh or Accomplishment</i>, 1916. (Translations by E. F., adapted by E. P. Gives some account of about twenty plays. The versions of E. F. seem to have been fragmentary and inaccurate; but wherever Mr. Pound had adequate material to work upon he has used it admirably.)</p>
 <p>See also general articles on the Japanese drama, such as A. Lloyd's in <i>Trans. of Asiatic Society of Japan</i>, 1908.</p>
 <p>Yone Noguchi: <i>Twelve Kyōgen</i> (text and translation), 1911.</p>
 <p>M. A. Hincks: <i>The Japanese Dance</i>, 32 pp., 1910.</p>
 <h3 align="CENTER">JAPANESE</h3>
 <p align="CENTER">(<i>Only a few important works are selected</i>)</p>
 <i></i><p>Kwadensho: the Later Kwadensho in 8 vols., first published c. 1600. (The British Museum possesses what is apparently an early eighteenth century reprint.)</p>
 <i></i><p>Nō no Shiori: by Ōwada Tateki, 6 vols. (Description of the <i>modus operandi</i> of 91 plays), 1903.</p>
 <i></i><p>Yōkyoku Hyōshaku: edited by Ōwada Tateki, 9 vols., 1907-8. Texts of about 270 plays, with commentary. Referred to by me as "Ōwada."</p>
 <i></i><p>Nōgaku Daijiten: by Masada and Amaya, 2 vols. (Dictionary of Nō.)</p>
 <p><a name="page_261"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 261</font></a></p>
 <i></i><p>Seami Jūroku-bu Shū: Works of Seami, 1909.</p>
 <i></i><p>Yokyoku Sōsho: edited by Y. Haga and N. Sasaki, 3 vols. (Texts of about 500 plays with short notes. Referred to by me as "Haga.")</p>
 <i></i><p>Zenchiku Shū: Works of Seami's son-in-law, 1917.</p>
 <i></i><p>Kyōgen Zenshū: Complete Collection of Farces, 1910.</p>
 <i></i><p>Jibyōshi Seigi: Yamazaki Gakudō, 1915. (A study of Nō-rhythm.)</p>
 <i></i><p>Yōkyoku Kaisetsu: Nō-plays explained in colloquial, by K. Kawashima, 1913.</p>
 <p>Magazines such as <i>Nōgaku Gwahō</i>, <i>Yōkyokukai</i>, etc.; picture postcards and albums of photographs such as <i>Nōgaku Mandai Kagami</i>, 1916.</p>
 <i></i><p>Ryōjin Hisshō: Folk-songs collected in 12th century and rediscovered in 1911.</p>
