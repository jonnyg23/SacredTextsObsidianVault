 <p><a name="page_95"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 95</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center"><i>APPENDIX</i>.</h3>  <h3 align="center"><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">A Short History of the Qabalah as Shown in its Bibliography</span>.</h3>  
 <p>Date <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">b.c.</span>? From earliest times this oral knowledge was prevalent amongst the Jews and others. It can be traced back into the night of time, on the monuments and in the sacred scrolls and hieroglyphs of all nations.</p>  
 <p><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">a.d.</span> 100-200. It is generally accepted by Qabalists that the various doctrines found in the <i>Sepher Yetzirah</i> or <i>Book of Becoming</i> (<i>Formation</i>), in the <i>Book of Splendour</i> (<i>Zohar</i>) and in the <i>Bahir</i>, were written down during this period by certain students after discussion with a teacher, probably the Rabbi Shimeon ben Yochai. From this time onward many MSS. were copied and circulated throughout Europe, being, however, carefully guarded by esotericists.</p>  
 <p>1070. Little is known of any actual Qabalistic writings during the period between the first and tenth centuries, although there are many MSS. now in the Vatican which treat of the doctrines. These are quoted by Bartolocci in his <i>Bibliotheca Magna Rabbinica</i> (vol. 4). There was, moreover, a floating body of esoteric wisdom known to students as "Secrets and Mysteries" (see Furst's <i>Bibliotheca Judaica</i>) during this epoch, for references to which see the works of <a href="errata.htm#6">Ibn</a> Gebirol (Isaac Myer's <i>Qaballah</i>).</p>  
 <p>1100-1400 <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">a.d.</span> The great outstanding work of this period was the inspired effort of Rabbi Moses de Leon, who either collected together the different manuscripts of the disciples of Rabbi Shimeon and edited and arranged them in book form as a complete work (<i>The Sepher Ha-Zohar</i>), or was impressed from higher spheresinspiredto give forth certain knowledge he had acquired through oral teaching and in dreams, etc., that intuitional knowledge which comes ever to the true disciple. Very many other Qabalistic works were written during this period, though few are actually called "Qabalah," this name being as it were an after-thought of the scholars and of comparatively modern invention. A name was required to cover the whole ground, to include all these "mysteries" and "superstitions," as Kircher calls them, and hence this name Qabalah was used, meaning the "Received Doctrine." It is said by the editor of the complete</p>  
 <p><a name="page_96"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 96</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> <i>Zohar</i> which Jean de Pauly translated, that the word Qabalah was first used by Rabbi Aaron Ashkenazi, but otherwise the Qabalistic doctrines were simply known as "Secrets and Mysteries,"</p>  
 <p>1150-1190. Maimonides works produced, chief amongst them being the <i>Moreh Nebochim</i>, of which there is a modern English translation by Friedlander, called <i>The Guide to the Perplexed</i>.</p>  
 <p>1190-? Isaac the Blind. The supposed writer of the <i>Sepher Ha-Bahir</i>, a work containing similar doctrines to those of the <i>Zohar</i> and <i>Yetzirah</i>. Treats of Reincarnation, Karma, the Trinity and Emanations, etc.</p>  
 <p>1190-1270. Nahmanides different commentaries produced. Many Qabalistic work ascribed to him and printed in later centuries. <i>Shaar Emunah</i>. <i>On Prayers</i>, <i>The Decalogue</i>, etc. <i>Penish Sepher Yetzirah</i> (Mantua, 1562, imp.). <i>Biur le-Sefer ha-Rimmon Eden Gan Elohim</i>, etc.</p>  
 <p>1200? <i>Commentary on the Ten Sephiroth</i>, by Azriel Ben Menahem (edited by N. A. Goldberg, 1850, Berlin). <i>Sepher ha-Milluim</i>, (1719, Mantua, Nachmanides) .</p>  
 <p>1270? <i>Midrash de R. Shimeon ben Yochai</i> by Moses de Leon. This is the famous Zohar already mentioned. It was written in Aramaic, and has been many times translated in different languages.</p>  
 <p>1270-1350-? <i>Poske Hilkot</i> by Recanati (1538, Bologna). He wrote also many Qabalistic commentaries, endeavoring to support the views of Qabalistic fellow students.</p>  
 <p>1310. <i>Ars Magna</i>, by Raymond Lully.</p>  
 <p>1370-1430. <i>Commentary on the Sepher Yetzirah</i> by Moses Botarel (Mantua, 1562; Zolkiev, 1745; Grodno, 1806).</p>  
 <p>1455. Works of Johann Reuchlin, <i>De Arte Cabalistica</i>, etc.</p>  
 <p>1460-1500? Commentary called <i>Ziyyuni</i>, by Menahem ben Meir (or Zioni), (printed at Cremona, 1559).</p>  
 <p>1463. Writings of Pico de Mirandola.</p>  
 <p>1487-1535. <i>De Occulta Philosophia</i> (many editions), a Qabalistic writing by H. Cornelius Agrippa.</p>  
 <p>1490. <i>De Harmonia Mundi</i>, by Francesco Zorri.</p>  
 <p>1493. Theophrastus Paracelsus (works).</p>
