 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_761"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_766">126:1</a> Here not the "Great Goddess," but as Commentator Nagoji Bhatta (cited post as NḄ.) says, "The Goddess" (Devī) "of the great"--viz., Brahmā, Viṣṇu, and Śiva, for it is by Her power that they enjoy their abode, and it is She whom even they worship--the Mother of all.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_762"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_767">126:2</a> The Asuras Śumbha and Niśumbha bereft the Devas of their dominion whereupon the latter prayed to the Goddess for help as follows.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_763"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_768">126:3</a> Nagoji says that Devī = <i>prakāśātmika</i>, that which is by its nature light and manifestation.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_764"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_769">126:4</a> <i>Mahādevī</i> (see note 1, <i>ante</i>).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_765"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_770">126:5</a> Because She is the cause of all auspicious things.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_766"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_771">126:6</a> <i>Śṛṣṭiśakti</i>, or <i>śakti</i> (power) of creation (N. B).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_767"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_772">126:7</a> <i>Bhadrāyai</i>. Literally, good = <i>rakṣanaśakti</i>, the <i>Vaiṣṇavaśakti</i>, which maintains (N. B.).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_768"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_773">127:1</a> <i>Nityāyai</i>; or She is the Śakti Nitya (N. B.).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_769"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_774">127:2</a> That is, She is <i>samhāraśakti</i> or <i>śakti</i> of dissolution, the <i>tāmasika śakti</i> (according the Commentator Gopal Chakravarti), as contrasted with Dhātrī, the <i>rājasika śakti</i>, and <i>indorūpā</i> (moon form), the <i>sāttvika śakti</i> (<i>vide post</i>).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_770"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_775">127:3</a> Daughter of Guru, the Lord of the Mountains.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_771"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_776">127:4</a> Creator and upholder.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_772"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_777">127:5</a> The moon here stands for all luminous things: or it may mean <i>indorūpā</i> in the technical sense of <i>yoga śāstra</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_773"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_778">127:6</a> <i>Sukhā</i> = <i>paramānandarūpā</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_774"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_779">127:7</a> That is, <i>aṇimādirūpā</i> (G. C.), the eight <i>siddhis</i>--<i>aṇimā</i>, <i>laghima</i>, etc.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_775"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_780">127:8</a> Gopal Chakravarti says this means <i>Rākṣasaśakti</i> (demonic power); also <i>alakṣmī</i> (misfortune). At the time of worship <a href="errata.htm#14">of</a> Lakṣmī (Devī of Prosperity) on the fifth day after <i>vijayadaśamī</i>, the Devī Alakṣmī is worshipped in the house in the form of a misshapen figure, and then thrown away.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_776"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_781">127:9</a> <i>Bhubritām</i>, which, according to G. C., means mountains, for wealth in the form of gems, etc., are found therein.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_777"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_782">127:10</a> Maheśvarī or Śivaśakti.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_778"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_783">128:1</a> Which means, according to Nagoji: "She who is known with difficulty (<i>duhkhajneyā</i>).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_779"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_784">128:2</a> As N. B. says: <i>Durgātsamsārātpārāngkaroti</i>, etc. G. C. says it means She whose abode is not known in either time or space.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_780"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_785">128:3</a> For She is not only <i>antaryāminī</i>, but remains even at the time of dissolution. She is feminine because She supports all things as their mother.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_781"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_786">128:4</a> <i>Khyatihprakritipuruṣayor bhedajnānām</i> (N. B.). <i>Viveka khyātih</i> is a term in Sānkhya denoting the cause of liberation, the recognition of the self (Puruṣa) by the self; not as one published rendering runs, "to fame;"</p>
 <p><a name="fn_782"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_787">128:5</a> As <i>tāmasika śakti</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_783"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_788">128:6</a> <i>Dhūmra</i>; that which is with smoke; the sacrificial rite; here the knowledge of the rites.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_784"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_789">128:7</a> <i>Atisaubhāgyā</i>. As such She is (N. B.) <i>vidyārūpinī</i>, as putting an end to the <i>samsāra</i>. For this reason She, as G. C. says, greatly delights all.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_785"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_790">128:8</a> <i>Atiraudrāyā</i>, because, as N. B. says, She, as <i>avidyā</i>, is the cause of the samsāra with all its terrors.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_786"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_791">129:1</a> <i>Jagatpratishtāyai</i> = (as N. B. says) <i>jagadupādānakāraṇam</i>; or, as, G. C. has it, She is <i>sarvāntaryāminī</i>, who dwells in the inmost being of all things.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_787"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_792">129:2</a> Which = (G. C.) <i>dyotanaśīla</i>, whose nature it is to lighten, or (N.) <i>devaśakti</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_788"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_793">129:3</a> Or <i>mulāvidyā</i> (Nagoji).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_789"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_794">129:4</a> <i>Namastasyai, namastasyai, namastasyai namo namah</i>. The refrain throughout the succeeding verses.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_790"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_795">129:5</a> <i>Cetana</i> = (Nagoji says) <i>chitśakti</i>, or mind. She Herself is <i>nirvikalpacitśakti</i>, but manifests as <i>savikalpacitśakti</i> in all worldly beings.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_791"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_796">129:6</a> The mind, as the aspect so named of the <i>antahkaraṇa</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_792"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_797">130:1</a> <i>Nidrārūpena</i>. According to Nagoji, <i>nidrā</i> = <i>suṣupti</i>, the state of dreamless sleep where all sense functions are at an end. According to G. C., <i>svapna</i> or dreaming sleep also.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_793"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_798">130:2</a> Nagoji says this word=<i>samsāratāpābhāvah</i>, or lack of worldly pain. The <i>samsāra</i> is like a burning flame. In its shadow there is coolness and peace. According to Gopal Chakravarti, however, it = <i>ātapābhāvah</i>. <i>Ātapāh</i> = <i>prakāśarūpatvātvidyā</i>, or knowledge on account of its giving light, and therefore as the lack of it She is <i>avidyā</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_794"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_799">130:3</a> <i>Śakt</i>i: power, action.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_795"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_800">131:1</a> Which, as Nagoji says, is the desire for that which is not one's own (<i>anātmīyā</i>); thirst for enjoyment, possession, individual life, etc.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_796"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_801">131:2</a> Which is the desire not to return evil to those who have done us wrong, notwithstanding our power to do so (N. B.).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_797"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_802">131:3</a> <i>Jātīrūpeṇa</i>: that which classifies and differentiates one body of beings from another.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_798"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_803">131:4</a> Which Nagoji says means the control of the senses and renunciation of all worldly things.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_799"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_804">132:1</a> That is, Nagoji says, <i>āstikatvam</i> (belief in God's existence); or, according to Gopal Chakravarti, also a firm and strong faith in Veda.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_800"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_805">132:2</a> Wealth, etc.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_801"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_806">132:3</a> That is, <i>jīvikā</i>, the callings of husbandmen, merchant, cattle-rearing, etc.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_802"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_807">133:1</a> Which Nagoji says is the desire of removing the pain of others.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_803"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_808">133:2</a> Which Nagoji defines as satisfaction with that which one possesses as distinguished from longing for what one has not got.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_804"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_809">133:3</a> She is <i>jananī</i>, and, as Nagoji says, <i>pālayitrī</i> (protectress).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_805"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_810">133:4</a> <i>Bhrānti</i>. A thoroughly Indian conception, though some modern Hindus have lost the sense of it. Gopal Chakravarti gives as an example the classical instance of the <i>jīva</i> taking mother-of-pearl to be silver, etc.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_806"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_811">134:1</a> <i>Cit</i> = <i>citśakti</i> (N. B.), and according to Gopal Chakravarti, who says the <i>jīva</i> is here meant, <i>caitanya</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_807"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_812">134:2</a> Indra.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_808"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_813">134:3</a> Queen and Ruler of the worlds.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_809"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_814">134:4</a> The Good Devas.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_810"></a><a href="htg21.htm#fr_815">134:5</a> Their evil enemies.</p>
