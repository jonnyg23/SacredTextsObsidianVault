 <p><a name="page_168"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 168</font></a> <a name="page_169"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 169</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">TRIPURASUNDARĪ</h3>  <h3 align="center">(TRIPURASUNDARĪSTOTRA)</h3>  
 <p align="center">1</p>  
 <p>I SEEK refuge with Tripurasundarī, <a name="fr_1063"></a><a href="#fn_1057"><font size="1">1</font></a><br> Who wanders in the Kadamba forest; <a name="fr_1064"></a><a href="#fn_1058"><font size="1">2</font></a><br> The spouse of the Three-eyed One, <a name="fr_1065"></a><a href="#fn_1059"><font size="1">3</font></a><br> Bank of cloud (in the sky of the heart) of numbers of sages, <a name="fr_1066"></a><a href="#fn_1060"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>

 <p>Whose hips defeat the mountain by their greatness. <a name="fr_1067"></a><a href="#fn_1061"><font size="1">1</font></a><br> Who is served by celestial women,<br> Whose eyes are like the newly blown lotus,<br> And who is dark as the colour of a freshly formed rain-cloud. <a name="fr_1068"></a><a href="#fn_1062"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p align="center">2</p>  
 <p>I seek refuge with Tripurasundarī,<br> The Spouse of the Three-eyed One,<br> Who dwells in the Kadamba forest,<br> And who is ever wandering;<br> The Large-eyed One who holds a golden <i>vīnā</i>, <a name="fr_1069"></a><a href="#fn_1063"><font size="1">3</font></a><br> Wearing a necklace of priceless gems,<br> Whose face is glowing with wine, <a name="fr_1070"></a><a href="#fn_1064"><font size="1">4</font></a><br> And who of Her mercy grants prosperity to Her devotees.</p>  
 <p align="center">3</p>  
 <p>Ever are we protected by Her whose abode is the Kadamba forest,<br> The weight of whose breasts are garlanded with glittering gems,<br> Whose breasts are rising, <a name="fr_1071"></a><a href="#fn_1065"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>

 <p align="center">4</p>  
 <p>I seek refuge with Tripurasundarī,<br> The Spouse of the Three-eyed One,<br> Who stays in the Kadamba forest,<br> Who is seated in the golden circle and dwells in the six lotuses, <a name="fr_1074"></a><a href="#fn_1068"><font size="1">3</font></a><br> Ever revealing like lightning the great power (of devotees), <a name="fr_1075"></a><a href="#fn_1069"><font size="1">4</font></a><br> Whose beauty is like that of the <i>Jaba</i> flower, <a name="fr_1076"></a><a href="#fn_1070"><font size="1">5</font></a><br> And whose brow is adorned with the full moon.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_1057"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1063">169:1</a> The Devī is so called as the Spouse of Śiva, destroyer of the <i>tripura</i>, or cities of the three Asuras--Kamalākṣa, Tarakākṣa, and Vidyun-māli. According to the Kālikā Purāṇa, Paraśiva is Tripurā, because he has three <i>pura</i> in Him, His body becoming triple upon the manifestation therein of Brahmā, Viṣṇu, and Śiva. The Devī is then the Śakti of Paramaśiva.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1058"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1064">169:2</a> The Kadamba (<i>Nauclea cadamba</i>) is a tree with orange fragrant blossom whereunder Kṛṣṇa played (see <i>Ādyākālisvarūpaśtotra</i> in Mahānirvāṇa Tantra). <i>Kadamba</i> also denotes number (multitude), and in this sense the <i>Kadamba</i> forest is the universe which the Devī permeates.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1059"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1065">169:3</a> Śiva with the central eye of wisdom. Śiva is also <i>Tryambaka</i>, because He is the father of the three Devas, Brahmā, Viṣṇu, and Rudra (Tarkālangkāra Commentary, Mahānirvāṇa Tantra). The Ṛgvidhāna uses it as equivalent of Mahādeva.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1060"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1066">169:4</a> <i>Muni</i>. As the bank of cloud gives water, so She quenches the spiritual thirst of <i>munis</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1061"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1067">170:1</a> <i>Nitambajitabhudaram</i>. <i>Nitamba</i> literally means buttocks, which, however, here reads rather absurdly in English, the side or hips being <i>pārśa</i>, <i>kakṣa</i>, or <i>shroni</i> (<i>cf</i>. tenth śloka of the <i>Karpurākhyastava</i> "<i>Samantādāpinastana-jaghanadhrikyauvanavatī</i>")</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1062"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1068">170:2</a> That is, of the dark blue colour seen when the blue of the sky appears through a freshly-formed black rain-cloud.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1063"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1069">170:3</a> A stringed musical instrument of that name.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1064"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1070">170:4</a> <i>Mukhasamullasattvārunim</i>. <i>Vāruni</i> is wine made from rice. Here and in following verses the divine ambrosia (<i>amṛta</i>) is referred to.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1065"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1071">170:5</a> "Rising" (<i>cf</i>. <i>Durgādhyāna</i> in "Devī Purāṇa") <i>pīnonnata payodharām</i>. As to weight and greatness, see <i>Annapūrṇādhyāna</i>, <i>Bhuvaneśvaristotra</i>, "<i>āpīvarastanatating tanuvrittamadhyām</i>," and Introduction.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1066"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1072">171:1</a> <i>Madārunakapolaya</i> (see n. 4, <a href="#page_170">p. 170</a>).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1067"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1073">171:2</a> <i>Lilayā</i>. Play (<i>līlā</i>) is the mark of a Deva, and the Devī's substance is play (<i>lilāmayī</i>). The Devī is Lalitā ("She who plays"): Padma Purāṇa says: "Having passed beyond the world She plays, hence She is called Lalitā." But the Creation is also Her play.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1068"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1074">171:3</a> <i>Ṣaḍāmbhu</i>--that is, the six cakra or centres in the human body: the <i>mulādhāra</i>, <i>svādihṣṭhāna</i>, <i>manipūra</i>, <i>anāhata</i>, <i>viśuddha</i>, and <i>ājnāpadmas</i> (see the translation of the Satcakra Nirūpaṇa from the Sanskrit, <i>The Serpent Power</i>). The Devī exists as Kuṇḍalinī in these <i>cakra</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1069"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1075">171:4</a> <i>Satatasiddhisaudaminim</i>. <i>Siddhi</i> (power so called), which lies latent, is instantly brought to light by Her.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1070"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1076">171:5</a> Scarlet hibiscus, the Tāntrik flower sacred to the Devī. </p>
 <p><a name="fn_1071"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1077">171:6</a> <i>Ṛṣi</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1072"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1078">172:1</a> The musical instrument which She holds and which rests on Her breast.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1073"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1079">172:2</a> For she is also Kamalā or Lakṣmī.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1074"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1080">172:3</a> <i>Madārunāvilochanam</i> (see <a href="#page_170">p. 170</a>. note 4).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1075"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1081">172:4</a> That is, charmer of Śiva who destroyed Kāmadeva with the fire from His eyes when the latter sought to distract him by thought of passion from the <i>yoga</i> in which he was engaged.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1076"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1082">172:5</a> <i>Smaretprathama puṣpinīm</i>, literally "as having the first 'flower'" which is used in the same symbolical sense as in English. The <i>puṣpotsava</i> is the religious festival held on its first appearance at puberty.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1077"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1083">172:6</a> <i>Rudhiravīndunīlambaram</i>--that is, stained with the <i>puṣpa</i> ("flower"). As this first shows itself when woman is ready to bear, so in the blue sky, which is the Devī's garment, signs are seen which herald Her creation.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1078"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1084">172:7</a> <i>Grihītamadhupānikām</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1079"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1085">172:8</a> <i>Madhuvighurnanetranchalam</i> (see <a href="#page_170">p. 170</a>, n. 4).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1080"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1086">173:1</a> <i>Ghanastana bharonnatām</i>: "heavy" (<i>cf.</i> <i>Annapūrṇadhyāna Annapradāna-niratām stanabhāranamrām</i>"); "high" (<i>cf</i>. <i>Durgādhyana</i>, <i>ante</i> Introduction); "close," so that, as it is said in the Kumarāsambhavam (chap. i.) of Kalidāsa: "Even the filament of a lotus could not be passed between them" ("<i>mrinālasūtrāntaramapyalabhyam</i>").</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1081"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1087">173:2</a> <i>Galitachikurām</i>. The Devī in this and other forms, as Kālī, Tārā and Chinnamastā is so represented. The epithet is a common one in Tantra (<i>cf</i>. <i>Karpūrādistotra</i>, verse 3).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1082"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1088">173:3</a> <i>Jaba</i>, v. <i>ante</i>. So also the Lalitā (verse 147), "whose body is like the China rose."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1083"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1089">173:4</a> See the <i>Ādyākālisvarūpa Stotra</i> in the Mahānirvāṇa Tantra.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1084"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1090">173:5</a> The Devī is, according to the Lalitā Sahasranāma (verse 59) <i>chāruhāsa</i> (with beautiful smile), indicating a certain state of consciousness (<i>prabodha</i>) of highest bliss.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1085"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1091">173:6</a> These are Her weapons. The Tantrarāja (Vāsana chap.) says: "Mind is the bow of sugar-cane, desire the noose, anger the goad, and the five subtle sources of the elements (<i>tanmātra</i>) the five arrows of flowers." But the Yoginīhridaya says: "The noose is <i>Iccāśakti</i>, and goad <i>Jnānāśakti</i>, and the bow and arrows <i>Kriyāśakti</i>."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1086"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1092">173:7</a> She deludes men with her <i>māyā</i>; hence the Lalitā (verse 137) calls Her "all-bewildering" (<i>sarvamohinī</i>). The Kurma Purāṇā says: "This <i>māyā</i> is dear to me by which the world is bewildered. I bewilder the whole universe with the Devas, Daityas, and men."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1087"></a><a href="htg27.htm#fr_1093">174:1</a> <i>Cikurabandhasairiṇdhrikām</i>: for Her hair, which is in some of Her aspects dishevelled, is in others beautifully arranged.</p>
