 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">VII</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Hu Yeh;</i> narrative. Where the provisions are most frugal, all the rules of polite intercourse may yet be preserved.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>A few gourd leaves that waved about<br>   Cut down and boiled;—the feast how spare! <a name="page_327"><font size="1" color="green">p. 327</font></a><br> But the good host his spirits takes,<br>   Pours out a cup, and proves them rare.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>A single rabbit on the mat,<br>   Or baked, or roast:—how small the feast!<br> But the good host his spirits takes,<br>   And fills the cup of every guest.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>A single rabbit on the mat,<br>   Roasted or broiled:—how poor the meal!<br> But the guests from the spirit vase<br>   Fill their host's cup, and drink his weal.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_4">4</a></font></span>A single rabbit on the mat,<br>   Roasted or baked:—no feast we think!<br> But from the spirit vase they take,<br>   Both host and guests, and joyous drink.</p></div>
