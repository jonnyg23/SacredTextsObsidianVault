 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">VI</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Ting Chih Fang Chung;</i> narrative. The praise of Duke Wên: his diligence, foresight, sympathy with the people, and prosperity.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>At dusk the <i>Ting</i> star passed on to the west,<br> And field work for the year was laid to rest.<br> At Ch‘u the duke his palace took in hand, <a name="page_54"><font size="1" color="green">p. 54</font></a><br> And by the sun fixed how its walls should stand.<br> All round about he planted many a tree,—<br> Hazels and chestnuts, <i>t‘ung</i> and <i>tzŭ</i>, and <i>i</i>,<br> And varnish trees. The grove would yield erelong<br> Abundant wood for lutes, to aid the voice of song.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>He climbed those ruined walls, thence to inspect<br> The site he wished for Ch‘u-ch‘iu to select.<br> His glance the land from Ch‘u to T‘ang mapped out<br> Noting the hills and smaller heights about,<br> He then came down, the mulberry trees to view,<br> And judged the soil, and learned its nature true.<br> These things once done, he asked the tortoise shell,<br> Answer auspicious got,—and all succeeded well.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>Thereafter, when there fell the copious showers,<br> He often called his groom, and in the hours<br> Of early dawn afield by starlight drove<br> Among the laborers, and to cheer them strove.<br> And many ways he had, not this alone,<br> In which his character distinguished shone,<br> To duty bound, assiduous in his cares;—<br> And blessing came,—three thousand steeds and mares.</p></div>
