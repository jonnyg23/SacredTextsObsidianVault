 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">III</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Yen Yen</i>; allusive and narrative. Chuang Chiang relates her grief at the departure of Tai Kuei, and celebrates that lady's virtue.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>With wings, not level, spread,<br>   About the swallows stir.<br> Homeward the lady sped,<br>   And I escorted her.<br> And when away from sight she passed,<br> Like rain, my tears came falling fast.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>The swallows fly about,<br>   Now up, now down, they dart.<br> She to her home set out,<br>   And I was loth to part.<br> Her form when distance from me kept,<br> Long time I stood, and silent wept. <a name="page_28"><font size="1" color="green">p. 28</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>Above, beneath, then cry<br>   The flying swallows vent.<br> Homeward she passed, and I<br>   Far with her southwards went.<br> Her form when distance from me bore<br> With bitter grief my heart was sore.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_4">4</a></font></span>This lady Chung loved me<br>   With feeling true and deep.<br> Docile and good was she,<br>   Nor failed the light to keep.<br> Unworthy me her deed and word<br> Taught to respect our former lord.</p></div>
