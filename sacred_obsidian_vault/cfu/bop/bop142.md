 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">IV</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Tung Mên Chih Ch‘ih;</i> allusive. The praise of some virtuous and intelligent lady.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>To steep your hemp, you seek the moat,<br>   Where lies the pool, th’ east gate beyond.<br> <i>I</i> seek that lady, good and fair,<br>   Who can to me in song respond. <a name="page_154"><font size="1" color="green">p. 154</font></a>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>To steep your grass-cloth plants, you seek<br>   The pool that near the east gate lies.<br> <i>I</i> seek that lady, good and fair,<br>   Who can with me hold converse wise.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>Out by the east gate, to the moat,<br>   To steep your rope rush, you repair.<br> Her pleasant converse to enjoy,<br>   <i>I</i> seek that lady, good and fair.</p></div>
