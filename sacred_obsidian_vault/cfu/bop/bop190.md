 <p><a name="page_222"><font size="1" color="green">p. 222</font></a></p>  <h4 align="center">VII</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Hung Yen;</i> allusive. The people, regathered into communities under King Hsüan, praise the officers by whom this had been accomplished.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>With rustling wings the wild geese fly,<br>   Round fields long strange to hand of toil.<br> Called by the officers in charge,<br>   We labor on the desert soil.<br> Sad is our state, but sadder still<br> The hearts no wedded love can fill.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>The wild geese fly about, and light<br>   Amid the marsh, where grain once shone.<br> We rear the walls as we are told;—<br>   Five thousand feet are quickly done<br> Great is the toil, and sore the pain,<br> But peaceful homes will rise again.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>The wild geese fly with plaintive note,<br>   That sadly suits our weary sighs.<br> But those whose orders we obey,—<br>   They see our pain; and they are wise.<br> If they had not been men of sense,<br> They had rebuked our insolence.</p></div>
