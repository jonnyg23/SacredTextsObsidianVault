 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">X</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Tsai Ch‘ü;</i> narrative. The open shamelessness of Wan Chiang in her meeting with her brother.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>  On comes her chariot, fast and loud,<br>     With screen of bamboos finely wove,<br>   And leather bright, vermilion-hued—<br>     Ch‘i’s daughter hastes to lawless love.<br> To this from Lu the road is smooth and plain;—<br> ’Twas but last night she started with her train.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>  Her four black steeds are beautiful;<br>     Soft are the reins the driver holds. <a name="page_114"><font size="1" color="green">p. 114</font></a><br>   The road from Lu is smooth and plain;—<br>     Ch‘i’s daughter's heart its joy unfolds.<br> Full of complacency is she; nor shame<br> Abashes her, nor fear of evil name.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>  Broad flow the waters of the Wên,<br>     And crowds of travelers go by.<br>   The road from Lu is smooth and plain;—<br>     She looks around with careless eye.<br> That many see her gives her no concern;<br> Her thoughts to her licentious fancy turn.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_4">4</a></font></span>  On sweep the waters of the Wên;<br>     More numerous are the travelers now.<br>   The road from Lu is smooth and plain;—<br>     Ch‘i’s daughter shows her brazen brow.<br> At ease and proud, she holds her onward way,<br> Careless of what all think of her display.</p></div>
