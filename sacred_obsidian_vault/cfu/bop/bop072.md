 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">V</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Chung Ku Yu T‘ui;</i> allusive. The sad case of a woman forced to separate from her husband by the pressure of famine.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>The valleys show the motherwort,<br>   Now scorched in each dry spot.<br> Behold a wife driven forth from home,<br>   Beneath hard famine's lot!<br> She sadly sighs, she sadly sighs,<br> From husband torn and dearest ties.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>The valleys show the motherwort,<br>   Now scorched where tall it rose.<br> Behold a wife driven forth from home,<br>   By stern misfortune's blows!<br> We hear her groans, we hear her groans,<br> As she her hapless fate bemoans.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>The valleys show the motherwort,<br>   Scorched in each dampest place.<br> Behold a wife driven forth from home—<br>   Bewail in vain her ease!<br> Her tears aye flow, her tears aye flow;<br> How’er she grieve, ne’er ends her woe!</p></div>
