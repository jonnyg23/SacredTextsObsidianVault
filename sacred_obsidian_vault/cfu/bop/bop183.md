 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">X</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Chan Lu;</i> allusive. A festal ode, appropriate to the convivial entertainment of the feudal princes at the royal court.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>The dew lies heavy all around,<br> Nor, till the sun shines, leaves the ground. <a name="page_210"><font size="1" color="green">p. 210</font></a><br> Far into night we feasting sit;<br> We drink, and none his place may quit.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>The dew lies heavy, and its gems<br> Stud the luxuriant, grassy stems.<br> The happy night with wassail rings;<br> So feasted here the former kings.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>The jujube and the willow tree<br> All fretted with the dew we see.<br> Each guest's a prince of noble line,<br> In whom the virtues all combine.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_4">4</a></font></span>The <i>t‘ung</i> and <i>i</i> their fruits display,<br> Pendent from every graceful spray.<br> My guests are joyous and serene,<br> No haggard eye, no ruffled mien.</p></div>
