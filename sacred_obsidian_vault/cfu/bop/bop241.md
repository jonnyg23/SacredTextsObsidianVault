 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">VIII</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Chien Chien Chih Shih;</i> narrative. Commemorating the hardships of a long and difficult expedition to the east, aggravated by great rains.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>How high those frowning rocks arise!<br>   With awe they fill the mind. <a name="page_328"><font size="1" color="green">p. 328</font></a><br> Our way through streams, o’er mountains lies;<br>   Toilsome the march we find.<br> Eastward our expedition goes,<br> Nor has our chief one hour's repose.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>Those frowning rocks the heights surmount,<br>   And fill the mind with dread.<br> O’er hills, through streams, our steps we count;<br>   When shall our march be sped?<br> Our warrior hastens on the track,<br> Nor thinks he of our drawing back.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>Look at the swine, with legs all white,<br>   Washed by the pools from stain!<br> The moon wades through the Hyads bright,<br>   Foretelling heavier rain.<br> He at whose word we eastward fare<br> No leisure has for other care.</p></div>
