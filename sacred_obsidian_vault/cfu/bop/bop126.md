 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">X</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Yu Ti Chih Tu; </i>metaphorical. Some one regrets the poverty of his circumstances, which prevented him from gathering around him companions whom he admired.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>On the left of the way, a russet pear tree<br> Stands there all alone,—a fit image of me.<br> There is that princely man! O that he would come,<br> And in my poor dwelling with me be at home!<br> In the core of my heart do I love him, but say,<br> Whence shall I procure him the wants of the day. <a name="page_136"><font size="1" color="green">p. 136</font></a>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>At the bend in the way a russet pear tree<br> Stands there all alone,—a fit image of me.<br> There is that princely man! O that he would come.<br> And rambling with me be himself here at home!<br> In the core of my heart I love him, but say,<br> Whence shall I procure him the wants of the day.</p></div>
