 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Book of Poetry</i>, tr. by James Legge, [1876], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">X</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Ho Ts‘ao Pu Huang;</i> allusive and narrative. The misery and murmuring of soldiers constantly employed on expeditionary services, and treated without any consideration.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>Yellow now is all the grass;<br> All the days in marching pass.<br> On the move is every man;<br> Hard work, far and near, they plan. <a name="page_330"><font size="1" color="green">p. 330</font></a>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span> Black is every plant become;<br> Every man is torn from home.<br> Kept on foot, our state is sad;—<br> As if we no feelings had!</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_3">3</a></font></span>Not rhinoceroses we!<br> Tigers do we care to be?<br> Fields like these so desolate<br> Are to us a hateful fate.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_4">4</a></font></span>Long-tailed foxes pleased may hide<br> ’Mong the grass, where they abide.<br> We, in box carts slowly borne,<br> On the great roads plod and mourn.</p></div>
