 <p><a name="page_116"><font size="1" color="green">p. 116</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center"><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Book IX. The Odes of Wei</span></h3>  <h4 align="center">I</h4>  
 <p></p><div style="font-size: 90%">The <i>Ko Chü;</i> narrative. The extreme parsimoniousness even of wealthy men in Wei.</div>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 20%; margin-right: 20%; font-size: 110%;"><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_1">1</a></font></span>Thin cloth of dolichos supplies the shoes,<br>   In which some have to brave the frost and cold.<br> A bride, when poor, her tender hands must use,<br>   Her dress to make, and the sharp needle hold.<br> This man is wealthy, yet he makes his bride<br> Collars and waistbands for his robes provide.  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="an_2">2</a></font></span>Conscious of wealth, he moves with easy mien;<br>   Politely on the left he takes his place;<br> The ivory pin is at his girdle seen;<br>   His dress and gait show gentlemanly grace.<br> Why do we brand him in our satire here?<br> ’Tis this,—his niggard soul provokes the sneer.</p></div>
