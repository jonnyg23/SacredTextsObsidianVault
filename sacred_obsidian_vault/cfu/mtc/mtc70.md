 <p><a name="page_154"><font size="1" color="green">p. 154</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">APPENDIX</h3>  
 <p><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">An</span> essay on Po Chü-i, whose poems occupy most of this book, will be found in "170 Chinese Poems." The fullest account of Li Po's life (with translations) is given in a paper read by me to the China Society, and published in the <i>Asiatic Review,</i> July, 1919. Notices of Ch‘ü Yüan, Wang Wei, Yüan Chēn, Wang Chien and Ou-yang Hsiu will be found in Giles's "Biographical Dictionary." To Wang Chien, Po Chü-i addressed several poems.</p>  
 <p>Of the 68 pieces in this book, 55 are now translated for the first time. Of the eight poems by Li Po, all but Number 6 have been translated before, some of them by several hands.</p>  
 <p>Among the poems by Po Chü-i, three (Nos. 11,12, and 44) have been translated by Woitsch <a name="fr_61"></a><a href="#fn_58"><font size="1">1</font></a> and one, (No. 43), very incorrectly, by Pfizmaier. Another (No. 21) was translated into rhymed verse by Prof. Giles in "Adversaria Sinica " (1914) , p. 323. Ou-yang Hsiu's "Autumn" was translated by Giles (with great freedom in many places) in his "Chinese Literature," p. 215.</p>
