 <center><i>NOTE:</i> </center><h3 align="CENTER">141. Tacoomah makes a Dance.</h3>
 <p>Medleys of this character seem to have been a popular form of entertainment and may still be common, though the examples I have were given me in every case by old men. They are composed of scraps of song or whole scenes from well-known Nansi stories, together with game-songs, imitations of animal sounds, and "rhyming," strung together much like our own musical medleys--the last line of one suggesting the first of the next. In this example, story-songs from numbers 97 and 86 are followed by a game dialogue; next by some animal imitations; last, by a specimen of Jamaica "rhyming." Other examples of this kind of improvised "rhyming" are:</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <font size="2"><p>"Mr. Might, jump up a height, after a kite,<br>
 And knock his eye, upon his hog-sty, and cry out 'hi!<br>
     oh, my! why should I die'."</p>
 <p>"There is a boat, and in the boat, is a goat, and has<br>
     a long coat, catch him under the throat."</p></font></dir>
