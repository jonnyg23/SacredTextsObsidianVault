 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>Arabian Poetry</i>, by W. A. Clouston, [1881], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">THE FRONTISPIECE.</h3>  
 <p>THIS is a lithographed reproduction, in facsimile (but only in black and white), of a page of a beautifully written and splendidly illuminated Arabic manuscript volume, in the possession of Mr. E. J. W. Gibb, whose translation of Mesīhī's Ode on Spring enriches the Appendix to the present work. The page contains the eleven first couplets of El-Būsīrī's celebrated Qasīda (Poem, or rather, Hymn) in praise of Muhammad, of which an English translation, by Mr. J. W. Redhouse, will be found in pages <a href="arp141.htm#page_319">319</a>-<a href="arp143.htm#page_341">341</a>. It is hardly necessary to state, what almost every English reader must already know, that Arabic, like most Oriental languages, is written from right to left; but it may be explained that the space in the centre of the page separates the first and second hemistichs of each verse. For example: the first couplet is contained in the <i>first</i> line, at the top of <i>each</i> column; the second couplet, in the <i>second</i> line of <i>each</i> column; and so on, reading across the central division. Mr. Redhouse has favoured me with a transliteration of this page (not every Arabist can correctly read any Arabic manuscript), and a translation of the titles and the customary invocation. The titles of the poem and of the first section, at the top of the page, are:</p>  
 <p align="center"><i>qasīdatun burdatun faslun fi ta‘dīli ’n-nafsi</i></p>  
 <p align="center">A Poem; a Mantle. A Section on the Justification of the Carnal Man.</p>  
 <p>Then follows the invocation which is invariably placed at the beginning of every Muslim composition, whether secular or religious:</p>  
 <p align="center"><i>bi ’smi ’llāhi ’r-rahmānī ’r-rahīmi</i></p>  
 <p>In the name of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">God</span>, the Most Merciful, the All-Compassionate.</p>  
 <p>Our old European authors in like manner always headed their</p>  
 <p><a name="page_viii"><font size="1" color="green">p. viii</font></a></p>  
 <p>writings with the sign of the cross, +. Thus, in the <i>King's Quair</i>, by James I., of Scotland:</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">And forthwithal my pen in hand I took,<br> And made a +, and thus began my book.</div>  
 <p>Modern Christians do not so literally follow the scriptural injunction: "In all thy ways acknowledge Him, and He shall direct thy paths." But with Muslims it is no empty form.</p>  
 <p>The English reader will be interested in observing, in the following four first couplets of El-Būsīrī's Poem, in italic characters, the movement of the qasīda rhyme:</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">1 <i>e min tezekkuri jīrāmin bi dhī-selemi<br>   mezejta dem‘an jerà min muqletin bi demi</i>  
 <p>2 <i>em hebeti ’r-rīhu min tilqā’i katzimetin<br>   wa ewmadza ’l-barqu fī ’tz-tzalmā’i min idzami</i></p>  
 <p>3 <i>fa mà li ‘ayney-ke in qulta ’kfufà hemetà<br>   wa mà li qalbi-ke in qulta ’stefiq yehimi</i></p>  
 <p>4 <i>e yahsibu ’s-sabbu enna ’l-hubba munketimun<br>   mà beyna munsejimin min-hu wa mudztarimi</i></p></div>  
 <p>The two halves of the first distich, as above, rhyme; and the final syllable (<i>mi</i>) of the second half of every succeeding distich, to the end of the poem, is the same as those of the hemistichs of the opening verse.</p>
