 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">The Maqámát of Badí‘ al-Zamán al-Hamadhání, tr. W.J. Prendergast [1915] at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">III. THE WORD MAQAMA (<font color="GREEN">…</font>)</h3>  
 <p>MAQAMA, plural Maqámát, from <font color="GREEN">…</font> he stood, primarily signifies an occasion of standing, or a place where one stands upright. Standing appear to have been not only the natural, but the conventional position of the speaker, e.g.</p>  
 <p></p><dir>(1) <font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br>     The people stood up to speak. <a name="fr_60"></a><a href="#fn_60"><font size="1">2</font></a>  
 <p>(2) I have heard that ‘Alí ibn al-Ḥusain was standing admonishing the people. <a name="fr_61"></a><a href="#fn_61"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>(3) Come near and eat, or, if thou wilt, stand and speak. <a name="fr_62"></a><a href="#fn_62"><font size="1">4</font></a></p></font></dir>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_12"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 12</font></a></p>  
 <p>The practice of standing to speak goes back to Homeric times:--</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">O Damaan, friends and heroes, men of Arie's company, seemly it is to listen to him who standeth to speak. <a name="fr_63"></a><a href="#fn_63"><font size="1">1</font></a></font></dir>  
 <p>According to Ibn Qutaiba (A. H. 276) reports of the literary discussions held in the assemblies of men of learning and culture received, early in the ‘Abbásid period (A. H. 132-656), the name Maqáma. <a name="fr_64"></a><a href="#fn_64"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>These literary reunions appear to have been a recognized institution. Saif al-Daula used to hold an assembly every night to which men of learning came and conversed in his presence; <a name="fr_65"></a><a href="#fn_65"><font size="1">3</font></a> and Tha‘álibí, in referring to the literary splendour of Bukhára in Hamadhání's time, mentions a remarkable gathering of the chief scholars of the day at the Court of that State. <a name="fr_66"></a><a href="#fn_66"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>Maqáma probably acquired the more restricted meaning of a discourse, exhortation or oration, between the time of Jáḥiz (ob. A.H. 255) and that of Hamadhání (ob. A.H. 398).</p>  
 <p>The extracts given below illustrate the various uses of the word from the time of the pre-Islámic poet Zuheir (end of the sixth century A.D.) to that of the author (end of the eleventh century A.D.) It is thus used by early writers:--</p>  
 <p></p><dir>(1) By Zuheir and quoted by Hamadhání:--  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br> And among them are <i>maqámát</i>--champions and the like--whose faces are fair,<br> And councils where words are followed by deeds. <a name="fr_67"></a><a href="#fn_67"><font size="1">5</font></a></font></dir></dir>  
 <p></p><dir>(2) By Abú Tammám (ob. A.H. 190):--  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br> Concerning every battlefield and in every <i>maqáma</i> (situation);<br> Which obtain from poetry covenants and contracts. <a name="fr_68"></a><a href="#fn_68"><font size="1">6</font></a></font></dir></dir>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_13"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 13</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><dir><dir><font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br> Of many a <i>maqáma</i>--speech--whose weapons have rendered other people's talk weak, wherein there are waves of language which cannot be cleared away,<br> Hast thou dispelled the: darkness with a decisive speech like unto a determining blow in the time of peril. <a name="fr_69"></a><a href="#fn_69"><font size="1">1</font></a></font>  
 <p><font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br> And if he is present in the <i>maqáma</i>--assembly or council--on the day of final decision,<br> Thou wilt see the equal of Luqmán the sage. <a name="fr_70"></a><a href="#fn_70"><font size="1">2</font></a></font></p></dir></dir>  
 <p></p><dir>(3) By al-Qattál:--  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br> In the presence of the <i>maqáma</i>--a company of people--I adjured Zíád to desist,<br> And I reminded him of the ties of relationship of Sa‘r and Haitham that bound us together. <a name="fr_71"></a><a href="#fn_71"><font size="1">3</font></a></font></dir></dir>  
 <p></p><dir>(4) By Jáḥiz (ob. A. H. 255):--  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br> And he sat down and the company were Arabs who were discussing tradition and citing proof passages and proverbs, and from history, battles and <i>maqámát</i>--speeches or orations. <a name="fr_72"></a><a href="#fn_72"><font size="1">4</font></a></font>  
 <p><font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br> Solitary ones who have not heard the barking of the dogs of <i>maqáma</i>--a company of Bedawín. <a name="fr_73"></a><a href="#fn_73"><font size="1">5</font></a></font></p></dir></dir>  
 <p></p><dir>(5) By Abú ‘Alí al-Qálí (ob. 356):--  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1"> <a name="fr_74"></a><a href="#fn_74"><font size="1">6</font></a> <font color="GREEN">…</font><br> Maqáma = Majlis, a company of people.</font></dir></dir>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_14"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 14</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><dir>(6) By Hamadhání:--  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br> There used to reach me of the <i>maqámát</i>--discourses and the like--and sayings of al-Iskanderí. <a name="fr_75"></a><a href="#fn_75"><font size="1">1</font></a></font>  
 <p><font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br> So wait for the end of his <i>maqáma</i>--a discourse. The word here refers to a stirring sermon which ‘Ísá ibn Hishám had been listening to. <a name="fr_76"></a><a href="#fn_76"><font size="1">2</font></a></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br> And him who enters the <i>maqámát</i>--companies or assemblies of respectable people. <a name="fr_77"></a><a href="#fn_77"><font size="1">3</font></a></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br> And of their distinguishing marks is the vileness of their <i>maqámát</i>--assemblies of chief men, or speeches. <a name="fr_78"></a><a href="#fn_78"><font size="1">4</font></a></font></p>  
 <p><font size="-1"><font color="GREEN">…</font><br> Verily he who has dictated four hundred <i>maqámát</i> on mendicity. <a name="fr_79"></a><a href="#fn_79"><font size="1">5</font></a></font></p></dir></dir>  
 <p>Although the maqámát were composed chiefly for assemblies of the learned and for the entertainment of the great, the word maqáma is applied by Hamadhání himself to the species of composition first associated with his name, and not to the people who assembled to listen to his discourses. It is in this restricted sense that it has come down to us.</p>  
 <p>As the extracts from different authors do, however, show that the word has the triple signification of an oratorical address or harangue, a collection of champions, or a company of people, I have preferred a transliteration to the rendering by the familiar, but unsatisfactory, term assembly.</p>
