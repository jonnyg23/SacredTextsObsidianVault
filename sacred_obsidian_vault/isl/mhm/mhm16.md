 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">The Maqámát of Badí‘ al-Zamán al-Hamadhání, tr. W.J. Prendergast [1915] at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">VIII. THE MAQAMA OF ADHARBAYJÁN</h3>  
 <p>SAID ‘ÍSÁ IBN HISHÁM: When wealth girded me with its flowing robe, I was suspected of being possessed of property that I had stolen, or of a treasure <a name="fr_276"></a><a href="#fn_276"><font size="1">5</font></a> that I had found, so the darkness of the night urged me to flee, and the horse carried me away. In my flight I traversed paths that had never been trodden before, and where a bird could not find its way, until I passed through the land of terror, crossed its frontiers, entered the protected domain of safety, and there found tranquility. I arrived at Adharbayján <a name="fr_277"></a><a href="#fn_277"><font size="1">6</font></a> and verily the camel's feet were abraded and the</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_51"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 51</font></a></p>  
 <p>stages travelled had consumed their flesh. And when I reached it,</p>  
 <p></p><dir>'We alighted intending the stay to be three days, <a name="fr_278"></a><a href="#fn_278"><font size="1">1</font></a><br> But it was so pleasant to us that we stopped there a month.'</dir>  
 <p>Now one day while I was in one of its streets, there suddenly appeared a man with a small drinking-vessel which he had placed under his arm, with a walking-stick with which he supported himself, with a tall round cap <a name="fr_279"></a><a href="#fn_279"><font size="1">2</font></a> which he had donned, <a name="fr_280"></a><a href="#fn_280"><font size="1">3</font></a> and a waist-wrapper <a name="fr_281"></a><a href="#fn_281"><font size="1">4</font></a> which he had put on. <a name="fr_282"></a><a href="#fn_282"><font size="1">5</font></a> He raised his voice and said: 'O God who createth things and causeth them to return again, the quickener of bones and the destroyer of them, the Creator of the sun and who causeth it to revolve; the Maker of the dawn to appear <a name="fr_283"></a><a href="#fn_283"><font size="1">6</font></a> and its Illuminator, who sendeth us bounteous benefits <a name="fr_284"></a><a href="#fn_284"><font size="1">7</font></a> and upholdeth the heavens <a name="fr_285"></a><a href="#fn_285"><font size="1">8</font></a> that they fall not upon us; the Creator of souls, male and female; <a name="fr_286"></a><a href="#fn_286"><font size="1">9</font></a> who hath made the sun <a name="fr_287"></a><a href="#fn_287"><font size="1">10</font></a> for a light, <a name="fr_288"></a><a href="#fn_288"><font size="1">11</font></a> the firmament for a roof <a name="fr_289"></a><a href="#fn_289"><font size="1">12</font></a> and the</p>

Said ‘Ísá ibn Hishám, I whispered to myself, 'This man is more eloquent than our al-Iskanderí, Abú’l-Fatḥ!' Then I turned a glance upon him and lo, by Heavens! it was Abú’l-Fatḥ! So I said: 'O Abú’l-Fatḥ! has thy mischief reached this land and thy hunting for game extended to this tribe?' Then he indited saying:--</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_276"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_276">50:5</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>a treasure</i>--property buried in the earth.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_277"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_277">50:6</a> <i>Adharbayján:</i> The Atropatene of the ancients the north-western and most important province of Persia. It was conquered by the Arabs under al-Mughíra in A.H. 20.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_278"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_278">51:1</a> <i>We alighted intending the stay to be three days:</i> This is an allusion to the Tradition: <font color="GREEN">…</font> Hospitality is for three days, see De Sacy, Ḥarírí, i, 177. Metre <i>táwil</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_279"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_279">51:2</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>Tall round cap:</i> The kind of head-dress called <i>Qalansuwah</i> of a Qáḍí said to be like a <font color="GREEN">…</font> a wine-jar, because high and round.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_280"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_280">51:3</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>donned</i>, the <font color="GREEN">…</font> a kind of head-dress which, according to Dozy, was worn by the ‘Abbásid Khalífas, their ministers, and the Qáḍís, and is still used in Syria.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_281"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_281">51:4</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>a waist-wrapper</i>, a kind of striped Indian cloth unsewn. The commentator says it is arabicized from a Sindhí word.</p>  
 <p>Cf. the mediaeval Latin <i>Calantica</i>, tegumentum capitis ad usum mulierum. A covering for the head of women, a kind of veil.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_282"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_282">51:5</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>He had put on:</i> Literally, he attired himself with a <i>Tailisan</i>, a cloak or mantle. Cf. Hebrew ‏פלית‎ a cover or cloak similar to the Roman pallium, especially the <i>Talith</i> the cloak of honour, the scholar or officer's distinction, adorned with fringes. Also the cloak of the leader in prayer (Jastrow, <i>Dictionary of the Targum</i>, p. 537). It is still worn by many of the professional and learned men in Muslim countries.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_283"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_283">51:6</a> <i>The Maker of the dawn to appear:</i> Qur’án, vi, 96.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_284"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_284">51:7</a> <i>Bounteous benefits:</i> An allusion to Qur’án, xxxi, 19,</p>
 <p><a name="fn_285"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_285">51:8</a> <i>Who upholdeth the heavens:</i> Qur’án, xxii, 64.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_286"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_286">51:9</a> <i>Male and female:</i> Literally, in pairs.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_287"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_287">51:10</a> <i>The sun:</i> <font color="GREEN">…</font> Literally, a lamp or its lighted wick. The latter is the proper, though not the more usual meaning, and is the one intended in Qur’án, xxiv, 35.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_288"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_288">51:11</a> <i>Who hath made the sun for a light:</i> An allusion to Qur’án, lxxi, 15.</p>  
 <p><font color="GREEN">…</font> Light, a lamp, apparently arabicized from the Syriac <i>Shirágá</i>, Persian <i>chiragh</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_289"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_289">51:12</a> <i>The firmament for a roof:</i> An allusion to Qur’án, xxi, 33.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_290"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_290">52:1</a> <i>The earth as a carpet:</i> an illusion to Qur’án, ii, 20.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_291"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_291">52:2</a> <i>The night for rest:</i> an allusion to Qur’án, vi, 96.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_292"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_292">52:3</a> <i>The day for labour:</i> an allusion to Qur’án, lxxviii. 11.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_293"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_293">52:4</a> <i>Who formeth the pregnant clouds:</i> an allusion to Qur’án, xiii, 13.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_294"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_294">52:5</a> <i>And sendeth in vengeance the thunderbolts:</i> an allusion to Qur’án, xiii, 14. This prayer is composed in the style of Qur’án, lxxi, 13-19.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_295"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_295">52:6</a> <i>I may rein her round:</i> Literally, I may turn her rope.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_296"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_296">52:7</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>Of pure origin:</i> Literally, purity hath raised him.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_297"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_297">52:8</a> <i>I am a mighty wanderer:</i> Metre, <i>Khafif</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_298"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_298">52:9</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>Toy:</i> A kind of whirling plaything which a boy turns round by means of a thread causing it to make a sound such as is termed <font color="GREEN">…</font> a small piece of wood in the middle of which is cut a notch and which is then tied with a string which, being pulled, turns round and is heard to make a sound such as is termed Imr al-Qais likens it to a swift horse. See Lyall's edition of the <i>Mu‘allaqát</i>, p. 23, verse 59.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_299"></a><a href="mhm16.htm#fr_299">52:10</a> <font color="GREEN">…</font> <i>Continually on the road</i>. Cf. <font color="GREEN">…</font> The Jinn that inhabit houses.</p>  
 <p>This maqáma has been translated by De Sacy. See his <i>Chrestomathie Arabe</i>, iii, 253.</p>
