 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Diwan of Zeb-un-Nissa</i>, by Magan Lal and Jessie Duncan Westbrook, [1913], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  
 <p><a name="page_6"><font size="1" color="green">p. 6</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">Thy pleasaunce, Princess, now is desolate; <br> Where once the gleaming water-courses traced<br> Their paths among the cypresses, a waste<br> Stretches beyond thy ruined garden-gate;<br> The rose is dead, the bulbul flown away, <br> And Zeb-un-Nissa but a memory.  
 <p>But where the rapt faquirs God's praises tell,<br> Where at the shrine the pious pilgrims meet,<br> Thy verses, Makhfi, holy tongues repeat,<br> Thy name is honoured and remembered well:<br> For through thy words they win a fleeting gleam<br> Of the Divine Belovèd of their dream.</p>  
 <p>So might we, even in an alien tongue,<br> Bring from thy mystic garden, where, apart,<br> Thou dwelt communing with thy burning heart,<br> These echoes of the songs that thou hast sung,<br> And catch thy vision of the Soul's Desire,<br> The immortal Phœnix with its wings of fire.<br>                                           J. D. W.</p></div>
