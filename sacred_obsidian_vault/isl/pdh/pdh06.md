 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>Poems from the Divan of Hafiz</i>, by Getrude Lowthian Bell, [1897], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h2 align="center">IV</h2>  
 <p>SLEEP on thine eyes, bright as narcissus flowers,<br> Falls not in vain<br> And not in vain thy hair's soft radiance showers<br> Ah, not in vain!</p>  
 <p>Before the milk upon thy lips was dry,<br> I said: "Lips where the salt of wit doth lie,<br> Sweets shall be mingled with thy mockery,<br> And not in vain!"</p>  
 <p>Thy mouth the fountain where Life's waters flow,<br> A dimpled well of tears is set below,<br> And death lies near to life thy lovers know,<br> But know in vain!</p>  
 <p>God send to thee great length of happy days<br> Lo, not for his own life thy servant prays;<br> Love's dart in thy bent brows the Archer lays,<br> Nor shoots in vain.</p>  
 <p>Art thou with grief afflicted, with the smart<br> Of absence, and is bitter toil thy part?<br> Thy lamentations and thy tears, oh Heart,<br> Are not in vain</p>  
 <p>Last night the wind from out her village blew,<br> And wandered all the garden alleys through,<br> Oh rose, tearing thy bosom's robe in two;<br> Twas not in vain!</p>  
 <p>And Hafiz, though thy heart within thee dies,<br> Hiding love's agony from curious eyes,<br> Ah, not in vain thy tears, not vain thy sighs,<br> Not all in vain!</p>
