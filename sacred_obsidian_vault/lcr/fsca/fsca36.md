 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>Fictitious and Symbolic Creatures in Art</i>, by John Vinycomb, [1909], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">Other Varieties of the Griffin</h3>  
 <p>Two other varieties of the griffin family, the "<i>Hippogriff</i>" and the "<i>Simoorgh</i>" appear in the highly wrought imaginings of the poets, and may here be very briefly alluded to. They do not, however, appear in British <a href="errata.htm#3">Heraldry</a>.</p>  
 <p><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Hippogryph</span>, or <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Hippogrif</span>, the winged horse whose father was a griffin and mother a filly (Greek, <i>hippos</i>, a horse, and <i>gryps</i>, a griffin)—a symbol of love. <a name="fr_16"></a><a href="#fn_16"><font size="1">*</font></a></p>  
 <p><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Simoorgh</span>, a sort of griffin or hippogryph, which took some of its breast feathers for Tahmura's helmet. This creature forms a very striking figure in the epic poems of Saadi and Ferdusi, the Persian poets.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_162"><font size="1" color="green">p. 162</font></a></p>  
 <p>Milton also makes allusion to this mythical creature:</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px"><small>"So saying he caught him up, and without wing<br>  Of hippogrif, bore through the air sublime<br>  Over the wilderness and o’er the plain."<br>                               <i>Paradise Regained</i>, iv.</small></div>
