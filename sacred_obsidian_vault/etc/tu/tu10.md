 <p><a name="page_59"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 59</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">SECTION VIII.</h3>  <h4 align="center">INÔ AND MELIKERTES.</h4>  
 <p>IN the myth of Inô and Melikertes we see no longer opposition between Day and Night, Sun and Moon, but kosmic harmony, the crescent-moon-goddess with the young sun in her arms. Inô, the daughter of Kadmos the 'Easterner,' <a name="fr_243"></a><a href="#fn_243"><font size="1">1</font></a> is married to Athamas, 'in Ionic Tammas,' <a name="fr_244"></a><a href="#fn_244"><font size="1">2</font></a> the Phoenician Tammuz, <a name="fr_245"></a><a href="#fn_245"><font size="1">3</font></a> the Akkadian Dumuzi, 'the Only-son,' <i>i.e.</i>, the solitary Sun-god, Melqarth, who goes forth to hunt alone. <a name="fr_246"></a><a href="#fn_246"><font size="1">4</font></a> By him she becomes the mother of Melikertes, the Phoenician Melqarth, or 'City-king;' his reduplicationthe sun of the next day; and when the raging AthamasHerakles Mainomenosin madness slays his eldest child by Inô, the latter with the infant Melikertes, leaps into the sea, and is subsequently known as Leukotheê, 'the White-goddess.' The obscure name Inô is probably a variant of Iuno, Juno, and from being a phase of Hêrê, 'the Gleaming-heaven,' she becomes the Queen-of-heaven, Lebhânâ, 'the Pale-shiner,'</p>

 <p>the White-moon-goddess, the horned Astartê, and as such she assists the storm-tossed Odysseus with her headband (<i>Kredemnon</i>), a moon-scarf of the lunar rays. <a name="fr_247"></a><a href="#fn_247"><font size="1">1</font></a> Such is 'Inô with-beautiful-ankle,' 'the moon walking in brightness,' whose kindly unicorn-horn drives away noxious things; the fostering mother who, like a Juno Matuta, nurtures the young Sun-god Dionysos, who is identical with Melqarth (Melek, Molekh), after the death of his own mother Semelê, <a name="fr_248"></a><a href="#fn_248"><font size="1">2</font></a> he being the chief of 'the precious things put forth by the moon.' Not far from the Phoenician settlement in Kythera <a name="fr_249"></a><a href="#fn_249"><font size="1">3</font></a> was 'a temple and oracle of Inô. They prophesy when asleep, since the goddess answers those who consult her by dreams. Water, pleasant to drink, flows from a sacred fount, and they call it the Fount of the Moon.' <a name="fr_250"></a><a href="#fn_250"><font size="1">4</font></a> According to an MS. Neo-Platonik Commentary of Olympiodoros on the <i>Phaidôn</i>, 'Inô is water, being marine.'
