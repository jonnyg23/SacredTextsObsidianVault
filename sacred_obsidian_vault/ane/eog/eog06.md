 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>The Epic of Gilgamish</i>, tr. by R. Campbell Thompson [1928], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">THE FOURTH TABLET.</h3>  <h4 align="center">THE ARRIVAL AT THE GATE OF THE FOREST.</h4>  
 <p>(<i>Of Column I about ll. </i>1-36<i> are mutilated or missing, there being actually the beginnings of only sixteen lines. When the text becomes connected the heroes have reached the Gate of the Forest</i>).</p>  
 <p align="center"><i>Column I</i>.</p>  
 <p align="center">(<i>Enkidu addresses the Gate</i>).</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">36.</font></span> <a name="fr_68"></a><a href="#fn_62"><font size="1">1</font></a>Enkidu lifted [his eyes] . . . and spake with the Gate as [t were human(?)]:<br> "O thou Gate of the Forest without understanding(? . . .<br> Sentience which thou hast not, . . . .</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_27"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 27</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">40.</font></span>I for (full) forty leagues have admired thy [wonderful] timber,<br> (Aye), till I sighted the towering Cedar . . . .<br> (O but) thy wood hath no peer (in the country) . . .<br> Six <i>gar</i> thy height, and two <i>gar</i> thy breadth . . .<br> <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">45.</font></span>(Sooth, but) thy stanchion (?), thy socket (?), <a name="fr_69"></a><a href="#fn_63"><font size="1">1</font></a> thy pivot (?), thy lock (?), and thy shutter (?),<br> [(All of them) must have been fashiond for thee] in the City of Nippur!<br> O, if I had but known, O Gate, that this was [thy grandeur],<br> This, too, the grace [of thy structure], then either an axe had I lifted<br> <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">50.</font></span>Or I had . . . or bound together . . . ."</p>  
 <p>(<i>Of the next Column remains a fragment, and that only presumed to belong to one of the above fragments from its appearance, which speaks of terror, a dream, and sorrow: "let me pray the gods . . . . may thy? god be . . . the father of the gods." Again, of the third Column there is only a small portion left of the right half </i>(<i>this fragment, too, being also presumed to belong to the same tablet as that above-mentioned</i>)<i>, speaking of Gilgamish, the Forest, and Enkidu. The fourth Column is entirely lost. Of Column V the latter part survives, in this case without any uncertainty. After a few broken lines it runs as follows, the first speaker being probably Enkidu, and the scene the Gate of the Forest</i>):</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">6.</font></span>". . . [O, haste] thee, withstand him, he will not [pursue(?) thee],<br> [We will] go on down into the wood not daunted, together (?)].<br> . . . Thou shall put on seven garments ..<br> . . . putting on, and six . . . (?) . . . "<br> <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">10.</font></span>He like a mighty wild bull . . .<br> Flung he the Portal afar, and [his] mouth was filld (with his challenge),<br> Cried to the Guard of the Forest: "Up (?) . . . !<br> [Tis I will challenge] Humbaba like to a . . ."</p>  
 <p align="center">(<i>A small gap</i>.)</p>  
 <p align="center"><i>Column VI</i>.</p>  
 <p align="center">(<i>Enkidu is speaking</i>)</p>  
 <p>"Trouble (?) [I foresee(?)] wherever I go . . . .<br> <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">5.</font></span>O my friend, I have [seen] <a name="fr_70"></a><a href="#fn_64"><font size="1">2</font></a> a dream which un- . . . ."<br> The day of the dream he had seen fulfilled . . . . <a name="fr_71"></a><a href="#fn_65"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>

 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_28"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 28</font></a></p>  
 <p>(Enkidu is stricken with fear at thought of the combat).<br> Enkidu lay for a day, [yea, a second]for Enkidu [lying]<br> <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">10.</font></span>(Prone) on his couch, was a third and a fourth day . . ., a fifth, sixth and seventh,<br> Eighth, ninth, [and tenth]. While Enkidu [lay in his] sickness . . ., th eleventh,<br> (Aye, till) the twelfth . . . on [his] couch was Enkidu [lying].<br> <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">15.</font></span>Calld he to Gilgamish, . . . . . . . . .<br> "(O but), my comrade, . . . hateth me . . because within Erech<br> I was afraid of the combat, and . . . My friend, who in battle . . ."</p>  
 <p align="center">(<i>A small gap in which Gilgamish has answered. Enkidu replies</i>):</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">26.</font></span>[Enkidu opend] his [mouth] and spake [unto Gilgamish, saying]:<br> ("Nay, but), [my friend, let us no wise] go down [to the depths of the Forest],<br> (For) tis my hands [have grown weak], and [my arms] are stricken with palsy."</p>  
 <p align="center">________________________________</p>  
 <p>Gilgamish opend his mouth and spake [unto Enkidu], saying:<br> <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">30.</font></span>"Shall we, O friend, [play] the coward? . . . . . .<br> . . . . thou shalt surpass them all(?) . . . .<br> [Thou, O] my friend, art cunning in warfare, art [shrewd(?)] in the battle,<br> (So) shalt thou touch the . . . and of [death] have no terror,</p>  
 <p align="center">(<i>Two difficult and mutilated lines</i>).</p>  
 <p>[So that] the palsy (now striking) thine arms [may] depart, and the weakness<br> Pass [from thy hands]! [Be brave(?)] and resist! O my comrade, together<br> We will go downlet the combat [in no wise diminish(?)] thy courage!<br> <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">40.</font></span>O forget death, and be fearful(?) of nothing(?) . . (for he who is) [valiant(?)],<br> Cautious (and) careful, by leading [the way] hath his own body guarded,<br> (He tis) will safeguard a comrade."<br>                                    A name by their [valour(?)] . .<br> They will establish. (And now) they together arrive at the barrier(?),<br> [Stilld into silence(?)] their speech, and they themselves (suddenly) stopping.</p>
