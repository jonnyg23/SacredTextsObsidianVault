 <p align="center"><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Number 2</span></p>  
 <p>This is a letter containing a complaint to the king concerning some gold which seems to have been missing. The text is numbered K 538 in the National Collection.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_184"><font size="1" color="green">p. 184</font></a></p>  
 <p>To the king my lord, thy servant Arad-Nabû. May there be peace to the king my lord; may the gods <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Assur</span>, <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Samas</span>, <a name="fr_523"></a><a href="#fn_520"><font size="1">1</font></a> <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Bel</span>, <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Zirpanitum</span>, <a name="fr_524"></a><a href="#fn_521"><font size="1">2</font></a> <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Nabû</span>, <a name="fr_525"></a><a href="#fn_522"><font size="1">3</font></a> <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Tasmetum</span>, <a name="fr_526"></a><a href="#fn_523"><font size="1">4</font></a> <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Istar</span> of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Nineveh</span> <a name="fr_527"></a><a href="#fn_524"><font size="1">5</font></a> (and) <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Istar</span> of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Arbela</span>, <a name="fr_528"></a><a href="#fn_525"><font size="1">6</font></a> these great gods, lovers of thy rule, let the king my lord live for a hundred years. May they satisfy the king my lord with old age and offspring.</p>  
 <p>The gold which, in the month Tisri, the <i>ittu</i>, the prefect of the palace, and I with them, missed—2 talents of standard gold (and) 6 talents of gold not standard—(this gold) the hands of the <i>rab-danibe</i> <a name="fr_529"></a><a href="#fn_526"><font size="1">7</font></a> placed in the house, he sealed it up, (and) the gold for the image of the kings <a name="fr_530"></a><a href="#fn_527"><font size="1">8</font></a> and for the image of the king's mother he gave not. Let the king my lord give command to the <i>ittu</i> (and) the prefect of the palace, that they may discover the gold. The beginning of the month is good. <a name="fr_531"></a><a href="#fn_528"><font size="1">9</font></a> Let them give it to the men. Let them do the work. <a name="fr_532"></a><a href="#fn_529"><font size="1">10</font></a></p>  
 <p> </p>

Since that time the text itself, with a translation, has been published by the Rev. S. A. Smith in his <i>Keilschrifttexte Asurbanipals</i> (Heft II, plate 7, and pp. 30–33); and I also contributed</p>

 <p>to the same work (p. 86), a "free" translation in English, which does not essentially differ from that given above. These translations are much better than that which I gave at first, the improvements being due to the advances which have been made in the science of Assyriology since that was published.</p>  
 <p>The principal difference in the translation occurs in the second part, this difference being caused by translating the word <i>nikhidhuni</i> by "we missed," instead of "sinned" or "transgressed." It is unlikely that a man would voluntarily accuse himself of being a thief, hence this rendering. The meaning of "to miss," however, attached to this root, occurs in Hebrew, Job v. 24, "thou shalt visit thy fold and <i>shalt miss</i> nothing," <a name="fr_534"></a><a href="#fn_531"><font size="1">1</font></a> so that the meaning here proposed for the word may be regarded as quite certain.</p>  
 <p>Another text referring to the making of images will be found in S. A. Smith's <i>Keilschrifttexte Asurbanipals</i>, Heft III, plates 12–13, and pp. 39–43.</p>
