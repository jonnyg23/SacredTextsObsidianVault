 <p><a name="fn_262"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_262">113:2</a> Or perhaps Abu-A, like the eponym of B.C. 887.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_263"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_263">113:3</a> According to George Smith.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_264"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_264">113:4</a> Or Tiglath-Baru. He is the second king of the name known to us.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_265"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_265">113:5</a> Not Malik. For the god or goddess A, the wife of the Sun-god, see my Hibbert Lectures on <i>The Religion of the Ancient Babylonians</i>, pp 177 <i>sqq</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_266"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_266">113:6</a> The Biblical Elimelech, "El is Moloch."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_267"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_267">113:7</a> "O Assur save me!"</p>
 <p><a name="fn_268"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_268">113:8</a> The reading of the name is doubtful. It is differently written in the <i>Annals of Assur-natsir-pal</i>, ii. 49. Perhaps it should be pronounced Belaku.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_269"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_269">114:1</a> "May the priest of Assur live long!"</p>
 <p><a name="fn_270"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_270">114:2</a> Also given as Samas-yubla.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_271"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_271">114:3</a> Also written Bir-Raman (Bir-Rimmon).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_272"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_272">114:4</a> Or perhaps Ner-Istar.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_273"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_273">114:5</a> "(Born) in the month Elul."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_274"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_274">114:6</a> Shalmaneser was twice eponym.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_275"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_275">114:7</a> "The Sun-god is Rimmon," like the name of Hadad- Rimmon, "Hadad is Rimmon," in Zech. xii. 11.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_276"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_276">115:1</a> The ideograph <i>khal</i> represents <i>asâpu</i>, "to prophesy" or "divine." See the name of the eponym for B.C. 670.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_277"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_277">115:2</a> According to other lists, Nebo-sarra-utsur. The proper eponym of the year may have died during his term of office, and a supplementary eponym appointed in his place.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_278"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_279">115:3</a> Omitted in the Chronicle.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_279"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_280">115:4</a> Shalmaneser III.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_280"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_281">115:5</a> "The Sun-god is El" or "god," like Jiphthah-el in Josh. xix. 14, or the Palestinian town of Ya’aqab-el ("Jacob is El," ? Hebron) and Yesephel ("Joseph is El"), mentioned by the Egyptian king Thothmes III.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_281"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_282">115:6</a> Or more probably Pan-Assur-la’mur, "I see not the face of Assur;" cf. Exodus xxxiii. 20.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_282"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_283">116:1</a> "(He is) my son."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_283"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_284">116:2</a> <i>I.e.</i> Zadkiel, Comp. the Hebrew name Zedekiah.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_284"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_285">116:3</a> Also written Beli-taggil, "he trusts in Bel."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_285"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_286">116:4</a> Also written Qi’su.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_286"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_287">116:5</a> Also written Assur-bela-yukin.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_287"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_288">116:6</a> The line is drawn here by List IV.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_288"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_289">116:7</a> The line is drawn here by Lists II and III. Probably Tiglath-pileser III seized the crown in B.C. 745, but was not universally recognised as king until B.C. 743.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_289"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_290">116:8</a> "O Bel of Harran (Genesis xi. 31) protect the lord."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_290"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_291">117:1</a> The line is drawn here by List III.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_291"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_292">117:2</a> The name of "Sennacherib the king" is inserted here in List II. In List IV the dividing-line is drawn after the name of Yupakhirra-Belu, and is followed by the name of Sennacherib.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_292"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_293">117:3</a> "He who belongs to the god of fertility," who was the god of Andakhu according to W. A. I., v. 16, 38.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_293"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_294">117:4</a> Sin-akhi-erba "the Moon-god has increased the brethren." In List III the name is written by error Assur-akhi-[erba] and a line is drawn both before and after it.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_294"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_295">117:5</a> Nabu-sarra-utsur, "O Nebo protect the king!"</p>
 <p><a name="fn_295"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_296">117:6</a> "The father (Bel) is exalted": the name is identical with the Biblical Abram.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_296"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_297">118:1</a> "Atar is El." Atar or Athar, as Schrader has shown, was the name of the goddess of the North Arabian tribe of Kedar, and enters into that of Atar-samain or "Athar of heaven" mentioned by Assur-bani-pal.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_297"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_298">118:2</a> "Born in the month of Tebet."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_298"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_299">118:3</a> The date is taken from George Smith.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_299"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_300">118:4</a> "The Arbelite."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_300"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_301">118:5</a> List I. ends here. The names which follow are derived from List III.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_301"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_302">118:6</a> Assigned to the year B.C. 656 by George Smith.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_302"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_303">118:7</a> List III ends here. The names which follow are derived by George Smith from various dated documents.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_303"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_304">119:1</a> "Rimmon have mercy on me," Barku or Barqu, "the lightning," the Hebrew Baraq, being a name of Rimmon.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_304"></a><a href="rp20227.htm#fr_305">119:2</a> Daddi, whose name indicates his Syrian origin, was eponym in the reign of Sin-sar-iskun, one of the last kings of Assyria.</p>
