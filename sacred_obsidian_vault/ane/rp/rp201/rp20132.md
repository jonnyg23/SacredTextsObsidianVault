 <p><a name="page_134"><font size="1" color="green">p. 134</font></a></p>  <h4 align="center">THIRD TABLET OF THE STORY OF THE CREATION</h4>  
 <p align="center"><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Obverse</span></p>  
 <p>17. "The gods have marched round her, <a name="fr_262"></a><a href="#fn_261"><font size="1">1</font></a> all of them;<br> 18. up to those whom thou hast created at her side I have gone."<br> 19. When they were <i>gathered</i> (?) beside her, <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Tiamat</span> they approached.<br> 20. The strong one (<span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Merodach</span>), the glorious, who desists not night or day,<br> 21. the exciter to battle, was disturbed in heart.<br> 22. Then they marshalled (their) forces; they create darkness.<br> 23. "The mother of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Khubur</span>, <a name="fr_263"></a><a href="#fn_262"><font size="1">2</font></a> the creatress of them all,<br> 24. I pursued with (my) weapons unsurpassed; (then) did the great snake(s) bite. <a name="fr_264"></a><a href="#fn_263"><font size="1">3</font></a><br> 25. With my teeth sharpened unsparingly did I bite.<br> 26. With poisoned breath like blood their bodies I filled.<br> 27. The raging vampires <a name="fr_265"></a><a href="#fn_264"><font size="1">4</font></a> I clothed with terror.<br> 28. I lifted up the lightning-flash, on high I launched (it). <a name="fr_266"></a><a href="#fn_265"><font size="1">5</font></a><br> 29. Their messenger <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Sar-baba</span> ……<br> 30. Their bodies were struck, but it pierced not their breasts.<br> 31. I made ready the dragon, the mighty serpent and the god <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Lakha(ma)</span>,</p>

 <p>32. the great reptile, the deadly beast and the scorpion-man, <a name="fr_267"></a><a href="#fn_266"><font size="1">1</font></a><br> 33. the devouring <a name="fr_268"></a><a href="#fn_267"><font size="1">2</font></a> reptiles, the fish-man <a name="fr_269"></a><a href="#fn_266"><font size="1">1</font></a> and the gazelle-god, <a name="fr_270"></a><a href="#fn_268"><font size="1">3</font></a><br> 34. lifting up (my) weapons that spare not, fearless of battle,<br> 35. strong through the law which (yields?) not before the foe.<br> 36.  <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_261"></a><a href="rp20132.htm#fr_262">134:1</a> <i>I’skhuru-si</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_262"></a><a href="rp20132.htm#fr_263">134:2</a> Khubur is identified with ’Su-edin on the eastern side of the Babylonian plain in W.A.I., ii. 50, 51. Professor Delitzsch suggests that the expression <i>ummu Khubur</i> may be the origin of the name Omorôka assigned by Berôssos to Tiamat.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_263"></a><a href="rp20132.htm#fr_264">134:3</a> <i>Ittaqur</i> from <i>naqaru</i>. In Hebrew the verb is used especially of piercing the eyes.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_264"></a><a href="rp20132.htm#fr_265">134:4</a> The <i>usumgalli</i> or "solitary monsters" were fabulous beasts who were supposed to devour the corpses of the dead, and were therefore not exactly vampires which devoured the living, but corresponded rather with one of the creatures mentioned in Is. xiii. 21, 22; xxxiv. 14.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_265"></a><a href="rp20132.htm#fr_266">134:5</a> <i>Umtas</i>[<i>sir</i>].</p>
 <p><a name="fn_266"></a><a href="rp20132.htm#fr_269">135:1</a> According to the 9th tablet of the Epic of Gisdhubar, "the scorpion-men" guard the gate between "the twin mountains" through which the sun passes at its rising and setting. The fish-man was Oannes, afterwards identified with Ea, who brought wisdom and culture to Chaldæa out of the Persian Gulf.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_267"></a><a href="rp20132.htm#fr_268">135:2</a> <i>Dapruti</i> (see W.A.I., v. 16, 80) from the same root as <i>diparatu</i>, "a flame."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_268"></a><a href="rp20132.htm#fr_270">135:3</a> The gazelle-god was identified by the later mythology of Babylonia, sometimes with Ea the god of Eridu, sometimes with Bel the god of Nipur: see my <i>Lectures on the Religion of the Ancient Babylonians</i>, pp. 283 <i>seq</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_269"></a><a href="rp20132.htm#fr_271">135:4</a> Kingu was the husband of Tiamat.</p>
