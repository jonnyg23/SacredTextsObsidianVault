 <p>1. Their spoil, their goods (and) their property<br> 2. I carried away. Their cities with fire<br> 3. I burned, I threw down (and) dug up,<br> 4. I reduced to mounds and ruins.<br> 5. Large troops of horses,<br> 6. mules, calves, and the possessions<br> 7. of their homesteads to a countless number<br> 8. I brought back. All the kings<br> 9. of the countries of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Nairi</span> alive my hand<br> 10. captured. To those kings<br> 11. I extended mercy, and<br> 12. spared their lives. Their captivity<br> 13. and their bondage in the presence of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Samas</span> my lord<br> 14. I liberated, and an oath by my great<br> 15. gods <a name="fr_225"></a><a href="#fn_224"><font size="1">1</font></a> unto future days for ever<br> 16. and ever that they should be (my) servants I made them swear.<br> 17. The children, the offspring of their kingdom,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_108"><font size="1" color="green">p. 108</font></a></p>  
 <p>18. as hostages I took.<br> 19. Twelve hundred horses (and) 2000 oxen<br> 20. I imposed upon them as tribute.<br> 21. In their countries I left them.</p>  
 <p align="center">_________</p>  
 <p>22. ’Siena king of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Dayaeni</span>,<br> 23. who did not submit to <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Asur</span> my lord,<br> 24. captive and bound to my city<br> 25. of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Asur</span> I brought; mercy<br> 26. I extended to him, and from my city of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Asur</span>,<br> 27. as the exalter of the great gods<br> 28. unto exaltation, alive<br> 29. I let him depart. The lands of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Nairi</span>,<br> 30. far-extending, I subdued throughout their whole extent,<br> 31. and all their kings<br> 32. I reduced beneath my feet.</p>  
 <p align="center">_________</p>  
 <p>33. In the course of the same campaign<br> 34. against the city of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Milidia</span>, <a name="fr_226"></a><a href="#fn_225"><font size="1">1</font></a> of the country of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Khani</span> <a name="fr_227"></a><a href="#fn_226"><font size="1">2</font></a> the great,<br> 35. violent (and) unsubmissive, I marched.<br> 36. The mighty onset of my battle they feared.<br> 37. My feet they took; I had mercy on them.<br> 38. The city itself I did not capture; their hostages<br> 39. I accepted. A homer by way of tax of lead<br> 40. as an annual tribute<br> 41. not to be intermitted I imposed upon them.</p>  
 <p align="center">_________</p>  
 <p>42. Tiglath-pileser, the destroyer, the quick-moving,<br> 43. the implacable, the deluge of battle.</p>

 <p>44. In the service of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Asur</span> my lord, my chariots<br> 45. and warriors I took. In the desert</p>

 <p>46. I made (my way). To the bank of the waters<br> 47. of the land of the <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Armayans</span>, <a name="fr_228"></a><a href="#fn_227"><font size="1">1</font></a> the enemies of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Asur</span> my lord,<br> 48. I marched. From opposite to the land of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">’Sukhi</span>, <a name="fr_229"></a><a href="#fn_228"><font size="1">2</font></a><br> 49. as far as the city of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Gargamis</span>, <a name="fr_230"></a><a href="#fn_229"><font size="1">3</font></a> of the land of the <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Hittites</span> (<i>Khatti</i>),<br> 50. in one day I plundered.<br> 51. Their soldiers I slew. Their spoil,<br> 52. their goods and their possessions<br> 53. to a countless number I carried back.<br> 54. The remains of their armies,<br> 55. who before the powerful (weapons) of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Asur</span> my lord<br> 56. had fled and had crossed the <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Euphrates</span>,<br> 57. after them in vessels of <i>inflated</i> (?) skins <a name="fr_231"></a><a href="#fn_230"><font size="1">4</font></a><br> 58. I crossed the <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Euphrates</span>;<br> 59. six of their cities which (were) at the foot of Mount <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Bisri</span> <a name="fr_232"></a><a href="#fn_231"><font size="1">5</font></a><br> 60. I captured; with fire I burned,<br> 61. I threw down (and) dug up. Their spoil, their goods<br> 62. and their possessions to my city of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Asur</span><br> 63. I brought.</p>

 <p>68. urged me, and between the mountains of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Elamuni</span><br> 69. <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Tala </span>and <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Kharu’sa</span> I made (my way).<br> 70. I conquered the land of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Mu’sri</span> throughout its circuit,<br> 71. I massacred their warriors.<br> 72. The cities I burned with fire, I threw down,<br> 73. I dug up. The armies of the land of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Qumanî</span><br> 74. to the help of the land of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Mu’sri</span><br> 75. had gone. On a mountain with them<br> 76. I fought. A destruction of them I made.<br> 77. To a single city, <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Arini</span>, at the foot of mount <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Ai’sa</span>,<br> 78. I drove and shut them up. My feet<br> 79. they took. The city itself I spared.<br> 80. Hostages, tribute and offering<br> 81. I laid upon them.</p>

 <p>82. In those days all the land of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Qumanî</span>,<br> 83. which had prepared to help <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Mu’sri</span>,<br> 84. gathered together all those countries, and<br> 85. to make conflict and battle<br> 86. were determined. With the violence of my powerful weapons,<br> 87. with 20,000 of their numerous troops<br> 88. on mount <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Tala</span> I fought.<br> 89. A destruction of them I made.<br> 90. Their strong forces I broke through.<br> 91. As far as mount <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Kharu’sa</span>, which (is) in front of the land of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Mu’sri</span>,<br> 92. I pursued their fugitives. The bodies<br> 93. of their warriors in the ravines of the mountain<br> 94. like a moon-stone I flung to the ground.<br> 95. Their corpses over the valleys and the high places of the mountains<br> 96. I spread. Their great fortresses<br> 97. I captured, with fire I burned,</p>  
 <p><a name="page_111"><font size="1" color="green">p. 111</font></a></p>  
 <p>98. I threw down (and) dug up, so that they became mounds and ruins.<br> 99. <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Khunu’sa</span> their fortified city<br> 100. like the flood of the deluge I overwhelmed.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_224"></a><a href="rp20126.htm#fr_225">107:1</a> Literally "the bann (<i>mamit</i>) of my great gods."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_225"></a><a href="rp20126.htm#fr_226">108:1</a> The classical Melitênê, now Malatiyeh, on the Euphrates.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_226"></a><a href="rp20126.htm#fr_227">108:2</a> This district of Kappadokia is called "Khani the Great," to distinguish it from another Khani near Babylon, whose king Tukulti-mer, son of Ilu-saba, dedicated a bronze ram's head, now in the British Museum, to the temple of the Sun-god at Sippara.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_227"></a><a href="rp20126.htm#fr_228">109:1</a> The Arameans.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_228"></a><a href="rp20126.htm#fr_229">109:2</a> The Shuhites of the Old Testament, who extended along the western banks of the Euphrates from the mouth of the Khabour to above that of the Belikh. "Bildad the Shuhite" (Job ii. II) would be Bel-Dadda, Dadda, as we learn from the cuneiform inscriptions, being a form of Hadad, the Syrian name of the god of heaven.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_229"></a><a href="rp20126.htm#fr_230">109:3</a> Carchemish, the Hittite capital on the Euphrates, between the mouth of the Sajur and Birejik, now represented by the mounds of Jerablûs.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_230"></a><a href="rp20126.htm#fr_231">109:4</a> <i>Sugase</i>, borrowed from the Accadian <i>’su</i>, "skin," and <i>gavsia</i> (whence the Semitic <i>gubsu</i>).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_231"></a><a href="rp20126.htm#fr_232">109:5</a> Now Tel-Basher.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_232"></a><a href="rp20126.htm#fr_233">109:6</a> <i>Musarbibu</i>, "subduer," according to M. Amiaud, who regards the word as an example of a <i>parel</i> conjugation (<i>Revue d’Assyriologie</i>, ii. 1, p. 12).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_233"></a><a href="rp20126.htm#fr_234">109:7</a> Mu’sri or Muzri lay to the north-east of Khorsabad, in the mountainous district now inhabited by the Missouri Kurds. The tribute of a <a name="page_110"><font size="1" color="green">p. 110</font></a> rhinoceros, yak, elephant, and apes, brought by its inhabitants to Shalmaneser II, must be explained on the supposition that the caravan road from the east passed through it.</p>
