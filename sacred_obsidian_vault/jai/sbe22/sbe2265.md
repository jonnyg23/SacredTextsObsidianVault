 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>Jaina Sutras, Part II (SBE22)</i>, tr. by Hermann Jacobi, [1884], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">SECOND LESSON.</h4>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing any sort (of diseases), should not talk of them in this way: 'He has got boils, or leprosy, &amp;c. (see I, 6, 1, § 3); his hand is cut, or his foot, nose, ear, lip is cut.' For as all such people, spoken to in such language, become</p>  
 <p><a name="page_153"><font size="1" color="green">p. 153</font></a></p>  
 <p>angry, hence, considering well, they should not speak to them in such language. (1)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing any sort (of good qualities), should speak thus: 'He is strong, powerful, vigorous, famous, well-formed, well-proportioned, handsome.' For as all such people, spoken to in such language, do not become angry, they should, considering well, speak to them in such language. (2)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing any sort of such things as walls or ditches, &amp;c. (see II, 3, 2, § 14), should not speak of them in this way: This is well-executed, finely executed, beautiful, excellent, (so done) or to be done;' they should not use such sinful, &amp;c., language. (3)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing walls, &amp;c., should speak about them in this way: 'This has been executed with great effort, with sin, with much labour; it is very magnificent, it is very beautiful, it is very fine, it is very handsome;' considering well, they should use such sinless, &amp;c., language. (4)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing food, &amp;c., prepared, should not speak about it in this way: 'This is well executed, finely executed, beautiful, excellent, (so done) or to be done;' considering well, they should not use such sinful, &amp;c., language. (5)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing food, &amp;c., prepared, should speak about it in this way: 'This has been executed with great effort, with sin, with much labour; it is very good, it is excellent, it is well seasoned, it is most delicious, it is most agreeable;' considering well, they should use such sinless, &amp;c., language. (6)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing a man, a cow, a buffalo, deer, cattle, a bird, a snake, an aquatic animal of</p>  
 <p><a name="page_154"><font size="1" color="green">p. 154</font></a></p>  
 <p>increased bulk, should not speak about them in this way: 'He (or it) is fat, round, fit to be killed or cooked;' considering well, they should not use such sinful, &amp;c., language. (7)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing a man, a cow, &amp;c., of increased bulk, should speak about them in this way: 'He is of increased bulk, his body is well grown, well compacted, his flesh and blood are abundant, his limbs are fully developed;' considering well, they should use such sinless, &amp;c., language. (8)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing any sort of cows (or oxen), should not speak about them in this way: 'These cows should be milked or tamed or covered, should draw a waggon or car;' considering well, they should not use such sinful, &amp;c., language. (9)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing any sort of cows (or oxen), should speak about them in this way: 'It is a young cow, a milch cow, she gives much milk, it is a short or a large one, a beast of burden;' considering well, they should use such sinless, &amp;c., language. (10)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing big trees in parks, on hills, or in woods, should speak about them in this way: 'These (trees) are fit for palaces, gates, houses, benches, bolts, boats, buckets, stools, trays, ploughs, mattocks(?), machines, poles, the nave of a wheel(?), ga<i>n</i><i>d</i>î <a name="fr_427"></a><a href="#fn_421"><font size="1">1</font></a>, seats, beds, cars, sheds;' considering well, they should not use such sinful, &amp;c., language. (11)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing big trees in parks, on hills, or in woods, should speak about them in this way: 'These trees are noble, high and round, big;</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_155"><font size="1" color="green">p. 155</font></a></p>  
 <p>they have many branches, extended branches, they are very magnificent,' &amp;c. (see § 4); considering well, they should use such sinless, &amp;c., language. (12)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing many wild fruits, should not speak about them in this way: 'They are ripe, they should be cooked or eaten, they are just in season, or soft, or they have just split;' considering well, they should not use such sinful, &amp;c., language. (13)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing many wild fruits, should speak about them in this way: They are very plentiful, they contain many seeds, they are fully grown, they have developed their proper shape;' considering well, they should use such sinless, &amp;c., language. (14)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing many vegetables, should not speak about them in this way: 'They are ripe, they are dark coloured, shining, fit to be fried or roasted or eaten;' considering well, they should not use such sinful, &amp;c., language. (15)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, seeing many vegetables, should speak about them in this way: 'They are grown up, they are fully grown, they are strong, they are excellent, they are run to seed, they have spread their seed, they are full of sap;' considering well, they should use such sinless, &amp;c., language. (16)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, hearing any sort of sounds, should not speak about them in this way: 'This is a good sound, this is a bad sound;' considering well, they should not use such sinless, &amp;c., language; but they should call them good, if they are good; bad, if they are bad; considering well, they should use such sinless, &amp;c., language. (17)</p>  
 <p>In the same manner they should speak about the</p>  
 <p><a name="page_156"><font size="1" color="green">p. 156</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> (five) colours, as black, &amp;c.; the (two) smells, as pleasant or unpleasant; the (five) tastes, as sharp &amp;c.; the (five) kinds of touch, as hard, &amp;c. (18)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun, putting aside wrath, pride, deceit, and greed, considering well, speaking with precision, what one has heard, not too quick, with discrimination, should employ language in moderation and restraint.</p>  
 <p>This is the whole duty, &amp;c.</p>  
 <p>Thus I say. (19)</p>  
 <p align="center">End of the Fourth Lecture, called Modes of Speech.</p>
