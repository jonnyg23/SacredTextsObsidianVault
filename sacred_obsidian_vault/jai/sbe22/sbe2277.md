 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>Jaina Sutras, Part II (SBE22)</i>, tr. by Hermann Jacobi, [1884], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">THIRTEENTH LECTURE.</h3>  
 <p>One should neither be pleased with nor prohibit the action of another which relates to one's self, and produces karman.</p>  
 <p>One should neither be pleased with nor prohibit it <a name="fr_481"></a><a href="#fn_475"><font size="1">1</font></a>;</p>  
 <p>If another (i.e. a householder) wipes [or rubs] the mendicant's feet; (1)</p>  
 <p>If he kneads or strokes them; (2) If he touches or paints them; (3)</p>  
 <p>If he smears or anoints them with oil, ghee, or marrow; (4)</p>  
 <p>If he rubs or shampoos them with Lodhra, ground drugs, powder, or dye; (5)</p>  
 <p>If he sprinkles or washes them with hot or cold water; (6)</p>  
 <p>If he rubs or anoints them with any sort of ointment; (7)</p>  
 <p>If he perfumes or fumigates them with any sort of incense; (8)</p>  
 <p>If he extracts or removes a splinter or thorn from them; (9)</p>  
 <p>If he extracts or removes pus or blood from them. (10)</p>  
 <p>If he wipes or rubs the mendicant's body, &amp;c. <a name="fr_482"></a><a href="#fn_476"><font size="1">2</font></a> (see §§ 2-8 down to) if he perfumes or fumigates it with any sort of incense. (11)</p>  
 <p>If he wipes or rubs a wound in (the mendicant's)</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_187"><font size="1" color="green">p. 187</font></a></p>  
 <p>body (&amp;c. <a name="fr_483"></a><a href="#fn_477"><font size="1">1</font></a>, down to) if he sprinkles or washes it with hot or cold water; (12)</p>  
 <p>If he cuts or incises it with any sharp instrument; if after having done so, he extracts or removes pus or blood from it. (13)</p>  
 <p>If he wipes or rubs a boil, abscess, ulcer, or fistula (&amp;c. <a name="fr_484"></a><a href="#fn_477"><font size="1">1</font></a>, down to) if he cuts or incises it with any sharp instrument; if after having done so, he extracts or removes pus or blood from it; (14)</p>  
 <p>If he removes, or wipes off, the sweat and uncleanliness on his body; (15)</p>  
 <p>If he removes, or wipes off, the dirt of his eyes, ears, teeth, or nails. (16)</p>  
 <p>If he cuts or dresses the long hair of his head or his brows or his armpits; (17)</p>  
 <p>If he removes, or wipes off, the nit or lice from his head. (18)</p>  
 <p>One should neither be pleased with nor prohibit it, if the other, sitting in the Aṅka or Paryaṅka posture, wipes or rubs (the mendicant's) feet; in this way the §§ 1-18 should be repeated here. (19)</p>  
 <p>One should neither be pleased with nor prohibit it, if the other, sitting in the Aṅka or Paryaṅka posture, fastens or ties a necklace of many or less strings, a necklace hanging down over the breast, a collar, a diadem, a garland, a golden string; (20)</p>  
 <p>If the other leading him to, or treating him in, a garden or a park, wipes or rubs (the mendicant's) feet, &amp;c. (all as above); similarly with actions done reciprocally. (21)</p>  
 <p>One should neither be pleased with nor prohibit it, if the other tries to cure him by pure charms;</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_188"><font size="1" color="green">p. 188</font></a></p>  
 <p>If the other tries to cure him by impure charms;</p>  
 <p>If he tries to cure him, digging up and cutting, for the sake of a sick monk, living bulbs, roots, rind, or sprouts. (22)</p>  
 <p>For sensation is the result of former actions; all sorts of living beings experience sensation.</p>  
 <p>This is the whole duty, &amp;c.</p>  
 <p>Thus I say. (23)</p>
