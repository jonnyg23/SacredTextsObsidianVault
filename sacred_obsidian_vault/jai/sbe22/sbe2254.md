 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>Jaina Sutras, Part II (SBE22)</i>, tr. by Hermann Jacobi, [1884], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h4 align="center">EIGHTH LESSON.</h4>  
 <p>A monk or a nun on a begging-tour should not accept juice of mangos, inspissated juice of mangos, juice of wood-apples, citrons, grapes, wild dates, pomegranates, cocoa-nuts, bamboos, jujubes, myrobalans, tamarinds, or any such-like liquor containing particles of the shell or skin or seeds, which liquor the layman, for the sake of the mendicant, pressed, strained, or filtered through a basket <a name="fr_360"></a><a href="#fn_354"><font size="1">1</font></a>, cloth, or a cow's tail; for such liquor is impure and unacceptable. (1)</p>  
 <p>When a monk or a nun on a begging-tour smells, in travellers' houses or garden houses or householders' houses or ma<i>th</i>s, the scent of food or drink or sweet scents, they should not smell them, being indifferent against smell, and not eager or desirous or greedy or covetous of the pleasant smell. (2)</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_109"><font size="1" color="green">p. 109</font></a></p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun on a begging-tour should not accept raw things which are not yet modified by instruments <a name="fr_361"></a><a href="#fn_355"><font size="1">1</font></a>, as bulbous roots, growing in water or dry ground, mustard stalks; for they are impure and unacceptable. The same holds good with regard to long pepper, ground long pepper, common pepper, ground common pepper, ginger or ground ginger. (3)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun on a begging-tour should not accept such raw fruits which are not yet modified by instruments, as those of Mango, Amrâ<i>t</i>aka, <i>Gh</i>i<i>g</i><i>gh</i>îrâ <a name="fr_362"></a><a href="#fn_356"><font size="1">2</font></a>, Surabhi <a name="fr_363"></a><a href="#fn_357"><font size="1">3</font></a>, Sallakî <a name="fr_364"></a><a href="#fn_358"><font size="1">4</font></a>; for they, &amp;c. (4)</p>  
 <p>The same holds good with regard to raw shoots which, &amp;c., as those of A<i>s</i>vattha, Nyagrodha, Pila<i>m</i>khu <a name="fr_365"></a><a href="#fn_359"><font size="1">5</font></a>, Nîyûra <a name="fr_366"></a><a href="#fn_360"><font size="1">6</font></a>, Sallakî. (5)</p>  
 <p>The same holds good with regard to raw berries which, &amp;c., as those of Kapittha <a name="fr_367"></a><a href="#fn_361"><font size="1">7</font></a>, pomegranate, or Pippala. (6)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun on a begging-tour should not accept raw, powdered fruits which are not well ground and still contain small seeds, as those of Umbara, Pila<i>m</i>khu, Nyagrodha, and A<i>s</i>vattha; for &amp;c. (7)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun on a begging-tour should not accept unripe wild rice <a name="fr_368"></a><a href="#fn_362"><font size="1">8</font></a>, dregs, honey, liquor, ghee, or sediments of liquor, if these things be old or if living beings are engendered or grow or thrive in</p>

 <p>

 <p><a name="page_110"><font size="1" color="green">p. 110</font></a></p>  
 <p>them, or are not taken out, or killed or destroyed in them. (8)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun on a begging-tour should not accept any such-like raw plants <a name="fr_369"></a><a href="#fn_363"><font size="1">1</font></a> as Ikshumeru, Aṅkakarelu, Ka<i>s</i>eru, Sa<i>m</i>ghâ<i>t</i>ika, Pûtiâlu. (9)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun on a begging-tour should not accept any such-like (vegetables) as Nymphaea or stalk of Nymphaea or the bulb of Nelumbium or the upper part or the filament of Lotus or any part of the plant. (10)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun on a begging-tour should not accept such-like raw substances as seeds or sprouts, growing on the top or the root or the stem or the knots (of a plant), likewise the pulp or blossoms of the plantain, cocoa-nut, wild date, and palmyra trees. (11)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun on a begging-tour should not accept any such-like raw unmodified substances as sugar-cane, which is full of holes, or withering or peeling off or corroded by wolves; or the points of reeds or the pulp of plantains. (12)</p>  
 <p>The same holds good with regard to garlic or its leaves or stalk or bulb or integument. (13) Likewise with regard to cooked fruits of Atthiya <a name="fr_370"></a><a href="#fn_364"><font size="1">2</font></a>, Tinduka <a name="fr_371"></a><a href="#fn_365"><font size="1">3</font></a>, Vilva <a name="fr_372"></a><a href="#fn_366"><font size="1">4</font></a>, <i>S</i>rîpar<i>n</i>î <a name="fr_373"></a><a href="#fn_367"><font size="1">5</font></a>. (14)</p>  
 <p>A monk or a nun on a begging-tour should not accept such raw, unmodified substances as corn, clumps of corn, cakes of corn, sesamum, ground sesamum, or cakes of sesamum.</p>  
 <p>This is the whole duty, &amp;c.</p>  
 <p>Thus I say. (15)</p>
