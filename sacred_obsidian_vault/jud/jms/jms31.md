 <p><a name="an_10_1">1</a>. Cf. <i>JE</i>, I, 546 ff.; Blau 86 ff.; Ashkenazi, <i>Taam Zekenim</i>, 56; <i>S. Ḥas.</i> 367, 1455, 1457. An early Yiddish "Vrauen Büchlein" (<i>Miẓvat HaNashim</i>, Venice 1552, ch. 47) reassured its pious readers that the woman who wears an amulet to the ritual bath "kein Sünde hat," and that it is no impediment to the performance of the rite and need not be removed.</p>  
 <p><a name="an_10_2">2</a>. Cf. <i>Shab.</i> 53a and Rashi; <i>Or Zarua</i> II, 18d, §83; <i>Oraḥ Ḥayim</i> 305:11; <i>Lev Tov</i> 6: 129, p. 68b; <i>HaḤayim</i> IV, 3; Perles, <i>Graetz Jubelschrift</i>, 35. On the corals see <i>Tashbeẓ</i> §60; <i>Responsa</i> of Meir of Rothenburg, ed. Lemberg, §140; Berliner, <i>Aus dem Leben</i>, 134; Zimmels, <i>Beiträge</i>, 118, n. 484.</p>  
 <p><a name="an_10_3">3</a>. <i>Shab.</i> 66a and Rashi (cf. A. Darmesteter and D. S. Blondheim, Les Gloses Françaises de Raschi, Paris 1929, 31, §246); <i>Maḥ. Vit.</i> 133, n. 35; <i>Rokeaḥ</i> §100; <i>Rabiah</i> 311, §221, and Aptowitzer's n. 10; Güd. I, 214; <i>Oraḥ Ḥayim</i> 303:24 and <i>Ber Heteb</i>, ad loc. A note in <i>Amude Shlomo</i> to <i>Semag</i> I, 65 reads: ‏ואני המעתיק מצאתי בבאורי מהרר״ז שוויינבר״ט וז״ל אבן תקומה אני ראיתי אבן הצץ שהיה בתוכו חלול ואבן קטן בתובו כעינבל בזוג ובן נברא ואמרו שהוא אבן תקומה‎. A thirteenth-century Latin ms. reports that "when the women of Salerno fear abortion, they carry with them the pregnant stone" (Thorndike, I, 740), and Wuttke (91-2) writes that a similar practice existed among the Germans.</p>  
 <p><a name="an_10_4">4</a>. <i>S. Ḥas.</i> 1463 and Güd. I, 204; cf. Frazer, <i>The Magic Art</i>, I, 587 f.; Grimm II, 72g, III, 443; Lowenthal, <i>A World Passed By</i>, 115; <i>Kiẓur Shelah</i>, In. <i>Pes.</i>, 142 and <i>Ber Heteb</i> on <i>Oraḥ Ḥayim</i> 477:2, n. 4; M. Schwab, <i>REJ</i>, XXIII (1891), 137. Berliner, op. cit., 102, suggests that the belief in certain German districts that a piece of <i>Judenmatz</i> in a house will protect it from fire, may be derived from this use of the Afikomen as an amulet. <i>Yore Deah</i> 305:15 and Lipez, 47; Grunwald, <i>MJV</i>, XIX (1906), III, 112, 114; Perles, <i>Graetz Jubelschrift</i>, 35. A 14th century Archbishop of Aix, Richard Mauvoisin, had a Jewish astrologer named Moses carve some seals on his pastoral ring to avert disease and bring him fortune (Thorndike, III, 19).</p>  
 <p><a name="an_10_5">5</a>. Cf. Samter, 175 ff.; Seligmann, <i>Mag. Heil- u. Schutzmittel</i>, 200 ff., which discuss the anti-demonic virtues of red. These works and also Elworthy, <i>The Evil Eye</i>, contain much information on this general subject.</p>

 <p><a name="an_10_10">10</a>. Soc.</i>, XXXVI [1917], 154, 156) and medieval, all are cut after the same pattern.</p>
