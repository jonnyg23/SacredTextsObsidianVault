 <p><a name="an_15_1">1</a>. Cf. Thorndike, I, 123; Güd. I, 81-2; <i>Semag</i>, Introd.; Gross, <i>Gallia Judaica</i>, 20; <i>Nishmat Ḥayim</i>, Introd.; see <i>JE</i>, IV, 654 ff. and A. Kristianpoller, "Traum und Traumdeutung," <i>Monumenta Talmudica</i>, IV, for the Biblical and Talmudic data on dreams; Bischoff, 63 ff., 158 ff.</p>  
 <p><a name="an_15_2">2</a>. <i>Or Zarua</i>, IV, 27d, §200; <i>Tashbeẓ</i>, §352; <i>Mordecai</i>, B.K. §2, P. 40c and Introd. to Krakau ed. of <i>Sheelot Uteshubot min HaShamayim</i> (see also <i>Shelah</i>, II, 201a); ibid., Livorno 1818 and Krakau 1895; <i>HB</i>, XIV (1874), 131; Malter, <i>Dreams</i>, 201-2; Güd. I, 81, n. 5; Löwinger, <i>Der Traum</i>, 20; Simon Duran (d. 1444) defended at length the reliability of dreams even in questions of strict science, as medicine and mathematics. Many questions in the science of medicine, he asserted, have been solved in dreams by the famous physicians Galen, Ibn Zohr, and others (Malter, op. cit., 203); cf. also Isidore Epstein, <i>The Responsa of Rabbi Simon b. Zemah Duran</i>, London 1930, 23 f., 39, 99.</p>  
 <p><a name="an_15_3">3</a>. Löwinger, op. cit., 6; the provision that a dream ban can be loosed only</p>  
 <p><a name="page_310"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 310</font></a></p>  
 <p>by ten men is found in the Talmud, <i>Ned.</i> 8a; cf. <i>Leket Yosher</i>, II, 80; <i>Pitron Ḥalomot</i>, III, 3, p. lot; Löwinger, 34. The same provision affecting a dream vow was ascribed to the Geonim: <i>Kol Bo</i>, 109d; <i>Yore Deah</i>, 210:2.</p>  
 <p><a name="an_15_4">4</a>. Thorndike, II, 605; <i>Ber.</i> 57b; <i>S. Ḥas.</i> 382, 1138; <i>Leket Yosher</i>, I, 41; <i>Pitron Ḥalomot</i>, Introd., p. 3, and I, 2, pp. 14 ff.; cf. also <i>S. Ḥas.</i> 634, 1522, etc.; <i>Nishmat Ḥayim</i>, III, 5.</p>  
 <p><a name="an_15_5">5</a>. <i>S. Ḥas.</i> 382; <i>Ẓiyuni</i>, 20a; <i>Nishmat Ḥayim</i>, loc. cit.; cf. also Löwinger, op. cit., 8, for a similar opinion of Saadia. The spirit us theory of dreams was popular in Europe, cf. Thorndike, II, 298.</p>  
 <p><a name="an_15_6">6</a>. <i>Ẓiyuni</i>, loc. cit.</p>  
 <p><a name="an_15_7">7</a>. Ibid., 19d; <i>Ḥochmat HaNefesh</i>, 4a-b, 6b; <i>S. Ḥas. B</i> 1131;<i>Raben</i>, 26; <i>Responsa</i> of Ḥayim b. Isaac <i>Or Zarua</i>, §164; Löwinger, op. cit., 20; <i>Maaseh Book</i>, II, 517, cf. <i>Shab.</i> 152a, b; Taanit 24b; <i>S. Ḥas.</i> 591, etc.</p>  
 <p><a name="an_15_8">8</a>. <i>Ḥag.</i> 5b; <i>Nishmat Ḥayim</i>, loc. cit.</p>  
 <p><a name="an_15_9">9</a>. Rashi, <i>San.</i> 30a and Yeb. 24b; <i>S. Ḥas.</i> 382, 1550; <i>Ḥochmat HaNefesh</i>, 4a-b, 66; <i>Orḥot Ẓadikim</i>, 63a; cf. Hai Gaon's remark (<i>Taam Zekenim</i>, 58): "Concerning what you have said, that every man has his own genius of dreams who appears as an old man to one, as a youth to another, we have heard that this is so, but we have not ourself beheld such an apparition, nor have we spoken with anyone who has."</p>  
 <p><a name="an_15_10">10</a>. <i>S. Ḥas.</i> 382; Maaseh <i>Rokeaḥ</i>, §130, p. 31; <i>Nishmat Ḥayim</i>, III, 27. Similar explanations of the causes of dreams are to be found in medieval Christian works; see Thorndike, II, 412, where the theories of Bartholomew of England (13th century) are given; he believed that dreams are produced by divine inspiration, by angelic administration, by diabolic illusion, or by natural and bodily causes. See also Löwinger, op. cit., 8, and <i>Pitron Ḥalamot</i>, I, 1:1, pp. 6-7, for slightly different analyses of the causes of dreams, and Thorndike, I, 358, for the view of Philo Judæus.</p>
