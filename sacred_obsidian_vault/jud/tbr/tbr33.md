 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><b>T</b>.</font></span></p><dir>the case of Hannah: "<i>Now Hannah, she was speaking in her heart</i>, <a name="fr_231"></a><a href="#fn_230"><font size="1">1</font></a> etc. Perhaps we should include them all (the Eighteen) at one sitting? He has expressly stated in the case of David: "<i>Evening, and morning, and noonday</i>," <a name="fr_232"></a><a href="#fn_231"><font size="1">2</font></a> etc. "Evening "that is, evening prayer; "morning"—that is, morning prayer; "noonday"—that is afternoon prayer (Minchah). Perhaps one should ask for what one needs and then depart (without saying the Benediction)? He has expressly stated in the case of Solomon: "<i>to hearken unto the cry and to the prayer</i>" <a name="fr_233"></a><a href="#fn_232"><font size="1">3</font></a> "cry"—that is chanting (in praise), and so he says: "<i>Cry aloud, ye righteous, in the LORD</i>," <a name="fr_234"></a><a href="#fn_233"><font size="1">4</font></a> etc. We do not say anything after "True and sure," <a name="fr_235"></a><a href="#fn_234"><font size="1">5</font></a> but we do say things after the Prayer, even though it be the Order of Confession of the Day of Atonement. <a name="fr_236"></a><a href="#fn_235"><font size="1">6</font></a>  
 <p>14.  <p>15. They who stand in the land of Israel set their intention opposite Jerusalem and pray; for it is said: "<i>and they pray towards this city</i>." <a name="fr_240"></a><a href="#fn_239"><font size="1">10</font></a></p>

 <p>17. A man may not stand upon a bed, nor upon a seat, nor upon a bench, and pray, for there may be no high position before God; <a name="fr_242"></a><a href="#fn_241"><font size="1">1</font></a> for it is said: "<i>Out of the depths have cried unto thee, O LORD</i>." <a name="fr_243"></a><a href="#fn_242"><font size="1">2</font></a> if he is old, or ill, behold, then it is permissible. <a name="fr_244"></a><a href="#fn_243"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>20. Suppose a man is standing and praying in a camp <a name="fr_245"></a><a href="#fn_244"><font size="1">4</font></a> or in an open highway, behold, he avoids an ass or an ass-driver or a seller of pots; <a name="fr_246"></a><a href="#fn_245"><font size="1">5</font></a> he does not pause [in his prayer]. They say concerning R. Chanina ben Dosa, that he was standing and praying, and an <i>‘arod</i> <a name="fr_247"></a><a href="#fn_246"><font size="1">6</font></a> bit him, and he made no pause. His disciples went and found it dead on the mouth of its hole. They said: Woe to the man whom an <i>‘arod</i> has bitten; [but] woe to the <i>‘arod</i> which bit ben Dosa.</p>  
 <p>21. We do not stand up to pray straightway from conversation, nor from laughter, nor from frivolity, nor from idle talk, but (we do) from learned discourse.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_215"></a><a href="tbr32.htm#fr_216">35:1</a> <i>ten Benedictions</i>. The first three and last three of the Eighteen, three for New Year's Day (SA, pp. 241 <i>sq</i>.), and one for the sabbath (see Laible).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_216"></a><a href="tbr32.htm#fr_217">35:2</a> <i>nine</i> [(<i>Benedictions</i>)]. Not adding a special Benediction about the sabbath.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_217"></a><a href="tbr32.htm#fr_218">35:3</a> SA, p. 238.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_218"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_219">35:4</a> <i>stand</i>. i.e. to say the Eighteen (<i>cf</i>. pp. <a href="tbr11.htm#page_4">4</a> <i>sq</i>.).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_219"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_220">35:5</a> <i>with solemnity</i>. The phrase is literally "with heaviness of head," <i>i.e.</i> with bending it down, but probably the expression is here solely metaphorical, in contrast to "lightness of head," <i>i.e.</i> irreverence, <i>cf</i>. <i>T</i>. III. 21 (<a href="#page_38">p. 38</a>). It is not far removed from the phrase "the poor in spirit" (Matt 5<sup>3</sup>).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_220"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_221">35:6</a> <i>God</i>. Lit.: the Place (<a href="tbr26.htm#page_29">p. 29</a>). B has "their Father who is in heaven."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_221"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_222">35:7</a> <i>the King</i>. An Israelite, for to a heathen king one must answer, for fear of the consequences (Bartenora).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_222"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_223">36:1</a> <i>a snake</i>. An ordinary one, which does not generally bite. But in the case of a viper or a scorpion, which certainly bite and kill, he should pause (Bartenora).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_223"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_224">36:2</a> <i>learning</i>. Lit.: "wisdom," and so throughout this section.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_224"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_225">36:3</a> <i>mind</i>. <i>da‘ath</i>, perhaps constitution.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_225"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_226">36:4</a> <i>Abba Saul</i>. Of the third generation of mishna teachers, c. 130-160 A.D.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_226"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_227">36:5</a> <i>memories technica</i>. Lit.: "sign."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_227"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_228">36:6</a> Ps. 10<sup>17</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_228"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_229">36:7</a> The section shows the earnestness with which the great teacher prayed.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_229"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_230">36:8</a> Dan. 6<sup>10</sup>. The phrases in the case of Daniel, Hannah, David, Solomon are literally "in Daniel," etc., and possibly are of the same type as on <a href="tbr59.htm#page_79">p. 79</a>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_230"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_231">37:1</a> 1 Sam. 1<sup>13</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_231"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_232">37:2</a> Ps. 55<sup>17</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_232"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_233">37:3</a> 1 Kings 8<sup>28</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_233"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_234">37:4</a> Ps. 33<sup>1</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_234"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_235">37:5</a> "True and sure" (<i>’Emeth wyatzib</i>), <i>cf</i>. <a href="tbr12.htm#page_7">p. 7</a>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_235"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_236">37:6</a> After the Prayer (<i>Tephillah</i>) serious variations may begin, exclusive of minor variations in the <i>Tephillah</i> itself.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_236"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_237">37:7</a> <i>God</i>. Lit.: "the Place."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_237"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_238">37:8</a> 1 Kings 8<sup>44</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_238"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_239">37:9</a> <i>their land</i>. Not verbally in 1 Kings 8<sup>44</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_239"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_240">37:10</a> Again, not verbally correct.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_240"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_241">37:11</a> <i>this place</i>. This also is not verbally exact.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_241"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_242">38:1</a> = the Place, and so, often.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_242"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_243">38:2</a> Ps. 130<sup>1</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_243"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_244">38:3</a> On this section see "<i>The Hebrew-Christian Messiah</i>," p. 237.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_244"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_245">38:4</a> <i>camp</i>. στρατεία.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_245"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_246">38:5</a> <i>a seller of pots</i> (<i>qaddar</i>). But Kahan's emendation in Laible to <i>qarrar</i>, or rather <i>qârār</i> (waggoner) may be accepted.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_246"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_247">38:6</a> <i>an </i>‘arod. Usually a wild ass, but here evidently a species of venomous snake. The story comes also in T. J. V. 1. (p. 9<sup>a</sup>), but rather differently in <i>T</i>. B. 33<sup>a</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_247"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_248">39:1</a> <i>the prophets of old</i>. Literally "the former prophets," but this technical term for the Books of Joshua, Judges, Samuel, and Kings can hardly be intended here.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_248"></a><a href="tbr33.htm#fr_249">39:2</a> For the principle compare the direction of the Massoretes to repeat Isa. 66<sup>23</sup> after ver. 24.</p>
