 <p><a name="page_76"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 76</font></a></p>  <h4 align="center">At Various Natural Phenomena, and at Good or Bad Tidings.</h4>  
 <p><i>M</i>. IX. 2. At <a name="fr_455"></a><a href="#fn_454"><font size="1">1</font></a> shooting stars, <a name="fr_456"></a><a href="#fn_455"><font size="1">2</font></a> and at earthquakes, and at lightnings, and at thunder, and at wind(s), one says: "Blessed be He whose power filleth the world." At mountains and hills, at seas and rivers and at deserts, he says: <font size="-1" color="green">"</font>Blessed be the Maker of the works of creation." R. Judah says: He who sees the Great Sea <a name="fr_457"></a><a href="#fn_456"><font size="1">3</font></a> says: "Blessed be He who made the Great Sea." [This he says] when he sees it at intervals. <a name="fr_458"></a><a href="#fn_457"><font size="1">4</font></a> At rains, and at good tidings, he says: "Blessed be the Good and the Doer of good," and at ill reports he says "Blessed be He who judgeth in truth."</p>  
 <p></p><dir><b>T</b>. VII. 1. The Benediction in the invitation <a name="fr_459"></a><a href="#fn_458"><font size="1">5</font></a> is derived from the Law, for it is said: "<i>And thou shalt eat and be full, and thou shalt bless</i>" <a name="fr_460"></a><a href="#fn_459"><font size="1">6</font></a>—this is the Benediction in the invitation; "<i>the LORD thy God</i>"—this is the first Benediction; "<i>for the. . . land</i>"—this is the Benediction for the land; "<i>good</i>" this is [the Benediction for] Jerusalem; and so it says: "<i>this goodly mountain, and Lebanon</i>." <a name="fr_461"></a><a href="#fn_460"><font size="1">7</font></a> "<i>Which he hath given thee</i>," <a name="fr_462"></a><a href="#fn_461"><font size="1">8</font></a>—this is [the Benediction] "who art Good and doest good." Whence even for the Law and even for the commandments? It is expressly said: "<i>Who gave thee</i>," and in another place he says: "<i>And I will give thee the tables of stone</i>." <a name="fr_464"></a><a href="#fn_463"><font size="1">2</font></a> R. Meir says: Whence [do we learn that] just as thou sayest a Benediction over what is good, so thou sayest a Benediction over what is evil. It is expressly said: "<i>Which the LORD thy God gave thee</i>," <a name="fr_465"></a><a href="#fn_464"><font size="1">3</font></a>—"thy God" means "thy Judge," in every judgment which He adjudges thee, whether for good or for punishment.  
 <p>3. He who sees a negro, or a man with red spots, or one with white spots, or a humpback, or a dwarf, says: "Blessed be He who makes creatures different." [He who sees] one who has lost a limb, or a lame man, or a blind man, or one afflicted with boils, says: "Blessed be He that judgeth in truth."</p>  
 <p>4. If one sees beautiful persons and beautiful trees he says: "Blessed be He who has beautiful creatures thus in His world." <a name="fr_466"></a><a href="#fn_465"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>5. He who sees the bow in the cloud says: "Blessed be He who is faithful in His. covenant, remembering the covenant." <a name="fr_467"></a><a href="#fn_466"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>  
 <p>6. If one walks in a burial-ground one says: "Blessed be He who knows the number of you all; He will judge you; He will raise you up; Blessed be He who is faithful in His word, restoring the dead to life."</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_454"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_455">76:1</a> SA, pp. 291 <i>sq</i>., has similar, but not quite identical, forms. Cf. Abrahams’ notes.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_455"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_456">76:2</a> <i>shooting stars</i>, or, "comets."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_456"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_457">76:3</a> <i>the Great Sea</i>. The Mediterranean.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_457"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_458">76:4</a> <i>at intervals</i>. Cf. <i>T</i>. VII. 6 (p. 78). At intervals of thirty days (Bartenora), thus excluding persons who live on the coast.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_458"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_459">76:5</a> <i>The Benediction in the invitation</i>. See <a href="tbr41.htm#page_59">p. 59</a>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_459"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_460">76:6</a> Deut. 8<sup>10</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_460"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_461">76:7</a> Deut. 3<sup>25</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_461"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_462">76:8</a> Deut. 810.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_462"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_463">77:1</a> <i>for the mountains and for the hills</i>. <i>i.e.</i> on seeing them.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_463"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_464">77:2</a> Exod. 24<sup>12</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_464"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_465">77:3</a> <i>the LORD thy God gave thee</i>. This is the sense of Deut. 8<sup>10</sup>, but verbally the quotation is found in 26<sup>11</sup>, where it refers to "good," and also in 28<sup>53</sup>, where it refers to punishment.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_465"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_466">77:4</a> The common text has, "who created beautiful creatures."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_466"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_467">77:5</a> <i>remembering the covenant</i>. His faithfulness is seen in His remembering it. The reference is to Gen. 9<sup>15</sup>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_467"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_468">78:1</a> <i>another way</i>. For this common use of "way" in the sense of manner of life ordered by religion, and so religion itself, <i>cf</i>. Acts 9<sup>2</sup>, 19<sup>9-23</sup>, 2<sup>24</sup>, 24<sup>22</sup>, and the all—comprehensive John 14<sup>6</sup>. Even the word Halaka is properly, we may suppose, "method of walk."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_468"></a><a href="tbr57.htm#fr_469">78:2</a> Cf. <a href="#page_76">p. 76</a>.</p>
