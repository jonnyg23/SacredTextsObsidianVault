 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_379"></a><a href="tsa36.htm#fr_383">119:2</a> Meaning uncertain. The majority of Jewish commentators treat as the name of an idol. A possible interpretation is, that it is an abbreviation of some transliterated unorthodox divine name, such as, for example, κοσμοπλάστης, or a disguised form of the Tetragrammaton. The criminal is then the blasphemer who utters the name "under a pseudonym" (<i>Sanh</i>. VII. 5). Although he cannot be legally stoned, he may become the object of irregular justice.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_380"></a><a href="tsa36.htm#fr_384">119:3</a> On the ground of Numb. 25. 6-15.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_381"></a><a href="tsa36.htm#fr_385">119:4</a> Legally, (<i>Makkoth</i> III. 2), he can only be punished by scourging.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_382"></a><a href="tsa36.htm#fr_386">120:1</a> See Josephus, <i>Bell</i>. VI. ll. 4; V. v. 2; <i>Ant</i>. XV. xi. 5; from which it is to be gathered that the Romans recognized that the Jews could put intruders to death. The well-known Temple barrier inscription discovered in 1871 reads: "No man of another nation to enter within the fence and enclosure round the Temple; and whoever is caught will have himself to blame that his death follows." Cf. J. Armitage Robinson, <i>St. Paul's Epistle to the Ephesians</i>, p. 160.</p>
