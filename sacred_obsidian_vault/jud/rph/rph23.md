 <p align="center">(JELLINEK)</p>  
 <p align="center">BIOGRAPHICAL SKETCHES ON THE ZOHAR</p>  
 <p align="center">A</p>  
 <p align="center">NAMES OF THE ZOHAR</p>  
 <p>Jewish authors adopted preferably the three following names for the grand monument of the Kabbalah:</p>  
 <p>1. ‏מדרשו‎ (Midroshau)--his Midrash, or, ‏מדרש של ר׳ שמעון בן יוחאי‎ (Midrosh shel R. Simeon ben Yohai)--the Midrash of Simeon ben Yohai. Under this name the Zohar appears with the following authors: Behai (died 1340), <a name="fr_715"></a><a href="#fn_715"><font size="1">1</font></a> Commentary to the Pentateuch, section Mishpatim; R. Simeon ben Zemach Duran (died 1444), Responsa, vol. III, questions 56 and 57; R. Meier ben Gabbai (born 1481), Abodath ha-Kodesh; <a name="fr_716"></a><a href="#fn_716"><font size="1">2</font></a> R. Judah Moscato (died 1580), Nefuzat Yehudah (fol. 1166, 211a). This name guarantees the genuineness of the work.</p>  
 <p>2. ‏מדרש יהי אור‎ (Midrash Y’hi Or)--Midrash Let There be Light. So named by Abraham ben Samuel Zacuto (flourished 1502) in his "Sefer ha-Yuhasin" under ‏רשב״י‎, and by Hayim Joseph David Azulai (died 1807) in his "Shem ha-G’dolim,"</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_309"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 309</font></a></p>  
 <p>vol. II, fol. 49b. The probable reason for adopting this title by these authors is to be found in the fact that a few manuscripts of the Zohar begin with the commentary to the verse ‏יהי אור‎--Let there be Light (Gen. 1, 3); or, what is more probable, to point out the illumination that is bound to accrue to the reader of this work.</p>  
 <p>3. ‏זהר‎ (Zohar), Splendor, Brightness. So called according to Daniel XII, 3. ‏והמשכילים יזהירו בזהר הרקיע‎--And the Wise shall shine as the Brightness of the firmament. Compare ‏זעיא מהימנא‎ Zohar, vol. III, fol. 64a in the Sulzbach edition. <a name="fr_717"></a><a href="#fn_717"><font size="1">3</font></a> This last name has become the predominating one since the author of "Yuhasin" in 1502.</p>  
 <p align="center">B</p>  
 <p align="center">EDITIONS OF THE ZOHAR</p>  
 <p>1. The first edition of the Zohar was published by R. Meier ben Ephraim and Jacob ben Naftali at Mantua in 1560. 3 vol. 4°.</p>  
 <p>2. In the same year it was again published in folio at Cremona. The preface to this edition, written by Yitzhak de Lattes, dates from 1558 to which the ‏פרט‎, ‏יריעאף יצריח‎ points.</p>  
 <p>3. According to R. Yisahar Beer (‏אמדי בינה‎, end of preface. Compare also Bartolocci, Magna Bibliotheca Rabbinica, vol. IV, p. 446) an edition of the Zohar appeared also at Venice.</p>  
 <p>4. Levi ben Kalonymos published a folio edition of the Zohar at Lublin in 1623. In this edition the numbering of the pages is the same as in that of the Cremona edition. Von Rosenroth considers this edition very faulty.</p>  
 <p>5. The last named scholar had a Zohar printed, also folio, at Sulzbach in 1684.</p>  
 <p>6. Another edition, patterned after the Mantua edition, appeared at Amsterdam in 1714.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_310"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 310</font></a></p>  
 <p>7. An edition printed at Constantinople in 1736 (Molitor, vol. I, p. 76).</p>  
 <p>8. Another edition at Amsterdam in 1805.</p>  
 <p>9. Milsahagi, author of ‏ספר דאב״יה‎ quotes also a Salonica edition.</p>  
 <p>Editions 2, 4 and 5, because printed in folio, are usually called ‏זהר גדול‎ (Zohar Godaul--Large Zohar); the others, because printed in quarto, are designated ‏זהר קטן‎ (Zohar Kotaun--Small Zohar).</p>  
 <p align="center">C</p>  
 <p align="center">ELEMENTS OF THE ZOHAR</p>  
 <p>Besides the</p>  
 <p>1. Zohar (‏זהר‎) proper which serves as commentary to the Pentateuch, this work contains also:</p>  
 <p>2. Sifra d-Zeniuta (Book of Mystery),</p>  
 <p>3. Idra Rabba (Great Assembly),</p>  
 <p>4. Idra Zutah (Small Assembly),</p>  
 <p>5. Saba (The old man),</p>  
 <p>6. Midrash Ruth <a name="fr_718"></a><a href="#fn_718"><font size="1">4</font></a> (fragments only),</p>  
 <p>7. Sefer ha-Bahir (Book of Brightness),</p>  
 <p>8: Tosefta (Addendum),</p>  
 <p>9. Raya Mehemna (The Faithful Shepherd),</p>  
 <p>10. Hekalot (Palaces),</p>  
 <p>11. Sitra Torah (The mysteries of the Law),</p>  
 <p>12. Midrash ha Ne’elam (The hidden Midrash),</p>  
 <p>13. Razi di Razin (Mystery of Mysteries),</p>  
 <p>14. Midrash Hassid (Midrash to Song of Songs),</p>  
 <p>15. Maamar Ta-Hazee (Treatise which begins with "come and see."),</p>  
 <p>16. Yanuka (Boy),</p>  
 <p>17. Pakuda (Explanation of the Law),</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_311"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 311</font></a></p>  
 <p>18. Hibbura Kadma-ah (Previous Work),</p>  
 <p>19. Mathnithin (Doctrines).</p>  
 <p>All these elements are to be found in the Sulzbach edition; the Mantua edition contains only Nos. 1, 2, 3, 4, 8, 9, 11 and 12. <a name="fr_719"></a><a href="#fn_719"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>  
 <p align="center">D</p>  
 <p align="center">TRANSLATIONS OF THE ZOHAR</p>  
 <p>1. A Hebrew translation of the Zohar (manuscript), written in 1506, from section ‏אחרי מות‎ to the end of the work and bearing the title ‏מראות העבאות‎ is in possession of S. D. Luzatto, professor at the Rabbinic College at Padua. Compare the Hebrew Year Book ‏ברם חמד‎ (Prague, Landau 8.) VII, 79.</p>  
 <p>2. Zevi Hirsch ben Yerachmiel of Krakau in his book ‏נחלת עבי‎ (Frankfort O. M. 1711), translated into Jargon some fragments of the Zohar. Wolf, B. H. I. 999.</p>  
 <p>3. The "Book of Mysteries," the Idra Rabba and the Idra Zutah were translated into Latin by Rosenroth in the second volume of his "Kabbalah denudata."</p>  
 <p>4. Several fragments of the Zohar have been translated into French by the author of this book, and by the translator into</p>
