 <h2 align="CENTER">APPENDIX VI.</h2>
 <h3 align="CENTER">List of Narratives, Apologues, and Stories of various kinds in the Writings of Kwang-dze.</h3>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK I.</h4>
 <p>Paragraph 1. The enjoyment of the Tâo by such vast creatures as the Khwän and the Phäng.</p>
 <p>2. The enjoyment and foolish judgments of smaller creatures. Big trees and Phäng Zû.</p>
 <p>3. Questions put by Thang to Kî. The Tâo in different men:--Yung-dze; Lieh-dze; and an ideal Tâoist. The Perfect man, the Spirit-like man, and the Sagely-minded man.</p>
 <p>4. Yâo wishing to resign the throne to Hsü Yû.</p>
 <p>5. Kien Wû and Lien Shû on the ideal Tâoist.</p>
 <p>6. A cap-seller of Sung. Yâo after visiting the four Perfect ones.</p>
 <p>7. Hui-dze and Kwang-dze:--the great calabashes; the hand-protecting salve; and the great Ailantus tree.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK II.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. Nan-kwo Dze-khî in a trance, and his disciple. The notes of heaven, earth, and man.</p>
 <p>4. 'In the morning three:'--the monkeys and their acorns.</p>
 <p>7. Yâo and Shun,--on the wish of the former to smite some small states.</p>
 <p>9. Lî Kî before and after her marriage.</p>
 <p>10. The penumbra and the shadow. Kwang-dze's dream that he was a butterfly.</p>
 <p><a name="page_299"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 299</font></a></p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK III.</h4>
 <p>Par. 2. King Wän-hui and his cook;--how the latter cut up his oxen.</p>
 <p>3. Kung-wän Hsien and the Master of the Left who had only one foot.</p>
 <p>4. The death of Lâo-dze; and adverse judgment on his life.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK IV.</h4>
 <p>Pars. 1, 2. Yen Hui and Confucius;--on the proposal of the former to go and convert the ruler of Wei.</p>
 <p>3, 4. Dze-kâo and Confucius;--on the mission of the former from Khû to Khî.</p>
 <p>5. Yen Ho and Kü Po-yü;--on the former's undertaking to be tutor to the wayward son of duke Ling of Wei.</p>
 <p>6. The master-mechanic and the great tree;--so large and old through its uselessness.</p>
 <p>7. Nan-po Dze-khî and the great tree, preserved by its uselessness. Trees of Sung cut down because of their good timber. Peculiarities exempting from death as sacrificial victims.</p>
 <p>8. The deformed object Shû and his worth.</p>
 <p>9. Rencontre between Confucius and the madman of Khû.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK V.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. Confucius explains the influence of the cripple Wang Thâi over the people of Lû.</p>
 <p>2. The fellow-students Dze-khân and the cripple Shän-thû Kiâ.</p>
 <p>3. Confucius and Toeless of Shû-shan. judgment of Toeless and Lâo-dze on Confucius.</p>
 <p>4. Duke Âi of Lû and Confucius;--on the ugly but most able and fascinating man, Âi-thâi Tho. Admiration for Confucius of duke Âi.</p>
 <p>5. The deformed favourites of duke Ling of Wei and duke Hwan of Khî. Argument between Kwang-dze and Hui-dze, growing out of the former's account of them.</p>
 <p><a name="page_300"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 300</font></a></p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK VI.</h4>
 <p>Par. 8. Nan-po Dze-khwei and the long-lived Nü Yü. How Pû-liang Î learned the Tâo.</p>
 <p>9. Four Tâoists, and the submission of Dze-yü, one of them, a poor deformed hunchback, to his lot, when he was very ill.</p>
 <p>10. The submission of Dze-lâi, another of the four, as his life was ebbing away.</p>
 <p>11. Three Tâoists, and the ways of two of them on the death of the third. Conversation on the subject between Confucius and Dze-kung.</p>
 <p>12. Confucius and Yen Hui on the mourning of Mäng-sun Zhâi.</p>
 <p>13. Î-<i>r</i> Dze and Hsü Yû. How the Tâo will remove the injuries of error, and regenerate the mind.</p>
 <p>14. Confucius and Yen Hui. The growth of the latter in Tâoism.</p>
 <p>15. Dze-yü and Dze-sang. The penury of the latter and submission to his fate.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK VII.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. Nieh Khüeh, Wang Î, and Phû-î-dze. That Shun was inferior in his Tâoistic attainments to the more ancient sovereign, Thâi.</p>
 <p>2. Kien Wû and the recluse Khieh-yü;--on the ideal of government.</p>
 <p>3. Thien Kan and a nameless man;--that non-action is the way to govern the world.</p>
 <p>4. Yang Dze-kü and Lâo Tan on the nameless government of the Intelligent Kings.</p>
 <p>5. Lieh-dze and his master Hû-dze. How the latter defeated the wizard of Käng.</p>
 <p>6. The end of Chaos, wrought by the gods of the southern and northern seas.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK VIII.</h4>
 <p>Par. 4. How two shepherd slaves lose their sheep in</p>
 <p><a name="page_301"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 301</font></a></p>
 <p>different ways. The corresponding cases of the righteous Po-î and the robber Kih.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK X.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. Murder of the ruler of Khî by Thien Khäng-dze, and his usurpation of the State.</p>
 <p>2. How the best and ablest of men, such as Lung-fäng, Pî-kan, Khang Hung, and Dze-hsü, may come to a disastrous end, and only seem to have served the purposes of such men as the robber Kih.</p>
 <p>3. Evils resulting from such able men as Zäng Shän, Shih Khiû, Yang Kû, Mo Tî, Shih Khwang, Khui, and Lû Kû.</p>
 <p>4. Character of the age of Perfect Virtue, and sovereigns who flourished in it in contrast with the time of Kwang-dze.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XI.</h4>
 <p>Par. 3. Zhui Khü and Lâo-dze. The latter denounces the meddling with the mind which began with Hwang-Tî, and the spread of knowledge, as productive of all evil.</p>
 <p>4. Hwang-Tî and Kwang Khäng-dze, his master, who discourses on the mystery of the Tâo, and how it promotes long life.</p>
 <p>5. Yün Kiang and Hung Mung, or the Leader of the Clouds and the Great Ether;--the wish of the former to nourish all things, and how they would be transformed by his doing nothing.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XII.</h4>
 <p>Par. 4. The loss and recovery by Yâo of his dark-coloured Pearl;--the Tâo.</p>
 <p>5. Hsü Yû's reply to Yâo on the character of Nieh Khüeh and his unfitness to take the place of Sovereign.</p>
 <p>6. Yâo rejects the good wishes for him of the Border-warden of Hwâ.</p>
 <p>7. Yü and Po-khang Dze-kâo. The latter vindicates his resignation of dignity and taking to farming.</p>
 <p>9. Confucius and Lâo-dze;--on the attitude to the Tâo of a great sage and ruler.</p>
 <p><a name="page_302"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 302</font></a></p>
 <p>10. Kiang-lü Mien and Ki Khêh;--on the counsel which the former had given to the ruler of Lû.</p>
 <p>11. Dze-kung and the old gardener;--argument of the latter in favour of the primitive simplicity, and remarks thereon by Confucius.</p>
 <p>12. Kun Mâng and Yüan Fung;--on the government of the sage; of the virtuous and kindly man; and of the spirit-like man.</p>
 <p>13. Män Wû-kwei and Khih-kang Man-khî;--that there had been confusion and disorder before the time of Shun; and the character of the age of Perfect Virtue.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XIII.</h4>
 <p>Par. 6. Yâo and Shun;--on the former's method of government.</p>
 <p>7. Confucius, wishing to deposit some writings in the royal Library, is repulsed by Lâo-dze. Argument between them on Benevolence and Righteousness in relation to the nature of man.</p>
 <p>8. Shih-khäng Khî and Lâo-dze;--the strange conferences between them, and the charges brought by the one against the other.</p>
 <p>10. Duke Hwan and the wheelwright Phien;--that the knack of an art cannot be conveyed to another, and the spirit of thought cannot be fully expressed in writing.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XIV.</h4>
 <p>Par. 2. Tang, a minister of Shang, and Kwang-dze on the nature of Benevolence.</p>
 <p>3. Pei-män Khäng and Hwang-Tî;--a description of Hwang-Tî's music, the Hsien-khih.</p>
 <p>4. Yen Yüan and Kin, the music-master of Lû, on the course of Confucius;--the opinion of the latter that it had been unsuccessful and was verging to entire failure.</p>
 <p>5. Confucius and Lâo-dze. The former has not yet got the Tâo, and Lâo-dze explains the reason.</p>
 <p>6. Confucius and Lâo-dze. Confucius talks of Benevolence</p>
 <p><a name="page_303"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 303</font></a></p>
 <p>and Righteousness; and how the tables are turned on him. He is deeply impressed by the other.</p>
 <p>7. Dze-kung, in consequence of the Master's report of his interview, goes also to see Lâo-Sze; and is nonplussed and lectured by him.</p>
 <p>8. Confucius sees Lâo-dze again, and tells him how he has profited from his instructions. The other expresses his satisfaction with him.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XVI.</h4>
 <p>Par. 2. The state of Perfect Unity, and its gradual Decay.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XVII.</h4>
 <p>Pars. 1-7. The Spirit-earl of the Ho and Zo of the Northern Sea;--on various metaphysical questions growing out of the doctrine of the Tâo.</p>
 <p>8. The khwei, the millipede, the serpent, the wind, the eye, and the mind;--how they had their several powers, but did not know how.</p>
 <p>9. Confucius in peril in Khwang is yet serene and hopeful.</p>
 <p>10. Kung-sun Lung and Mâu of Wei. The Frog of the dilapidated well, and the Turtle of the Eastern Sea. The greatness of Kwang-dze's teachings.</p>
 <p>11. Kwang-dze refuses the invitation of the king of Khû to take office. The wonderful tortoise-shell of the king.</p>
 <p>12. Hui-dze and Kwang-dze. The young phoenix and the owl.</p>
 <p>13. Hui-dze and Kwang-dze;--how Kwang-dze understood the enjoyment of fishes.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XVIII.</h4>
 <p>Par. 2. Hui-dze and Kwang-dze;--vindication by the latter of his behaviour on the death of his wife.</p>
 <p>3. Mr. Deformed and Mr. One-foot;--their submission under pain and in prospect of death.</p>
 <p>4. Kwang-dze and the skull;--what he said to it, and its appearance to him at night in a dream.</p>
 <p><a name="page_304"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 304</font></a></p>
 <p>5. The sadness of Confucius on the departure of Yen Hui for Khî; and his defence of it to Dze-kung. The appearance of a strange bird in Lû, and his moralizings on it.</p>
 <p>6. Lieh-dze and the skull. The transmutations of things.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XIX.</h4>
 <p>Par. 2. Lieh-dze and Kwan Yin;--on the capabilities of the Perfect man.</p>
 <p>3. Confucius and the hunchback, who was skilful at catching cicadas with his rod.</p>
 <p>4. The boatman on the gulf of Khang-shan, and his skill.</p>
 <p>5. Thien Khâi-kih and duke Wei of Kâu;--on the best way to nourish the higher life. How it was illustrated by Thien's master, and how enforced by Confucius.</p>
 <p>6. The officer of sacrifice and his pigs to be sacrificed.</p>
 <p>7. Duke Hwan gets ill from seeing a ghostly sprite, and how he was cured.</p>
 <p>8. The training of a fighting-cock.</p>
 <p>9. Confucius and the swimmer in the gorge of Lü.</p>
 <p>10. Khing, the worker in rottlera wood, and the bell-frame;--how he succeeded in making it as he did.</p>
 <p>11. Tung-yê Kî and his chariot-driving;--how his horses broke down.</p>
 <p>12. The skill of the artisan Shui.</p>
 <p>14. The weakling Sun Hsiû and the Master Dze-pien Khing-dze, with his disciples.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XX.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. Kwang-dze and his disciples;--the great tree that was of no use, and the goose that could not cackle.</p>
 <p>2. Î-liâo of Shih-nan and the marquis of Lû;--how the former presses it on the marquis to go to an Utopia of Tâoism in the south, to escape from his trouble and sorrow.</p>
 <p>3. Pei-kung Shê and prince Khing-kî;--how the former collected taxes and made a peal of bells.</p>
 <p>4. How the Thâi-kung Zän condoled with Confucius on his distresses, and tried to convert him to Tâoism.</p>
 <p><a name="page_305"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 305</font></a></p>
 <p>5. Confucius and Dze-sang Hû. The Tâoistic effect of their conversation on the former. The dying charge of Shun to Yü.</p>
 <p>6. Kwang-dze in rags before the king of Wei. The apologue of the climbing monkey.</p>
 <p>7. Confucius and Yen Hui;--on occasion of the perilous situation between Khän and Zhâi. Confucius expounds the principles that supported him.</p>
 <p>8. Kwang-dze's experiences in the park of Tiâo-ling;--has the character of an apologue.</p>
 <p>9. The Innkeeper's two concubines;--the beauty disliked and the ugly one honoured.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXI.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. Thien Dze-fang and the marquis Wän of Wei.</p>
 <p>2. Wän-po Hsüeh-dze and the scholars of the Middle States.</p>
 <p>3. Confucius and Yen Hui;--on the incomprehensibleness to the latter of the Master's course.</p>
 <p>4. Conversation between Confucius and Lâo-dze on the beginning of things.</p>
 <p>5. Kwang-dze and duke Âi of Lû;--on the dress of the scholar.</p>
 <p>6. Pâi-lî Hsî.</p>
 <p>7. The duke of Sung and his map-drawers.</p>
 <p>8. King Wän and the old fisherman of Zang. Confucius and Yen Hui on king Wän's dream about the fisherman.</p>
 <p>9. The archery of Lieh-dze and Po-hwän Wû-zän.</p>
 <p>10. Kien Wû, and Sun Shû-âo, the True man. Confucius's account of the True man. The king of Khû and the ruler of Fan.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXII.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. Knowledge, Dumb Inaction, Head-strong Stammerer, and Hwang-Tî on the Tâo.</p>
 <p>3. Nieh Khüeh questioning Phei-î about the Tâo.</p>
 <p>4. Shun and his minister Khäng;--that man is not his own.</p>
 <p><a name="page_306"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 306</font></a></p>
 <p>5. Confucius and Lâo Tan;--on the Perfect Tâo.</p>
 <p>6. Tung-kwo Dze's question to Kwang-dze about where the Tâo was to be found, and the reply.</p>
 <p>7. Â-ho Kan, Shän Näng, Lâo-lung Kî, Yen Kang;--Grand Purity, Infinitude, Do-nothing, and No-beginning:--on what the Tâo is.</p>
 <p>8. Star-light and Non-entity.</p>
 <p>9. The Minister of War and his forger of swords.</p>
 <p>10. Zin Khiû and Confucius;--how it was before heaven and earth.</p>
 <p>11. Confucius and Yen Hui:--No demonstration to welcome, no movement to meet.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXIII.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. Käng-sang Khû and the people about Wei-lêi hill.</p>
 <p>2. Käng-sang Khû and his disciples. He repudiates being likened by them to Yâo and Shun.</p>
 <p>3. Käng-sang Khû and the disciple Nan-yung Khû.</p>
 <p>4-12. Lâo-dze lessoning Nan-yung Khû on the principles of Tâoism.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXIV.</h4>
 <p>Pars. 1, 2. Hsü Wû-kwei, Nü Shang, and the marquis Wû of Wei:--Hsü's discourses to the marquis.</p>
 <p>3. Hwang-Tî, with six attending sages, in quest of the Tâo, meets with a wise boy herding horses.</p>
 <p>5. Debate between Kwang-dze and Hui-dze, illustrating the sophistry of the latter.</p>
 <p>6. The artisan Shih cleans the nose of a statue with the wind of his axe; but declines to try his ability on a living subject.</p>
 <p>7. Advice of Kwan Kung on his death-bed to duke Hwan of Khî about his choice of a successor to himself.</p>
 <p>8. The king of Wû and the crafty monkey. His lesson from its death to Yen Pû-î.</p>
 <p>9. Nan-po Dze-khî and his attendant Yen Khäng-dze.</p>
 <p><a name="page_307"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 307</font></a></p>
 <p>The trance is the highest result of the Tâo. Practical lesson to be drawn from it.</p>
 <p>10. Confucius at the court of Khû along with Sun Shû-âo and Î-liâo.</p>
 <p>11. Dze-khî, and his eight sons, with the physiognomist Kiû-fang Yän.</p>
 <p>12. Nieh Khüeh meets Hsü Yû fleeing from the court of Yâo.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXV.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. Zeh-yang seeking an introduction to the king of Khû. Î Kieh, Wang Kwo, and the recluse Kung-yüeh Hsiû.</p>
 <p>3. The ancient sovereign Zän-hsiang; Thang, the founder of the Shang dynasty; Confucius; and Yung-khäng Dze.</p>
 <p>4. King Yung of Wei and his counsellors:--on his desire and schemes to be revenged on Thien Mâu of Khî. Tâi Zin-zän and his apologue about the horns of a snail.</p>
 <p>5. Confucius and the Recluse at Ant-hill in Khû.</p>
 <p>6. The Border-warden of Khang-wû's lessons to Dze-lâo. Kwang-dze's enforcement of them.</p>
 <p>7. Lâo-dze and his disciple Po Kü:--that the prohibitions of Law provoke to transgression.</p>
 <p>8. The conversion to Tâoism of Kü Po-yü.</p>
 <p>9. Confucius and the historiographers;--about the honorary title of duke Ling of Wei.</p>
 <p>10. Little Knowledge and the Correct Harmonizer:--on the Talk of the Hamlets and Villages.</p>
 <p>11. On the namelessness of the Tâo; and that Tâo is but a borrowed or metaphorical name.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXVI.</h4>
 <p>Par. 2. Against delaying to do good when it is in one's power to do it. The apologue of Kwang-dze meeting with a goby on the road.</p>
 <p>3. The big fish caught by the son of the duke of Zän.</p>
 <p>4. The Resurrectionist Students.</p>
 <p><a name="page_308"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 308</font></a></p>
 <p>5. How Lâo Lâi-dze admonished Confucius.</p>
 <p>6. The dream of the ruler Yüan of Sung about a tortoise.</p>
 <p>7. Hui-dze and Kwang-dze;--on the use of being useless.</p>
 <p>11. Illustrations of the evil accruing from going to excess in action, or too suddenly taking action.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXVII.</h4>
 <p>Par. 2. Kwang-dze and Hui-dze on Confucius;--did he change his views in his sixtieth year?</p>
 <p>3. Confucius and his other disciples:--on Zäng-dze and his twice taking office with different moods of mind.</p>
 <p>4. Yen Khing Dze-yû tells his Master Tung-kwo Dze-khî of his gradual attainments.</p>
 <p>5. The penumbrae and the shadows.</p>
 <p>6. Lâo-dze's lessoning of Yang Dze-khî, and its effects on him.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXVIII.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. Yâo's proffers of the throne to Hsü Yû and Dze-kâu Kih-fû. Shun's proffers of it to Dze-kâu Kih-po, to Shan Küan, and to the farmer of Shih-hû. Thâi-wang Than-fû and the northern tribes. Prince Sâu of Yüeh.</p>
 <p>2. Counsel of Dze-hwâ Dze to the marquis Kâo of Han.</p>
 <p>3. The ruler of Lû and the Tâoist Yen Ho, who hides himself from the advances of the other.</p>
 <p>4. Lieh-dze and his wife, on his declining a gift from the ruler of Käng.</p>
 <p>5. The high-minded and resolute sheep-butcher Yüeh, and king Kâo of Khû.</p>
 <p>6. The poor Yüan Hsien and the wealthy Dze-kung. Zäng-dze, in extreme poverty, maintaining his high and independent spirit. The satisfaction of Confucius in Yen Hui refusing, though poor, to take any official post.</p>
 <p>7. Prince Mâu of Kung-shan, living in retirement, was not far from the Tâo.</p>
 <p>8. Confucius and the disciples Yen Hui, Dze-lû, and Dze-kung, during the perilous time between Khän and Zhâi.</p>
 <p><a name="page_309"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 309</font></a></p>
 <p>9. Shun and the northerner Wû-kâi who refuses the throne. Thang, and Pien Sui and Wû Kwang, who both refused it.</p>
 <p>10. The case of the brothers Po-î and Shû-khî, who refused the proffers of king Wû.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXIX.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. The visit of Confucius to the robber Kih, and interview between them.</p>
 <p>2. Dze-kang and Mân Kâu-teh (Mr. Full of Gain-recklessly-got) on the pursuit of wealth.</p>
 <p>3. Mr. Dissatisfied and Mr. Know-the-Mean;--on the pursuit and effect of riches.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXX.</h4>
 <p>How Kwang-dze dealt with the king of Kâo and his swordsmen, curing the king of his love of the sword-fight. The three Swords.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXXI.</h4>
 <p>Confucius and the Old Fisherman;--including the story of the man who tried to run away from his shadow.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXXII.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. Lieh-dze and the effect of his over-manifestation of his attractive qualities. Failure of the warnings of his master.</p>
 <p>2. The sad fate of Hwan of Käng, a Confucianist, who resented his father's taking part with his Mohist brother.</p>
 <p>5. Kû Phing-man and his slaughtering the dragon.</p>
 <p>8. Kwang-dze's rebuke or Zhâo Shang for pandering to the king of Sung, and thereby getting gifts from him.</p>
 <p>9. Description to duke Âi of Lû of Confucius by Yen Ho as unfit to be entrusted with the government.</p>
 <p>11. Khâo-fû the Correct, and his humility.</p>
 <p>12. Kwang-dze's rebuke of the man who boasted of having received chariots from the king of Sung, and comparison of him to the boy who stole a pearl from under the chin of the Black Dragon when he was asleep.</p>
 <p><a name="page_310"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 310</font></a></p>
 <p>13. Kwang-dze declines the offer of official dignity. The apologue of the sacrificial ox.</p>
 <p>14. Kwang-dze, about to die, opposes the wish of his disciples to give him a grand burial. His own description of what his burial should be.</p>
 <h4 align="CENTER">BOOK XXXIII.</h4>
 <p>Par. 1. The method of the Tâo down to the time of Confucius.</p>
 <p>2. The method of Mo Tî and his immediate followers.</p>
 <p>3, 4. The method of Mo's later followers.</p>
 <p>5. The method of Kwan Yin and Lâo-dze.</p>
 <p>6. The method of Kwang-dze.</p>
 <p>7. The ways of Hui Shih, Kung-sun Lung, and other sophists.</p>
