 <h3 align="CENTER">46.</h3>
 <p>46. 1. When the Tâo prevails in the world, they send back their swift horses to (draw) the dung-carts.</p>
 <p><a name="page_89"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 89</font></a></p>
 <p>When the Tâo is disregarded in the world, the war-horses breed in the border lands.</p>
 <p>2. There is no guilt greater than to sanction ambition; no calamity greater than to be discontented with one's lot; no fault greater than the wish to be getting. Therefore the sufficiency of contentment is an enduring and unchanging sufficiency.</p>
 <p><a name="img_08900"></a>
 <img src="img/08900.jpg">, 'The Moderating of Desire or Ambition.' The chapter shows how the practice of the Tâo must conduce to contentment and happiness.</p>
