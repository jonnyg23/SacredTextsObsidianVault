 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Horapollo on hieroglyphics.</font></span>The next writer of importance on hieroglyphics is <b>Horapollo</b>, who towards the close of the IVth century of our era composed a work called Ἱερογλυφικά; this book was translated into Greek by one Philip, of whom nothing is known. Wiedemann thinks that it was originally written in Coptic, which, in the middle ages, was usually called</p>  
 <p><a name="page_124"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 124</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> "Egyptian," and not in ancient Egyptian. <a name="fr_33"></a><a href="#fn_33"><font size="1">1</font></a> In this work are given the explanations of a number of ideographs which occur, for the most part, in Ptolemaïc inscriptions; but, like the list of those given by Chaeremon, no <i>phonetic</i> values of the signs are given. Nevertheless the list is of considerable interest. The best edition of Horapollo is that of Conrad Leemans, <a name="fr_34"></a><a href="#fn_34"><font size="1">2</font></a> but the text was edited in a handy form, with an English translation and notes by Samuel Sharpe and Dr. Birch, by J. Cory, in 1840.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Mediaeval writers on hieroglyphics.</font></span>In more modern times the first writer at any length on hieroglyphics was Athanasius Kircher, the author of some ponderous works <a name="fr_35"></a><a href="#fn_35"><font size="1">3</font></a> in which he pretended to have found the key to the hieroglyphic inscriptions, and to translate them. Though a man of great learning, it must be plainly said that, judged by scholars of to-day, he would be considered an impostor. In his works on Coptic <a name="fr_36"></a><a href="#fn_36"><font size="1">4</font></a> there are, no doubt, many interesting facts, but mixed with them is such an amount of nonsense that <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Kircher and Jablonski.</font></span>Jablonski says touching one of his statements, "Verum hic ut in aliis plurimis fucum lectoribus fecit Jesuita ille, et fumum vendidit"; from the same writer also, Kircher's arrogant assertions called forth the remark, "Kircherus, in quo semper plus inest ostentationis, quam solidae eruditionis." <a name="fr_37"></a><a href="#fn_37"><font size="1">5</font></a> It is impossible to understand what grounds Kircher had for his statements and how he arrived at his results; as for his translations, they have <i>nothing</i> correct in them. Here is one taken at random from <i>Oedipus</i> </p>

 <p>and his translation runs:—"Vitale providi Numinis dominium, quadruplicem Mundani liquoris substantiam dominio confert Osiridis, cujus unà cum Mendesio foecundi Numinis dominio, benefica virtute influente, omnia quae in Mundo sunt, vegetantur, animantur, conservantur." Other writers on hieroglyphics whose works Kircher consulted were John Peter Bolzanius Valerianus, <a name="fr_38"></a><a href="#fn_38"><font size="1">1</font></a> and Mercati, <a name="fr_39"></a><a href="#fn_39"><font size="1">2</font></a> but no good results followed their investigations. In the year 1770 Joseph de Guignes<span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">De Guignes and Zoëga.</font></span> determined the existence of groups of characters having determinatives, <a name="fr_40"></a><a href="#fn_40"><font size="1">3</font></a> and four years later he published his <i>Mémoire</i>, <a name="fr_41"></a><a href="#fn_41"><font size="1">4</font></a> in which he tried to prove that the epistolographic and symbolic characters of the Egyptians were to be found in the Chinese characters, and that the Chinese nation was nothing but an Egyptian colony. In 1797 Zoëga made a step in the right direction, and came to the conclusion <a name="fr_42"></a><a href="#fn_42"><font size="1">5</font></a> that the hieroglyphics were letters and that the cartouches contained royal names. A few years later <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Silvestre de Sacy and Akerblad.</font></span>Silvestre de Sacy published a</p>

 <p><a name="page_126"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 126</font></a></p>  
 <p>letter on the inscriptions on the Rosetta Stone, <a name="fr_43"></a><a href="#fn_43"><font size="1">1</font></a> and the work of this learned man was soon after followed by that of Akerblad who, in a letter to M. de Sacy <a name="fr_44"></a><a href="#fn_44"><font size="1">2</font></a> discussed the demotic inscription on the recently discovered Rosetta Stone, and published an alphabet of the demotic characters, from which a large number were adopted in after times by Young and Champollion. It would seem that Akerblad never gained the credit which was due to him for his really clever work, and it will be seen from the facts quoted in the following pages, how largely the success of Young's labours on the Demotic inscription on the Rosetta Stone depended on those of Akerblad. But side by side with the letters of de Sacy and Akerblad and the learned works of Young and Champollion, there sprang into existence a mass of literature full of absurd statements and theories written by men having no qualifications for expressing opinions on hieroglyphic matters. <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Absurd theories of the contents of Egyptian texts.</font></span>Thus the Comte de Pahlin in his <i>De l’étude des Hiéroglyphes</i>, <a name="fr_45"></a><a href="#fn_45"><font size="1">3</font></a> hesitated not to say that the inscription on one of the porticoes of the Temple at Denderah contained a translation of the hundredth Psalm, composed to invite all people to enter into the house of the Lord. The same author said that to produce the books of the Bible, which were written on papyri, it was only necessary to translate the Psalms of David into Chinese and to write them in the ancient characters of that language. <a name="fr_46"></a><a href="#fn_46"><font size="1">4</font></a> Lenoir considered the Egyptian inscriptions to contain Hebrew compositions, <a name="fr_47"></a><a href="#fn_47"><font size="1">5</font></a> and Lacour thought that they contained Biblical phrases. <a name="fr_48"></a><a href="#fn_48"><font size="1">6</font></a> Worse than all these wild theories was the belief in the works of the Kircher school of investigators, and in the accuracy of the statements made by <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Warburton's views on an Egyptian alphabet.</font></span>Warburton, <a name="fr_49"></a><a href="#fn_49"><font size="1">7</font></a> who, it must be confessed,</p>
