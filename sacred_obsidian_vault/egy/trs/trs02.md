 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Greek writers upon Egyptian hieroglyphics.</font></span>To <b>Hecataeus</b> of Miletus, <a name="fr_5"></a><a href="#fn_5"><font size="1">1</font></a> who visited Egypt between B.C. 513-501, we owe, through <b>Herodotus</b>, much knowledge of Egypt, and he must be considered the earliest Greek writer upon Egypt. <b>Hellanitus</b> of Mytilene, B.C. 478-393, shows in his Αιγυπτιακὰ that he has some accurate knowledge of the meaning of some hieroglyphic words. <a name="fr_6"></a><a href="#fn_6"><font size="1">2</font></a> Democritus wrote upon the hieroglyphics of Meroë, <a name="fr_7"></a><a href="#fn_7"><font size="1">3</font></a> but this work is lost. <b>Herodotus</b> says that the Egyptians used two quite different kinds of writing, one of which is called sacred (hieroglyphic), the other common <a name="fr_8"></a><a href="#fn_8"><font size="1">4</font></a> (demotic). <b>Diodorus</b> says that the Ethiopian letters are called by the Egyptians "hieroglyphics." <a name="fr_9"></a><a href="#fn_9"><font size="1">5</font></a> <b>Strabo</b>, speaking of the obelisks at Thebes, says that there are inscriptions upon them which proclaim the riches and power of their kings, and that their rule extends even to Scythia, Bactria, and India. <a name="fr_10"></a><a href="#fn_10"><font size="1">6</font></a> <b>Chaeremon</b> of Naucratis, who lived in the first half of the first century after Christ, <a name="fr_11"></a><a href="#fn_11"><font size="1">7</font></a> and who must be an entirely different person from Chaeremon the companion of Aelius Gallus (B.C. 25),</p>

 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Greek writers upon Egyptian hieroglyphics.</font></span>derided by Strabo, <a name="fr_12"></a><a href="#fn_12"><font size="1">1</font></a> and charged with lying by Josephus, <a name="fr_13"></a><a href="#fn_13"><font size="1">2</font></a> wrote a work on Egyptian hieroglyphics <a name="fr_14"></a><a href="#fn_14"><font size="1">3</font></a>, περὶ τῶν ἱερῶν γραμμάτων, which has been lost. He appears to have been attached to the great library of Alexandria, and as he was a "sacred scribe," it may therefore be assumed that he had access to many important works on hieroglyphics, and that he understood them. He is mentioned by Eusebius <a name="fr_15"></a><a href="#fn_15"><font size="1">4</font></a> as Χαιρήμων ὁ ἱερογραμματεύς, and by Suidas, <a name="fr_16"></a><a href="#fn_16"><font size="1">5</font></a> but neither of these writers gives any information as to the contents of his work on hieroglyphics, and we should have no idea of the manner of work it was but for the extract preserved by <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">John Tzetzes on Egyptian hieroglyphics.</font></span><b>John Tzetzes</b> (Τζέτζης, born about AḌ. 1110, died after AḌ. 1180). Tzetzes was a man of considerable learning and literary activity, and his works <a name="fr_17"></a><a href="#fn_17"><font size="1">6</font></a> have value on account of the lost books which are quoted in them. In his <i>Chiliades</i> <a name="fr_18"></a><a href="#fn_18"><font size="1">7</font></a> (Bk. V., line 395) he speaks of ὁ Αἰγύπτιος ἱερογραμματεὺς Χαιρήμων, and refers to Chaeremon's διδάγματα τῶν ἱερῶν γραμμάτων. In his Exegesis of Homer's Iliad he gives an extract from the work itself, and we are able to see at once that it was written by one who was able to give his information at first hand. This interesting extract was first brought to the notice of the world by the late Dr. Birch, who published a paper on it in the <i>Transactions of the Royal Society of Literature</i>, Vol. III., second series, 1850, pp. 385-396. In it he quoted the Greek text of the extract, from the edition of Tzetzes' <i>Exegesis</i>, first published by Hermann, <a name="fr_19"></a><a href="#fn_19"><font size="1">8</font></a> and added remarks and hieroglyphic characters illustrative of it, together with the scholia of Tzetzes, the text of which he emended in places.  <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Greek translation of Egyptian text by Hermapion.</font></span>One of the most valuable extracts from the works of Greek and Roman writers on Egypt is that from a translation of an Egyptian obelisk by <b>Hermapion</b>, preserved by Ammianus Marcellinus; <a name="fr_24"></a><a href="#fn_24"><font size="1">2</font></a> unfortunately, however, neither the name of Hermapion's work nor the time in which he lived is known. This extract consists of the Greek translation of six lines of hieroglyphics: three lines are from the south side of the obelisk, one line from the east side, and a second and a third line from the other sides. A comparison of the Greek extract with any inscription of Rameses II. on an obelisk shows at once that Hermapion must have had a certain accurate knowledge of hieroglyphics; his translation of the lines, however does not follow consecutively. The following examples will show that the <span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Comparison of Greek translation with the Egyptian text.</font></span>Greek, in many cases, represents the Egyptian very closely. Λέγει Ἥλιος βασιλεῖ Ῥαμέστῃ· δεδώρημαί σοι ἀνὰ πᾶσαν οἰκουμένην μετὰ χαρᾶς βασιλεύιν, ὃν Ἥλιος φιλεῖ =</p>

 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Flaminian obelisk.</font></span>The Flaminian obelisk, from which the Egyptian passages given above are taken, was brought from Heliopolis to Rome by Augustus, and placed in the Circus Maximus, <a name="fr_25"></a><a href="#fn_25"><font size="1">1</font></a> whence it was dug out; it now stands in the Piazza del Popolo at Rome, where it was set up by Pope Sixtus V. in 1589. <a name="fr_26"></a><a href="#fn_26"><font size="1">2</font></a> This obelisk was originally set up by Seti I., whose inscriptions occupy the middle column of the north, south, and west sides; the other columns of hieroglyphics record the names and titles of Rameses II. who, in this case, appropriated the obelisk of his father, just as he did that of Thothmes III. The obelisk was found broken into three pieces, and in order to render it capable of sustaining itself, three palms’ length was cut from the base. The texts have been published by Kircher, <i>Oedipus Aegyptiacus</i>, t. iii. p. 213; by Ungarelli, <i>Interpretatio Obeliscorum Urbis</i>, Rome, 1842, p. 65, <i>sqq</i>.,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_120"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 120</font></a></p>  
 <p>plate 2; and by Bonomi, who drew them for a paper on this obelisk by the Rev. G. Tomlinson in Trans. Royal Soc. Lit., Vol. I. Second Series, p. 176 ff. For an account of this obelisk, see Zoëga, <i>De Origine et Usu Obeliscorum</i>, Rome, 1797, p. 92.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Champollion's estimate of Clement's statements on hieroglyphics.</font></span>The next Greek writer whose statements on Egyptian hieroglyphics are of value is Clement of Alexandria, who flourished about AḌ. 191-220. According to Champollion, "un seul auteur grec, . . . . . . . . . a démêlé et signalé, dans l’écriture égyptienne sacrée, les élémens phonétiques, lesquels en sont, pour ainsi dire, le principe vital <a name="fr_27"></a><a href="#fn_27"><font size="1">1</font></a> . . . . . Clément d’Alexandrie s’est, lui seul, occasionnellement attaché à en donner une idée claire; et ce philosophe chrétien était, bien plus que tout autre, en position d’en être bien instruit. Lorsque mes recherches et l’étude constante des monuments égyptiens m’eurent conduit aux résultats précédemment exposés, je dus revenir sur ce passage de Saint Clément d’Alexandrie, que j’ai souvent cité, pour savoir si, à la faveur des notions que j’avais tirées d’un examen soutenu des inscriptions hiéroglyphiques, le texte de l’auteur grec ne deviendrait pas plus intelligible qu’il ne l’avait paru jusquelà. J’avoue que ses termes me semblèrent alors si positifs et si clairs, et les idées qu’il renferme si exactement conformes à ma théorie de l’écriture hiéroglyphique, que je dus craindre aussi de me livrer à une illusion et à un entraînement dont tout me commandait de me défier." <a name="fr_28"></a><a href="#fn_28"><font size="1">2</font></a> From the above it will be seen what a high value Champollion placed on the statements concerning the hieroglyphics by Clement, and they have, in consequence, formed the subject of various works by eminent authorities. In his <i>Précis</i> (p. 328), Champollion gives the extract from Clement with a Latin translation and remarks by Letronne. <a name="fr_29"></a><a href="#fn_29"><font size="1">3</font></a> Dulaurier in his <i>Examen d’un passage des Stromates de Saint Clément d’Alexandrie</i>, Paris, 1833, again published the passage and gave many explanations of words in it, and commented learnedly upon it.  <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Translation of extract from Clement.</font></span>"For example, those that are educated among the Egyptians first of all learn that system of Egyptian characters which is styled EPISTOLOGRAPHIC; secondly, the HIERATIC, which the sacred scribes employ; lastly and finally the HIEROGLYPHIC. The hieroglyphic sometimes speaks plainly by means of the letters of the alphabet, and sometimes uses symbols, and when it uses symbols, it sometimes (<i>a</i>) speaks plainly by imitation, and sometimes (<i>b</i>) describes in a figurative way, and sometimes (c) simply says one thing for another in accordance with certain secret rules. Thus (<i>a</i>) if they desire to write <i>sun</i> or <i>moon</i>, they make a circle or a crescent in plain imitation of the form. And when (<i>b</i>) they describe figuratively (by transfer and transposition without violating the natural meaning of words), they completely alter some things and make manifold changes in the form of others. Thus, they hand</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_122"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 122</font></a></p>  
 <p>down the praises of their kings in myths about the gods which they write up in relief. Let this be an example of the third form (<i>c</i>) in accordance with the secret rules. While they represent the stars generally by snakes’ bodies, because their course is crooked, they represent the sun by the body of a beetle, for the beetle moulds a ball from cattle dung and rolls it before him. And they say that this animal lives under ground for six months, and above ground for the other portion of the year, and that it deposits its seed in this globe and there engenders offspring, and that no female beetle exists."</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Three kinds of Egyptian writing.</font></span>From the above we see that Clement rightly stated that the Egyptians had three kinds of writing:—epistolographic, hieratic and hieroglyphic. The epistolographic is that kind which is now called "demotic," and which in the early days of hieroglyphic decipherment was called "enchorial." The hieratic is the kind commonly found on papyri. The hieroglyphic kind is described as, I. <i>cyriologic</i>, that is to say, by means of figurative phonetic characters, <i>e.g.</i>, <a name="img_12200"></a>
 <img src="img/12200.jpg"> <i>emsuḥ</i>, "crocodile," and II. <i>symbolic</i>, that is to say, by actual representations of objects, <i>e.g.</i>, <a name="img_12201"></a>
 <img src="img/12201.jpg"> "goose," <a name="img_12202"></a>
 <img src="img/12202.jpg"> "bee," and so on The symbolic division is subdivided into three parts: I. <i>cyriologic by imitation</i>, <i>e.g.</i>, <a name="img_12203"></a>
 <img src="img/12203.jpg">, a vase with water flowing from it represented a "libation "; II. <i>tropical</i>, <i>e.g.</i>, <a name="img_12204"></a>
 <img src="img/12204.jpg">, a crescent moon to represent "month," <a name="img_12205"></a>
 <img src="img/12205.jpg">, a reed and palette to represent "writing" or "scribe"; and III. <i>enigmatic</i>, <i>e.g.</i>, <a name="img_12206"></a>
 <img src="img/12206.jpg">, a beetle, to represent the "sun." <a name="fr_31"></a><a href="#fn_31"><font size="1">1</font></a> In modern Egyptian Grammars the matter is stated more simply, and we see that hieroglyphic signs are used in two ways: I. Ideographic, II. Phonetic. <a name="img_12207"></a>
 <img src="img/12207.jpg"> <i>māu</i>, "water," is an instance of the first method, and <a name="img_12208"></a>
 <img src="img/12208.jpg"> <i>m-s-u-ḥ</i>, is an instance of the second. Ideographic signs are used as <i>determinatives</i>, and are either <i>ideographic</i> or <i>generic</i>. Thus after <a name="img_12209"></a>
 <img src="img/12209.jpg"> <i>mȧu</i>, "cat," a cat <a name="img_12210"></a>
 <img src="img/12210.jpg"> is placed, and is an <i>ideographic</i> determinative; but <a name="img_12211"></a>
 <img src="img/12211.jpg">, heaven with a star in it, written after a <a name="img_12212"></a>
 <img src="img/12212.jpg"> <i>ḳerḥ</i>, is a</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_123"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 123</font></a></p>  
 <p><i>generic</i> determinative. Phonetic signs are either <i>Alphabetic</i> as <a name="img_12300"></a>
 <img src="img/12300.jpg"> <i>a</i>, <a name="img_12301"></a>
 <img src="img/12301.jpg"> <i>b</i>, <a name="img_12302"></a>
 <img src="img/12302.jpg"> <i>k</i>, or <i>Syllabic</i>, as <a name="img_12303"></a>
 <img src="img/12303.jpg"> <i>men</i>, <a name="img_12304"></a>
 <img src="img/12304.jpg"> <i>chen</i>, etc.</p>  
 <p>Porphyry the Philosopher, who died about AḌ. 305, says of Pythagoras: <a name="fr_32"></a><a href="#fn_32"><font size="1">1</font></a>—</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_12305"></a>
 </p><p align="CENTER">
 <a href="img/12305.jpg"><img src="tn/12305.jpg"><br><font size="-3">Click to enlarge</font></a></p><span class="margnote"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">Pythagoras and hieroglyphics.</font></span>  
 <p>"And in Egypt he lived with the priests and learnt their wisdom and the speech of the Egyptians and three sorts of writing, epistolographic and hieroglyphic and symbolic, which sometimes speak in the common way by imitation and sometimes describe one thing by another in accordance with certain secret rules." Here it seems that Porphyry copied Clement inaccurately. Thus he omits all mention of the Egyptian writing called "hieratic," and of the subdivision of hieroglyphic called "cyriologic," and of the second sub- division of the symbolic called "tropic."
