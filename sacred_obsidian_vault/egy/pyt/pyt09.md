 <h3 align="center">6. MOSTLY SERPENT CHARMS, UTTERANCES 226-243.</h3>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 226</i>.</p>
 
 <p>225a. To say: One serpent is encircled by another serpent,</p>
 
 <p>225b. when a toothless (?) calf born on pasture-land is encircled.</p>
 
 <p>225c. Earth, devour that which has come forth from thee. Monster (beast),
 lie down, glide away.</p>
 
 <p>226a. A servant (holy person) who belongs to the Ennead (pelican) is fallen
 in water.</p>
 
 <p>226b. Serpent, turn over that Rē may see thee.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 227</i>.</p>
 
 <p>227a. To say: The head of the great black bull was cut off.</p>
 
 <p>227b. <i>Hpn.w</i>-serpent, this is said
 to thee. <i>Ḫśr?-n<u>t</u>r</i>-scorpion, this
 is said to thee:</p>
 
 <p>227c. Turn over, glide into the ground. I have said this to thee.</p>
 
 <p><a name="page_71"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 71</font></a></p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 228</i>.</p>
 
 <p>228a. To say: Face falls on face; face sees face.</p>
 
 <p>228b. A knife, coloured black and green, goes out against it, until it has
 swallowed that which it has licked.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 229</i>.</p>
 
 <p>229a. To say: This is the finger-nail of Atum,</p>
 
 <p>229b. which is upon the dorsal vertebra of the <i>Nḥb.w-kȝ.w</i> (serpent) and which caused the strife in <i>Wn.w</i> to cease.</p>
 
 <p>229c. Fall, glide away.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 230</i>.</p>
 
 <p>230a. To say: Be thy two poison-glands in the ground; be thy two rows of
 ribs in the hole.</p>
 
 <p>230b. Pour out the liquid. The two kites stand there.</p>
 
 <p>230c. Thy mouth is closed by the hangman's tool; the mouth of the hangman's
 tool is closed by the <i>mȝfd.t</i> (lynx).</p>
 
 <p>230d. The one made tired is bitten by a serpent.</p>
 
 <p>231a. O Rē, N. has bitten the earth; N. has bitten Geb.</p>
 
 <p>231b. N. has bitten the father of him who bit him.</p>
 
 <p>231c. This is the being who has bitten N., (though) N. did not bite him.</p>
 
 <p>232a. It is he who is come against N., (though) N. does not go against him;</p>
 
 <p>232b. the second moment after he saw N., the second moment after he
 perceived N.</p>
 
 <p>232c. If thou bitest N., he will make one (piece) of thee; if thou regardest
 N., he will make two of thee.</p>
 
 <p>233a. The <i>nw</i>-serpent (male) is
 bitten by the <i>n.t</i>-serpent (female);
 the <i>n.t</i>-serpent is bitten by the <i>nw</i>-serpent.</p>
 
 <p>233b. Heaven is protected magically; earth is protected magically; the
 "manly" who is behind mankind is protected magically.</p>
 
 <p>234a. The god whose head is blind is protected magically; thou thyself,
 scorpion, art protected magically.</p>
 
 <p>234b. These are the two knots (charm) of Elephantiné which are in the mouth
 of Osiris,</p>
 
 <p>234c. which Horus knotted concerning the backbone.</p>
 
 <p><a name="page_72"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 72</font></a></p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 231</i>.</p>
 
 <p>235a. To say: Thy bone is a harpoon-point by which thou wilt be harpooned.
 Hearts are checked; the nomads are in the place of the spear,</p>
 
 <p>235b. they are cast down. That is, the god <i>Ḥmn</i>.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 232</i>.</p>
 
 <p>236a. To say: <i>Mti</i>, <i>Mti</i>, <i>Mti</i>,
 <i>Mti</i>;</p>
 
 <p>236b. <i>Tiw</i>, his mother, <i>Tiw</i>, his mother; <i>Miti</i>, <i>Miti</i>.</p>
 
 <p>236c. Be thou watered (washed), O desert; (let there be) water, not sand.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 233</i>.</p>
 
 <p>237a. To say: The serpent which came forth from the earth is fallen; the
 flame which came forth from Nun is fallen.</p>
 
 <p>237b. Fall; glide away.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 234</i>.</p>
 
 <p>238a. To say: A face is upon thee; thou who art on thy belly. Descend on thy
 backbone, thou who art in thy <i>nȝw.t</i>-bush.</p>
 
 <p>238b. Give away before the serpent who is provided with her two heads.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 235</i>.</p>
 
 <p>239a. To say: <i>Kwtiw</i>, <i>Imḥw</i>, <i>Imḥw</i>.</p>
 
 <p>239b. Thou hast raped the two keepers of the stone door-jamb of <i>It-ti-i-iȝ-i</i>.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 236</i>.</p>
 
 <p>240. To say: <i>Kbbhititibiti Šś</i>,
 son of <i>Ḥifg.t</i>, that is thy name.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 237</i>.</p>
 
 <p>241a. To say: Spittle, which is not dried up (in dust?), (which has not)
 disappeared (flown) into the house of his mother,</p>
 
 <p>241b. serpent (beast), lie down.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 238</i>.</p>
 
 <p>242a. To say: The bread of thy father belongs to thee, <i>Iki-nhii</i>;</p>
 
 <p>242b. thine own bread belongs to thy father and to thee, <i>Ik</i>(<i>i</i>)<i>-nhii</i>.</p>
 
 <p><a name="page_73"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 73</font></a></p>
 
 <p>242c. jewelry, oil, <i>Ḫi-tȝw</i>,
 that is thine ox, the renowned, for whose deed this is being done.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 239</i>.</p>
 
 <p>243a. To say: The white crown is gone forth; she has devoured the Great.</p>
 
 <p>243b. The tongue of the white crown has devoured the Great, yet the tongue
 was not seen.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 240</i>.</p>
 
 <p>244a. To say: The uraeus-serpent belongs to heaven; the centipede of Horus,
 belongs in the earth.</p>
 
 <p>244b. Horus was an ox-herd when he trod on (things). N. treads upon the walk
 (gliding-place) of Horus,</p>
 
 <p>244c. while N. knows not him who is not known.</p>
 
 <p>245a. A face is, upon thee, thou who art in his (thy) <i>nȝw.t</i>-bush; mayest thou be lain on thy back, thou who art in his
 (thy) hole.</p>
 
 <p>245b. Meat-cooker of Horus, escape into the earth. O let the beast, O
 desert, glide away.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 241</i>.</p>
 
 <p>246a. To say: "Spitting of the wall"; "Vomiting of the
 brick,"</p>
 
 <p>246b. that which comes out of thy mouth is thrown back against thyself.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 242</i>.</p>
 
 <p>247a. To say: Extinguished is the flame. The flame-serpent is not found in
 the house of him who possesses Ombos.</p>
 
 <p>247b. It is a serpent, which will bite, which has slipped back into the
 house of him whom it will bite, that it may remain in it.</p>
 
 <p align="center"><i>Utterance
 243</i>.</p>
 
 <p>248a. To say: Two <i>ḥtś</i>-sceptres
 and two <i>ḥtś</i>-sceptres are for both <i><u>d</u>m</i>-cords, (to say) twice, as bread
 which is withheld from thee.</p>
 
 <p>248b. Art thou then really here, art thou then really there? O slave, go
 away.</p>
