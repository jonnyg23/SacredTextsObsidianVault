 <h3 align="CENTER">THE TWENTY-SECOND CEREMONY.</h3>
 <font face="Times Roman,Times New Roman"><p>The next offering was the <i>ah</i> which according to Dümichen was not a bread-cake, but a lump of cooked meal, like the <i>Puls</i> of the Romans and the <i>Polenta</i> of the modern Italians. According to Maspero, it was a flat cake mixed with fat, and perhaps sweetened, and folded like a pancake. Whilst the SEM</p>
 <p><a name="page_82"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 82</font></a></p>
 <p>priest presents this the Kher heb pronounces a formula, which in the Unas text seems to mean,</p>
 <p>"The darkness (or, the night) becometh denser and denser," and in the text of Peta-Amen-apt,</p>
 <p>"The <i>ah</i> food is spread out before thee like a field."</p>
 <p>It is clear that in the one text there is a play of words in <i>ahah</i> and <i>akka</i>, and in the other in <i>ahah</i> and <i>ah</i>, but the exact meanings of the sentences are unknown because we do not understand the allusions.</p>
 <p> </p>
 <p align="CENTER"><a name="img_08200"></a>
 <img src="img/08200.jpg"><br>
 </p></font><font face="Times Roman,Times New Roman" size="1">The Sem priest presenting the <i>Ah</i> meal</font><font face="Times Roman,Times New Roman">.
 <p> </p>
 <p><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font> Dümichen thought that the word <i>akka</i>, i.e., "darkness or night," referred to the colour of the <i>ah</i>-cake, and that it might have been baked to a brown colour which was so dark in comparison with the <i>tept</i> cake that it appeared to be black.</p>
