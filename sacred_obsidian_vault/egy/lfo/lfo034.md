 <h3 align="CENTER">THE THIRTY-SIXTH CEREMONY.</h3>
 <font face="Times Roman,Times New Roman"><p>In the next ceremony the SEM priest offered two linen bandlets, or scarves, or sashes, called <i>unkhu</i> to the deceased, and as he did so the Kher heb said-</p>
 <p>"Watch thou in peace. The goddess TAAT watcheth in peace, the goddess TAAT watcheth in peace. The Eye of Horus which is in the city of Pe-Tep watcheth in peace. The Eye of Horus which is in the temple houses of Net watcheth in peace. Receive thou the milk-[white] and bleached bandlets of the goddess Ur-a. Cause ye, O bandlets, that the Two Lands may submit to this Unas, even as they bow down before Horus, and make ye the Two Lands to be in awe of Unas, even as they are terrified before Set. Tarry ye before Unas in his divinity. Open ye his way at the head of the KHU (or, Spirits), and let him stand at the head of the Spirits. O Anpu-Khenti Amenti, forwards, forwards, to the Osiris Unas."</p>
 <p><a name="page_93"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 93</font></a></p>
 <p>The two bandlets are assumed to have been made by the goddess Taat, who presided over the apparel of the deceased, and provided them with raiment made by her own hands; as she wove the bandlets she wove into them her magical protection, which not only preserved their wearers from the discomforts of nakedness, but assured to them the respect of the gods and spirits who</p>
 <p> </p>
 <p align="CENTER"><a name="img_09300"></a>
 <img src="img/09300.jpg"><br>
 The Sem priest presenting two bandlets.</p>
 <p> </p>
 <p>saw them. One bandlet also carried with it the protection of the Horus-god who dwelt in the city of Pe-Tep, or Buto, and the other the protection of the Horus-god who dwelt in the city of Net (Neith). Horus of re-Tep was the son of Isis-Uatchit, and Horus of Sais was the son of Net, the goddess to whom</p>
 <p><a name="page_94"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 94</font></a></p>
 <p>the invention of the art of weaving was attributed. The city of Sais was famous in all ages for the production of textile fabrics, and here was situated "Hetmenkh," the "Temple of woven stuffs." <a name="fr_67"></a><a href="#fn_67"><font size="1">1</font></a> The opening lines of this passage in the Liturgy are differently translated by Dümichen and Maspero. In his translation of the text of Unas Maspero rendered the signs <a name="img_09400"></a>
 <img src="img/09400.jpg"> by "Vêts-toi," "clothe thyself," but later he came to the conclusion that <a name="img_09401"></a>
 <img src="img/09401.jpg"> is not the equivalent of <a name="img_09402"></a>
 <img src="img/09402.jpg">, and that it is to be read <i>res</i>, i.e., "Watch," or "Keep vigil." Thus his renderings are:--</p></font>
 <p align="CENTER"></p><center><table cellspacing="0" border="0" cellpadding="9" width="798">
 <tr><td width="50%" valign="TOP">
 <p><font face="Times Roman,Times New Roman">Vêts-toi en paix! Vêts ton vêtement en paix! Que Taït se vête--<i>Vêtements de fête, deux</i>--en paix! il d'Hor dans Doup, en paix! il d'Horus dans les demeures de Nit, en paix! Reçois le linge blanc! Donne qu'elles se courbent pour cet Ounas, les deux terres, &amp;c.</font></p></td>
 <td width="50%" valign="TOP">
 <font face="Times Roman,Times New Roman"><p>Veille en paix! veille Tait en paix, veille Taitit en paix! il d'Hor qui est dans Dopou en paix, veille il d'Hor qui est dans les <i>Châteaux de Nit</i> en paix! La plus brillante(?) des nourrices(?), celle qui orne le maitre du tombeau (?), donne que les deux terres se courbent devant ce Pepi Nofirkerî, &amp;c. <a name="fr_68"></a><a href="#fn_68"><font size="1">2</font></a></p></font></td>
 </tr>
 </table>
 </center>
 
 <font face="Times Roman,Times New Roman"><p align="CENTER"> </p>
 <p>The rendering of Dümichen agrees with the older rendering of Maspero as far as "Nit, en paix!" but</p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p>
 </p>
 <p><a name="page_95"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 95</font></a></p>
 <p>for the following words he has "Nimin in Empfang die milchfarbige und die gebleichte von der Göttin Ur-a. Bewirket, dass sich in Ehrfurcht beugen die Länder," &amp;c. <a name="fr_69"></a><a href="#fn_69"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>
 <p>At this stage in the service an interval appears to have been allowed to the deceased to absorb the meat and drink offerings which had been presented to him, and to take possession of the bandlets, &amp;c. In the temples the presentation of offerings to the god was nearly always accompanied by a burning of incense, <a name="fr_70"></a><a href="#fn_70"><font size="1">2</font></a> for the gods rejoiced in the mingled odours of the objects offered and the burning incense.</p>
