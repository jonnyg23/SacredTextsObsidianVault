 <p><a name="page_32"><font size="1" color="green">p. 32</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER II.</h3>  <h4 align="center">ON THE GAMMADION OR SWASTIKA.</h4>  
 <p><small>I. Geographical distribution of the <i>gammadion</i>.—Different patterns of the <i>gammadion</i>.—Its common occurrence amongst all the nations of the Old World, with the exception of the Egyptians, the Phœnicians, the Mesopotamians, and the Persians.—The <i>fylfot</i>.—The <i>swastika</i>.</small></p>  
 <p>II. Previous interpretations of the <i>gammadion</i>.—Opinions of Messrs. George Birdwood, Alexander Cunningham, Waring, W. Schwartz, Emile Burnouf, R. P. Greg, Ludwig Müller, and others.</p>  
 <p>III. Probable meaning of the <i>gammadion</i>.—The <i>gammadion</i> a charm.—The <i>gammadion</i> symbolical of the solar movement, and, by analogy, of the heavenly bodies in general.—The arms of the <i>gammadion</i> are rays which move.—Connection between the <i>tétrascèle</i> and the <i>triscèle</i>.—Figures connected with the <i>gammadion</i>.—Equivalence of the <i>gammadion</i> and certain solar images.—The Three Steps of Vishnu.—Lunar <i>tétrascèles</i>.</p>  
 <p>IV. Cradle of the <i>gammadion</i>.—Was it conceived simultaneously in several places?—Uniformity of its meaning and use.—Discussion as to its Aryan or Pelasgic origin.—Information furnished by the "whorls" of Hissarlik and the prehistoric pottery of Northern Italy.—The question is archæological, not ethnical.—Conclusions.</p>  <h4 align="center">I. <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Geographical Distribution of the Gammadion</span>.</h4>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig014"></a>
 <img src="img/fig014.jpg" alt="FIG. 14. GAMMADIONS."></p>  
 <p align="center"><small><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Fig. 14. Gammadions</span></small></p>  
 <p><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">The</span> name <i>gammadion</i> is given to that form of cross whose extremities are bent back at right</p>  
 <p><a name="page_33"><font size="1" color="green">p. 33</font></a></p>  
 <p>angles, as if to form four <i>gammas</i> joined together at the base (fig. 14).</p>  
 <p>It may be called a cross <i>pattée</i> when the bent parts end in a point so as to form a sort of foot (<i></i><a href="#img_fig015">fig. 15a</a>), and a cross with hooks when the arms after being bent a first time are again twisted either inwards (<i></i><a href="#img_fig015">15b</a>), or outwards (<i></i><a href="#img_fig015">15c</a>). Lastly, it takes the name of <i>tétrascèle</i> when the arms are rounded off whilst curving backwards (<i></i><a href="#img_fig015">15d</a>).</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig015"></a>
 <img src="img/fig015.jpg" alt="FIG. 15. VARIETIES OF THE GAMMADION."></p>  
 <p align="center"><small><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Fig. 15. Varieties of the Gammadion</span></small></p>  
 <p>With the exception of the solar Disk and the Greek Cross there are few symbolical marks so widely distributed.</p>  
 <p>Dr. Schliemann, exploring the <i>débris</i> of the towns piled upon the plateau of Hissarlik, beginning with the second or "burnt city," which the learned explorer identifies with the Ilium of Priam, <a name="fr_51"></a><a href="#fn_51"><font size="1">1</font></a> found innumerable <i>gammadions</i>, especially amongst the decorations of those terra cotta disks which have been thought to be "whorls," and which served perhaps as <i>ex voto</i>. <a name="fr_52"></a><a href="#fn_52"><font size="1">2</font></a> It likewise ornaments certain idols of feminine shape, which recall roughly the appearance of the Chaldæan Ishtar; in one of these statuettes, a leaden one, it occupies the centre of the triangle denoting the belly. <a name="fr_53"></a><a href="#fn_53"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>In Greece, as in Cyprus and at Rhodes, it first appears on that pottery with geometrical ornamentation which constitutes the second period of</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_34"><font size="1" color="green">p. 34</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Grecian ceramics; <a name="fr_54"></a><a href="#fn_54"><font size="1">1</font></a> it then passes to those vases with decorations taken from living objects, whose appearance seems to coincide with the development of Phœnician influences on the shores of Greece. <a name="fr_55"></a><a href="#fn_55"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>It is seen on the archaic pottery of Cyprus, and of Rhodes, and of Athens, on both sides of the conventional Tree, so frequently reproduced on the inscribed monuments of Hither Asia between two monsters facing each other (see further on, <a href="mosy11.htm#img_pl04">pl. iv</a>.). It appears on an Athenian vase in a burial scene, three times repeated in front of the funeral car. <a name="fr_56"></a><a href="#fn_56"><font size="1">3</font></a> On a vase from Thera several <i>gammadions</i> are reproduced round an image of the Persian Artemis. <a name="fr_57"></a><a href="#fn_57"><font size="1">4</font></a> At Mycenæ it figures on ornaments collected during Dr. Schliemann's excavations. <a name="fr_58"></a><a href="#fn_58"><font size="1">5</font></a> At Pergamus it adorns the balustrade of the portico which surrounded the temple of Athene, and at Orchomenus the sculptured roof of the so-called nuptial chamber in the palace of the Treasury. <a name="fr_59"></a><a href="#fn_59"><font size="1">6</font></a> Lastly, when the introduction of money disclosed a new outlet for the symbolic forms of religion and of art, it became a favourite emblem in the coinage, not only of the Archipelago and of Greece Proper, but also of Macedon, Thrace, Crete, Lycia, and Paphlagonia.</p>  
 <p>From Corinth, where it figures amongst the most ancient mint marks, it passed to Syracuse under Timoleon, to be afterwards spread abroad on the</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_35"><font size="1" color="green">p. 35</font></a></p>  
 <p>coins of Sicily and of Magna Græcia. <a name="fr_60"></a><a href="#fn_60"><font size="1">1</font></a> In Northern Italy it was known even before the advent of the Etruscans, for it has been met with on pottery dating from the terramare civilization. <a name="fr_61"></a><a href="#fn_61"><font size="1">2</font></a> It appears also on the roof of those ossuaries, in the form of a hut, which reproduce on a small scale the wicker hovels of the people of that epoch. <a name="fr_62"></a><a href="#fn_62"><font size="1">3</font></a> In the Villanova period it adorns vases with geometrical decorations found at Cære, Chiusi, Albano, and at Cumæ, <a name="fr_63"></a><a href="#fn_63"><font size="1">4</font></a> and when Etruria became accessible to oriental influences it appears on <i>fibulæ</i> and other golden ornaments. <a name="fr_64"></a><a href="#fn_64"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>  
 <p>At a still later period it is found on the breasts of personages decorating the walls of a Samnite tomb near Capua; <a name="fr_65"></a><a href="#fn_65"><font size="1">6</font></a> lastly it appears as a <i>motif</i> for decoration in the Roman mosaics. It is singular that at Rome itself it has not been met with on any monument prior to the third, or perhaps the fourth, century of our era. About that period the Christians of the Catacombs had no hesitation in including it amongst their representations of the Cross of Christ. Not only did they carve it upon the tombs, but they also used it to ornament the garments of certain priestly personages, such as the <i>fossores</i>, and even the tunic of the Good Shepherd. <a name="fr_66"></a><a href="#fn_66"><font size="1">7</font></a> At Milan it forms a row of curved Crosses round the pulpit of St. Ambrose.</p>  
 <p>On the other hand, it appears to have been</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_36"><font size="1" color="green">p. 36</font></a></p>  
 <p>widely distributed throughout the provinces of the Roman empire, especially among the Celts, where in many cases it is difficult to decide whether it is connected with imported civilization, or with indigenous tradition. From Switzerland, and even from the Danubian countries, to the most remote parts of Great Britain, it has been found on vases, on metal plates, on <i>fibulæ</i>, on sword belts, and on arms. <a name="fr_67"></a><a href="#fn_67"><font size="1">1</font></a> In England it adorns fragments of mosaics collected from the ruins of several villas, <a name="fr_68"></a><a href="#fn_68"><font size="1">2</font></a> as well as a funeral urn unearthed in a mound of the bronze age. <a name="fr_69"></a><a href="#fn_69"><font size="1">3</font></a> In Gaul it is observed frequently enough on coins ranging from the third century B.C. to the third century of our era, and even later, for it is met with on a Merovingian piece. <a name="fr_70"></a><a href="#fn_70"><font size="1">4</font></a> We may add that it already figures on fragments of pottery and even on terra cotta matrices found in a lacustrine city in Lake Bourget. <a name="fr_71"></a><a href="#fn_71"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>  
 <p>In Belgium, we meet with it at Estinnes (Hainault) and at Anthée (Province of Namur) in tile <i>débris</i> dating back to the Roman epoch. <a name="fr_72"></a><a href="#fn_72"><font size="1">6</font></a> It is also seen repeated several times, in association with the Lotus-flower, among the inscriptions on tombstones discovered, some years ago, in the Belgo-Roman cemetery of Juslenville, near Pepinster (<a href="#img_fig016">fig. 16</a>).</p>  
 <p>An interesting discussion, arose in the <i>Institut archéologique liégeois</i> as to whether—in spite of the invocation D[iis] M[anibus]—the presence of the <i>gammadion</i> did not imply the Christian</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_37"><font size="1" color="green">p. 37</font></a></p>  
 <p>character of this sepulchral monument. <a name="fr_73"></a><a href="#fn_73"><font size="1">1</font></a> To the arguments brought forward to refute this theory we may add that a sepulchral <i>stele</i> of an unquestionably</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig016"></a>
 <img src="img/fig016.jpg" alt="FIG. 16. TOMBSTONE FROM JUSLENVILLE. (Institut archéologique liègeois, vol. x. (1870), pl. xiii.)"></p>  
 <p align="center"><small><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Fig. 16. Tombstone from Juslenville</span><br> (<i>Institut archéologique liègeois</i>, vol. x. (1870), pl. xiii.)</small></p>  
 <p>pagan character, discovered in Algeria, offers an analogous combination of two <i>gammadions</i> placed over a Wheel.</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig017"></a>
 <img src="img/fig017.jpg" alt="FIG. 17. LIBYAN SEPULCHRAL STELE. (Proceedings of the Soc. franc. de numism. et d’archéol., vol. ii., pl. iii. 3.)"></p>  
 <p align="center"><small><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Fig. 17. Libyan Sepulchral Stele</span>.<br> (Proceedings of the <i>Soc. franc. de numism. et d’archéol.</i>, vol. ii., pl. iii. 3.)</small></p>  
 <p>The fact may be mentioned that in the middle of the Christian era, eleven or twelve centuries later, the <i>gammadion</i> reappears on a sepulchre in</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_38"><font size="1" color="green">p. 38</font></a></p>  
 <p>the same Belgian province. On a tombstone of the fourteenth century, discovered in 1871, during the construction of a tunnel at Huy, three personages are sculptured, one of whom is a priest clothed in a chasuble, and on this chasuble three bands of <i>gammadions</i> can be distinctly seen. <a name="fr_74"></a><a href="#fn_74"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>  
 <p>The <i>gammadion</i>, associated with the Wheel, as well as with the Thunderbolt, likewise adorns votive altars found, in England, and near the Pyrenees, on the site of Roman encampments. <a name="fr_75"></a><a href="#fn_75"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig018"></a>
 <img src="img/fig018.jpg" alt="FIG. 18. ALTAR IN THE TOULOUSE MUSEUM. (Reveu archéologique de 1880, vol. xl. p. 17.)"></p>  
 <p align="center"><small><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Fig. 18. Altar in the Toulouse Museum</span>.<br> (<i>Reveu archéologique de</i> 1880, vol. xl. p. 17.)</small></p>  
 <p>At Velaux, in the Bouches-du-Rhône department, there has been found the headless statue of a god sitting cross-legged, who bore on its breast a row of crocketed crosses surmounting another row of equilateral crosses. <a name="fr_76"></a><a href="#fn_76"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>In Ireland, however, and in Scotland, the <i>gammadion</i> seems really to have marked Christian sepulchres, for it is met with on tombstones associated with Latin Crosses. <a name="fr_77"></a><a href="#fn_77"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>The Rev. Charles Graves, Bishop of Limerick, has described an ogham stone found in an abandoned</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_39"><font size="1" color="green">p. 39</font></a></p>  
 <p>cemetery in Kerry, which he believes to belong to the sixth century; it bears an arrow between two <i>gammadions</i>. <a name="fr_78"></a><a href="#fn_78"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>  
 <p>The Anglo-Saxons gave to the <i>gammadion</i> the name of <i>fylfot</i>, from the Norse <i>fiöl</i> (full, <i>viel</i> = "numerous"), and <i>fot</i> (foot). <a name="fr_79"></a><a href="#fn_79"><font size="1">2</font></a> It has been observed on pottery and funeral urns of the bronze age in Silesia, in Pomerania, and the eastern islands of Denmark. In the following ages it is met with on ornaments, on sword-hilts, on golden brackets, on sculptured rocks, and on tombstones. <a name="fr_80"></a><a href="#fn_80"><font size="1">3</font></a> Amongst the Scandinavians it ended by combining, doubtlessly</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig019"></a>
 <img src="img/fig019.jpg" alt="FIG. 19. CROSS ON A RUNIC STONE FROM SWEDEN. (LUDWIG MÜLLER, p. 94, fig. a.)"></p>  
 <p align="center"><small><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Fig. 19. Cross On A Runic Stone From Sweden</span>.<br> (<span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Ludwig Müller</span>, p. 94, fig. a.)</small></p>  
 <p>under the influence of Christianity, with the Latin Cross.</p>  
 <p>In an old Danish church it ornaments baptismal fonts which date from the early times of Christianity. <a name="fr_81"></a><a href="#fn_81"><font size="1">4</font></a> In Iceland, according to Mr. Hjaltalin, it is still in use at the present day as a magic sign. <a name="fr_82"></a><a href="#fn_82"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>  
 <p>Amongst the Slays and Finns it has not yet been found, save in a sporadic state, and about the period of their conversion to Christianity only. We may remark, by the way, that it is very difficult</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_40"><font size="1" color="green">p. 40</font></a></p>  
 <p>to determine the age and nationality of the terra cotta or bronze objects on which it has been observed in countries of mixed or superposed races, such as Hungary, Poland, Lithuania and Bohemia.</p>  
 <p>In the Caucasus, M. Chantre has met with it on ear-drops, ornamental plates, sword-hilts, and other objects found in burial-places dating back to the bronze period and the first iron age. <a name="fr_83"></a><a href="#fn_83"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>  
 <p>Amongst the Persians its presence has been pointed out on some Arsacian and Sassanian coins only. <a name="fr_84"></a><a href="#fn_84"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>The Hittites introduced it on a bas-relief at Ibriz, in Lycaonia, where it forms a border on the dress of a king, or priest, who offers up a sacrifice to a god. <a name="fr_85"></a><a href="#fn_85"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>The Phœnicians do not seem to have known or, at least, to have used it, except on some of the coins which they struck in Sicily in imitation of Greek pieces. A coin of Byzacium on which it is figured, near the head of Astarte, dates from the reign of Augustus. <a name="fr_86"></a><a href="#fn_86"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>It is not met with either in Egypt, in Assyria, or in Chaldæa.</p>  
 <p>In India it bears the name of <i>swastika</i>, when its arms are bent towards the right (<i></i><a href="#img_fig014">fig. 14a</a>), and <i>sauwastika</i> when they are turned in the other direction (<i></i><a href="#img_fig014">fig. 14b</a>). The word <i>swastika</i> is a derivative of <i>swasti</i>, which again comes from <i>su</i> = well, and the verb <i>asti</i> = it is; the expression would seem therefore to correspond with a Greek formula—εὐ εστὶ, and, in fact, amongst the Hindus</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_41"><font size="1" color="green">p. 41</font></a></p>  
 <p>as amongst the Buddhists, its representation has always passed for a propitious sign. <a name="fr_87"></a><a href="#fn_87"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>  
 <p>The grammarian Panini mentions it as a character used for earmarking cattle. We see in the <i>Ramayana</i> that the ships of the fleet, on which Bharata embarked for Ceylon, bore, doubtlessly on their bows, the sign of the <i>swastika</i>. <a name="fr_88"></a><a href="#fn_88"><font size="1">2</font></a> Passing now to inscribed monuments we find the <i>gammadion</i> on the bars of silver, shaped like dominos, which, in certain parts of India, preceded the use of money proper. <a name="fr_89"></a><a href="#fn_89"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>It even appears upon a coin of Krananda, which is held to be the oldest Indian coin, and which is</p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig020"></a>
 <img src="img/fig020.jpg" alt="FIG. 20. ANCIENT INDIAN COIN. (Archæological Survey of India, vol. x., pl. ii., fig. 8.)"></p>  
 <p align="center"><small><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Fig. 20. Ancient Indian Coin</span>.<br> (<i>Archæological Survey of India</i>, vol. x., pl. ii., fig. 8.)</small></p>  
 <p>likewise remarkable as exhibiting the first representation of the <i>trisula</i>. <a name="fr_90"></a><a href="#fn_90"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>Occurring frequently at the beginning and the end of the most ancient Buddhist inscriptions, several examples of it are to be seen on the Foot-Prints of Buddha sculptured at Amravati. <a name="fr_91"></a><a href="#fn_91"><font size="1">5</font></a> The <i>swastika</i> represents, moreover, according to Buddhist tradition, the first of the sixty-five marks which distinguished the Master's feet, whilst the</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_42"><font size="1" color="green">p. 42</font></a></p>  
 <p>fourth is formed by the <i>sauwastika</i>, and the third by the <i>nandyavarta</i>, a kind of labyrinth, which, in the manner of the Greek <i>meander</i>, may be connected with the <i>gammadion</i>. <a name="fr_92"></a><a href="#fn_92"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>  
 <p align="center"><a name="img_fig021"></a>
 <img src="img/fig021.jpg" alt="FIG. 21. THE NANDYAVARTA."></p>  
 <p align="center"><small><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Fig. 21. The Nandyavarta</span>.</small></p>  
 <p>It must be observed that amongst the Jains, the <i>gammadion</i> is regarded as the emblem of Suparsva, the seventh of the twenty-four <i>Tirthankaras</i>, whilst the <i>nandyavarta</i> is that of the eighteenth. <a name="fr_93"></a><a href="#fn_93"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>Even at the present day, according to Mr. Taylor, the Hindus, at the time of the new year, paint a <i>gammadion</i> in red at the commencement of their account books, and, in their weddings and other ceremonies, they sketch it in flour on the floors of their houses. <a name="fr_94"></a><a href="#fn_94"><font size="1">3</font></a> It also figures at the end of manuscripts of a recent period, at least under a form which, according to M. Kern, is a development of the <i>tétrascèle</i>. <a name="fr_95"></a><a href="#fn_95"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>The <i>gammadion</i> has been likewise preserved to the present time amongst the Buddhists of Tibet, where the women make use of it in the ornamentation of their skirts, and where it is placed on the</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_43"><font size="1" color="green">p. 43</font></a></p>  
 <p>breasts of the dead. <a name="fr_96"></a><a href="#fn_96"><font size="1">1</font></a> In China—where it bears the name of <i>ouan</i>—and in Japan it adorns vases, caskets, and the representations of divinities, as may be seen in the Musée Guimet at Paris; it is even figured upon the breasts of certain statues of Buddha and the Buddhisattvas, where, according to M. Paléologue, it would seem to symbolize the heart. <a name="fr_97"></a><a href="#fn_97"><font size="1">2</font></a> According to another interpretation, given by the Annamite bonzes, it might be the cicatrice of a spear-thrust received by Buddha; but these bonzes, according to M. G. Dumoutier, continue to venerate this symbol without understanding it. <a name="fr_98"></a><a href="#fn_98"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>In the Woolwich Arsenal the <i>gammadion</i> may be seen upon a cannon captured at the Taku forts by the English. According to M. G. Dumoutier it is nothing else than the ancient Chinese character <i>che</i>, which implies the idea of perfection, of excellence, and would seem to signify the renewal and the endless duration of life. <a name="fr_99"></a><a href="#fn_99"><font size="1">4</font></a> In Japan, according to M. de Milloué, it represents the number 10,000, which symbolizes that which is infinite, perfect, excellent, and is employed as a sign of felicity. <a name="fr_100"></a><a href="#fn_100"><font size="1">5</font></a> A statue of the Buddhisattva Jiso, in the Musée Guimet, rests upon a pedestal ornamented with <i>swastikas</i>.</p>  
 <p>Lastly let us conclude this long recital, which is in danger of becoming tedious without hope of being complete, by mentioning the presence of the <i>gammadion</i> in Africa, on bronzes brought from Coomassie by the last English Ashantee</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>
