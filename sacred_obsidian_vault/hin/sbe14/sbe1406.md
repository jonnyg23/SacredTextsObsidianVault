   <h3 align="center">CHAPTER III.</h3>  
 <p>1. (Brâhma<i>n</i>as) who neither study nor teach the Veda nor keep sacred fires become equal to <i>S</i>ûdras; <a name="fr_117"></a><a href="#fn_117"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>  
 <p>2. And they quote a verse of Manu on this (subject), 'A twice-born man, who not having studied the Veda applies himself to other (and worldly study), soon falls, even while living, to the condition of a <i>S</i>ûdra, and his descendants after him.' <a name="fr_118"></a><a href="#fn_118"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>3. '(A twice-born man) who does not know the <a name="fr_119"></a><a href="#fn_119"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_17"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 17</font></a></p>  
 <p><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font> Veda (can)not be (called) a Brâhma<i>n</i>a, nor he who lives by trade, nor he who (lives as) an actor, nor he who obeys a <i>S</i>ûdra's commands, nor (he who like) a thief (takes the property of others), nor he who makes his living by the practice of medicine.'</p>  
 <p>4. 'The king shall punish that village where Brâhma<i>n</i>as, unobservant of their sacred duties and ignorant of the Veda, subsist by begging; for it feeds robbers.'</p>  
 <p>5. 'Many thousands (of Brâhma<i>n</i>as) cannot form a (legal) assembly (for declaring the sacred law), if they have not fulfilled their sacred duties, are unacquainted with the Veda, and subsist only by the name of their caste.' <a name="fr_120"></a><a href="#fn_120"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>  
 <p>6. 'That sin which dunces, perplexed by ignorance and unacquainted with the sacred law, declare (to be duty) shall fall, increased a hundredfold, on those who propound it.' <a name="fr_121"></a><a href="#fn_121"><font size="1">6</font></a></p>  
 <p>7. 'What four or (even) three (Brâhma<i>n</i>as) who have completely studied the Vedas proclaim, that must be distinctly recognised as the sacred law, not (the decision) of a thousand fools.' <a name="fr_122"></a><a href="#fn_122"><font size="1">7</font></a></p>  
 <p>8. 'Offerings to the gods and to the manes must always be given to a <i>S</i>rotriya alone. For gifts</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_18"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 18</font></a></p>  
 <p>bestowed on a man unacquainted with the Veda, reach neither the ancestors nor the gods.'</p>  
 <p>9. 'If a fool lives even in one's house and a (Brâhma<i>n</i>a) deeply learned in the Veda lives at a great distance, the learned man shall receive the gift. The sin of neglecting (a Brâhma<i>n</i>a is not incurred) in (the case of) a fool.' <a name="fr_123"></a><a href="#fn_123"><font size="1">9</font></a></p>  
 <p>10. 'The offence of neglecting a Brâhma<i>n</i>a cannot be committed against a twice-born man who is ignorant of the Veda. For (in offering sacrifices) one does not pass by a brilliant fire and throw the oblations into ashes.' <a name="fr_124"></a><a href="#fn_124"><font size="1">10</font></a></p>  
 <p>11. An elephant made of wood, an antelope made of leather, and a Brâhma<i>n</i>a ignorant of the Veda, those three have nothing but the name (of their kind). <a name="fr_125"></a><a href="#fn_125"><font size="1">11</font></a></p>  
 <p>12. 'Those kingdoms, where ignorant men eat the food of the learned, will be visited by drought; or (some other) great evil will befall (them).'</p>  
 <p>13. If anybody finds treasure (the owner of) which is not known, the king shall take it, giving one sixth to the finder. <a name="fr_126"></a><a href="#fn_126"><font size="1">13</font></a></p>  
 <p>14. If a Brâhma<i>n</i>a who follows the six (lawful) occupations, finds it, the king shall not take it.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_19"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 19</font></a></p>  
 <p>15. They declare that the slayer commits no crime by killing an assassin. <a name="fr_127"></a><a href="#fn_127"><font size="1">15</font></a></p>  
 <p>16. Now they quote also (the following verses): 'An incendiary, likewise a poisoner, one who holds a weapon in his hand (ready to kill), a robber, he who takes away land, and he who abducts (another man's) wife, these six are called assassins (âtatâyin).'</p>  
 <p>17. 'He may slay an assassin who comes with the intention of slaying, even though he knows the whole Veda together with the Upanishads; by that (act) he (does) not (incur the guilt of) the slayer of a Brâhma<i>n</i>a.' <a name="fr_128"></a><a href="#fn_128"><font size="1">17</font></a></p>  
 <p>18. 'He who slays an assassin learned in the Veda and belonging to a noble family, does not incur by that act the guilt of the murderer of a learned Brâhma<i>n</i>a.; (in) that (case) fury recoils upon fury.'</p>  
 <p>19. Persons who sanctify the company are, a Tri<i>n</i>â<i>k</i>iketa, one who keeps five fires, a Trisupar<i>n</i>a, one who (knows the texts required for) the four sacrifices (called A<i>s</i>vamedha, Purushamedha, Sarvamedha, and Pit<i>ri</i>medha), one who knows the Vâ<i>g</i>asaneyi-<i>s</i>âkhâ of the White Ya<i>g</i>ur-veda, one who knows the six Aṅgas, the son of a female married according to the Brâhma-rite, one who knows the first part of the Sâma-veda Sa<i>m</i>hitâ, one who sings the Gyesh<i>th</i>asâman, one who knows the Sa<i>m</i>hitâ and the Brâhma<i>n</i>a, one who studies (the treatises on) the sacred law, one whose ancestors to the ninth degree, both <a name="fr_129"></a><a href="#fn_129"><font size="1">19</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_20"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 20</font></a></p>  
 <p>on the mother's and on the father's side, are distinctly known to have been <i>S</i>rotriyas, and learned men and Snâtakas.</p>  
 <p>20. (Four students of) the four Vedas, one who knows the Mîmâ<i>m</i>sâ, one who knows the Aṅgas, a teacher of the sacred law, and three eminent men who are in three (different) orders, (compose) a (legal) assembly consisting at least of ten (members). <a name="fr_130"></a><a href="#fn_130"><font size="1">20</font></a></p>  
 <p>21. He who initiates (a pupil) and teaches him the whole Veda is called the teacher (â<i>k</i>ârya). <a name="fr_131"></a><a href="#fn_131"><font size="1">21</font></a></p>  
 <p>22. But he who (teaches) a portion (of the Veda only is called) the sub-teacher (upâdhyâya);</p>  
 <p>23. So is he who (teaches) the Aṅgas of the Veda.</p>  
 <p>24. A Brâhma<i>n</i>a and a Vai<i>s</i>ya may take up arms in self-defence, and in (order to prevent) a confusion of the castes. <a name="fr_132"></a><a href="#fn_132"><font size="1">24</font></a></p>  
 <p>25. But that (trade of arms) is the constant (duty) of a Kshatriya, because he is appointed to protect (the people). <a name="fr_133"></a><a href="#fn_133"><font size="1">25</font></a></p>  
 <p>26. Having washed his feet and his hands up to <a name="fr_134"></a><a href="#fn_134"><font size="1">26</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_21"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 21</font></a></p>  
 <p>the wrist, and sitting with his face turned towards the east or towards the north, he shall thrice sip water out of the Tîrtha sacred to Brahman, (i.e.) the part of the hand above the root of the thumb, without uttering any sound;</p>  
 <p>27. He shall twice wipe (his mouth with the root of the thumb);.</p>  
 <p>28. He shall touch the cavities (of the head) with water;</p>  
 <p>29. He shall pour water on his head and on the left hand;</p>  
 <p>30. He shall not sip water while walking, standing., lying down or bending forward. <a name="fr_135"></a><a href="#fn_135"><font size="1">30</font></a></p>  
 <p>31. A Brâhma<i>n</i>a (becomes pure) by (sipping) water, free from bubbles and foam, that reaches his heart,</p>  
 <p>32. But a Kshatriya by (sipping water) that reaches his throat,</p>  
 <p>33. A Vai<i>s</i>ya by (sipping water) that wets his palate,</p>  
 <p>34. A woman and a <i>S</i>ûdra by merely touching water (with the lips).</p>  
 <p>35. Water (for sipping may) even (be taken) out of a hole in the ground, if it is fit to slake the thirst of cows. <a name="fr_136"></a><a href="#fn_136"><font size="1">35</font></a></p>  
 <p>36. (He shall not purify himself with water) which has been defiled with colours, perfumes, or flavouring substances, nor with such as is collected in unclean places. <a name="fr_137"></a><a href="#fn_137"><font size="1">36</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_22"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 22</font></a></p>  
 <p>37. Drops (of saliva) falling from the mouth, which do not touch a limb of the body, do not make (a man) impure. <a name="fr_138"></a><a href="#fn_138"><font size="1">37</font></a></p>  
 <p>38. If, after having sipped water, he sleeps, eats, sneezes, drinks, weeps or bathes, or puts on a dress, he must again sip water, <a name="fr_139"></a><a href="#fn_139"><font size="1">38</font></a></p>  
 <p>39. Likewise, if he touches (that part of) the lips on which no hair grows. <a name="fr_140"></a><a href="#fn_140"><font size="1">39</font></a></p>  
 <p>40. No defilement is caused by the hair of the moustache (entering the mouth). <a name="fr_141"></a><a href="#fn_141"><font size="1">40</font></a></p>  
 <p>41. If (remnants of food) adhere to the teeth, (they are pure) like the teeth, and he is purified by swallowing those which (become detached) in the mouth. <a name="fr_142"></a><a href="#fn_142"><font size="1">41</font></a></p>  
 <p>42. He is not defiled by the drops which fall on his feet, while somebody gives to others water for sipping; they are stated to be equally (clean) as the ground. <a name="fr_143"></a><a href="#fn_143"><font size="1">42</font></a></p>  
 <p>43. If, while occupied with eatables, he touches any impure substance, then he shall place that thing (which he holds in his hand) on the ground, sip water and afterwards again use it. <a name="fr_144"></a><a href="#fn_144"><font size="1">43</font></a></p>  
 <p>44. Let him sprinkle with water all objects (the purity of) which may be doubtful.</p>  
 <p>45. 'Both wild animals killed by dogs, and fruit thrown by birds (from the tree), what has been spoilt by children, and what has been handled by women,' <a name="fr_145"></a><a href="#fn_145"><font size="1">45</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_23"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 23</font></a></p>  
 <p>46. 'A vendible commodity tendered for sale and what is not dirtied by gnats and flies that have settled on it,' <a name="fr_146"></a><a href="#fn_146"><font size="1">46</font></a></p>  
 <p>47. 'Likewise water collected on the ground that quenches the thirst of cows,--enumerating all these things, the Lord of created beings has declared them to be pure.' <a name="fr_147"></a><a href="#fn_147"><font size="1">47</font></a></p>  
 <p>48. Anything defiled by unclean (substances) becomes pure when the stains and the smell have been removed by water and earth. <a name="fr_148"></a><a href="#fn_148"><font size="1">48</font></a></p>  
 <p>49. (Objects) made of metal must be scoured with ashes, those made of clay should be thoroughly heated by fire, those made of wood should be planed, and (cloth) made of thread should be washed. <a name="fr_149"></a><a href="#fn_149"><font size="1">49</font></a></p>  
 <p>50. Stones and gems (should be treated) like objects made of metal, <a name="fr_150"></a><a href="#fn_150"><font size="1">50</font></a></p>  
 <p>51. Conch-shells and pearl-shells like gems,</p>  
 <p>52. (Objects made of) bone like wood, <a name="fr_151"></a><a href="#fn_151"><font size="1">52</font></a></p>  
 <p>53. Ropes, chips (of bamboo), and leather become pure (if treated) like clothes, <a name="fr_152"></a><a href="#fn_152"><font size="1">53</font></a></p>  
 <p>54. (Objects) made of fruits, (if rubbed) with (a brush of) cow hair, <a name="fr_153"></a><a href="#fn_153"><font size="1">54</font></a></p>  
 <p>55. Linen cloth, (if smeared) with a paste of yellow mustard (and washed afterwards with water). <a name="fr_154"></a><a href="#fn_154"><font size="1">55</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_24"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 24</font></a></p>  
 <p>56. But land becomes pure, according to the degree of defilement, by sweeping (the defiled spot), by smearing it with cowdung, by scraping it, by sprinkling (water) or by heaping (pure earth) on (it). <a name="fr_155"></a><a href="#fn_155"><font size="1">56</font></a></p>  
 <p>57. Now they quote also (the following verses): 'Land is purified by these four methods, by digging, burning, scraping, being trodden on by cows, and fifthly by being smeared with cowdung.' <a name="fr_156"></a><a href="#fn_156"><font size="1">57</font></a></p>  
 <p>58. 'A woman is purified by her monthly discharge, a river by its current, brass by (being scoured with) ashes, and an earthen pot by another burning.' <a name="fr_157"></a><a href="#fn_157"><font size="1">58</font></a></p>  
 <p>59. 'But an earthen vessel which has been defiled by spirituous liquor, urine, ordure, phlegm, pus, tears, or blood cannot be purified even by another burning.'</p>  
 <p>60. 'The body is purified by water, the internal organ by truth, the soul by sacred learning and austerities, and the understanding by knowledge.'</p>  
 <p>61. Gold is purified by water alone, <a name="fr_158"></a><a href="#fn_158"><font size="1">61</font></a></p>  
 <p>62. Likewise silver,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_25"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 25</font></a></p>  
 <p>63. Copper is cleansed by acids. <a name="fr_159"></a><a href="#fn_159"><font size="1">63</font></a></p>  
 <p>64. The Tîrtha sacred to the Gods lies at the root of the little finger, <a name="fr_160"></a><a href="#fn_160"><font size="1">64</font></a></p>  
 <p>65. That sacred to the <i>Ri</i>shis in the middle of the fingers,</p>  
 <p>66. That sacred to Men at the tips of the fingers,</p>  
 <p>67. That sacred to Agni (fire) in the middle of the hand,</p>  
 <p>68. That sacred to the Manes between the forefinger and the thumb.</p>  
 <p>69. He shall honour (his food at) the evening and morning meals (saying), 'It pleases me,' <a name="fr_161"></a><a href="#fn_161"><font size="1">69</font></a></p>  
 <p>70. At meals in honour of the Manes (saying), I have dined well,' <a name="fr_162"></a><a href="#fn_162"><font size="1">70</font></a></p>  
 <p>71. At (a dinner given on the occasion of) rites procuring prosperity (saying), 'It is perfect: <a name="fr_163"></a><a href="#fn_163"><font size="1">71</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>

 <p><a name="fn_117"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_117">16:1</a> <b>III</b>. I read <i>S</i>ûdrasadharmâ<i>n</i>a<i>h</i>, 'equal to <i>S</i>ûdras,' instead of <i>s</i>ûdrakarmâ<i>n</i>a<i>h</i>, which occurs in MS. B. only. K<i>ri</i>sh<i>n</i>apa<i>n</i><i>d</i>ita explains the latter reading by <i>s</i>ûdravatkarma yeshu te <i>s</i>ûdravatteshvâ<i>k</i>ara<i>n</i>îyamityartha<i>h</i>, 'shall be treated like <i>S</i>ûdras.' But the verses quoted in the following Sûtras show that the former reading is the better one.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_118"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_118">16:2</a> Identical with Manu II, 168.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_119"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_119">16:3</a> This and the following nine verses are, as the word 'iti,' which the best MSS. give at the end of Sûra 12, quotations. <a name="page_17"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 17</font></a> An<i>ri</i>k, 'who does not know the Veda,' means, literally, 'unacquainted with the Rig-veda.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_120"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_120">17:5</a> This verse, which is identical with Manu XII, 114, and the next two are intended to show that a Brâhma<i>n</i>a who, neglects the study of the Veda, is unfit to decide points of the sacred law, which are not settled either by the Sm<i>ri</i>ti or the <i>S</i>ruti, and become a member of a parishad or' Pa<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_121"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_121">17:6</a> The verse contains a better version of Manu XII, 115.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_122"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_122">17:7</a> Regarding the term Vedapâraga, see Gautama V, 20, note. Itareshâm, 'fools,' means literally, 'different from (those who have mastered the Vedas).'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_123"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_123">18:9</a>-10. Regarding the crime of 'neglecting a Brâhma<i>n</i>a,' see Manu VIII, 392-393, where fines are prescribed for neglecting to invite to dinner worthy neighbours and <i>S</i>rotriyas.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_124"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_124">18:10</a> A learned Brâhma<i>n</i>a resembles a sacrificial fire, see e.g. below, XXX, 2-3; Âpastamba I, 1, 3, 44.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_125"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_125">18:11</a> Manu II, 157. K<i>ri</i>sh<i>n</i>apa<i>n</i><i>d</i>ita and MS. B. give the ungrammatical construction which occurs in Manu and other Dharma<i>s</i>âstras, while the other MSS. read more correctly, 'ya<i>s</i><i>k</i>a kâsh<i>th</i>amayo h. ya<i>s</i><i>k</i>a <i>k</i>armamayo m.' &amp;c.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_126"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_126">18:13</a>-14. This rule agrees exactly with Gautama X, 45; see also Vish<i>n</i>u III, 56-61. The matter is introduced here in order to show the prerogative of a learned Brâhma<i>n</i>a. Regarding the six lawful occupations, see-above, II, 13-14.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_127"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_127">19:15</a> Vish<i>n</i>u V, 189-192. The connexion of this subject with the main topic consists therein that it furnishes an instance where learning does not protect a Brâhma<i>n</i>a.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_128"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_128">19:17</a> I read with the majority of the MSS., 'api vedântapâragam,' instead of 'vedântaga<i>m</i> ra<i>n</i>e,' as K<i>ri</i>sh<i>n</i>apa<i>n</i><i>d</i>ita has.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_129"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_129">19:19</a> For the explanations of the terms left untranslated, see the <a name="page_20"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 20</font></a> note on Âpastamba II, 8, 17, 22; Gautama XV, 28; and the notes on Vish<i>n</i>u LXXXIII, 2-21. Regarding the meaning of <i>Kh</i>andoga, 'one who knows the first part of the Sâma-veda Sa<i>m</i>hitâ,' see Weber, Hist. Ind. Lit., p. 63, note 59. 'One who knows the Sa<i>m</i>hitâ and the Brâhma<i>n</i>a, i.e. of the Rig-veda.'--K<i>ri</i>sh<i>n</i>apa<i>n</i><i>d</i>ita. Regarding the various classes of Snâtakas, see Âpastamba I, 11, 30, 1-3.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_130"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_130">20:20</a> Manu XII, 111. K<i>ri</i>sh<i>n</i>apa<i>n</i><i>d</i>ita reads <i>k</i>âturvidyas trikalpî <i>k</i>a, 'one who knows the four Vedas and one who knows three different Kalpa-sûtras.' My translation, follows the reading of the MSS., <i>k</i>âturvidya<i>m</i> vikalpî <i>k</i>a, which is corroborated by the parallel passage of Baudhâyana I, 1, 8, '<i>k</i>âturvaidya<i>m</i> vikalpî <i>k</i>a.' The explanation of the latter word is derived from Govindasvâmin. 'Men who are in three orders, i.e. a student, a householder, and ascetic,' see Gautama XXVIII, 49.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_131"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_131">20:21</a>-23. Vish<i>n</i>u XXIX, 1-2.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_133"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_133">20:25</a> Vish<i>n</i>u II, 6.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_134"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_134">20:26</a>-34. Vish<i>n</i>u LXII, 1-9.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_135"></a><a href="sbe1406.htm#fr_135">21:30</a> K<i>ri</i>sh<i>n</i>apa<i>n</i><i>d</i>ita is probably right in thinking that the word vâ, 'or,' inserted before 'bending forward,' is intended to forbid other improper acts, gestures or postures, which are reprehended in other Sm<i>ri</i>tis.</p>
