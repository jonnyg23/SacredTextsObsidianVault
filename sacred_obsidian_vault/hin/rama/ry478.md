 <h3 align="CENTER">CANTO CVI.: GLORY TO THE SUN.</h3>
 <p>
 There faint and bleeding fast, apart<br>
 Stood Rávan raging in his heart.<br>
 Then, moved with ruth for Ráma's sake,<br>
 Agastya  <a name="fr_1021"></a><a href="#fn_1021"><font size="1">2b</font></a> came and gently spake:<br>
 'Bend, Ráma, bend thy heart and ear<br>
 The everlasting truth to hear<br>
 Which all thy hopes through life will bless<br>
 And crown thine arms with full success.<br>
 The rising sun with golden rays,<br>
 Light of the worlds, adore and praise:<br>
 The universal king, the lord<br>
 By hosts of heaven and fiends adored.<br>
 He tempers all with soft control.<br>
 He is the Gods' diviner soul;<br>
 And Gods above and fiends below<br>
 And men to him their safety owe.<br>
 He Brahmá, Vishnu, S'iva, he<br>
 Each person of the glorious Three,<br>
 Is every God whose praise *?? tell,<br>
 The King of Heaven,  <a name="fr_1022"></a><a href="#fn_1022"><font size="1">3b</font></a> the Lord of Hell:  <a name="fr_1023"></a><a href="#fn_1023"><font size="1">4b</font></a><br>
 Each God revered from times of old,<br>
 The Lord of War,  <a name="fr_1024"></a><a href="#fn_1024"><font size="1">5b</font></a> the King of Gold:  <a name="fr_1025"></a><a href="#fn_1025"><font size="1">6b</font></a><br>
 
 </p><p>
 
 </p><p>
 
 </p><p>
 
 </p><p>
 
 </p><p>
 
 </p><p>
 
 </p><p>
 <a name="page_492"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 492</font></a><br>
 
 </p><p>
 Mahendra, Time and Death is he,  <a name="fr_1026"></a><a href="#fn_1026"><font size="1">1</font></a><br>
 The Moon, the Ruler of the Sea.<br>
 He hears our praise in every form,--<br>
 The manes,  <a name="fr_1027"></a><a href="#fn_1027"><font size="1">2</font></a> Gods who ride the storm,  <a name="fr_1028"></a><a href="#fn_1028"><font size="1">3</font></a><br>
 The As'vins,  <a name="fr_1029"></a><a href="#fn_1029"><font size="1">4</font></a> Manu,  <a name="fr_1030"></a><a href="#fn_1030"><font size="1">5</font></a> they who stand<br>
 Round Indra,  <a name="fr_1031"></a><a href="#fn_1031"><font size="1">6</font></a> and the Sádhyas'  <a name="fr_1032"></a><a href="#fn_1032"><font size="1">7</font></a> band<br>
 He is the air, and life and fire,<br>
 The universal source and sire:<br>
 He brings the seasons at his call,<br>
 Creator, light, and nurse of all.<br>
 His heavenly course he joys to run,<br>
 Maker of Day, the golden sun.<br>
 The steeds that whirl his car are seven,  <a name="fr_1033"></a><a href="#fn_1033"><font size="1">8</font></a><br>
 The flaming steeds that flash through heaven.<br>
 Lord of the sky, the conqueror parts<br>
 The clouds of night with glistering darts.<br>
 He, master of the Vedas' lore,<br>
 Commands the clouds' collected store:<br>
 He is the rivers' surest friend;<br>
 He bids the rains, and they descend.<br>
 Stars, planets, constellations own<br>
 Their monarch of the golden throne.<br>
 Lord of twelve forms,  <a name="fr_1034"></a><a href="#fn_1034"><font size="1">9</font></a> to thee I bow,<br>
 Most glorious King of heaven art thou.<br>
 O Ráma, he who pays aright<br>
 Due worship to the Lord of Light<br>
 Shall never fall oppressed by ill,<br>
 But find a stay and comfort still.<br>
 Adore with all thy heart and mind<br>
 This God of Gods, to him resigned;<br>
 And thou his saving power shalt know<br>
 Victorious o'er thy giant foe.  <a name="fr_1035"></a><a href="#fn_1035"><font size="1">10</font></a><br>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_1020"></a><a href="ry477.htm#fr_1020">491:1b</a>  I omit Cantos CIV. and CV. in which
 the fight is renewed and Rávan severely
 reprimands his charioteer for timidity and
 want of confidence in his master's prowess,
 and orders him to charge straight at Ráma
 on the next occasion.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1021"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1021">491:2b</a>  The celebrated saint who has on
 former occasions assisted Ráma with his
 gifts and counsel.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1022"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1022">491:3b</a>  Indra.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1023"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1023">491:4b</a>  Yama.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1024"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1024">491:5b</a>  Kartikeva.*</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1025"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1025">491:6b</a>  Kuvera.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1026"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1026">492:1</a>  Varun.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1027"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1027">492:2</a>  The Pitris, forefathers or spirits of the
 dead, are of two kinds, either the spirits
 of the father, grandfathers and great-
 grandfathers of an individual or the
 progenitors of mankind generally, to both of
 whom obsequial worship is paid and oblations
 of food are presented.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1028"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1028">492:3</a>  The Maruts or Storm-Gods.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1029"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1029">492:4</a>  The Heavenly Twins, the Castor and
 Pollux of the Hindus.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1030"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1030">492:5</a>  The Man <i>par excellence</i>, the
 representative man and father of the human race
 regarded also as God.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1031"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1031">492:6</a>  The Vasus, a class of deities originally personifications of natural phenomena.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1032"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1032">492:7</a>  A class of celestial beings who dwell
 between the earth and the sun.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1033"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1033">492:8</a>  The seven horses are supposed to
 symbolize the seven days of the week.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1034"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1034">492:9</a>  One for each month in the year.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1035"></a><a href="ry478.htm#fr_1035">492:10</a>  This Canto does not appear in the
 Bengal recension. It comes in awkwardly
 and may I think be considered as an interpolation, but I paraphrase a portion of
 it as a relief after so much fighting and
 carnage, and as an interesting glimpse of
 the monotheistic ideas which underlie
 the Hindu religion. The hymn does not
 readily lend itself to metrical translation,
 and I have not attempted here to give a
 faithful rendering of the whole. A literal
 version of the text and the commentary
 given in the Calcutta edition will be found
 in the Additional Notes.
 
 </p><p>
 A canto is here omitted. It contains
 fighting of the ordinary kind between
 Ráma and Rávan, and a description of
 sights and sounds of evil omen foreboding
 the destruction of the giant.</p>
