 <p>9. And (this is) appropriate, on account of the extension.</p>
 <p>Since the pra<i>n</i>ava, which is a part of the udgîtha, is introduced as the subject of meditation in the first prapâ<i>th</i>aka of the <i>Kh</i>ândogya, and extends over the later vidyâs also, it is appropriate to assume that also in the clause 'the gods took the udgîtha'--which stands in the middle--the term udgîtha denotes the pra<i>n</i>ava. Expressions such as 'the cloth is burned' show that frequently the whole denotes</p>
 <p><a name="page_636"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 636</font></a></p>
 <p>the part.--The conclusion from all this is that in the <i>Kh</i>ândogya the object of meditation is constituted by the pra<i>n</i>ava--there termed udgîtha--viewed under the form of prâ<i>n</i>a; while in the Vâ<i>g</i>asaneyaka the term udgîtha denotes the whole udgîtha, and the object of meditation is he who produces the udgîtha, i.e. the udgât<i>ri</i>, viewed under the form of prâ<i>n</i>a. And this proves that the two vidyâs are separate.--Here terminates the adhikara<i>n</i>a of 'difference.'</p>
