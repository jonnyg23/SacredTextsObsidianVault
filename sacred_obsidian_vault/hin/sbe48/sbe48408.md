 <p>24. And although (they both be) meditations on man; on account of others not being recorded.</p>
 <p>In the Taittiriyaka as well as the <i>Kh</i>ândogya we meet with a meditation on man (purusha-vidyâ), in which parts of the sacrifice are fancifully identified with the parts of</p>
 <p><a name="page_644"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 644</font></a></p>
 <p>the human body.--Here the Pûrvapakshin maintains that these two meditations are identical; for, he says, both meditations have the same name (purusha-vidyâ), and the same character as stated above; and as the Taittirîyaka mentions no fruit of the meditation, the fruit declared in the <i>Kh</i>ândogya holds good for the Taittirîyaka also, and thus there is no difference of fruit.--This view the Sûtra negatives. Although both meditations are meditations on man, yet they are separate 'on account of the others not being recorded,' i.e. on account of the qualities recorded in one <i>s</i>âkhâ not being recorded in the other. For the Taittirîyaka mentions the three libations, while the <i>Kh</i>ândogya does not, and so on. The character of the two meditations thus differs. And there is a difference of result also. For an examination of the context in the Taittirîyaka shows that the purusha-vidyâ is merely a subordinate part of a meditation on Brahman, the fruit of which the text declares to be that the devotee reaches the greatness of Brahman; while the <i>Kh</i>ândogya meditation is an independent one, and has for its reward the attainment of long life. The two meditations are thus separate, and hence the details of one must not be included in the other.--Here terminates the adhikara<i>n</i>a of 'the meditation on man.'</p>
