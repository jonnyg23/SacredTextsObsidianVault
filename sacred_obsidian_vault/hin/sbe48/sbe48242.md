 <p>39. Finiteness or absence of omniscience.</p>
 <p>'Or' here has the sense of 'and.' If the Lord is under the influence of the ad<i>ri</i>sh<i>t</i>a, it follows that, like the individual soul, he is subject to creation, dissolution, and so on, and that he is not omniscient. The Pa<i>s</i>upati theory cannot therefore be accepted.--It is true that the Sûtra, 'but in case of conflict (with Scripture) it is not to be regarded' (Pû. Mî. Sû. I, 3, 3), has already established the non-acceptability of all views contrary to the Veda; the present adhikara<i>n</i>a, however, raises this question again in order specially to declare that the Pa<i>s</i>upati theory <i>is</i> contrary to the Veda. Although the Pâ<i>s</i>upata and the <i>S</i>aiva systems exhibit some features which are not altogether contrary to the Veda, yet they are unacceptable because they rest on an assumption contrary to the Veda, viz. of the difference of the general, instrumental and material causes, and imply an erroneous interchange of higher and lower entities.--Here terminates the adhikara<i>n</i>a of 'Pa<i>s</i>upati.'</p>
