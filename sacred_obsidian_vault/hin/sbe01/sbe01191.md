 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="../../img/tn_hin.jpg"></a><br><i>The Upanishads, Part 1 (SBE01)</i>, by Max Müller, [1879], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">FOURTH KHA<i>N</i><i>D</i>A <a name="fr_436"></a><a href="#fn_434"><font size="1">1</font></a>.</h3>  
 <p>1. He begins with: 'That indeed was the oldest in the worlds <a name="fr_437"></a><a href="#fn_435"><font size="1">2</font></a>;'--for that (the Brahman) is verily the oldest in the worlds.</p>  
 <p>2. 'Whence was born the fierce one, endowed with brilliant force;'--for from it was born the fierce one, who is endowed with brilliant force.</p>  
 <p>3. 'When born he at once destroys the enemies;'--for he at once when born struck down the evil one.</p>  
 <p>4. 'He after whom all friends rejoice;'--verily all friends are the creatures, and they rejoice after him, saying, 'He has risen, he has risen <a name="fr_438"></a><a href="#fn_436"><font size="1">3</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>5. 'Growing by strength, the almighty <a name="fr_439"></a><a href="#fn_437"><font size="1">4</font></a>;'--for he (the sun) does grow by strength, the almighty.</p>  
 <p>6. 'He, as enemy, causes fear to the slave;'--for everything is afraid of him.</p>  
 <p>7. 'Taking the breathing and the not-breathing;'--this means the living and the lifeless.</p>  
 <p>8. 'Whatever has been offered at feasts came to thee;'--this means everything is in thy power.</p>  
 <p>9. 'All turn their thought also on thee <a name="fr_440"></a><a href="#fn_438"><font size="1">5</font></a>;'--this</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>

 </p>

 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_180"><font size="1" color="green">p. 180</font></a></p>  
 <p>means all these beings, all minds, all thoughts also turn to thee.</p>  
 <p>10. 'When these two become three protectors;'--i. e. when these two united beget offspring.</p>  
 <p>11. He who knows this, gets offspring and cattle.</p>  
 <p>12. 'Join what is sweeter than sweet (offspring) with the sweet (the parents);'--for the couple (father and mother) is sweet, the offspring is sweet, and he thus joins the offspring with the couple.</p>  
 <p>13. 'And this (the son, when married) being very sweet, conquered through the sweet;'--i. e. the couple is sweet, the offspring is sweet, and thus through the couple he conquers offspring <a name="fr_441"></a><a href="#fn_439"><font size="1">1</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>14. This is declared by a <i>Ri</i>shi <a name="fr_442"></a><a href="#fn_440"><font size="1">2</font></a>: 'Because he (Pra<i>g</i>âpati) raised his body (the hymn tad id âsa or the Veda in general) in the body (of the sacrificer)' (therefore that Nishkevalya hymn is praised);--i. e. this body, consisting of the Veda, in that corporeal form (of the sacrificer).</p>  
 <p>15. 'Then let this body indeed be the medicine of that body;'--i. e. this body, consisting of the Veda, of that corporeal form (of the sacrificer).</p>  
 <p>16. Of this (the first foot of Rv. X, 120, 1) the eight syllables are Gâyatrî, the eleven syllables are Trish<i>t</i>ubh, the twelve syllables are <i>G</i>agatî, the ten syllables are Virâ<i>g</i>. The Virâ<i>g</i>, consisting of ten syllables, rests in these three metres <a name="fr_443"></a><a href="#fn_441"><font size="1">3</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>17. The word purusha, consisting of three syllables, that indeed goes into the Virâ<i>g</i> <a name="fr_444"></a><a href="#fn_442"><font size="1">4</font></a>.</p>

 <p><a name="page_181"><font size="1" color="green">p. 181</font></a></p>  
 <p>18. Verily, these are all metres, these (Gâyatrî, Trish<i>t</i>ubh, Gagatî) having the Virâ<i>g</i> as the fourth. In this manner this day is complete in all metres to him who knows this.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_439"></a><a href="sbe01191.htm#fr_441">180:1</a> All these are purely fanciful interpretations.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_440"></a><a href="sbe01191.htm#fr_442">180:2</a> Not to be found in our <i>S</i>âkhâ of the Rig-veda.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_441"></a><a href="sbe01191.htm#fr_443">180:3</a> These metres are obtained by a purely arbitrary counting of syllables in the hymn tadidâsa, which really consists of Trish<i>t</i>ubh verses.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_442"></a><a href="sbe01191.htm#fr_444">180:4</a> If we simply count syllables, the first and second feet of the <a name="page_181"><font size="1" color="green">p. 181</font></a> first verse consist of ten syllables only, the fourth of nine or ten. In order to bring them to the right number, the word purusha is to be added to what is a Virâ<i>g</i>, i.e. to the first, the second, and fourth feet. We thus get:</p>  
 <p>tad id âsa bhuvaneshu <i>g</i>yesh<i>th</i>am pu<br> yato <i>g</i>a<i>g</i>ña ugras tveshan<i>ri</i>m<i>n</i>o ru<br> sadyo ga<i>g</i>ñâno ni ri<i>n</i>âti <i>s</i>atrûn<br> anu ya<i>m</i> vi<i>s</i>ve madanti ûmâ<i>h</i> sha<i>h</i>.</p>
