 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="../../img/tn_hin.jpg"></a><br><i>The Upanishads, Part 1 (SBE01)</i>, by Max Müller, [1879], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">TWENTY-FIFTH KHA<i>N</i><i>D</i>A.</h3>  
 <p>1. 'The Infinite indeed is below, above, behind, before, right and left--it is indeed all this.</p>  
 <p>'Now follows the explanation of the Infinite as</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_124"><font size="1" color="green">p. 124</font></a></p>  
 <p>the I: I am below, I am above, I am behind, before, right and left--I am all this.</p>  
 <p>2. 'Next follows the explanation of the Infinite as the Self: Self is below, above, behind, before, right and left--Self is all this.</p>  
 <p>'He who sees, perceives, and understands this, loves the Self, delights in the Self, revels in the Self, rejoices in the Self--he becomes a Svarâ<i>g</i>, (an autocrat or self-ruler); he is lord and master in all the worlds.</p>  
 <p>'But those who think differently from this, live in perishable worlds, and have other beings for their rulers.</p>  
 <hr>
 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_320"></a><a href="sbe01157.htm#fr_322">123:1</a> Bhûman is sometimes translated by grandeur, the superlative, the akme. It is the highest point that can be reached, the infinite and the true.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_321"></a><a href="sbe01158.htm#fr_323">123:2</a> This phrase reminds one of the last verse in the No sad âsîd hymn, where, likewise, the expression of the highest certainty is followed by a misgiving that after all it may be otherwise. The commentator takes yadi vâ in the sense of, if you ask in the highest sense, then I say no; for the Infinite cannot rest in anything, not even in greatness.</p>
