 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="../../img/tn_hin.jpg"></a><br><i>The Upanishads, Part 1 (SBE01)</i>, by Max Müller, [1879], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">SECOND KHA<i>N</i><i>D</i>A.</h3>  
 <p>1. When he recites the Nishkevalya hymn addressed to Indra (Rv. X, 50), pra vo mahe, he inserts a Nivid <a name="fr_483"></a><a href="#fn_481"><font size="1">2</font></a> (between the fourth and fifth verses). Thus he clearly places strength in himself (in the <i>s</i>astra, in the bird, in himself).</p>  
 <p>2. They are Trish<i>t</i>ubhs and <i>G</i>agatîs.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_195"><font size="1" color="green">p. 195</font></a></p>  
 <p>3. There they say: 'Why does he insert a Nivid among mixed Trish<i>t</i>ubhs and <i>G</i>agatîs <a name="fr_484"></a><a href="#fn_482"><font size="1">1</font></a>?' But surely one metre would never support the Nivid of this day, nor fill it: therefore he inserts the Nivid among mixed Trish<i>t</i>ubhs and <i>G</i>agatîs.</p>  
 <p>4. Let him know that this day has three Nivids: the Vasa hymn is a Nivid, the Vâlakhilyas <a name="fr_485"></a><a href="#fn_483"><font size="1">2</font></a> are a Nivid, and the Nivid itself is a Nivid. Thus let him know that day as having three Nivids.</p>  
 <p>5. Then follow the hymns vane na vâ (Rv. X, 29) and yo <i>g</i>âta eva (Rv. II, 12). In the fourth verse of the former hymn occur the words anne samasya yad asan manîshâ<i>h</i>, and they serve for the winning of proper food.</p>  
 <p>6. Then comes an insertion. As many Trish<i>t</i>ubh and <i>G</i>agatî verses <a name="fr_486"></a><a href="#fn_484"><font size="1">3</font></a>, taken from the ten Ma<i>n</i><i>d</i>alas and addressed to Indra, as they insert (between the two above-mentioned hymns), after changing them into B<i>ri</i>hatîs, so many years do they live beyond the (usual) age (of one hundred years). By this insertion age is obtained.</p>  
 <p>7. After that he recites the Sa<i>g</i>anîya hymn, wishing that cattle may always come to his offspring.</p>  
 <p>8. Then he recites the Târkshya hymn <a name="fr_487"></a><a href="#fn_485"><font size="1">4</font></a>. Târkshya is verily welfare, and the hymn leads to welfare. Thus (by reciting the hymn) he fares well <a name="fr_488"></a><a href="#fn_486"><font size="1">5</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_196"><font size="1" color="green">p. 196</font></a></p>  
 <p>9. Then he recites the Ekapadâ (indro vi<i>s</i>va<i>m</i> vi râ<i>g</i>ati), wishing, May I be everything at once, and may I thus finish the whole work of metres <a name="fr_489"></a><a href="#fn_487"><font size="1">1</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>10. In reciting the hymn indra<i>m</i> vi<i>s</i>vâ aviv<i>ri</i>dhan (Rv. I, 11) he intertwines the first seven verses by intertwining their feet <a name="fr_490"></a><a href="#fn_488"><font size="1">2</font></a>. There are seven prâ<i>n</i>as (openings) in the head, and he thus places seven prâ<i>n</i>as in the head. The eighth verse (half-verse) he does not intertwine <a name="fr_491"></a><a href="#fn_489"><font size="1">3</font></a>. The eighth is speech, and he thinks, May my speech never be intertwined with the other prâ<i>n</i>as. Speech therefore, though dwelling in the same abode as the other prâ<i>n</i>as, is not intertwined with them.</p>  
 <p>11. He recites the Virâ<i>g</i> verses <a name="fr_492"></a><a href="#fn_490"><font size="1">4</font></a>. Verily, Virâ<i>g</i> verses are food, and they thus serve for the gaining of food.</p>  
 <p>12. He ends with the hymn of Vasish<i>th</i>a <a name="fr_493"></a><a href="#fn_491"><font size="1">5</font></a>, wishing, May I be Vasish<i>th</i>a!</p>  
 <p>13. But let him end with the fifth verse, esha stomo maha ugrâya vâhe, which, possessing the word mahat, is auspicious.</p>  
 <p>14. In the second foot of the fifth verse the word dhuri occurs. Verily, dhu<i>h</i> (the place where the horse is fastened to the car) is the end (of the car). This day also is the end (of the sacrifice which lasts a whole year) <a name="fr_494"></a><a href="#fn_492"><font size="1">6</font></a>. Thus the verse is fit for the day.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>

 <p><a name="page_197"><font size="1" color="green">p. 197</font></a></p>  
 <p>15. In the third foot the word arka is auspicious.</p>  
 <p>16. The last foot is: 'Make our glory high as heaven over heaven.' Thus wherever Brahmanic speech is uttered, there his glory will be, when he who knows this finishes with that verse. Therefore let a man who knows this, finish (the Nishkevalya) with that verse.</p>
