 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>The Grihya Sutras, Part 2 (SBE30)</i>, by Hermann Oldenberg, [1892], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">PRA<i>S</i>NA II, PA<i>T</i>ALA 8, SECTION 18.</h3>  
 <p>1. Now we shall explain the opening and the conclusion (of the annual course of study).</p>  
 <p>2. <a name="fr_703"></a><a href="#fn_703"><font size="1">2</font></a> During the fortnight that precedes the <i>S</i>rava<i>n</i>â</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_242"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 242</font></a></p>  
 <p>full moon, when the herbs have appeared, under (the Nakshatra) Hasta or on the full-moon day (itself), the opening ceremony of the (annual course of) study (is performed).</p>  
 <p>3. Having put wood on the fire and performed the rites down to the Vyâh<i>ri</i>ti oblations, he sacrifices (with his pupils) to the <i>Ri</i>shis of the Kâ<i>n</i><i>d</i>as: 'To Pra<i>g</i>âpati, the <i>Ri</i>shi of a Kâ<i>n</i><i>d</i>a, svâhâ! To Soma, the <i>Ri</i>shi of a Kâ<i>n</i><i>d</i>a, svâhâ! To Agni, the <i>Ri</i>shi of a Kâ<i>n</i><i>d</i>a, svâhâ! To the Vi<i>s</i>ve devâs, the <i>Ri</i>shis of a Kâ<i>n</i><i>d</i>a, svâhâ! To Svayambhû, the <i>Ri</i>shi of a Kâ<i>n</i><i>d</i>a, svâhâ!'these are the <i>Ri</i>shis of the Kâ<i>n</i><i>d</i>as. Or (he sacrifices) to the names of the Kâ<i>n</i><i>d</i>as, to the Sâvitrî, to the <i>Ri</i>g-veda, the Ya<i>g</i>ur-veda, the Sâma-veda, the Atharva-veda, and to Sadasaspati.</p>  
 <p>4. Having (thus) sacrificed, they repeat the first three Anuvâkas,</p>  
 <p>5. Or the beginnings of all Kâ<i>n</i><i>d</i>as.</p>  
 <p>6. He enters upon (sacrificing) the <i>G</i>aya, &amp;c. (oblations; see above, I, 1, 3, 8).</p>  
 <p>7. After all rites down to the Svish<i>t</i>ak<i>ri</i>t oblation have been performed, they stop studying three days or one day; then they should go on studying so as to commence where they have broken off: so say the teachers.</p>  
 <p>8. <a name="fr_704"></a><a href="#fn_704"><font size="1">8</font></a> During the fortnight that precedes the Taishî full moon, under (the Nakshatra) Rohi<i>n</i>î or on the full-moon day (itself), the Utsarga (or conclusion of the term of study) is celebrated.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_243"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 243</font></a></p>  
 <p>9. <a name="fr_705"></a><a href="#fn_705"><font size="1">9</font></a> (The teacher) with his pupils goes in an easterly or northerly direction, and where they find a pleasant water with a pleasant bathing-place, they dive into it and perform three suppressions of the breath with the Agharmarsha<i>n</i>a hymn (<i>Ri</i>g-veda X, 190 = Taitt. Ar. X, 1, 13. 14.). Holding purifiers (i.e. Darbha blades) in their hands they bathe with the three (verses), 'Ye waters, ye are wholesome' (Taitt. Sa<i>m</i>h. IV, 1, 5, 1), with the four (verses), 'The gold-coloured, pure, purifying waters' (T.S.V, 6, 1, 1 seq.), and with the Anuvâka, '(Soma) which clears itself, the heavenly being' (Taitt. Br. I, 4, 8): giving the Darbha blades to each other and feigning to try to seize (??) each other.</p>  
 <p>10. Then they arrange on a pure spot that is inclined towards the east, seats of eastward-pointed Darbha grass, so that they end in the north</p>
