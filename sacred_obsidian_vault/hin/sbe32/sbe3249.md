 <p><a name="page_386"><font size="1" color="green">p. 386</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">MA<i>N</i><i>D</i>ALA VII, HYMN 59.</h3>  <h4 align="center">ASH<i>T</i>AKA V, ADHYÂYA 4, VARGA 29-30.</h4>  <h4 align="center"><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">To the Maruts and Rudra</span>.</h4>  
 <p>1. Whom you protect again and again, O gods, and whom you lead, to him, O Agni, Varu<i>n</i>a, Mitra, Aryaman, and Maruts, yield your protection.</p>  
 <p>2. He who sacrifices, O gods, overcomes his enemies by your protection on a happy day. He who gives to your delight, spreads forth his dwelling, spreads out much food.</p>  
 <p>3. This Vasish<i>th</i>a will not despise even the last among you, O Maruts; drink <a href="sbe3249.htm#an_7:59:3:1">1</a> all of you, to-day, at my libation here, full of desire.</p>  
 <p>4. Your help does not indeed fail that man in battle to whom you granted it, O men! Your newest favour has turned hither, come quick then, ye who wish to drink.</p>  
 <p>5. O ye whose gifts are cheering, come to drink the (juice of the Soma) flowers: these are your libations, O Maruts, for I gave them to you, do not go elsewhere!</p>  
 <p>6. Sit down on our altar and protect <a href="sbe3249.htm#an_7:59:6:1">1</a> us, to give us brilliant riches. O Maruts, who never miss the Soma mead, hail to you here to enjoy yourselves.</p>  
 <p>7. Having adorned their bodies, the swans with dark blue backs came flying in secret <a href="sbe3249.htm#an_7:59:7:1">1</a>—the whole flock sat down all around me, like gay men, delighting in the Soma offering.</p>  
 <p>8. O Maruts, that hateful man who beyond our thoughts tries to hurt us, O Vasus, may he catch the snares of Drub, kill him with your hottest bolt!</p>  
 <p><a name="page_387"><font size="1" color="green">p. 387</font></a></p>  
 <p>9. O you Maruts, full of heat, here is the libation; be pleased to accept it, O you who destroy the enemies by your help <a href="sbe3249.htm#an_7:59:9:1">1</a>.</p>  
 <p>10. O you who accept the domestic sacrifices <a href="sbe3249.htm#an_7:59:10:1">1</a>, come hither, O Maruts, do not keep away, you who are bounteous by your help <a href="sbe3249.htm#an_7:59:10:2">2</a>.</p>  
 <p>11. O Maruts, strong and wise, with sun-bright skins, I choose the sacrifice for you here and there <a href="sbe3249.htm#an_7:59:11:1">1</a></p>  
 <p>12. We sacrifice to Tryambaka <a href="sbe3249.htm#an_7:59:12:1">1</a>, the sweet-scented, wealth-increasing (Rudra). May I be detached from death, like a gourd from its stem, but not <a href="sbe3249.htm#an_7:59:12:2">2</a> from the immortal <a href="sbe3249.htm#an_7:59:12:3">3</a>.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_388"><font size="1" color="green">p. 388</font></a></p>  <h4 align="center">NOTES.</h4>  
 <p>Ascribed to Vasish<i>th</i>a. Verse 12 addressed to Rudra.</p>  
 <p>Verse 3 occurs SV. I, 241; verse 8, AV. VII, 77, 2; TS. IV, 3, 13, 3; MS. IV, 10, 5; verse 9, AV. VII, 77, 1; TS. IV, 3, 13, 3; MS. IV, 10, 5; verse 10, TS. IV, 3, 13, 5; MS. IV, 10, 5; verse 11, TÂ. I, 4, 3; MS. IV, 10, 3; verse 12, VS. III, 60; AV. XIV, 1, 17; TS. I, 8, 6, 2; MS. I, 10, 4; TA. X, 56; <i>S</i>at. Br. II, 6, 2, 12.</p>  
 <p align="justify'">Metre, 1, 3, 5 B<i>ri</i>hatî; 2, 4, 6 Satob<i>ri</i>hatî; 7, 8 Trish<i>t</i>ubh; 9, 10, 11 Gâyatrî; 12 Anush<i>t</i>ubh.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 2.</h5>  
 <p>With pâda a compare I, 110, 7; with c and d, VIII, 27, 16.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 3.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_7:59:3:1">1</a>. SV. has pibantu, and as a various reading the comment. gives pivanta. Suté sá<i>k</i>â is a standing phrase.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 6.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_7:59:6:1">1</a>. I cannot see how avitá can stand for avishta (Delbrück, Verb, 186; Whitney, Gram. § 908). I translate as if the text gave ávatâ.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 7.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_7:59:7:1">1</a>. On the secret approach of the Maruts, see I, 88, 5.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 8.</h5>  
 <p>The text in the AV. VII, 77, 2, is bad, yó no márto maruto durh<i>ri</i><i>n</i>âyús, práti mu<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>atâm sá<i>h</i>, and tápasâ for hánmanâ. The TS. IV, 3, 13, 3, has tirá<i>h</i> satyâ´ni. It reads besides, yó no márto vasavo durh<i>ri</i><i>n</i>âyús tirá<i>h</i> satyâ´ni maruta<i>h</i> <i>g</i>íghâ<i>m</i>̐sât druhá<i>h</i> pâ´<i>s</i>am, and tápasâ. Tirá<i>h</i> <i>k</i>ittâ´ni may mean 'beyond all conception,' as Grassmann takes it, or 'unobserved,' as B.-R. suggest. Tirá<i>h</i> satyâ´ni might mean 'in spite of all pledges,' but that is probably an emendation. All this shows the unsettled state of Vedic tradition, outside that of the Rig-veda; see Oldenberg, Prolegomena, p. 328.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 9.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_7:59:9:1">1</a>. Ûtî´, taken here as a dative, by Lanman, p. 382.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_389"><font size="1" color="green">p. 389</font></a></p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 10.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_7:59:10:1">1</a>. On the Maruts g<i>ri</i>hamedhina<i>h</i>, see <i>S</i>at. Br. II, 5, 3, 4. Possibly the Maruts may be called g<i>ri</i>hamedhas, i. e. g<i>ri</i>hasthas, performing the G<i>ri</i>hya sacrifices. See on these names TS. I, 8, 4, 1; 2.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_7:59:10:2">2</a>. The last pâda in the TS. is pramu<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>ánto no á<i>m</i>hasa<i>h</i>.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 11.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_7:59:11:1">1</a>. On ihéha, see Delbrück, Syntax, p. 51. It means 'here and there,' that is, 'again and again.'</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 12.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_7:59:12:1">1</a>. Tryambaka is a name of Rudra, but its original meaning is doubtful. Some commentators explain it by 'three-eyed,' but its natural meaning would be 'having three mothers.' The <i>S</i>at. Br. II, 6, 2, 9, derives it from Stry-ambikâ, because Ambikâ, Rudra's sister, shares the sacrifice with him.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_7:59:12:2">2</a>. On mâ with optative, see Delbrück, Synt. Forsch. I, 194; Syntax, 338, 361, Anm. 1.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_7:59:12:3">3</a>. That am<i>ri</i>tât is right, not, as Grassmann suggests, am<i>ri</i>ta, is clear from the parallel forms, prétó mu<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>âmi nâ´múta<i>h</i>, or itó mukshîya mâ´múta<i>h</i>. Pischel in Z. D. M. G. XL, 121, demands too much logical accuracy from a poet; see AV. XIV, I, 17; VS. III, 60.</p>  
 <p>All scholars seem to agree that this hymn is a composite hymn, and that it breaks the law of decrease in the number of verses. It begins with three Pragâthas, verses 1 and 2, 3 and 4, 5 and 6, which may be in their right place. Then follow two Trish<i>t</i>ubhs, 7 and 8, which may form a hymn by themselves. The next three Gâyatrîs, which clearly belong together, are a later addition; so is the last verse, which ought to stand in the Atharva rather than in the Rig-veda. The Pada text does not divide this last verse. See on this subject, Oldenberg, Z. D. M. G. XXXVIII, 449 seq., Proleg. 200; 511; Bergaigne, Recherches sur l’histoire de la Sa<i>m</i>hitâ, II, 10.</p>
