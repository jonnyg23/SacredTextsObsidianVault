 <p><a name="page_221"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 221</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">KA<i>N</i><i>D</i>IKÂ 5.</h3>  
 <p>1. Now (follows) the Adhyâyopâkara<i>n</i>a (i.e. the ceremony by which the annual course of study is opened);</p>  
 <p>2 <a name="fr_535"></a><a href="#fn_534"><font size="1">2-3</font></a>. When the herbs appear, (when the moon stands in conjunction) with <i>S</i>rava<i>n</i>a, in the <i>S</i>râva<i>n</i>a month,</p>  
 <p>3. Or on the fifth (Tithi of that month), under (the Nakshatra) Hasta.</p>  
 <p>4 <a name="fr_536"></a><a href="#fn_535"><font size="1">4</font></a>. Having sacrificed the two Â<i>g</i>ya portions, he should offer Â<i>g</i>ya oblations (to the following deities, viz.) Sâvitrî, Brahman, Belief, Insight, Wisdom, Memory, Sadasaspati, Anumati, the metres, and the <i>Ri</i>shis.</p>  
 <p>5. He then sacrifices grains with curds (with the following texts):</p>  
 <p>6. 'I praise Agni the Purohita'this one verse (Rig-Veda I, 1, 1),</p>  
 <p>7 <a name="fr_537"></a><a href="#fn_536"><font size="1">7</font></a>. 'The Kushumbhaka (mungoose?) has said it''If thou criest, O bird, announce luck to us Sung by <i>G</i>amadagni''In thy abode the whole world rests'</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_222"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 222</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> 'Come to our sacrifice, O you that are worthy of sacrifice, with care''Whosoever, be he ours, be he alien''Look on, look about''Come here, Agni, the Maruts' friend''The oblation, O king, cooked for thee'each time two verses;</p>  
 <p>8. 'United is your will' (Rig-veda X, 191, 4)this one verse;</p>  
 <p>9 <a name="fr_538"></a><a href="#fn_537"><font size="1">9</font></a>. 'That blessing and bliss we choose'this one verse.</p>  
 <p>10 <a name="fr_539"></a><a href="#fn_538"><font size="1">10</font></a>. When he intends to study (the Veda together with pupils), he should, while the pupils take hold of him, sacrifice to those deities, and sacrifice to (Agni) Svish<i>t</i>ak<i>ri</i>t, and partake of the grains with curds; then (follows) the 'cleaning.'</p>  
 <p>11 <a name="fr_540"></a><a href="#fn_539"><font size="1">11</font></a>. Sitting down to the west of the fire on Darbha grass, the tufts of which are directed towards the east, he should put Darbha blades into a water-pot, and making a Brahmâ<i>ñ</i><i>g</i>ali (i.e. joining his hands as a sign of veneration for the Brahman), he should murmur (the following texts):</p>  
 <p>12. The Vyâh<i>ri</i>tis preceded by (the syllable) Om (stand first); (these) and the Sâvitrî he should repeat three times and then recite the beginning of the Veda.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_223"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 223</font></a></p>  
 <p>13. In the same way at the Utsarga (i.e. at the ceremony performed at the end of the term of Vedic study).</p>  
 <p>14. He should study six months.</p>  
 <p>15 <a name="fr_541"></a><a href="#fn_540"><font size="1">15</font></a>. One who has performed the Samâvartana (should live during that time) according to the regulations for Brahma<i>k</i>ârins.</p>  
 <p>16 <a name="fr_542"></a><a href="#fn_541"><font size="1">16</font></a>. The others according to the rules.</p>  
 <p>17 <a name="fr_543"></a><a href="#fn_542"><font size="1">17</font></a>. Some say that he should have intercourse with his wife.</p>  
 <p>18. That (is a practice) sacred to Pra<i>g</i>âpati.</p>  
 <p>19. This (Upâkara<i>n</i>a) they call vârshika (i.e. belonging to the rainy season).</p>  
 <p>20 <a name="fr_544"></a><a href="#fn_543"><font size="1">20</font></a>. On the middle Ash<i>t</i>akâ they offer food to those deities, and descend into water.</p>  
 <p>21. They satiate those same deities (with water oblations),</p>  
 <p>22. (And besides) the Â<i>k</i>âryas, the <i>Ri</i>shis, and the Fathers.</p>  
 <p>23 <a name="fr_545"></a><a href="#fn_544"><font size="1">23</font></a>. This is the Utsar<i>g</i>ana.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_534"></a><a href="sbe29138.htm#fr_535">221:2-3</a> 5, 2, 3. Perhaps the division of these Sûtras should be altered, so that <i>s</i>râva<i>n</i>asya would belong to Sûtra 2. In this case we should have to translate, '2. When the herbs appear, (on a day on which the moon stands in conjunction) with <i>S</i>rava<i>n</i>a. 3. Or on the fifth (Tithi) of the <i>S</i>râva<i>n</i>a month, under (the Nakshatra) Hasta.' Comp. <i>s</i>râva<i>n</i>asya pa<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>amîm, Par. II, 10, 2. If we count the month beginning with the bright fortnight, and assume that the full moon day of <i>S</i>râva<i>n</i>a falls, as the name of the month implies, on <i>S</i>rava<i>n</i>a, the fifth Tithi of that month will fall indeed on Hasta. Comp. on the dates of the Upâkara<i>n</i>a, Prof. Weber's remarks, Die vedischen Nachrichten von den Naxatra II, 322, and on the special symbolical signification of the Nakshatra <i>S</i>rava<i>n</i>a in this connection, my note on <i>S</i>âṅkhâyana IV, 5, 2.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_535"></a><a href="sbe29138.htm#fr_536">221:4</a> On the two Â<i>g</i>ya portions, comp. above, I, 3, 5; 10, 13 seqq.</p>
