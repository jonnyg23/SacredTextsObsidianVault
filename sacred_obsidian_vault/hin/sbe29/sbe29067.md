 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>The Grihya Sutras, Part 1 (SBE29)</i>, by Hermann Oldenberg, [1886], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">KHA<i>N</i><i>D</i>A 5.</h3>  
 <p>1 <a name="fr_252"></a><a href="#fn_251"><font size="1">1</font></a>. Now (follows) the Upâkara<i>n</i>a (i.e. the ceremony by which the annual course of study is opened).</p>  
 <p>2 <a name="fr_253"></a><a href="#fn_252"><font size="1">2</font></a>. When the herbs appear, under the Nakshatra Hasta or <i>S</i>rava<i>n</i>a,</p>  
 <p>3. Let him make oblations of the flour of fried barley and of grains, mixed with curds and ghee, with the (whole) Veda, verse by verse: thus say some (teachers).</p>  
 <p>4 <a name="fr_254"></a><a href="#fn_253"><font size="1">4</font></a>. Or with the first verses of the Sûktas and Anuvâkas.</p>  
 <p>5. With the first verses of the Adhyâyas and of the sections belonging to the (different) <i>Ri</i>shis, according to Mâ<i>n</i><i>d</i>ûkeya.</p>  
 <p>6. But Kaushîtaki has said:</p>  
 <p>7. 'I praise Agni the Purohita' (Rig-veda I, 1, 1), this one verse,</p>  
 <p>8. 'The Kushumbhaka (mungoose?) has said it;' 'If thou criest, O bird, announce luck to us;' 'Sung by <i>G</i>amadagni;' 'In thy abode the whole world rests;'</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_113"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 113</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> <font size="-1" color="green">'</font>Come to our sacrifice, O you that are worthy of sacrifice, with care;' 'Whosoever, be he ours, be he alien;' 'Look on, look about;' 'Come here, Agni, the Maruts friend;' 'The oblation, O king, cooked for thee:' each time two verses,</p>  
 <p>9 <a name="fr_255"></a><a href="#fn_254"><font size="1">9</font></a>. 'That blessing and bliss we choose'this one verse (the first and last verse of each Ma<i>n</i><i>d</i>ala).</p>  
 <p>10. (Taking something) of the remnants of the sacrificed (food) they partake of that sacrificial food with this (verse), 'I praised Dadhikrâvan' (Rig-veda IV, 39, 6).</p>  
 <p>11. They sip water, sit down,</p>  
 <p>12. Murmur the Mahâvyâh<i>ri</i>tis, the Sâvitrî, and the auspicious hymns commencing from the beginning of the Veda,</p>  
 <p>13. And cause the teacher to pronounce auspicious wishes.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_114"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 114</font></a></p>  
 <p>14. Of this (ceremony) it is also said,</p>  
 <p>15. 'Desirous (of acquiring) for the hymns inexhaustible vigour, reverence, and also soundness, the <i>Ri</i>shis, by the power of their austerities, have discovered the Upâkarman.</p>  
 <p>16 <a name="fr_256"></a><a href="#fn_255"><font size="1">16</font></a>. 'Therefore a constant performer of the six kinds of works should, in order that his Mantras might be successful, perform the Upâkarmanso they sayif he wishes for success of his (holy) works.</p>  
 <p>17 <a name="fr_257"></a><a href="#fn_256"><font size="1">17</font></a>. 'At the time of the Upâkarman and of the Utsarga an interruption (of the Veda-study) shall take place for (three days and) three nights, likewise at the Ash<i>t</i>akâs for one day and one night, and so on the last night of each season.'</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_251"></a><a href="sbe29067.htm#fr_252">112:1</a> 5, 1. As to the Upâkara<i>n</i>a, see the statements of Professor Weber in his second article on the Nakshatras, Abhandlungen der Berliner Akademie, 1861, p. 338, and of Professor Bühler in his notes on Âpastamba, S.B.E., II, pp. 110, 111.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_252"></a><a href="sbe29067.htm#fr_253">112:2</a> The Nakshatra <i>S</i>rava<i>n</i>a is evidently considered as particularly fit for this occasion because of its name containing an allusion to <i>s</i>ruti, &amp;c.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_253"></a><a href="sbe29067.htm#fr_254">112:4</a> I have followed Nârâya<i>n</i>a, but perhaps I ought to have translated, 'Sûktas or Anuvâkas,' and in the fifth Sûtra, 'Adhyâyas or the sections, &amp;c.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_254"></a><a href="sbe29067.htm#fr_255">113:9</a> According to Kaushîtaki, the oblations are made with the first and last <i>ri</i><i>k</i>as of each Ma<i>n</i><i>d</i>ala. The last <i>ri</i><i>k</i> of the tenth Ma<i>n</i><i>d</i>ala quoted here, ta<i>k</i> <i>kh</i>a<i>m</i> yor â v<i>ri</i><i>n</i>îmahe, is different from the verse with which our Sa<i>m</i>hitâ (the <i>S</i>âkala Sa<i>m</i>hitâ of the Rig-veda) closes. It is well known that ta<i>k</i> <i>kh</i>a<i>m</i> yor â v<i>ri</i><i>n</i>îmahe is the last verse in the Bâshkala <i>S</i>âkhâ which was adopted by the <i>S</i>âṅkhâyana school (comp. Indische Studien, IV, 431; Weber, Verzeichniss der Berliner Sanskrit-Handschriften, p. 314, &amp;c.; Indische Literaturgeschichte, second edition, Nachtrag, p. 2). It was also known long since that the Bâshkala Sâkhâ of the Rig-veda contains eight hymns more than the <i>S</i>âkala <i>S</i>âkhâ. The <i>K</i>ara<i>n</i>avyûha Bhâshya (comp. Dr. von Schroeder's Introduction to his excellent edition of the Maitrâya<i>n</i>î Sa<i>m</i>hitâ, vol. i, p. xxiv), known to me through the kindness of Professor Weber, tells which eight hymns these are. There it is said (folio 22 of Professor Weber's MS.) that in the Bâshkala Sa<i>m</i>hitâ there followed after VIII, 48 the first two of the Vâlakhilya hymns, after VIII, 94 the Vâlakhilya hymns 3-7, and at the end of the whole collection the so-called sa<i>m</i><i>g</i><i>ñ</i>âna hymn (see Professor Max Müller's edition, vol. vi, p. 32), which ends with the very verse quoted in our Sûtra, ta<i>k</i> <i>kh</i>a<i>m</i> yor â v<i>ri</i><i>n</i>îmahe.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_255"></a><a href="sbe29067.htm#fr_256">114:16</a> The six kinds of works are, performing sacrifices (ya<i>g</i>ana), officiating at the sacrifices of others (yâ<i>g</i>ana), studying the Veda (adhyayana), teaching the Veda to others (adhyâpana), giving (dâna), and accepting gifts (pratigraha). Nârâya<i>n</i>a.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_256"></a><a href="sbe29067.htm#fr_257">114:17</a> Concerning the Utsarga, see chap. 6. This <i>S</i>loka occurs also Manu IV, 119 with the reading kshepa<i>n</i>am instead of kshapa<i>n</i>am ('kshapa<i>n</i>a<i>m</i> <i>kh</i>andasâ<i>m</i> virâma anadhyâya<i>h</i>,' Nârâya<i>n</i>a). Kshapa<i>n</i>am is correct.</p>
