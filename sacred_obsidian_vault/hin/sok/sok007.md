 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Songs of Kabir</i>, tr. by Rabindranath Tagore, intro. by Evelyn Underhill, [1915], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="CENTER">VI</h3> 
 <p>I. 83. <i>candâ jhalkai yahi gha<b>t</b> mâhî<u>n</u></i></p> 
 <p>THE moon shines in my body, but my blind eyes cannot see it:<br> The moon is within me, and so is the sun.<br> The unstruck drum of Eternity is sounded within me; but my deaf ears cannot hear it.</p> 
 <p>So long as man clamours for the <i>I</i> and the <i>Mine</i>, his works are as naught:<br> When all love of the <i>I</i> and the <i>Mine</i> is dead, then the work of the Lord is done.<br> For work has no other aim than the getting of knowledge:<br> When that comes, then work is put away.</p> 
 <p>The flower blooms for the fruit: when the fruit comes, the flower withers. <a name="page_50"><font size="1" color="green">p. 50</font></a><br> The musk is in the deer, but it seeks it not within itself: it wanders in quest of grass.</p>
