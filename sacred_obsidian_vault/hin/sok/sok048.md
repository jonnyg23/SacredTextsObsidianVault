 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>The Songs of Kabir</i>, tr. by Rabindranath Tagore, intro. by Evelyn Underhill, [1915], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="CENTER">XLVII</h3> 
 <p>I. 102. <i>tarvar ek mûl vin <b>t</b>hâ<b>d</b>â</i></p> 
 <p>THERE is a strange tree, which stands without roots and bears fruits without blossoming;<br> It has no branches and no leaves, it is lotus all over.<br> Two birds sing there; one is the Guru, and the other the disciple:<br> The disciple chooses the manifold fruits of life and tastes them, and the Guru beholds him in joy.<br> What Kabîr says is hard to understand: "The bird is beyond seeking, yet it is most clearly visible. The Formless is in the midst of all forms. I sing the glory of forms."</p>
