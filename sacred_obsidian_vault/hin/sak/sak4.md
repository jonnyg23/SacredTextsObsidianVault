 <h2 align="center">BOOK IV.</h2>
 
 <hr width="12.5%" align="center">
 
 <p>   Now, by means of a collection of narratives, recognized in the Institutes, the means of discriminative knowledge are to be displayed: so, for this purpose the Fourth Book is commenced.</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Soul set right by hearing the truth.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_1"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 1.</font><a href="#pp_1"><font color="RED">*</font></a> As in the case of the king's son, from instruction as to the truth [comes discrimination between soul and Nature].</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_1a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> 'Discrimination' is supplied from the concluding aphorism of the preceding section. The meaning is: as, in the case of the king's son, discrimination is produced by instruction as to the truth. The story, here, is as follows: A certain king's son, in consequence of his being born under the [unlucky] star of the tenth portion<a name="page_284_fr_2"></a><a href="#page_284_note_2"><font size="1">2</font></a> [of the twenty-seven portions into which the ecliptic is divided], having been expelled from his city, and reared by a certain forester, remains under the idea, that 'I am a forester.' Having learned that he is alive, a certain minister informs him: 'Thou art not a forester; thou art a king's son.'
 
 <!------------------------page 285------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_285"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 285</font></a>
 
 As he, immediately, having abandoned the idean of his being an outcast, betakes himself to his true royal state, saying, 'I am a <i>king</i>,' so, too, it [the Soul], in consequence of the instruction of some kind person, to the effect that 'Thou, who didst originate from the First Soul, which manifests itself merely as pure Thought, art [thyself,] a portion thereof,' having abandoned the idea of its being Nature [or of being something material or phenomenal], rests simply upon its own nature, saying, 'Since I am the son of Brahmá, I am, myself, Brahmá, and not something mundane, different therefrom:' such is the meaning.
 
 </p><p>   <a name="pp_1b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> He exhibits another story, to prove that even women,
 
 <!------------------------page 286------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_286"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 286</font></a>
 
 <i>Śúdras</i>, &amp;c., may gain the [one desirable] end, through a Bráhman, by hearing the instructions of a Bráhman:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Even when the instruction is not addressed to the hearer.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_2"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 2.</font><a href="#pp_2"><font color="RED">*</font></a> As in the case of the goblin, even when the instruction was for the sake of another, [the chance hearer may be benefited].</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_2a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> That is to say: though the instruction in regard to the truth was being delivered, by the venerable Kṛishṉa, for Arjuna's benefit, knowledge of the distinction [between Soul and Nature] was produced in the case of a goblin standing near [and overhearing the discourse]: and so it may happen in the case of others, too.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_2b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> And, if knowledge is not produced from <i>once</i> instructing, then a repetition of the instruction is to be made; to which effect he adduces another story:</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 287------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_287"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 287</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Necessity of inculcation.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_3"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 3.</font><a href="#pp_3"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Repetition [is to be made], if not, from once instructing, [the end be gained].</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_3a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> That is to say: a repetition of instruction, also, is to be made; because, in the <i>Chhándogya</i> [<i>Upanishad</i>],<a name="page_287_fr_1"></a><a href="#page_287_note_1"><font size="1">1</font></a> and the like; there is mention of Áruṉi, and others, as having more than once instructed Śwetaketu and others.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_3b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> With a view to the removal of desire, he sets forth, with an illustration, the fragility, &amp;c., of Soul's accompaniments:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Transitoriness of mundane things.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_4"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 4.</font><a href="#pp_4"><font color="RED">*</font></a> As in the case of father and son; since both are seen; [to one, to die, and the other, to be born].</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_4a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> That is to say: Discrimination takes place, through dispassion, in consequence of its being inferred, in respect of one's own self, also, that there is death and birth; since these are seen in the case of father and son. This has
 
 <!------------------------page 288------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_288"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 288</font></a>
 
 been stated as follows: 'The coming into being, and the departure, of Soul [entangled in Nature],<a name="page_288_fr_1"></a><a href="#page_288_note_1"><font size="1">1</font></a> may be inferred from [the case of] father and son.'
 
 </p><p>   <a name="pp_4b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> He next explains, by illustrative stories, the subservients to the <i>perfecting</i> of knowledge in him in whom knowledge has arisen, and who is devoid of passion:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Voluntary abandonment distinguished from involuntary.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_5"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 5.</font><a href="#pp_5"><font color="RED">*</font></a> One experiences pleasure or pain [alternatively], from [voluntary] abandonment or [forcible] separation; as in the case of a hawk.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_5a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> That is to say: since people become happy by the abandonment of things, and unhappy by [forcible] separation from them, acceptance of them ought not to be made; 'as in the case of a hawk.'<a name="page_288_fr_4"></a><a href="#page_288_note_4"><font size="1">4</font></a> For a hawk, when he has food [before him], if he be driven away<a name="page_288_fr_5"></a><a href="#page_288_note_5"><font size="1">5</font></a> by any one, is grieved
 
 <!------------------------page 289------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_289"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 289</font></a>
 
 at being separated from the food; [but] if, of his own accord, he leaves it, then he is free from grief.</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   How Soul ought to abandon Nature.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_6"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 6.</font><a href="#pp_6"><font color="RED">*</font></a> As in the case of a snake and its skin.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_6a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> That is to say: as a snake readily abandons its old skin, from knowing that it ought to be quitted, just so he who desires liberation should abandon Nature, experienced through a long period, and effete, when he knows that it ought to be quitted. Thus it has been said: 'As a snake . . . its old skin,' &amp;c.</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 290------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_290"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 290</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_6b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> And, when abandoned, he should not again accept Nature and the rest. So, in regard to this, he says:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Its resumption prohibited.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_7"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 7.</font><a href="#pp_7"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Or as an amputated hand.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_7a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> As no one takes back again an amputated hand, just so this [Nature], when abandoned, he should not readmit: such is the meaning. The word 'Or' is used in the sense of moreover;' [the import of the conjunction being superadditive, not alternative].</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Duty to be sacrificed to salvation.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_8"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 8.</font><a href="#pp_8"><font color="RED">*</font></a> What is not a means [of liberation is] not to be thought about, [as this conduces only] to bondage; as in the case of Bharata.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_8a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> That which is not an immediate cause of Discrimination, even though it may be a duty, still is 'not to be thought about;' i.e., intention of the mind towards the performance thereof is not to be made; since it tends to Bondage, from its making us forget Discrimination. 'As in the case of Bharata:' that is to say, as was the case
 
 <!------------------------page 291------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_291"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 291</font></a>
 
 with the royal sage Bharata's cherishing Dínánátha's<a name="page_291_fr_1"></a><a href="#page_291_note_1"><font size="1">1</font></a> fawn, though [this was] in accordance with duty.</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Company to be avoided.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_9"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 9.</font><a href="#pp_9"><font color="RED">*</font></a> From [association with] many there is obstruction to concentration, through passion, &amp;c.; as in the case of a girl's shells.<a name="page_291_fr_4"></a><a href="#page_291_note_4"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_9a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> Association is not to be made with many; because, when there is association with many, there is disturbance, through the manifestation of Passion, &amp;c., which destroys concentration; as a jingling is produced by the mutual
 
 <!------------------------page 292------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_292"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 292</font></a>
 
 contact of the shells on a girl's wrist: such is the meaning.
 
 </p><p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Even that of one.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_10"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 10.</font><a href="#pp_10"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Just so, from [the company of] two, also.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_10a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> Just so, even from two there is obstruction to concentration; therefore one ought to abide quite <i>alone</i>: such is the meaning.</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Blessedness of those who expect nothing.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_11"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 11.</font><a href="#pp_11"><font color="RED">*</font></a> He who is without hope is happy; like Pingalá.<a name="page_292_fr_3"></a><a href="#page_292_note_3"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_11a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> Having abandoned hope, let a man become possessed of the happiness called contentment; 'like Pingalá;' that is to say, as the courtesan called Pingalá, desiring a lover, having found no lover, being despondent, became happy, when she had left off hoping.</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 293------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_293"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 293</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_11b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> But then, granting that Pain may cease, on the cessation of hope, yet how can there be <i>happiness</i>, in the absence of causes thereof? It is replied: That natural happiness, resulting from the predominance of Purity in the mind, which remains obscured by hope, itself resumes its influence, on the departure of hope; as is the case with the coolness of water which [supposed natural coolness] had been hindered [from manifesting itself,] by heat: there is not, in this case, any need of <i>means</i>. And it is laid down that precisely this is happiness of Soul.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_11c"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>c</i>.</font> Since it is an obstructer of Concentration, exertion with a view to experience is not to be made, since this will be effected quite otherwise; as he states:</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 294------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_294"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 294</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Exertion needless.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_12"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 12.</font><a href="#pp_12"><font color="RED">*</font></a> [One may be happy,] even without exertion; like a serpent happy in another's house.
 
 </p><p>   <a name="pp_12a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> Supply, 'he may be happy.' The rest is simple. So it has been said:<a name="page_294_fr_1"></a><a href="#page_294_note_1"><font size="1">1</font></a> 'The building of a house is, assuredly, painful, and in no way pleasant. A serpent, having entered the dwelling made by another [e.g., a rat], does find confort.'</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_12b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font>From Institutes, and from preceptors, only the essence is to be accepted; since, otherwise, it may be impossible to concentrate the attention, from there being, by reason of implications,<a name="page_294_fr_3"></a><a href="#page_294_note_3"><font size="1">3</font></a> discussions, &amp;c., discrepancies in declared unessential parts, and from the multiplicity of topics. So he says:</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 295------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_295"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 295</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   A bee-like eclecticism recommended.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_13"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 13.</font><a href="#pp_13"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Though he devote himself to many Institutes and teachers, a taking of the essence [is to be made]; as is the case with the bee.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_13a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> Supply 'is to be made.' The rest is simple. Thus it has been said: 'From small Institutes, and from great, the intelligent man should take, from all quarters, the essence; as the bee does from the flowers.'</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_13b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> Be the other means what they may, the direct possession of Discrimination is to be effected only by intentness, through maintaining Meditation; as he tells us:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Intentness on one object.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_14"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 14.</font><a href="#pp_14"><font color="RED">*</font></a> The Meditation is not interrupted of him whose mind is intent on one object; like the maker of arrows.<a name="page_295_fr_3"></a><a href="#page_295_note_3"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_14a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> As, in the case of a maker of arrows, with his mind intent solely on the making of an arrow, the exclusion of
 
 <!------------------------page 296------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_296"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 296</font></a>
 
 other thoughts is not interrupted even by a king's passing at his side, so, too, of him whose mind is intent on one point there is in no way an 'interruption of meditation,' i.e., a failure to exclude other thoughts.
 
 </p><p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Rules not to be transgressed with impunity.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_15"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 15.</font><a href="#pp_15"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Through transgression of the enjoined rules there is failure in the aim; as in the world.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_15a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> Whatever rule, for the practisers of Concentration, has been laid down in the Institutes, if it be transgressed, then the end, viz., the effecting of knowledge, is not attained. 'As in the world.' That is to say: just as, in ordinary life, if the enjoined procedures, &amp;c., in regard to a medicine, or the like, be neglected, this or that effect thereof will not be obtained.</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 297------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_297"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 297</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_15b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> He states, further, that, if the rules be <i>forgotten</i>, the end will not be gained:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Rules must not be forgotten.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_16"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 16.</font><a href="#pp_16"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Moreover, if they be forgotten; as in the case of the female frog.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_16a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> This is plain. And the story of the female frog is this: A certain king, having gone to hunt, saw a beautiful damsel in the forest. And she, being solicited in marriage by the king, made this stipulation: 'When water shall be shown to me by thee, then I must depart.' But, on one occasion, when wearied with sport, she asked the king, 'Where is water?' The king, too, forgetting his agreement, showed her the water. Then she, having become the she-frog <i>Kámarúpiṇí</i>,<a name="page_297_fr_2"></a><a href="#page_297_note_2"><font size="1">2</font></a> daughter of the king of the frogs, entered the water. And then the king, though he sought her with nets, &amp;c., did not regain her.</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 298------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_298"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 298</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_16b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> He mentions a story with reference to the necessity of reflecting on the words of the teacher, as well as hearing them:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Reflexion necessary, as well as hearing.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_17"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 17.</font><a href="#pp_17"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Not even though instruction be heard is the end gained, without reflexion; as in the case of Virochana.<a name="page_298_fr_4"></a><a href="#page_298_note_4"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_17a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> By 'reflexion' is meant such consideration as determines the import of the teacher's words. Without this, though the instruction be heard, knowledge of the truth does not necessarily follow; for it is written, that, though hearing the instruction of Prajápati, Virochana, as
 
 <!------------------------page 299------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_299"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 299</font></a>
 
 between Indra and Virochana, wanted discrimination, from want of reflexion:
 
 </p><p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Of this further.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_18"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 18.</font><a href="#pp_18"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Of those two, it [reflexion,] was seen in the case of Indra [only].</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_18a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> Of those two who are mentioned, [indicated] by the expression 'of those two,' reflexion [was seen, &amp;c.]. And, as between those two, viz., Indra and Virochana, reflexion was seen in the case of Indra: such is the meaning.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_18b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> And he tells us, that, by him who desires to understand thoroughly, attendance on the teacher should be practised for a long time:</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 300------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_300"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 300</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   The process requires time.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_19"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 19.</font><a href="#pp_19"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Having performed reverence, the duties of a student, and attendance, one has success after a long time; as in his case.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_19a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> 'As in his case.' That is to say: as in the case of Indra, so in the case of another, too, only after having practised, under a preceptor, reverence, study of the <i>Vedas</i>, service, &amp;c., is there 'success,' i.e., the revelation of truth; not otherwise.</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   The time for the process may embrace successive states of being.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_20"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 20.</font><a href="#pp_20"><font color="RED">*</font></a> There is no determination of the time; as in the Case of Vámadeva.<a name="page_300_fr_3"></a><a href="#page_300_note_3"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_20a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> In the arising of knowledge, there is 'no determination of the time,' as, for instance, in its taking place only from causes dependent on the senses. 'As in the Case of Vámadeva.' That is to say: as, in consequence of causes pertaining to a previous life, knowledge arose, in the case of Vámadeva, even when in embryo, so it may in the case of another.</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 301------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_301"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 301</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_20b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> But then, since it is written, that the means of knowledge need be nothing other than devotion to those [viz., Brahmá, &amp;c.,] who [unlike the Absolute,] have Qualities, knowledge may result from <i>this</i>. Why, then, a hard and subtle process of Concentration? To this he replies:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Inferior means not altogether unprofitable.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_21"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 21.</font><a href="#pp_21"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Through devotion to something under a superinduced form, [attainment to, or approach towards, knowledge takes place] <i>by degrees</i>; as in the case of those who devote themselves to sacrifices.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_21a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> Supply 'there is attainment.' Through devotion to Souls, e.g., Brahmá, Vishṉu, Śiva, under the forms superinduced on them, the effecting of knowledge takes place 'by degrees,' i.e., by the successive attainment of
 
 <!------------------------page 302------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_302"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 302</font></a>
 
 the worlds of Brahmá, &amp;c., or else through the purification or the Good principle, &amp;c., but not <i>directly</i>; as is the case with sacrificers [whose slaughter of animals, requiring to be expiated, throws them back, so far, in the road to emancipation]: such is the meaning.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_21b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> He tells us, that, moreover, there is no certainty that successive rise to the worlds of Brahmá, &amp;c., would effect knowledge:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Scriptural proof that heaven gives not liberation.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_22"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 22.</font><a href="#pp_22"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Moreover, after the attainment of what [like the world of Brahmá,] is other [than the state of emancipated soul], there is return [to mundane existence]; because it is written [in the 5th <i>Prapáṭhaka</i> of the <i>Chhándogya Upanishad</i><a name="page_302_fr_4"></a><a href="#page_302_note_4"><font size="1">4</font></a>]: 'From conjunction with the five fires there is birth,' &amp;c.</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 303------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_303"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 303</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_22a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> He exhibits an illustration, to the effect that the effecting of knowledge takes place only in the case of him who is free from passion:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Discrimination illustrated.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_23"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 23.</font><a href="#pp_23"><font color="RED">*</font></a> By him who is free from passion what is to be left is left, and what is to be taken is taken; as in the case of the swan and the milk.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_23a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> That is to say: only by him who is free from passion is there a quitting 'of what is to be left,' i.e., of Nature, &amp;c., and a taking 'of what is to be taken,' i.e., of Soul; as it is only the swan,—and not the crow, or the like,—that, out of milk and water mingled, by means of leaving the unimportant water, takes the valuable milk, [as the Hindus insist that it does].</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 304------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_304"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 304</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_23b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> He tells us that both of these also take place in consequence of association with a perfect<a name="page_304_fr_1"></a><a href="#page_304_note_1"><font size="1">1</font></a> man:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Benefit of good society.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_24"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 24.</font><a href="#pp_24"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Or through association with one who has obtained excellence;<a name="page_304_fr_4"></a><a href="#page_304_note_4"><font size="1">4</font></a> as in the case thereof.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_24a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> That is to say: moreover, from association with him by whom 'excellence,' i.e., excellence in knowledge, has been obtained, the aforesaid [discrimination] takes place; just as in the case of the swan, [<a href="#pp_23">§ 23</a>]; as, in the case of Alarka, Discrimination manifested itself spontaneously, merely through simple association with Dattátreya.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_24b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> He tells us that we ought not to associate with those who are infected with desire:</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 305------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_305"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 305</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Danger of unsuitable society.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_25"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 25.</font><a href="#pp_25"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Not of his own accord should he go near one who is infected with desire; like the parrot.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_25a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> Association is not to be made, voluntarily, with a person infected with desire. 'Like the parrot.' That is to say: just as the bird [called a] parrot, by reason of its being exceedingly beautiful, does not [by going near people,] act in a rash manner, through fear of being imprisoned by those who covet it for its beauty.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_25b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> And he states the harm of association with those who labour under desire:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Of this further.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_26"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 26.</font><a href="#pp_26"><font color="RED">*</font></a> [Else he may become] bound, by conjunction with the cords; as in the case of the parrot.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_26a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> And, in the case of associating with those persons, he may become bound, 'by conjunction with the cords,' i.e., by conjunction with their Desire, &amp;c., [the Qualities, punningly compared to cords]; just 'as in the case of the
 
 <!------------------------page 306------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_306"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 306</font></a>
 
 parrot;' that is to say, just as the bird [called a] parrot becomes bound by the cords, i.e., the ropes, of the hunter.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_26b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> He determines, by two [aphorisms]. the means of [effecting] dispassion:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Means of dispassion.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_27"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 27.</font><a href="#pp_27"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Not by enjoyment is desire appeased; as in the case of the saint.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_27a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> That is to say: as, in the case of the saint, Saubhari,<a name="page_306_fr_3"></a><a href="#page_306_note_3"><font size="1">3</font></a> desire was not appeased by enjoyment, so, also in the case of others, it is not.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_27b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> But, further:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Of this further.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_28"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 28.</font><a href="#pp_28"><font color="RED">*</font></a> From seeing the fault of both.</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 307------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_307"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 307</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_28a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> That is to say: only 'from seeing the fault,' e.g., of being changeable, of consisting of pain, &amp;c., 'of both,' i.e., of Nature and her productions, does the appeasing of desire take place; just as in the case of the saint [<a href="#pp_27">§ 27</a>]. For it is written, that Saubhari, just from seeing the evil of society, was afterwards dispassionate.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_28b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> He tells us that incompetency even to accept instruction attaches to him who is infected with the fault of desire, &amp;c:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Agitation excludes instruction.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_29"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 29.</font><a href="#pp_29"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Not in the case of him whose mind is disturbed does the seed of instruction sprout; as in the case of Aja.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_29a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> In him whose mind is disturbed by desire, &amp;c., not even does a sprout spring up from that seed of the tree of knowledge which is in the shape of instruction. 'As in the case of Aja.' That is to say: as not a sprout from
 
 <!------------------------page 308------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_308"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 308</font></a>
 
 the seed of instruction, though delivered to him by Vasishṭha, sprang up in the king named Aja, whose mind was disturbed by grief for his wife.<a name="page_308_fr_2"></a><a href="#page_308_note_2"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_29b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> What need of more?</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Of this further.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_30"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 30.</font><a href="#pp_30"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Not even a mere semblance [of this true knowledge arises in him whose mind is disturbed]; as in the case of a foul mirror.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_30a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> Even superficial knowledge does not arise, from instruction, in one whose mind is disturbed, through the obstruction caused by its wandering away, e.g., to other objects; as an object is not reflected in a foul mirror, through the obstruction caused by the impurities: such is the meaning.</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 309------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_309"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 309</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_30b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> Or, if knowledge should spring up in any kind of way, still it may not, he tells us, be in accordance with the instruction:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Knowledge not necessarily perfect knowledge.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_31"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 31.</font><a href="#pp_31"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Nor, even though sprung therefrom, is that [knowledge, necessarily,] in accordance therewith; like the lotus.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_31a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> Though sprung 'therefrom,' i.e., from instruction, knowledge is not [necessarily,] in accordance with the instruction, in case this has not been entirely understood. 'Like the lotus.' That is to say: just as the lotus, though the seed be of the best, is not in accordance with the seed, when the mud is faulty. The mind of the student is compared to the mud [in which the lotus-seed was sown].</p>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 310------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_310"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 310</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_31b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> But then, since the Soul's end is, indeed, gained by [the attainment of] supernatural power in the worlds [<a href="#pp_21a">§ 21. <i>a</i>.</a>] of Brahmá, &amp;c., to what purpose is the effecting of knowledge, with so much toil, for liberation? To this he replies:</p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <table align="left" cellpadding="5" width="33%">
 
 <tr><td><small><i>   Heaven not perfect bliss.</i></small></td></tr>
 
 </table>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_32"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>Aph</i>. 32.</font><a href="#pp_32"><font color="RED">*</font></a> Not even on the attainment of glorification has that been done which was to be done; as is the case with the perfection<a name="page_310_fr_4"></a><a href="#page_310_note_4"><font size="1">4</font></a> of the objects worshipped, as is the case with the perfection of the objects worshipped.</p>
 
 <p>   <a name="pp_32a"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>a</i>.</font> Even though one attain to supernatural power, 'that has not been done which was to be done,' i.e., the end has not been gained; because it is attended by the grief of deficiency and excess. 'As is the case with the perfection of the objects worshipped.' That is to say: as, though the possession of perfection [so called,] belongs to 'the objects
 
 <!------------------------page 311------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_311"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 311</font></a>
 
 worshipped,' i.e., to Brahmá, &amp;c., [still] that has not been done which was to be done; since it is written, that even <i>these</i>, while in the sleep of Concentration, &amp;c., [still] <i>practise</i> Concentration, [from fear of losing what they have attained to]. Just in like manner is the case with him who, by the worship of these, has attained to their supernatural power. Such is the meaning.
 
 </p><p>   <a name="pp_32b"></a><font color="GREEN"><i>b</i>.</font> So much for the Fourth Book, that of Tales, in the Commentary, composed by Vijnána Bhikshu, on Kapila's Declaration of the Sánkhya.</p>
