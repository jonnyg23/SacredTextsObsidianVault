 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="../../img/tn_hin.jpg"></a><br><i>The Minor Law Books (SBE33)</i>, by Julius Jolly, [1889], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">XVIII. RESCISSION OF PURCHASE AND SALE.</h3>  
 <p>1. <a name="fr_1135"></a><a href="#fn_1134"><font size="1">1</font></a> This set of rules concerning the law of agreements has been briefly stated; disputes arising from purchase and sale shall be treated next.</p>  
 <p>2. Two sorts of property are distinguished, immovable and movable; when a purchase is concluded, the term 'vendible property' (pa<i>n</i>ya) is applied to both.</p>  
 <p>3. <a name="fr_1136"></a><a href="#fn_1135"><font size="1">3</font></a> The purchaser shall examine a chattel himself and show it to others; when, after examining and approving it, he has accepted it, he is not at liberty to return it again.</p>  
 <p>4. <a name="fr_1137"></a><a href="#fn_1136"><font size="1">4</font></a> The foolish man who sells an article, though acquainted with its blemish, shall have to pay twice its value (to the vendee), and (a fine of) the same amount (to the king).</p>  
 <p>5. <a name="fr_1138"></a><a href="#fn_1137"><font size="1">5</font></a> What has been sold by one intoxicated or insane, or at a very low price, or under the impulse of fear, or by one not his own master, or by an idiot, shall be relinquished (by the purchaser, or it) may be recovered (from the purchaser) by forcible means.</p>
