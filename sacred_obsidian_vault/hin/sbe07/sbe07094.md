 <a name="page_271"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 271</font></a><h1 align="CENTER">XCII.</h1>
 <p>1. Protecting (one attacked by robbers, or by tigers, or otherwise in danger) is more meritorious than any (other) gift.</p>
 <p>[XCII. 1, 2. M. IV, 232; Y. I, 211.--3. M. IV, 230-5. M. {footnote p. 272} IV, 231; Y. I, 208.--8, 9. Y. I, 204, 205.--10. Y. I, 210.--10-12. M. IV, 231.--12, 13. Y. I, 210.--13, 14. M. IV, 230.--19, 20. M. IV, 232; Y. I, 211.--21-23. M. IV, 229, 232.--21. Y. I, 210.--27. M. IV, 232; Y. I, 211.--28-32. Y. I, 211.--31. M. IV, 230.]</p>
 <p><a name="page_272"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 272</font></a></p>
 <p>2. By doing so he obtains that place of abode (after death) which he desires himself.</p>
 <p>3. By giving land he obtains the same (heavenly reward).</p>
 <p>4. By giving land to the extent of a bull's hide only he is purified from every sin.</p>
 <p>5. By giving a cow he attains heaven.</p>
 <p>6. A giver of ten milch cows (obtains) the mansion of cows (after death).</p>
 <p>7. A giver of a hundred milch cows enters the mansions of Brahman (after death).</p>
 <p>8. He who gives (a milch cow) with gilt horns, with hoofs covered with silver, with a tail wound with a string of pearls, with a milk-pail of white copper, and with a cover of cloth, shall reside in heaven for as many years as the cow has hairs on her body;</p>
 <p>9. Particularly, if it is a brown cow.</p>
 <p>10. He who has given a tamed bull is (equal in virtue to) a giver of ten milch cows.</p>
 <font size="2"><p>[4. Nand. define., 'a bull's hide' as a measure of surface 300 Hastas (see X, 2, note) long by ten Hastas broad. See, however, V, 183.</p>
 <p>8. According to a Sm<i>ri</i>ti quoted by Nand., the gold upon the horns of the cow shall weigh ten Suvar<i>n</i>as, the silver on her hoofs ten Palas, the white copper of which the milk-pail is made fifty Palas, and she shall have copper on her back, which must also weigh fifty Palas.</p>
 <p>9. 'The meaning is, that a brown cow sends even his ancestors as far as the seventh degree to heaven, as Yâ<i>g</i>ñavalkya (I, 205) says.' (Nand.)]</p>
 </font><p><a name="page_273"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 273</font></a></p>
 <p>11. The giver of a horse attains the mansion of Sûrya (the sun-god).</p>
 <p>12. The giver of a garment (attains) the mansion of <i>K</i>andra (the god of the moon).</p>
 <p>13. By giving gold (he attains) the mansion of Agni (the god of fire).</p>
 <p>14. By giving silver (rûpya, he obtains) beauty (rûpa).</p>
 <p>15. By giving dishes (pâtra) made of (gold or silver or other) metal he renders himself worthy (pâtra) to obtain everything he may desire.</p>
 <p>16. By giving clarified butter, honey, or oil (he acquires) freedom from disease;</p>
 <p>17. The same by giving (boiled or otherwise dressed) drugs.</p>
 <p>18. By giving salt (lava<i>n</i>a, he obtains) personal charms (lâva<i>n</i>ya).</p>
 <p>19. By giving grain (produced in the rainy season, such as Syâmâka grain, he acquires) satiation;</p>
 <p>20. The same (effect is obtained) by giving grain (produced in winter or spring, such as wild turmeric or wheat).</p>
 <p>21. A giver of food (obtains) all the rewards (enumerated above).</p>
 <p>22. By giving grain (of any of the kinds not mentioned before, such as Kulattha or Kodrava grain, he obtains) good fortune.</p>
 <p>23. A giver of sesamum (obtains) such offspring as he desires.</p>
 <p>24. A giver of fuel (obtains) an excellent digestive power;</p>
 <p>25. And he obtains victory in every fight.</p>
 <p>26. By giving a seat (he obtains) high rank.</p>
 <p>27. By giving a bed.(of the kind declared above,</p>
 <p><a name="page_274"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 274</font></a></p>
 <p>XC, 7, he procures) a wife (possessed of the qualities mentioned above).</p>
 <p>28. By giving a pair of shoes (he obtains) a carriage yoked with mules.</p>
 <p>29. By giving an umbrella (he attains) heaven.</p>
 <p>30. By giving a fan or a chowrie (he obtains) prosperity in travelling.</p>
 <p>31. By giving a house (he receives) the post of governor of a town.</p>
 <p>32. Whatever a man is most fond of in this world (himself) and what his family like best, all that he must bestow upon a virtuous (Brâhma<i>n</i>a), if he wishes it to become imperishable.</p>
