 <a name="page_131"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 131</font></a><h1 align="CENTER">XXXIII.</h1>
 <p>1. Now man has three most dangerous enemies, called carnal desire, wrath, and greed.</p>
 <font size="2"><p>[17. <sup>1</sup> I. e. a Brâhma<i>n</i>a for whom the ceremony of initiation has been performed (Nand.) This proverb is also found in the Nîti<i>s</i>âstra 1,55, in the Mahâbhârata II, 1385 seq., &amp;c., and in other works. See Böhtlingk, Ind. Sprüche, 6163, 2456, &amp;c.</p>
 <p>XXXIII. 1. Âpast. I, 8, 23, 4, 5.</p>
 <p>1. The mention which has been made in the preceding section, that on or rules of conduct, of the breach of the vow of {footnote p. 132} chastity and the penance for it (see XXVIII, 48, 49), causes him (Vish<i>n</i>u) to discuss the law of penance (Prâya<i>sk</i>itta). This is done in the following section, to which Chapter XXXIV serves as Introduction. (Nand.) The section on Prâya<i>sk</i>itta extends as far as Chapter LVII.]</p>
 </font><p><a name="page_132"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 132</font></a></p>
 <p>2. They are specially dangerous to the order of householders, because they have (houses, wives, and other) property.</p>
 <p>3. Man, being overcome by those (three enemies), commits crimes in the highest degree, high crimes, minor crimes, and crimes in the fourth degree;</p>
 <p>4. Also crimes effecting loss of caste, crimes degrading to a mixed caste, and crimes rendering the perpetrator unworthy (to receive alms and the like);</p>
 <p>5. And crimes causing defilement, and miscellaneous offences.</p>
 <p>6. This is the threefold path to hell, destructive of self: carnal desire, wrath, and greed: therefore must a man shun those three vices.</p>
