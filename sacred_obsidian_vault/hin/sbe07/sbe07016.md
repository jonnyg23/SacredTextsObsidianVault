 <a name="page_60"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 60</font></a><h1 align="CENTER">XIV.</h1>
 <p>1. Now follows the (rule regarding the ordeal by) sacred libation.</p>
 <p>2. Having invoked terrible deities (such as Durgâ, the Âdityas or others, the defendant) must drink three handfuls of water in which (images of) those deities have been bathed,</p>
 <font size="2"><p>[XIII. 3, 5-7. Y. II, 110, 111.</p>
 <p>XIV. 2, 4, 5. Y. II, 112, 113.]</p>
 </font><p><a name="page_61"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 61</font></a></p>
 <p>3. Uttering at the same time the words, 'I have not done this,' with his face turned towards the deity (in question).</p>
 <p>4. He to whom (any calamity) happens within a fortnight or three weeks (such as an illness, or fire, or the death of a relative, or a heavy visitation by the king),</p>
 <p>5. Should be known to be guilty; otherwise (if nothing adverse happens to him), he is freed from the charge. A just king should honour (with presents of clothes, ornaments, &amp;c.) one who has cleared himself from guilt by an ordeal.</p>
