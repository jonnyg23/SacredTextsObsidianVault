 <a name="page_23"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 23</font></a><h1 align="CENTER">IV.</h1>
 <p>1. The (very small mote of) dust which may be discerned in a sun-beam passing through a lattice is called trasare<i>n</i>u (trembling dust).</p>
 <p>2. Eight of these (trasare<i>n</i>us) are equal to a nit.</p>
 <p>3. Three of the latter are equal to a black mustard-seed.</p>
 <p>4. Three of these last are equal to a white mustard-seed.</p>
 <p>5. Six of these are equal to a barley-corn.</p>
 <p>6. Three of these equal a K<i>ri</i>sh<i>n</i>ala.</p>
 <font size="2"><p>[6. Krishnala (literally, 'seed, of the Guñ<i>g</i>â creeper') is another {footnote p. 24} name for Raktikâ or Ratî, the lowest denomination in general use. According to Prinsep (Useful Tables, p. 97) it equals 1.875 grains = 0.122 grammes of the metrical system. According to Thomas (see Colebrooke's Essays, ed. by Cowell, I, p. 529, note) it equals 1.75 grains.]</p>
 </font><p><a name="page_24"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 24</font></a></p>
 <p>7. Five of these equal a Mâsha.</p>
 <p>8. Twelve of these are equal to half an Aksha.</p>
 <p>9. The weight of half an Aksha, with four Mâshas added to it, is called a Suvar<i>n</i>a.</p>
 <p>10. Four Suvar<i>n</i>as make a Nishka.</p>
 <p>11. Two K<i>ri</i>sh<i>n</i>alas of equal weight are equal to one Mâshaka of silver.</p>
 <p>12. Sixteen of these are equal to a Dhara<i>n</i>a (of silver).</p>
 <p>13. A Karsha (or eighty Raktikâs) of copper is called Kârshâpa<i>n</i>a.</p>
 <p>14. Two hundred and fifty (copper) Pa<i>n</i>as are declared to be the first (or lowest) amercement, five hundred are considered as the middlemost, and a thousand as the highest.</p>
