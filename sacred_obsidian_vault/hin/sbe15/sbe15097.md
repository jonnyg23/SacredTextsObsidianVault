 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="../../img/tn_hin.jpg"></a><br><i>The Upanishads, Part 2 (SBE15)</i>, by Max Müller, [1879], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>   <h3 align="center">FOURTH BRÂHMA<i>N</i>A <a name="fr_623"></a><a href="#fn_623"><font size="1">2</font></a>.</h3>  
 <p>1. The earth is the essence of all these things, water is the essence of the earth, plants of water, flowers of plants, fruits of flowers, man of fruits, seed of man.</p>  
 <p>2. And Pra<i>g</i>âpati thought, let me make an abode for him, and he created a woman (<i>S</i>atarûpâ).</p>  
 <p>Tâ<i>m</i> <a name="fr_624"></a><a href="#fn_624"><font size="1">3</font></a> s<i>ri</i>sh<i>t</i>vâdha upâsta, tasmât striyam adha upâsîta. Sa etam prâ<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>a<i>m</i> grâvâ<i>n</i>am âtmana eva samudapârayat, tenainâm abhyas<i>ri</i><i>g</i>at.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_216"><font size="1" color="green">p. 216</font></a></p>  
 <p>3. Tasyâ vedir upastho, lomâni barhi<i>s</i>, <i>k</i>armâdhishava<i>n</i>e, samiddho <a name="fr_625"></a><a href="#fn_625"><font size="1">1</font></a> madhyatas, tau mushkau. Sa yâvân ha vai vâ<i>g</i>apeyena ya<i>g</i>amânasya loko bhavati tâvân asya loko bhavati ya eva<i>m</i> vidvân adhopahâsa<i>m</i> <i>k</i>araty a sa <a name="fr_626"></a><a href="#fn_626"><font size="1">2</font></a> strî<i>n</i>a<i>m</i> suk<i>ri</i>ta<i>m</i> v<i>ri</i>ṅkte 'tha ya idam avidvân adhopahâsa<i>m</i> <i>k</i>araty âsya striya<i>h</i> suk<i>ri</i>ta<i>m</i> v<i>ri</i><i>ñ</i><i>g</i>ate.</p>  
 <p>4. Etad dha sma vai tadvidvân Uddâlaka Âru<i>n</i>ir âhaitad dha sma vai tadvidvân Nâko Maudgalya âhaitad dha sma vai tadvidvân Kumârahârita âha, bahavo maryâ brâhma<i>n</i>âyanâ <a name="fr_627"></a><a href="#fn_627"><font size="1">3</font></a> nirindriyâ visuk<i>ri</i>to'smâl lokât prayanti <a name="fr_628"></a><a href="#fn_628"><font size="1">4</font></a> ya ida<i>m</i> avidvâ<i>m</i>so 'dhopahâsa<i>m</i> <i>k</i>arantîti. Bahu vâ <a name="fr_629"></a><a href="#fn_629"><font size="1">5</font></a> ida<i>m</i> suptasya va <i>g</i>âgrato vâ reta<i>h</i> skandati,</p>  
 <p>5. Tad abhim<i>ri</i><i>s</i>ed anu vâ mantrayeta yan me 'dya reta<i>h</i> p<i>ri</i>thivîm askântsîd yad oshadhîr apy asarad yad apa<i>h</i>, idam aha<i>m</i> tad reta âdade punar mâm aitv indriyam punas te<i>g</i>a<i>h</i> punar bhaga<i>h</i>, punar agnayo <a name="fr_630"></a><a href="#fn_630"><font size="1">6</font></a> dhish<i>n</i>yâ yathâsthâna<i>m</i> kalpantâm, ity anâmikâṅgush<i>th</i>âbhyâm âdâyântare<i>n</i>a stanau vâ bhruvau vâ nim<i>ri</i><i>ñ</i><i>g</i>yât <a name="fr_631"></a><a href="#fn_631"><font size="1">7</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>6. If a man see himself in the water <a name="fr_632"></a><a href="#fn_632"><font size="1">8</font></a>, he should</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_217"><font size="1" color="green">p. 217</font></a></p>  
 <p>recite the following verse: 'May there be in me splendour, strength, glory, wealth, virtue.'</p>  
 <p>She is the best of women whose garments are pure <a name="fr_633"></a><a href="#fn_633"><font size="1">1</font></a>. Therefore let him approach a woman whose garments are pure, and whose fame is pure, and address her.</p>  
 <p>7. If she do not give in <a name="fr_634"></a><a href="#fn_634"><font size="1">2</font></a>, let him, as he likes, bribe her (with presents). And if she then do not give in, let him, as he likes, beat her with a stick or with his hand, and overcome her <a name="fr_635"></a><a href="#fn_635"><font size="1">3</font></a>, saying: 'With manly strength and glory I take away thy glory,'--and thus she becomes unglorious <a name="fr_636"></a><a href="#fn_636"><font size="1">4</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>8. If she give in, he says: 'With manly strength and glory I give thee glory,'--and thus they both become glorious.</p>  
 <p>9. Sa yâm i<i>k</i><i>kh</i>et kâmayeta meti tasyâm artha<i>m</i> nish<i>t</i>âya <a name="fr_637"></a><a href="#fn_637"><font size="1">5</font></a> mukhena mukha<i>m</i> sandhâyopastham asyâ abhim<i>ri</i><i>s</i>ya <i>g</i>aped aṅgâdaṅgât sambhavasi h<i>ri</i>dayâd adhi <i>g</i>âyase, sa tvam aṅgakashâyo <a name="fr_638"></a><a href="#fn_638"><font size="1">6</font></a> 'si digdhaviddhâm <a name="fr_639"></a><a href="#fn_639"><font size="1">7</font></a> iva mâdayemâm amûm mayîti <a name="fr_640"></a><a href="#fn_640"><font size="1">8</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>10. Atha yâm i<i>k</i><i>kh</i>en na garbha<i>m</i> dadhîteti <a name="fr_641"></a><a href="#fn_641"><font size="1">9</font></a> tasyâm artha<i>m</i> nish<i>t</i>âya mukhena mukha<i>m</i> sandhâyâbhiprâ<i>n</i>yâpânyâd indriye<i>n</i>a te retasâ reta âdada ity aretâ <a name="fr_642"></a><a href="#fn_642"><font size="1">10</font></a> eva bhavati.</p>  
 <p>

 <p>11. Atha yâm i<i>k</i><i>kh</i>ed garbha<i>m</i> dadhîteti tasyâm artha<i>m</i> nish<i>t</i>âya mukhena mukha<i>m</i> sandhâyâpânyâbhiprâ<i>n</i>yâd indriye<i>n</i>a te retâsa reta âdadhâmîti garbhi<i>n</i>y eva bhavati.</p>  
 <p>12. Now again, if a man's wife has a lover and the husband hates him, let him (according to rule) <a name="fr_643"></a><a href="#fn_643"><font size="1">1</font></a> place fire by an unbaked jar, spread a layer of arrows in inverse order <a name="fr_644"></a><a href="#fn_644"><font size="1">2</font></a>, anoint these three arrow-heads <a name="fr_645"></a><a href="#fn_645"><font size="1">3</font></a> with butter in inverse order, and sacrifice, saying: 'Thou hast sacrificed in my fire, I take away thy up and down breathing, I here <a name="fr_646"></a><a href="#fn_646"><font size="1">4</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>'Thou hast sacrificed in my fire, I take away thy sons and cattle, I here.'</p>  
 <p>'Thou hast sacrificed in my fire, I take away thy sacred and thy good works, I here.'</p>  
 <p>'Thou hast sacrificed in my fire, I take away thy hope and expectation, I here.'</p>  
 <p>He whom a Brâhma<i>n</i>a who knows this curses, departs from this world without strength and without good works. Therefore let no one wish even for sport with the wife of a <i>S</i>rotriya <a name="fr_647"></a><a href="#fn_647"><font size="1">5</font></a> who knows this, for he who knows this, is a dangerous enemy.</p>  
 <p>13. When the monthly illness seizes his wife, she</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_219"><font size="1" color="green">p. 219</font></a></p>  
 <p>should for three days not drink from a metal vessel, and wear a fresh dress. Let no V<i>ri</i>shala or V<i>ri</i>shalî (a <i>S</i>ûdra man or woman) touch her. At the end of the three days, when she has bathed, the husband should make her pound rice <a name="fr_648"></a><a href="#fn_648"><font size="1">1</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>14. And if a man wishes that a white son should be born to him, and that he should know one Veda, and live to his full age, then, after having prepared boiled rice with milk and butter, they should both eat, being fit to have offspring.</p>  
 <p>15. And if a man wishes that a reddish <a name="fr_649"></a><a href="#fn_649"><font size="1">2</font></a> son with tawny eyes should be born to him, and that he should know two Vedas, and live to his full age, then, after having prepared boiled rice with coagulated milk and butter, they should both eat, being fit to have offspring.</p>  
 <p>16. And if a man wishes that a dark son should be born to him with red eyes, and that he should know three Vedas, and live to his full age, then, after having prepared boiled rice with water and butter, they should both eat, being fit to have offspring.</p>  
 <p>17. And if a man wishes that a learned daughter should be born to him, and that she should live to her full age, then, after having prepared boiled rice with sesamum and butter, they should both eat, being fit to have offspring.</p>  
 <p>18. And if a man wishes that a learned son should be born to him, famous, a public man, a popular speaker, that he should know all the Vedas, and that</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_220"><font size="1" color="green">p. 220</font></a></p>  
 <p>he should live to his full age, then, after having prepared boiled rice with meat and butter, they should both eat, being fit to have offspring. The meat should be of a young or of an old bull.</p>  
 <p>19. And then toward morning, after having, according to the rule of the Sthâlîpâka (pot-boiling), performed the preparation of the Â<i>g</i>ya (clarified butter <a name="fr_650"></a><a href="#fn_650"><font size="1">1</font></a>), he sacrifices from the Sthâlîpâka bit by bit, saying: 'This is for Agni, Svâhâ! This is for Anumati, Svâhâ! This is for the divine Savit<i>ri</i>, the true creator, Svâhâ!' Having sacrificed, he takes out the rest of the rice and eats it, and after having eaten, he gives it to his wife. Then he washes his hands, fills a water-jar, and sprinkles her thrice with it, saying: 'Rise hence, O Vi<i>s</i>vâvasu <a name="fr_651"></a><a href="#fn_651"><font size="1">2</font></a>, seek another blooming girl, a wife with her husband.'</p>  
 <p>20. Then he embraces her, and says: 'I am Ama (breath), thou art Sâ (speech) <a name="fr_652"></a><a href="#fn_652"><font size="1">3</font></a>. Thou art Sâ (speech), I am Ama (breath). I am the Sâman, thou art the <i>Ri</i><i>k</i> <a name="fr_653"></a><a href="#fn_653"><font size="1">4</font></a>. I am the sky, thou art the earth. Come, let us strive together, that a male child may be begotten <a name="fr_654"></a><a href="#fn_654"><font size="1">5</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_221"><font size="1" color="green">p. 221</font></a></p>  
 <p>21. Athâsyâ ûrû vihâpayati, vi<i>g</i>ihîthâ<i>m</i> dyâvâp<i>ri</i>thivî iti tasyâm artha<i>m</i> nish<i>t</i>âya mukhena mukha<i>m</i> sandhâya trir enâm anulomâm <a name="fr_655"></a><a href="#fn_655"><font size="1">1</font></a> anumârsh<i>t</i>i, Vish<i>n</i>ur yoni<i>m</i> kalpayatu, Tvash<i>t</i>â rûpâ<i>n</i>i pi<i>m</i>satu, âsi<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>atu Pra<i>g</i>âpatir Dhâtâ garbha<i>m</i> dadhatu te. Garbha<i>m</i> dhehi Sinîvâli, garbha<i>m</i> dhehi p<i>ri</i>thush<i>t</i>uke, garbha<i>m</i> te A<i>s</i>vinau devâv âdhattâm pushkarasra<i>g</i>au.</p>  
 <p>22. Hira<i>n</i>mayî ara<i>n</i>î yâbhyâ<i>m</i> nirmanthatâm <a name="fr_656"></a><a href="#fn_656"><font size="1">2</font></a> a<i>s</i>vinau <a name="fr_657"></a><a href="#fn_657"><font size="1">3</font></a>, ta<i>m</i> te garbha<i>m</i> havâmahe <a name="fr_658"></a><a href="#fn_658"><font size="1">4</font></a> da<i>s</i>ame mâsi sûtave. Yathâgnigarbhâ p<i>ri</i>thivî, yathâ dyaur indre<i>n</i>a garbhi<i>n</i>î, vâyur di<i>s</i>â<i>m</i> yathâ garbha eva<i>m</i> garbha<i>m</i> dadhâ<i>m</i>î te 'sav iti <a name="fr_659"></a><a href="#fn_659"><font size="1">5</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>23. Soshyantîm <a name="fr_660"></a><a href="#fn_660"><font size="1">6</font></a> adbhir abhyukshati. Yathâ vâyu<i>h</i> <a name="fr_661"></a><a href="#fn_661"><font size="1">7</font></a> pushkari<i>n</i>î<i>m</i> sami<i>ñ</i><i>g</i>ayati sarvata<i>h</i>, evâ te garbha e<i>g</i>atu sahâvaitu garâyu<i>n</i>â. Indrasyâya<i>m</i> vra<i>g</i>a<i>h</i> k<i>ri</i>ta<i>h</i> sârga<i>l</i>a<i>h</i> <a name="fr_662"></a><a href="#fn_662"><font size="1">8</font></a> sapari<i>s</i>raya<i>h</i> <a name="fr_663"></a><a href="#fn_663"><font size="1">9</font></a>, tam indra nir<i>g</i>ahi garbhe<i>n</i>a sâvarâ<i>m</i> <a name="fr_664"></a><a href="#fn_664"><font size="1">10</font></a> saheti.</p>

 <p>24 <a name="fr_665"></a><a href="#fn_665"><font size="1">1</font></a>. When the child is born, he prepares the fire, places the child on his lap, and having poured p<i>ri</i>shadâ<i>g</i>ya, i.e. dadhi (thick milk) mixed with gh<i>ri</i>ta (clarified butter) into a metal jug, he sacrifices bit by bit of that p<i>ri</i>shadâ<i>g</i>ya, saying: 'May I, as I increase in this my house, nourish a thousand! May fortune never fail in his race, with offspring and cattle, Svâhâ!'</p>  
 <p>'I offer to thee. in my mind the vital breaths which are in me, Svâhâ!'</p>  
 <p>'Whatever <a name="fr_666"></a><a href="#fn_666"><font size="1">2</font></a> in my work I have done too much, or whatever I have here done too little, may the wise Agni Svish<i>t</i>ak<i>ri</i>t make this right and proper for us, Svâhâ!'</p>  
 <p>25. Then putting his mouth near the child's right ear, he says thrice, Speech, speech <a name="fr_667"></a><a href="#fn_667"><font size="1">3</font></a>! After</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_223"><font size="1" color="green">p. 223</font></a></p>  
 <p>that he pours together thick milk, honey, and clarified butter, and feeds the child with (a ladle of) pure gold <a name="fr_668"></a><a href="#fn_668"><font size="1">1</font></a>, saying: 'I give thee Bhû<i>h</i>, I give thee Bhuva<i>h</i>, I give thee Sva<i>h</i> <a name="fr_669"></a><a href="#fn_669"><font size="1">2</font></a>. Bhûr, Bhuva<i>h</i>, Sva<i>h</i>, I give thee all <a name="fr_670"></a><a href="#fn_670"><font size="1">3</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>26 <a name="fr_671"></a><a href="#fn_671"><font size="1">4</font></a>. Then he gives him his name, saying: 'Thou art Veda;' but this is his secret name <a name="fr_672"></a><a href="#fn_672"><font size="1">5</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>27. Then he hands the boy to his mother and gives him her breast, saying: 'O Sarasvatî, that breast of thine which is inexhaustible, delightful, abundant, wealthy, generous, by which thou cherishest all blessings, make that to flow here <a name="fr_673"></a><a href="#fn_673"><font size="1">6</font></a>.'</p>  
 <p>28 <a name="fr_674"></a><a href="#fn_674"><font size="1">7</font></a>. Then he addresses the mother of the boy:</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>

 </p>

 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_224a"><font size="1" color="green">p. 224a</font></a></p>  
 <p>'Thou art I<i>l</i>â Maitrâvaru<i>n</i>î: thou strong woman hast born a strong boy. Be thou blessed with strong children thou who hast blessed me with a strong child.'</p>  
 <p>And they say of such a boy: 'Ah, thou art better than thy father; ah, thou art better than thy grandfather. Truly he has reached the highest point in happiness, praise, and Vedic glory who is born as the son of a Brâhma<i>n</i>a that knows this.'</p>

 <p><a name="fn_622"></a><a href="sbe15096.htm#fr_622">215:1</a> According to the rules laid down in the proper Grihya-sûtras.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_623"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_623">215:2</a> This Brâhma<i>n</i>a is inserted here because there is supposed to be some similarity between the preparation of the <i>S</i>rîmantha and the Putramantha, or because a person who has performed the <i>S</i>rîmantha is fit to perform the Putramantha. Thus <i>S</i>aṅkara says: Prâ<i>n</i>adar<i>s</i>ina<i>h</i> <i>s</i>rîmantha<i>m</i> karma k<i>ri</i>tavata<i>h</i> putramanthe 'dhikâra<i>h</i>. Yadâ putramantha<i>m</i> <i>k</i>ikîrshati tadâ srîmantha<i>m</i> k<i>ri</i>tvâ <i>ri</i>tukâla<i>m</i> patnyâ<i>h</i> (brahma<i>k</i>arye<i>n</i>a) pratîkshata iti.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_624"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_624">215:3</a> I have given those portions of the text which did not admit of translation into English, in Sanskrit. It was not easy, however, to determine always the text of the Kânva-<i>s</i>âkhâ. Poley's text is not always correct, and Roer seems simply to repeat it. <i>S</i>aṅkara's commentary, which is meant for the Kânva text, becomes very short towards the end of the Upanishad. It is quite sufficient for the purpose of a translation, but by no means always for restoring a correct text. MS. Wilson 369, which has been assigned to the Kâ<i>n</i>va-<i>s</i>âkhâ, and which our Catalogue attributes to the same school, gives the Mâdhyandina text, and so does MS. Mill 108. I have therefore collated two MSS. of the India Office, which Dr. Rost had the kindness to select for me, MS. 375 and MS. 1973, which I call A. and B.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_625"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_625">216:1</a> Roer reads samidho, but <i>S</i>aṅkara and Dvivedagaṅga clearly presuppose samiddho, which is in A. and B.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_626"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_626">216:2</a> Roer has âsâ<i>m</i> sa strî<i>n</i>âm, Poley, A. and B. have âsâ<i>m</i> strî<i>n</i>âm. <i>S</i>aṅkara. (MS. Mill 64) read â sa strî<i>n</i>âm, and later on âsya striya<i>h</i>, though both Roer and Poley leave out the â here too (â asyeti <i>kh</i>eda<i>h</i>).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_627"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_627">216:3</a> Brâhma<i>n</i>âyanâ<i>h</i>, the same as brahmabandhava<i>h</i>, i.e. Brâhmans by descent only, not by knowledge.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_628"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_628">216:4</a> Naraka<i>m</i> ga<i>k</i><i>kh</i>antîtyartha<i>h</i>. Dvivedagaṅga.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_629"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_629">216:5</a> Bahu vâ svalpa<i>m</i> vâ.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_630"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_630">216:6</a> The Mâdhyandina text has agnayo, and Dvivedagaṅga explains it by dhîsh<i>n</i>yâ agnaya<i>h</i> <i>s</i>arîrasthitâ<i>h</i>. Poley and Roer have punar agnir dhish<i>n</i>yâ, and so have A. and B.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_631"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_631">216:7</a> Nirm<i>ri</i><i>g</i>yât, A.; nim<i>ri</i><i>ñ</i><i>g</i>yât, B.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_632"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_632">216:8</a> Dvivedagaṅga adds, retoyonâv udake reta<i>h</i>si<i>k</i>as tatra sva<i>k</i><i>kh</i>âyâdar<i>s</i>ane prâya<i>s</i><i>k</i>ittam âha.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_633"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_633">217:1</a> Trirâtravrata<i>m</i> k<i>ri</i>tvâ <i>k</i>aturtha 'hni snâtâm.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_634"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_634">217:2</a> Instead of connecting kâmam with dadyât, Dvivedagaṅga explains it by yathâ<i>s</i>akti.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_635"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_635">217:3</a> Atikram, scil. maithunâya.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_636"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_636">217:4</a> Bandhyâ durbhagâ.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_637"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_637">217:5</a> Nish<i>t</i>âya, A. B.; nish<i>th</i>âya, Roer, Poley; the same in § 10.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_638"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_638">217:6</a> Sa tvam aṅgânâ<i>m</i> kashâyo raso 'si.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_639"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_639">217:7</a> Vishalipta<i>s</i>araviddhâm m<i>ri</i>gîm iva.</p>  
 <p>
 </p><p><a name="fn_640"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_640">217:8</a> Mâdayeti is the reading of the Mâdhyandina text. Poley, Roer, A. and B. read mâdayemâm amûm mayîti. Ânandagiri has m<i>ri</i>gîm ivâmûm madiyâ<i>m</i> striyam me mâdaya madva<i>s</i>â<i>m</i> kurv ityartha<i>h</i>. Dvivedagaṅga explains mâdayeti.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_641"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_641">217:9</a> Rûpabhra<i>m</i><i>s</i>ayauvanahânibhayât.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_642"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_642">217:10</a> Agarbhi<i>n</i>î.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_643"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_643">218:1</a> Âvasathyâgnim eva pra<i>g</i>vâlya.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_644"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_644">218:2</a> Pa<i>s</i><i>k</i>imâgra<i>m</i> dakshi<i>n</i>âgra<i>m</i> vâ yathâ syât tathâ.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_645"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_645">218:3</a> Tisra<i>h</i> is left out by Roer and Poley, by A. and B.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_646"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_646">218:4</a> I have translated according to the Kâ<i>n</i>va text, as far as it could be made out. As there are four imprecations, it is but natural that tisra<i>h</i> should be left out in the Kâ<i>n</i>va text. It is found in the Mâdhyandina text, because there the imprecations are only three in number, viz. the taking away of hope and expectation, of sons and cattle, and of up and down breathing. Instead of asâv iti, which is sufficient, the Mâdhyandina text has asâv iti nâma g<i>ri</i>h<i>n</i>âti, and both Ânandagiri and Dvivedagaṅga allow the alternative, âtmana<i>h</i> <i>s</i>atror vâ nâma grih<i>n</i>âti, though asau can really refer to the speaker only.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_647"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_647">218:5</a> Roer reads dvâre<i>n</i>a; Poley, A. and B. dâre<i>n</i>a; the Mâdhyandinas <a name="page_219"><font size="1" color="green">p. 219</font></a> <i>g</i>âyâyâ. <i>S</i>aṅkara, according to Roer, interprets dvâre<i>n</i>a, but it seems that dvâre<i>n</i>a is used here in the singular, instead of the plural. See Pâraskara G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtras I, 11.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_648"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_648">219:1</a> To be used for the ceremony described in § 14 seq.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_649"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_649">219:2</a> Kapilo var<i>n</i>ata<i>h</i> piṅgala<i>h</i> piṅgâksha<i>h</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_650"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_650">220:1</a> Karu<i>m</i> <i>s</i>rapayitvâ.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_651"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_651">220:2</a> Name of a Gandharva, as god of love. See <i>Ri</i>g-veda X, 85, 22. Dvivedagaṅga explains the verse differently, so that the last words imply, I come together with my own wife.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_652"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_652">220:3</a> Because speech is dependent on breath, as the wife is on the husband. See <i>Kh</i>ând. Up. I, 6, 1.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_653"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_653">220:4</a> Because the Sâma-veda rests on the <i>Ri</i>g-veda.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_654"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_654">220:5</a> This is a verse which is often quoted and explained. It occurs in the Atharva-veda XIV, 71, as 'amo 'ham asmi si tva<i>m</i>, sâmâham asmy <i>ri</i>k tvam, dyaur aham p<i>ri</i>thivî tvam; tâv iha sam bhavâva pra<i>g</i>âm â ganayâvahai.'</p>  
 <p>Here we have the opposition between ama<i>h</i> and sâ, while in the Ait. Brâhma<i>n</i>a VIII, 27, we have amo 'ham asmi sa tvam, giving ama<i>h</i> in opposition to sa. It seems not unlikely that this <a name="page_221"><font size="1" color="green">p. 221</font></a> was an old proverbial formula, and that it meant originally no more than 'I am he, and thou art she.' But this meaning was soon forgotten. In the <i>Kh</i>ând. Up. I, 6, 1, we find sâ explained as earth, ama as fire (Sacred Books of the East, vol. i, p. 13). In the Ait. Brâhma<i>n</i>a sâ is explained as <i>Ri</i><i>k</i>, ama as Sâman. I have therefore in our passage also followed the interpretation of the commentary, instead of rendering it, 'I am he, and thou art she; thou art she, and I am he.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_655"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_655">221:1</a> Anulomam, mûrdhânam ârabhya pâdântam.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_656"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_656">221:2</a> Nirmathitavantau.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_657"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_657">221:3</a> A<i>s</i>vinau devau, Mâdhyandina text.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_658"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_658">221:4</a> Dadhâmahe, Mâdhyandina text. Instead of sûtave, A. has sûyate, B. sûtaye.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_659"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_659">221:5</a> Iti nâma g<i>ri</i>h<i>n</i>âti, Mâdhyandina text. <i>S</i>aṅkara says, asâv iti tasyâ<i>h</i>. Ânandagiri says, asâv iti patyur vâ nirde<i>s</i>a<i>h</i>; tasyâ nâma g<i>ri</i>h<i>n</i>âtîti pûrve<i>n</i>a sambandha<i>h</i>. Dvivedagaṅga says, ante bhartâsâv aham iti svâtmano nâma g<i>ri</i>h<i>n</i>âti, bhâryâyâ vâ.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_660"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_660">221:6</a> See Pâraskara G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtra I. 16 seq.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_661"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_661">221:7</a> Vatâ<i>h</i>, M.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_662"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_662">221:8</a> Argadayâ nirodhena saha vartamâna<i>h</i> sârga<i>d</i>a<i>h</i>, Dvivedagaṅga.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_663"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_663">221:9</a> Sapari<i>s</i>raya<i>h</i>, pari<i>s</i>raye<i>n</i>a parivesh<i>t</i>anena garâyu<i>n</i>â sahita<i>h</i>, Dvivedagaṅga.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_664"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_664">221:10</a> Sâvarâm is the reading given by Poley, Roer, A. and B. <a name="page_222"><font size="1" color="green">p. 222</font></a> Ânandagiri explains: garbhani<i>h</i>sara<i>n</i>ânantara<i>m</i> yâ mâ<i>m</i>sape<i>s</i>î nirga<i>k</i><i>kh</i>ati sâvarâ, tâ<i>m</i> <i>k</i>a nirgamayety artha<i>h</i>. Dvivedagaṅga (ed. Weber) writes: nirgamyamânamâ<i>m</i>sape<i>s</i>î sâ-avara<i>s</i>abdavâ<i>k</i>yâ, ta<i>m</i> sâvara<i>m</i> <i>k</i>a nirgamaya.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_665"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_665">222:1</a> These as well as the preceding rules refer to matters generally treated in the G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtras; see Â<i>s</i>valâyana, G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtras I, 13 seq.; Pâraskara, G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtras I, 11 seq.; <i>S</i>âṅkâkyana, G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtras I, 19 seq. It is curious, however, that Â<i>s</i>valâyana I, 13, 1, refers distinctly to the Upanishad as the place where the pu<i>m</i>savana and similar matters were treated. This shows that the Upanishads were known before the composition of the G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtras, and explains perhaps, at least partially, why the Upanishads were considered as rahasya. Â<i>s</i>valâyana says, 'Conception, begetting of a boy, and guarding the embryo are to be found in the Upanishad. But if a man does not read the Upanishad, let him know that he should feed his wife,' &amp;c. Nârâya<i>n</i>a explains that Â<i>s</i>valâyana here refers to an Upanishad which does not exist in his own <i>S</i>âkhâ, but he objects to the conclusion that therefore the garbhâdhâna and other ceremonies need not be performed, and adds that some hold it should be performed, as prescribed by <i>S</i>aunaka and others.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_666"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_666">222:2</a> Â<i>s</i>valâyana, G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtra I, 10, 23.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_667"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_667">222:3</a> Trayîlaksha<i>n</i>â vâk tvayi pravi<i>s</i>atv iti <i>g</i>apato 'bhiprâya<i>h</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_668"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_668">223:1</a> Cf. Pâraskara G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtras I, 16, 4, anâmikayâ suvar<i>n</i>ântarhitayâ; <i>S</i>âṅkhâyana, G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtras I, 24, prâ<i>s</i>aye<i>g</i> <i>g</i>âtarupe<i>n</i>a.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_669"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_669">223:2</a> Bhûr bhuva<i>h</i> sva<i>h</i> are explained by Dvivedagaṅga as the <i>Ri</i>g-veda, Ya<i>g</i>ur-veda, and Sâma-veda. They might also be earth, air, and heaven. See <i>S</i>âṅkhâyana, G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtras 1, 24; Bhur <i>ri</i>gveda<i>m</i> tvayi dadhâmi, &amp;c.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_670"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_670">223:3</a> The Mâdhyandinas add here another verse, which the father recites while he strokes his boy: 'Be a stone, be an axe, be pure gold. Thou art my Self, called my son; live a hundred harvests.' The same verse occurs in the Â<i>s</i>valâyana G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtras I, 15, 3.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_671"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_671">223:4</a> The two ceremonies, here described, are the âyushya-karman and the medhâ<i>g</i>anana. They are here treated rather confusedly. Pâraskara (G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtras I, 16, 3) distinguishes the medhâ<i>g</i>anana and the âyushya. He treats the medhâ<i>g</i>anana first, which consists in feeding the boy with honey and clarified butter, and saying to him bhûs tvayi dadhâmi, &amp;c. The âyushya consists in repeating certain verses in the boy's ear, wishing him a long life, &amp;c. In Â<i>s</i>valâyana's G<i>ri</i>hya-sûtras, I, 15, 1 contains the âyushya, I, 15, 2 the medhâganana. <i>S</i>âṅkhâyana also (I, 24) treats the âyushya first, and the medhâ<i>g</i>anana afterwards, and the same order prevails in the Mâdhyandina text of the B<i>ri</i>hadâranyaka-upanishad.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_672"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_672">223:5</a> In the Mâdhyandina text these acts are differently arranged.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_673"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_673">223:6</a> <i>Ri</i>g-veda I, 164, 49.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_674"></a><a href="sbe15097.htm#fr_674">223:7</a> These verses are differently explained by various commentators. Ânandagiri explains i<i>l</i>â as stutyâ, bhogyâ. He derives Maitrâvaru<i>n</i>î <a name="page_224"><font size="1" color="green">p. 224</font></a> from Maitrâvaru<i>n</i>a, i.e. Vasish<i>th</i>a, the son of Mitrâvaru<i>n</i>au, and identifies her with Arundhatî. Dvivedagaṅga takes i<i>d</i>â as bhogyâ, or i<i>d</i>âpâtrî, or p<i>ri</i>thivîrûpâ, and admits that she may be called Maitrâvaru<i>n</i>î, because born of Mitrâvaru<i>n</i>au. Vîre is rightly taken as a vocative by Dvivedagaṅga, while Ânandagiri explains it as a locative, mayi nimittabhûte. One expects a<i>g</i>î<i>g</i>ana<i>h</i> instead of a<i>g</i>îganat, which is the reading of A. and B. The reading of the Mâdhyandinas, â<i>g</i>î<i>g</i>anathâ<i>h</i>, is right grammatically, but it offends against the metre, and is a theoretical rather than a real form. If we read a<i>g</i>î<i>g</i>ana<i>h</i>, we must also read akara<i>h</i>, unless we are prepared to follow the commentator, who supplies bhavatî.</p>
