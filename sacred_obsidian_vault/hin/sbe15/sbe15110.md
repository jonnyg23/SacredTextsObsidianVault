 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="../../img/tn_hin.jpg"></a><br><i>The Upanishads, Part 2 (SBE15)</i>, by Max Müller, [1879], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">FIFTH QUESTION.</h3>  
 <p>1. Then <i>S</i>aivya Satyakâma asked him:--'Sir, if some one among men should meditate here until death on the syllable Om, what would he obtain by it?'</p>  
 <p>2. He replied: 'O Satyakâma, the syllable Om (AUM) is the highest and also the other Brahman;</p>  
 <p><a name="page_282"><font size="1" color="green">p. 282</font></a></p>  
 <p>therefore he who knows it arrives by the same means <a name="fr_911"></a><a href="#fn_908"><font size="1">1</font></a> at one of the two.</p>  
 <p>3. If he meditate on one Mâtrâ (the A) <a name="fr_912"></a><a href="#fn_909"><font size="1">2</font></a>, then, being enlightened by that only, he arrives quickly at the earth <a name="fr_913"></a><a href="#fn_910"><font size="1">3</font></a>. The <i>Ri</i><i>k</i>-verses lead him to the world of men, and being endowed there with penance, abstinence, and faith, he enjoys greatness.</p>  
 <p>4. If he meditate with <a name="fr_914"></a><a href="#fn_911"><font size="1">4</font></a> two Mâtrâs (A + U) he arrives at the Manas <a name="fr_915"></a><a href="#fn_912"><font size="1">5</font></a>, and is led up by the Ya<i>g</i>us-verses to the sky, to the Soma-world. Having enjoyed greatness in the Soma-world, he returns again.</p>  
 <p>5. Again, he who meditates with this syllable AUM of three Mâtrâs, on the Highest Person, he comes to light and to the sun. And as a snake is freed from its skin, so is he freed from evil. He is led up by the Sâman-verses to the Brahma-world <a name="fr_916"></a><a href="#fn_913"><font size="1">6</font></a>; and from him, full of life (Hira<i>n</i>yagarbha, the lord of the Satya-loka <a name="fr_917"></a><a href="#fn_914"><font size="1">7</font></a>), he learns <a name="fr_918"></a><a href="#fn_915"><font size="1">8</font></a> to see the all-pervading, the Highest Person. And there are these two <i>S</i>lokas:</p>  
 <p>6. The three Mâtrâs (A + U + M), if employed separate, and only joined one to another, are mortal <a name="fr_919"></a><a href="#fn_916"><font size="1">9</font></a>;</p>

 <p><a name="page_283"><font size="1" color="green">p. 283</font></a></p>  
 <p>but in acts, external, internal, or intermediate, if well performed, the sage trembles not <a name="fr_920"></a><a href="#fn_917"><font size="1">1</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>7. Through the <i>Ri</i><i>k</i>-verses he arrives at this world, through the Ya<i>g</i>us-verses at the sky, through the Sâman-verses at that which the poets teach,--he arrives at this by means of the Oṅkâra; the wise arrives at that which is at rest, free from decay, from death, from fear,--the Highest.'</p>

 <p><a name="fn_912"></a><a href="sbe15110.htm#fr_915">282:5</a> Literally the mind, but here meant for the moon, as before. It is clear that manasi belongs to sampadyate, not, as the Dîpikâ and Roer think, to dhyâyîta. Some take it for svapnâbhimânî Hira<i>n</i>yagarbha<i>h</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_913"></a><a href="sbe15110.htm#fr_916">282:6</a> The world of Hira<i>n</i>yagarbha<i>h</i>, called the Satyaloka.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_915"></a><a href="sbe15110.htm#fr_918">282:8</a> Tadupade<i>s</i>eneti yâvat.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_916"></a><a href="sbe15110.htm#fr_919">282:9</a> Because in their separate form, A, U, M, they do not mean the Highest Brahman.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_917"></a><a href="sbe15110.htm#fr_920">283:1</a> The three acts are explained as waking, slumbering, and deep sleep; or as three kinds of pronunciation, târa-mandra-madhyama. They are probably meant for Yoga exercises in which the three Mâtrâs of Om are used as one word, and as an emblem of the Highest Brahman.</p>
