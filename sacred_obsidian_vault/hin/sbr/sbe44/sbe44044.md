 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:8:4">11:8:4</a></font></span></p>  <h3 align="center">FOURTH BRÂHMA<i>N</i>A.</h3>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:8:4:1">11:8:4:1</a></font></span>1. Now, once upon a time, a tiger killed the samrâ<i>g</i>-cow <a name="fr_401"></a><a href="#fn_401"><font size="1">1</font></a> of those (who were sacrificing) with (the king of the) Ke<i>s</i>in as their G<i>ri</i>hapati <a name="fr_402"></a><a href="#fn_402"><font size="1">2</font></a>. He (the king <a name="fr_403"></a><a href="#fn_403"><font size="1">3</font></a>) said to his fellow-sacrificers, 'What atonement is there for this?' They replied, 'There is no atonement for this: Kha<i>n</i><i>d</i>ika Audbhâri alone knows an atonement for it; but he certainly desires as much as this, and worse than this <a name="fr_404"></a><a href="#fn_404"><font size="1">4</font></a>, (to happen) to thee.'</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:8:4:2">11:8:4:2</a></font></span>2. He said, 'Charioteer, put to my horses; I shall drive thither: if so be he will tell me, I shall succeed (with my sacrifice); but if he will have me die, I shall be shattered along with the shattered sacrifice.'</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:8:4:3">11:8:4:3</a></font></span>3. Having put to the horses, he drove off, and</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_132"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 132</font></a></p>  
 <p>came thither <a name="fr_405"></a><a href="#fn_405"><font size="1">1</font></a>. When he (Kha<i>n</i><i>d</i>ika) saw <a name="fr_406"></a><a href="#fn_406"><font size="1">2</font></a> him, he said, 'Seeing that there are those skins on deer, we break their ribs and cook them: the skin of the black antelope is attached to my neck <a name="fr_407"></a><a href="#fn_407"><font size="1">3</font></a>--is it with thoughts such as these that thou hast dared to drive over to me?'</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:8:4:4">11:8:4:4</a></font></span>4. 'Not so,' he replied; 'a tiger has killed my samrâ<i>g</i>-cow, reverend sir; if so be thou wilt tell me, I shall succeed; but if thou wilt have me die, I shall be shattered along with the shattered sacrifice.'</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:8:4:5">11:8:4:5</a></font></span>5. He said, 'I will take counsel with my counsellors <a name="fr_408"></a><a href="#fn_408"><font size="1">4</font></a>.' Having called them to counsel, he said, 'If I tell him, his race, not mine, will prevail here <a name="fr_409"></a><a href="#fn_409"><font size="1">5</font></a>,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_133"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 133</font></a></p>  
 <p>but I shall gain the (other) world; and if I do not tell him, my own race, not his, will prevail here, but he will gain the (other) world.' They said, 'Do not tell him, reverend sir, for, surely, this (the earth) is the Kshatriya's world <a name="fr_410"></a><a href="#fn_410"><font size="1">1</font></a>.' He replied, 'Nay, I will tell him: there are more nights <a name="fr_411"></a><a href="#fn_411"><font size="1">2</font></a> up yonder.'</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:8:4:6">11:8:4:6</a></font></span>6. And, accordingly, he then said to him,--'Having offered the Sp<i>ri</i>tis <a name="fr_412"></a><a href="#fn_412"><font size="1">3</font></a>, he (the Adhvaryu) should say, "Drive up another (cow)!" and that one should be thy samrâ<i>g</i>-cow <a name="fr_413"></a><a href="#fn_413"><font size="1">4</font></a>.'--'[Having offered. with,] "From the moon I take thy mind, hail!--From the sun I take thine eye, hail!--From the wind I take thy breathings, hail!--From the regions I take thine ear, hail!--From the waters I take thy blood, hail!--From the earth</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_134"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 134</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> I take thy body, hail!" let him then say, "Drive up another (cow)!" and that one shall be thy samrâg cow!' He then departed from thence <a name="fr_414"></a><a href="#fn_414"><font size="1">1</font></a>, and, verily, members of the Ke<i>s</i>in race are born here even to this day.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_401"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_401">131:1</a> That is the cow which supplies the milk for the Pravargya; this milk, when heated, being called 'gharma (heat)' or 'samrâ<i>g</i> (sovereign king).' See part ii, p. 104, note 3.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_402"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_402">131:2</a> G<i>ri</i>hapati, or house-lord, master of the house, is the title of the principal sacrificer at a sacrificial session (sattra).--According to Sâya<i>n</i>a, the Ke<i>s</i>ina<i>h</i> were a race of nobles (râ<i>g</i>âna<i>h</i>), who, on this occasion, were performing a 'sattra,' and are therefore styled 'householders' (g<i>ri</i>hapati);--ke<i>s</i>ino nâma râ<i>g</i>âna<i>h</i> sattrayâgam anutish<i>th</i>anto g<i>ri</i>hapataya âsu<i>h</i>, Sâya<i>n</i>a thus takes 'ke<i>s</i>i-g<i>ri</i>hapataya<i>h</i>,' not as a bahuvrîhi, but as a tatpurusha (karmadhâraya, 'the Ke<i>s</i>in householders') which would, however, require the accent on the second member of the compound.--Though all those taking part in a sacrificial session ought to be Brahmans, the rule does not seem to have been strictly observed. Cf. part iv, introd., p. xxv; Weber, Ind. Stud. X, pp. 25 94.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_403"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_403">131:3</a> G<i>ri</i>hapatishu pradhânabhûta<i>h</i> ke<i>s</i>irâ<i>g</i>a<i>h</i>, Sây.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_404"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_404">131:4</a> That is, that even a greater misfortune should happen to thee,--atyanta<i>m</i> pâpayukta<i>m</i> govadhâdidoshayuktam ity artha<i>h</i>, Sây.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_405"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_405">132:1</a> Sâya<i>n</i>a makes Kha<i>n</i><i>d</i>ika the subject of this last verb:--sa ha ratham a<i>s</i>vai<i>h</i> sa<i>m</i>yo<i>g</i>ya Kha<i>n</i><i>d</i>ikasamîpa<i>m</i> yayau; so<font size="-1"><sup>ऽ</sup></font>pi Kha<i>n</i><i>d</i>ika<i>h</i> ke<i>s</i>inam â<i>g</i>agâma, gatvâ <i>k</i>a vivakta<i>m</i> (? vivikta<i>m</i>) Ke<i>s</i>ina<i>m</i> pratikhyâya nirâk<i>ri</i>tya sadayam eva prathamam uvâ<i>k</i>a. He thus seems not to allow here to 'yâ' the meaning of 'to drive,' but to take 'yayau' in the sense of 'he went thither.' It might, of course, also mean 'he set off.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_406"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_406">132:2</a> Sâya<i>n</i>a apparently takes 'prati-khyâ' in the sense of 'to refuse admittance to, to reject,' 'abweisen.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_407"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_407">132:3</a> Sâya<i>n</i>a's comment on this passage is as follows:--'O Ke<i>s</i>in, the skin of the cow that yields the gharma-milk is worn by thee on the neck: those (i.e. suchlike) skins, indeed, are (i.e. are seen) on deer; and having broken (i.e., torn to pieces) the "p<i>ri</i>sh<i>t</i>i" (i.e. the small-sized does) amongst them we cook them: that black-antelope skin is fastened on my neck.' Kha<i>n</i><i>d</i>ika having spoken thus, the king said, 'No, this is not my intention.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_408"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_408">132:4</a> Literally, those that should be consulted, whom further on Sâya<i>n</i>a calls 'âptâ<i>h</i>' or trusty men.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_409"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_409">132:5</a> Or, perhaps, the people here (the Ke<i>s</i>ins) will become his, not mine; cf. Delbrück, Altind. Syntax, pp. 32; 141 (two different renderings). Sâya<i>n</i>a, on the other hand, takes 'pra<i>g</i>â,' not in the sense either of 'family' or 'people,' but in that of '(sacred) knowledge'--perhaps with reference to the threefold science (the Veda) as the <a name="page_133"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 133</font></a> thousandfold progeny of Vâ<i>k</i>, speech (cf. IV, 5, 8, 4; 6, 7, 3; V, 5, 5, 12)--which Kha<i>n</i><i>d</i>ika would thus lose, whilst, by imparting the sacred knowledge, he would gain a seat in heaven.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_410"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_410">133:1</a> Sâya<i>n</i>a's comment is not very intelligible, the MS. being more than usually corrupt on this last page:--eva<i>m</i>vidhe virodha udbhâvitê sati to âptâ û<i>k</i>u<i>h</i>, he bhagavo vidyâ<i>m</i> mâ vo<i>k</i>a<i>h</i>, kshatriyasya loko na bhavishyatîti; nanu tavânu<i>s</i>aya<i>h</i> (? appanage, domain, following) sa tasya nâsti; aya<i>m</i> vâva ayam eva khalu, kshatriyasya lokas tasmât sauspatrâter (?) evam ukte sati sadve<i>k</i>enarâpatra bhavânti (!) ato vakshyâmy evety uvâ<i>k</i>a.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_411"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_411">133:2</a> That is, days,--by giving up a brief life of earthly power and glory, he gains eternal life.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_412"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_412">133:3</a> That is, oblations performed with a view of 'taking hold (sp<i>ri</i>)' of something; cf. Katy. <i>S</i>rautas. XXV, 6, 11. 12.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_413"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_413">133:4</a> The particle 'iti' here causes some difficulty of construction which would be removed by the latter clause being taken as part of the Adhvaryu's speech; though Kâtyâyana, it is true, does not recognise it as such. Perhaps, however, Kha<i>n</i><i>d</i>ika's speech ends here, and what follows up to 'that one shall be thy samrâ<i>g</i>-cow' has to be taken as a ritualistic insertion, in which case the final 'iti' would have some such meaning as 'having been told thus.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_414"></a><a href="sbe44044.htm#fr_414">134:1</a> Sâya<i>n</i>a takes this thus:--'Thus instructed, Ke<i>s</i>in disappeared (or, passed away, vanished, utsasâda vinash<i>t</i>a<i>h</i>) from that region (tato de<i>s</i>ât)'--after which there is a lacuna in the MS. Perhaps, however, it is Kha<i>n</i><i>d</i>ika, rather than Ke<i>s</i>in, to which this refers,--he (and his race) then, indeed, passed away from that region, whilst the Ke<i>s</i>ins flourished.</p>
