 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1">11:5:1</a></font></span></p>  <h3 align="center">FIFTH ADHYÂYA. FIRST BRÂHMA<i>N</i>A.</h3>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:1">11:5:1:1</a></font></span>1. The nymph Urva<i>s</i>î loved Purûravas <a name="fr_228"></a><a href="#fn_228"><font size="1">3</font></a>, the son of I<i>d</i>â. When she wedded him, she said,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_69"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 69</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> 'Thrice a day shalt thou embrace <a name="fr_229"></a><a href="#fn_229"><font size="1">1</font></a> me; but do not lie with me against my will <a name="fr_230"></a><a href="#fn_230"><font size="1">2</font></a>, and let me not see thee naked, for such is the way to behave to us women.'</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:2">11:5:1:2</a></font></span>2. She then dwelt with him a long time, and was even with child of him, so long did she dwell with him. Then the Gandharvas <a name="fr_231"></a><a href="#fn_231"><font size="1">3</font></a> said to one another, 'For a long time, indeed, has this Urva<i>s</i>î dwelt among men: devise ye some means how she may come back to us.' Now, a ewe with two lambs was tied to her couch: the Gandharvas then carried off one of the lambs.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:3">11:5:1:3</a></font></span>3. 'Alas,' <a href="errata.htm#0"></a>she cried, 'they are taking away my darling <a name="fr_232"></a><a href="#fn_232"><font size="1">4</font></a>, as if I were where there is no hero and no man!' They carried off the second, and she spake in the selfsame manner.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:4">11:5:1:4</a></font></span>4. He then thought within himself, 'How can that be (a place) without a hero and without a man where I am?' And naked, as he was, he sprang</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_70"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 70</font></a></p>  
 <p>up after them: too long he deemed it that he should put on his garment. Then the Gandharvas produced a flash of lightning, and she beheld him naked even as by daylight. Then, indeed, she vanished: 'Here I am back,' he said, and lo! she had vanished <a name="fr_233"></a><a href="#fn_233"><font size="1">1</font></a>. Wailing with sorrow he wandered all over Kurukshetra. Now there is a lotus-lake there, called Anyata<i>h</i>plakshâ: He walked along its bank; and there nymphs were swimming about in the shape of swans <a name="fr_234"></a><a href="#fn_234"><font size="1">2</font></a>.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:5">11:5:1:5</a></font></span>5. And she (Urva<i>s</i>î), recognising him, said, 'This is the man with whom I have dwelt.' They then said, 'Let us appear to him!'--'So be it!' she replied; and they appeared to him <a name="fr_235"></a><a href="#fn_235"><font size="1">3</font></a>.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:6">11:5:1:6</a></font></span>6. He then recognised her and implored her (<i>Ri</i>g-veda X, 95, I), 'Oh, my wife, stay thou, cruel in mind <a name="fr_236"></a><a href="#fn_236"><font size="1">4</font></a>: let us now exchange words! Untold, these secrets of ours will not bring us joy in days to</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_71"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 71</font></a></p>  
 <p>come;'--'Stop, pray, let us speak together!' this is what he meant to say to her.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:7">11:5:1:7</a></font></span>7. She replied (X, 95, 2), 'What concern have I with speaking to thee? I have passed away like the first of the dawns. Purûravas, go home again: I am like the wind, difficult to catch;'--'Thou didst not do what I had told thee; hard to catch I am for thee, go to thy home again!' this is what she meant to say.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:8">11:5:1:8</a></font></span>8. He then said sorrowing (X, 95, 14), 'Then will thy friend <a name="fr_237"></a><a href="#fn_237"><font size="1">1</font></a> rush away <a name="fr_238"></a><a href="#fn_238"><font size="1">2</font></a> this day never to come back, to go to the farthest distance: then will he lie in Nir<i>ri</i>ti's <a name="fr_239"></a><a href="#fn_239"><font size="1">3</font></a> lap, or the fierce wolves will devour him; Thy friend will either hang himself, or start forth; or the wolves, or dogs, will devour him!' this is what he meant to say.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:9">11:5:1:9</a></font></span>9. She replied (X, 95, 15), 'Purûravas, do not die! do not rush away! let not the cruel wolves devour thee! Truly, there is no friendship with women, and theirs are the hearts of hyenas <a name="fr_240"></a><a href="#fn_240"><font size="1">4</font></a>;'--</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_72"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 72</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> 'Do not take this to heart! there is no friendship with women: return home!' this is what she meant to say.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:10">11:5:1:10</a></font></span>10. (<i>Ri</i>g-veda X, 95, 16), 'When changed in form, I walked among mortals, and passed the nights there during four autumns <a name="fr_241"></a><a href="#fn_241"><font size="1">1</font></a> ate a little ghee, once a day, and even now I feel satisfied therewith <a name="fr_242"></a><a href="#fn_242"><font size="1">2</font></a>.'--This discourse in fifteen verses has been handed down by the Bahv<i>ri</i><i>k</i>as <a name="fr_243"></a><a href="#fn_243"><font size="1">3</font></a>. Then her heart took pity on him <a name="fr_244"></a><a href="#fn_244"><font size="1">4</font></a>.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:11">11:5:1:11</a></font></span>11. She said, 'Come here the last night of the year from now <a name="fr_245"></a><a href="#fn_245"><font size="1">5</font></a>: then shalt thou lie with me for one night, and then this son of thine will have been born.' He came there on the last night of the year, and lo, there stood a golden palace <a name="fr_246"></a><a href="#fn_246"><font size="1">6</font></a>! They then</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_73"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 73</font></a></p>  
 <p>said to him only this (word) <a name="fr_247"></a><a href="#fn_247"><font size="1">1</font></a>, 'Enter!' and then they bade her go to him.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:12">11:5:1:12</a></font></span>12. She then said, 'To-morrow morning the Gandharvas will grant thee a boon, and thou must make thy choice.' He said, 'Choose thou for me!'--She replied, 'Say, Let me be one of yourselves!' In the morning the Gandharvas granted him a boon; and he said, 'Let me be one of yourselves!'</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:13">11:5:1:13</a></font></span>13. They said, 'Surely, there is not among men that holy form of fire by sacrificing wherewith one would become one of ourselves.' They put fire into a pan, and gave it to him saying, 'By sacrificing therewith thou shalt become one of ourselves.' He took it (the fire) and his boy, and went on his way home. He then deposited the fire in the forest, and went to the village with the boy alone. [He came back and thought] 'Here I am back;' and lo! it had disappeared <a name="fr_248"></a><a href="#fn_248"><font size="1">2</font></a>: what had been the fire was an A<i>s</i>vattha tree (ficus religiosa), and what had been the pan was a <i>S</i>amî tree (mimosa suma). He then returned to the Gandharvas.</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:14">11:5:1:14</a></font></span>14. They said, 'Cook for a whole year a mess of rice sufficient for four persons; and taking each time three logs from this A<i>s</i>vattha tree, anoint them with ghee, and put them on the fire with</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_74"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 74</font></a></p>  
 <p>verses containing the words "log" and "ghee": the fire which shall result therefrom will be that very fire (which is required).'</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:15">11:5:1:15</a></font></span>15. They said, 'But that is recondite (esoteric), as it were. Make thyself rather an upper ara<i>n</i>i <a name="fr_249"></a><a href="#fn_249"><font size="1">1</font></a> of A<i>s</i>vattha wood, and a lower ara<i>n</i>i of Sami wood: the fire which shall result therefrom will be that very fire.'</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:16">11:5:1:16</a></font></span>16. They said, 'But that also is, as it were, recondite. Make thyself rather an upper ara<i>n</i>i of A<i>s</i>vattha wood, and a lower ara<i>n</i>i of A<i>s</i>vattha wood: the fire which shall result therefrom will be that very fire.'</p>  
 <p><span class="margnote"><font color="GREEN" size="-1"><a name="11:5:1:17">11:5:1:17</a></font></span>17. He then made himself an upper ara<i>n</i>i of A<i>s</i>vattha wood, and a lower ara<i>n</i>i of A<i>s</i>vattha wood; and the fire which resulted therefrom was that very fire: by offering therewith he became one of the Gandharvas. Let him therefore make himself an upper and a lower ara<i>n</i>i of A<i>s</i>vattha wood, and the fire which results therefrom will be that very fire: by offering therewith he becomes one of the Gandharvas.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_228"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_228">68:3</a> King Purûravas, of the lunar race of kings, is considered the <a name="page_69"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 69</font></a> son of Budha (the planet Mercury, and son of Soma). On this myth (based on the hymn <i>Ri</i>g-veda S. X, 95) see Prof. Max Müller, Oxford Essays (1856), p. 61 seqq.; (reprinted in Chips from a German Workshop, II, p. 102 seqq.); A. Kuhn, Herabkunft des Feuers and des Göttertranks, p. 81 seqq. (2nd ed. p. 73 seqq.); Weber, Ind. Streifen I, p. 16 seqq.; K. F. Geldner, in Pischel and Geldner's Vedische Studien I, p. 244 seqq.; cf. H. Oldenberg, Religion des Veda, p. 213.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_229"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_229">69:1</a> Vaitasena da<i>n</i><i>d</i>ena hatâd,--vaitaso da<i>n</i><i>d</i>a<i>h</i> pu<i>m</i>vya<i>ñ</i><i>g</i>anasya nâma; ukta<i>m</i> hi Yâskena, <i>s</i>epo vaitasa iti pu<i>m</i>spra<i>g</i>ananasyeti (Nir. III, 22), Sây.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_230"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_230">69:2</a> Akâmâ<i>m</i> kâmarahitâ<i>m</i> suratâbhilâsharahitâ<i>m</i> <i>k</i>a mâ<i>m</i> mâ sma nipadyâsai nig<i>ri</i>hya mâ<i>m</i> prâpnuyâ<i>h</i>, Sây.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_231"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_231">69:3</a> The Gandharvas are the natural companions and mates of the Apsaras, or nymphs.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_232"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_232">69:4</a> Literally, 'my son,'--madîya<i>m</i> putratvena svîk<i>ri</i>tam ura<i>n</i>advayam, Sây.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_233"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_233">70:1</a> Cf. C. Gaedicke, Der Accusativ im Veda (1880), p. 211. Previous translators had assigned the words 'punar emi' (I come back) to Urva<i>s</i>î; and in view of the corresponding passage in <a href="sbe44025.htm#11:5:1:13">paragraph 13</a>, the new interpretation is just a little doubtful.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_234"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_234">70:2</a> The text has 'âti,' some kind of water-bird--<i>g</i>ala<i>k</i>arapakshivi<i>s</i>esha<i>h</i>, Sây.--(probably Gr. νῆσσα; Lat. anas, anat-is; Anglo-S. æned, Germ. Ente).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_235"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_235">70:3</a> That is, they became visible, or rather recognisable to him by showing themselves in their real forms,--pakshirûpa<i>m</i> vihâya svakîyena rûpe<i>n</i>a prâdur babhûvu<i>h</i>, Sây.--In Kâlidâsa's plays, both Urva<i>s</i>î and <i>S</i>akuntalâ become invisible by means of a magic veil (tiraskari<i>n</i>î, 'making invisible') with which has been compared the magic veil by which the swan-maidens change their form. A. Weber, Ind. Stud. I, p. 197; A. Kuhn, Herabkunft, p. 91.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_236"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_236">70:4</a> Manasâ tish<i>th</i>a ghore,--possibly it may mean, 'O cruel one, be thou constant in (thy) mind;' or, as Kuhn takes it, 'pay attention, O cruel one.' Sâya<i>n</i>a, however, takes it as above.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_237"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_237">71:1</a> This is a doubtful rendering (Max Müller; Gespiele, A. Weber) of 'sudeva,'--Göttergenoss (the companion of the gods), Kuhn; 'dem die Götter einst hold waren' (he who was formerly favoured by the gods), Grassmann; Sudeva, Ludwig.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_238"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_238">71:2</a> Or, will fall down (Max Müller, Weber); sich in’s Verderben stürzen (will rush to his destruction), Kuhn;--forteilen (hasten away), Grassmann; verloren gehen (get lost), Ludwig; sich in den Abgrund stürzen, Geldner;--'mahâprasthâna<i>m</i> kuryât' (he will set out on the great journey, i.e. die), Sâya<i>n</i>a. The Brâhma<i>n</i>a seems to propose two different renderings,--to throw oneself down (hang oneself), or, to start forth.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_239"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_239">71:3</a> Nir<i>ri</i>ti is the goddess of decay or death.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_240"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_240">71:4</a> The meaning of 'sâlâv<i>ri</i>ka,' also spelled '<i>s</i>âlâv<i>ri</i>ka' (? house-wolves), is doubtful; cf. H. Zimmer, Altindisches Leben, p. 8. Prof. Weber, Ind. Stud. I, p. 413, makes the suggestion that 'wehrwolves' may be intended.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_241"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_241">72:1</a> The words 'râtrî<i>h</i> <i>s</i>arada<i>s</i> <i>k</i>atasra<i>h</i>' may also be taken in the sense of 'four nights of the autumn' (Max Müller, A. Kuhn). It needs hardly to be remarked that 'nights' means days and nights, and 'autumns' years.--Sâya<i>n</i>a takes the passage in the sense of 'four delightful (râtrî<i>h</i> ramayitrî<i>h</i>) autumns or years.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_242"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_242">72:2</a> Literally, I walk (or go on, keep) being satisfied therewith. Prof. Geldner, however, takes it in an ironical sense, 'das Bischen liegt mir jetzt noch schwer im Magen' ('even now I have quite enough of that little').</p>
 <p><a name="fn_243"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_243">72:3</a> That is, the theologians of the <i>Ri</i>g-veda. As Prof. Weber points out, the hymn referred to, in the received version, consists not of fifteen but of eighteen verses, three of which would therefore seem to be of later origin (though they might, of course, belong to a different recension from that referred to by the Brâhma<i>n</i>a).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_244"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_244">72:4</a> Or, according to Prof. Geldner, 'Then he touched her heart (excited her pity).'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_245"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_245">72:5</a> Literally, the yearliest night, i.e. the 360th night, the last night of a year from now, or, this night next year: it is the night that completes the year, just as 'the fifth' completes the number 'five;'--sa<i>m</i>vatsaratamî<i>m</i> sa<i>m</i>vatsarapûra<i>n</i>îm antimâ<i>m</i> râtrim, Sây. Cf. Delbrück, Altind. Syntax, p. 195.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_246"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_246">72:6</a> Hira<i>n</i>yavimitâni hira<i>n</i>yanirmitâni saudhâni, Sây.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_247"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_247">73:1</a> Thus also A. Kuhn, and Sâya<i>n</i>a, tato hainam ekam û<i>k</i>ur etat, prapadyasveti,--enam Purûravasa<i>m</i> tatratyâ <i>g</i>anâ idam ekam û<i>k</i>u<i>h</i>, Sây.--The word 'ekam' might also be taken along with 'enam' (Max Müller, Weber, Geldner),--'they said this to him alone' (? they bade him enter alone without his attendants).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_248"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_248">73:2</a> See above, <a href="sbe44025.htm#11:5:1:4">paragraph 4</a> and note  on <a href="#page_70">p. 70</a>. According to the other interpretation we should have to translate:--He then deposited the fire in the forest, and went to the village with the boy alone, thinking, 'I (shall) come back.' [He came back] and lo! it had disappeared.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_249"></a><a href="sbe44025.htm#fr_249">74:1</a> That is, a churning-stick used for producing fire; see part i, p. 275; p. 294, note 3.</p>
