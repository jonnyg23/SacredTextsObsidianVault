   <h3 align="center">CHAPTER XII.</h3>  
 <p>1. A <i>S</i>ûdra who intentionally reviles twice-born men by criminal abuse, or criminally assaults them with blows, shall be deprived of the limb with which he offends. <a name="fr_1155"></a><a href="#fn_1154"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_239"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 239</font></a></p>  
 <p>2. If he has criminal intercourse with an Âryan woman, his organ shall be cut off, and all his property be confiscated. <a name="fr_1156"></a><a href="#fn_1155"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>3. If (the woman had) a protector, he shall be executed after (having undergone the punishments prescribed above). <a name="fr_1157"></a><a href="#fn_1156"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>4. Now if he listens intentionally to (a recitation of) the Veda, his ears shall be filled with (molten) tin or lac.</p>  
 <p>5. If he recites (Vedic texts), his tongue shall be cut out.</p>  
 <p>6. If he remembers them, his body shall be split in twain.</p>  
 <p>7. If he assumes a position equal (to that of twice-born men) in sitting, in lying down, in conversation or on the road, he shall undergo (corporal) punishment. <a name="fr_1158"></a><a href="#fn_1157"><font size="1">7</font></a></p>  
 <p>8. A Kshatriya (shall be fined) one hundred (Kârshâpa<i>n</i>as) if he abuses a Brâhma<i>n</i>a, <a name="fr_1159"></a><a href="#fn_1158"><font size="1">8</font></a></p>  
 <p>9. In case of an assault, twice as much.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_240"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 240</font></a></p>  
 <p>10. A Vai<i>s</i>ya (who abuses a Brâhma<i>n</i>a, shall pay) one and a half (times as much as a Kshatriya). <a name="fr_1160"></a><a href="#fn_1159"><font size="1">10</font></a></p>  
 <p>11. But a Brâhma<i>n</i>a (who abuses) a Kshatriya (shall pay) fifty (Kârshâpa<i>n</i>as), <a name="fr_1161"></a><a href="#fn_1160"><font size="1">11</font></a></p>  
 <p>12. One half of that (amount if he abuses) a Vai<i>s</i>ya, <a name="fr_1162"></a><a href="#fn_1161"><font size="1">12</font></a></p>  
 <p>13. (And if he abuses) a <i>S</i>ûdra, nothing. <a name="fr_1163"></a><a href="#fn_1162"><font size="1">13</font></a></p>  
 <p>14. A Kshatriya and a Vai<i>s</i>ya (who abuse one another shall pay the same fines) as a Brâhma<i>n</i>a and a Kshatriya. <a name="fr_1164"></a><a href="#fn_1163"><font size="1">14</font></a></p>  
 <p>15. (The value of) property which a <i>S</i>ûdra unrighteously acquires by theft, must be repaid eightfold. <a name="fr_1165"></a><a href="#fn_1164"><font size="1">15</font></a></p>  
 <p>16. For each of the other castes (the fines must be) doubled. <a name="fr_1166"></a><a href="#fn_1165"><font size="1">16</font></a></p>  
 <p>17. If a learned man offends, the punishment shall be very much increased. <a name="fr_1167"></a><a href="#fn_1166"><font size="1">17</font></a></p>  
 <p>18. If fruits, green corn, and vegetables are appropriated in small amounts, (the fine is) five K<i>ri</i>sh<i>n</i>alas (of copper). <a name="fr_1168"></a><a href="#fn_1167"><font size="1">18</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_241"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 241</font></a></p>  
 <p>19. If damage is done by cattle, the responsibility falls on the owner.</p>  
 <p>20. But if (the cattle) were attended by a herdsman, (it falls) on the latter. <a name="fr_1169"></a><a href="#fn_1168"><font size="1">20</font></a></p>  
 <p>21. (If the damage was done) in an unenclosed field near the road, (the responsibility falls) on the herdsman and on the owner of the field.</p>  
 <p>22. Five Mâshas (are the fine to be paid) for (damage done by) a cow, <a name="fr_1170"></a><a href="#fn_1169"><font size="1">22</font></a></p>  
 <p>23. Six for a camel or a donkey,</p>  
 <p>24. Ten for a horse or a buffalo,</p>  
 <p>25. Two for each goat or sheep.</p>  
 <p>26. If all is destroyed, (the value of) the whole crop (must be paid and a fine in addition).</p>  
 <p>27. If (a man) always neglects the prescribed (duties) and does that which is forbidden, his property beyond (the amount required for) raiment and food shall be taken from him (until he amends). <a name="fr_1171"></a><a href="#fn_1170"><font size="1">27</font></a></p>  
 <p>28. He may take, as his own, grass for a cow, and fuel for his fire, as well as the flowers of creepers and trees and their fruit, if they be unenclosed. <a name="fr_1172"></a><a href="#fn_1171"><font size="1">28</font></a></p>  
 <p>29. The legal interest for money lent (is at the rate of) five Mâshas a month for twenty (Kârshâpa<i>n</i>as). <a name="fr_1173"></a><a href="#fn_1172"><font size="1">29</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_242"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 242</font></a></p>  
 <p>30. Some (declare, that this rate should not be paid) longer than a year. <a name="fr_1174"></a><a href="#fn_1173"><font size="1">30</font></a></p>  
 <p>31. If (the loan) remains outstanding for a long time, the principal may be doubled (after which interest ceases). <a name="fr_1175"></a><a href="#fn_1174"><font size="1">31</font></a></p>  
 <p>32. A loan secured by a pledge that is used (by the creditor) bears no interest; <a name="fr_1176"></a><a href="#fn_1175"><font size="1">32</font></a></p>  
 <p>33. Nor money tendered, nor (a debt due by a debtor) who is forcibly prevented (from paying). <a name="fr_1177"></a><a href="#fn_1176"><font size="1">33</font></a></p>  
 <p>34. (Special forms of interest are) compound interest, periodical interest, <a name="fr_1178"></a><a href="#fn_1177"><font size="1">34</font></a></p>  
 <p>35. Stipulated interest, corporal interest, daily interest, and the use of a pledge. <a name="fr_1179"></a><a href="#fn_1178"><font size="1">35</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_243"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 243</font></a></p>  
 <p>36. The interest on products of animals, on wool, on the produce of a field, and on beasts of burden (shall) not (increase) more than the fivefold (value of the object lent). <a name="fr_1180"></a><a href="#fn_1179"><font size="1">36</font></a></p>  
 <p>37. The property of (a person who is) neither an idiot nor a minor, having been used by strangers before his eyes for ten years, (belongs) to him who uses it, <a name="fr_1181"></a><a href="#fn_1180"><font size="1">37</font></a></p>  
 <p>38. (But) not (if it is used) by <i>S</i>rotriyas, ascetics, or royal officials. <a name="fr_1182"></a><a href="#fn_1181"><font size="1">38</font></a></p>  
 <p>39. Animals, land, and females are not lost (to the owner) by (another's) possession. <a name="fr_1183"></a><a href="#fn_1182"><font size="1">39</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_244"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 244</font></a></p>  
 <p>40. The heirs shall pay the debts (of a deceased person). <a name="fr_1184"></a><a href="#fn_1183"><font size="1">40</font></a></p>  
 <p>41. Money due by a surety, a commercial debt, a fee (due to the parents of the bride), debts contracted for spirituous liquor or in gambling, and a fine shall not involve the sons (of the debtor). <a name="fr_1185"></a><a href="#fn_1184"><font size="1">41</font></a></p>  
 <p>42. An (open) deposit, a sealed deposit, an object lent for use, an object bought (but not paid), and a pledge, being lost without the fault of the holder, (shall not involve) any blameless person. <a name="fr_1186"></a><a href="#fn_1185"><font size="1">42</font></a></p>  
 <p>43. A man who has stolen (gold) shall approach the king, with flying hair, holding a club in his hand, and proclaim his deed. <a name="fr_1187"></a><a href="#fn_1186"><font size="1">43</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_245"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 245</font></a></p>  
 <p>44. Whether he be slain or be pardoned, he is purified (of his guilt).</p>  
 <p>45. If the king does not strike, the guilt falls on him. <a name="fr_1188"></a><a href="#fn_1187"><font size="1">45</font></a></p>  
 <p>46. Corporal punishment (must) not (be resorted to in the case) of a Brâhma<i>n</i>a. <a name="fr_1189"></a><a href="#fn_1188"><font size="1">46</font></a></p>  
 <p>47. Preventing (a repetition of) the deed, publicly proclaiming his crime, banishment, and branding (are the punishments to which a Brâhma<i>n</i>a, may be subjected). <a name="fr_1190"></a><a href="#fn_1189"><font size="1">47</font></a></p>  
 <p>48. That (king) who does not do his duty (by inflicting punishment) becomes liable to perform a penance. <a name="fr_1191"></a><a href="#fn_1190"><font size="1">48</font></a></p>  
 <p>49. (A man who) knowingly (becomes) the servant (of a thief shall be treated) like a thief, <a name="fr_1192"></a><a href="#fn_1191"><font size="1">49</font></a></p>  
 <p>50. Likewise he who (knowingly) receives (goods) from (a thief or) an unrighteous man.</p>  
 <p>51. The award of the punishment (must be regulated) by a consideration (of the status) of the criminal, of his (bodily) strength, of (the nature of) the crime, and whether the offence has been repeated. <a name="fr_1193"></a><a href="#fn_1192"><font size="1">51</font></a></p>  
 <p>52. Or a pardon (may be given) in accordance with the opinion of an assemblage of persons learned in the Vedas.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_1177"></a><a href="sbe0276.htm#fr_1178">242:34</a> For this and the next Sûtra, see also Colebrooke I, Digest 35-45, in the notes on which latter text the various explanations of these terms, found here, have been fully discussed. 'If a large or a small interest is taken on condition that the loan is to be repaid on a certain date, and that, in case of non-payment, 'it is to be trebled or quadrupled, that is called periodical interest'--Haradatta.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1178"></a><a href="sbe0276.htm#fr_1179">242:35</a> 'Where the lender and the borrower, having regard to the country, the time, the object, and the condition (of the borrower), agree between themselves (on a certain Tate), e.g. of ten per cent per mensem, that is called stipulated interest. Corporal interest is that which is payable by bodily labour. Thus B<i>ri</i>haspati says, "Corporal interest is that connected with work." But Vyâsa explains it thus, "Corporal interest is that which arises from the work (or use) of a (pledged female quadruped) to be <a name="page_243"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 243</font></a> milked, or of (a male) to carry burdens." Kâtyâyana explains the daily interest (lit. the interest resembling the growth of the lock on the head), "That which is taken daily is called daily interest." . . . 'E.g. for a Prastha of grain lent a handful of grain is taken daily.'--Haradatta.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1179"></a><a href="sbe0276.htm#fr_1180">243:36</a> Colebrooke I, Digest 62. Haradatta mentions also another explanation of the Sûtra: 'Another (commentator) says, " If products of animals and the rest have been bought, and the price is not paid at once, that may increase fivefold by the addition of interest, but not, to a greater sum."'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1180"></a><a href="sbe0276.htm#fr_1181">243:37</a> Manu VIII, 147-148; Yâ<i>g</i><i>ñ</i>avalkya II, 24.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1181"></a><a href="sbe0276.htm#fr_1182">243:38</a> Haradatta adds that in the case of a <i>S</i>rotriya and of an ascetic, the owner may allow the use of his property for a long time, desiring to acquire merit by doing so, and that fear may prevent him from opposing the king's servants. Hence prolonged possession by such persons does not necessitate the conclusion that the owner had given up his rights. As ascetics cannot possess any property, the Sûtra must refer to their occupying an empty house which has an owner.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1182"></a><a href="sbe0276.htm#fr_1183">243:39</a> Manu VIII, 149; Yâ<i>g</i><i>ñ</i>avalkya II, 25. The translation given above agrees with an explanation of the Sûtra which Haradatta mentions, but rejects. He himself prefers the following: 'Animals, i.e. quadrupeds; land, i.e. a field, a garden, and the like; females, i.e. female slaves and the like. No long possession of animals and the rest is necessary in order to acquire the rights of ownership over them. Even after a short period they become the  property of the possessor. For how (would it be possible that) a person, who himself wants butter-milk and the like, should allow a cow which he himself has bought, and which gives daily a Drona of milk, to be milked in the house of another person?' &amp;c. &amp;c.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1183"></a><a href="sbe0276.htm#fr_1184">244:40</a> Manu VIII, 162; Yâ<i>g</i><i>ñ</i>avalkya II, 51.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1184"></a><a href="sbe0276.htm#fr_1185">244:41</a> Manu VIII, 159-160; Yâ<i>g</i><i>ñ</i>avalkya II, 47, 54; Colebrooke I, Digest 202. Taking into account the parallel passages of Manu and Yâ<i>g</i><i>ñ</i>avalkya, Haradatta very properly restricts this rule to a bail for the personal appearance of an offender. In explanation of the expression 'a commercial debt' he gives the following instance: 'If a person has borrowed money from somebody on the condition that he is to repay the principal together with the gain thereon, and if he dies in a foreign country, while travelling in order to trade, then that money shall not be repaid by the son.' The instance explaining the term 'fee' (<i>s</i>ulka) is as follows: 'If a person has promised a fee (to the parents of a woman) and dies after the wedding, then that fee does not involve his son, i.e. need not be paid by him.' The word <i>s</i>ulka is, however, ambiguous, and may also mean 'a tax or toll.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_1185"></a><a href="sbe0276.htm#fr_1186">244:42</a> Manu VIII, 189; Yâ<i>g</i><i>ñ</i>avalkya II, 59-66; Colebrooke II, Digest I, 29. Haradatta declares the meaning to be, that in case the bailee was guilty of no negligence and took the same care of the deposits &amp;c. as of his own property, neither he nor his heirs need make good the value of those which were lost or destroyed.</p>
