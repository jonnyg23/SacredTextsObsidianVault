   <h3 align="center">PRA<i>S</i>NA I, PA<i>T</i>ALA 5, KHA<i>N</i><i>D</i>A 17.</h3>  
 <p>1. A <i>S</i>ûdra touches him, (then he shall leave off eating). <a name="fr_375"></a><a href="#fn_374"><font size="1">1</font></a></p>  
 <p>2. Nor shall he eat sitting in the same row with unworthy people. <a name="fr_376"></a><a href="#fn_375"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>3. Nor shall he eat (sitting in the same row with persons) amongst whom one, whilst they eat, rises and gives his leavings to his pupils or sips water; <a name="fr_377"></a><a href="#fn_376"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>4. Nor (shall he eat) where they give him food, reviling him. <a name="fr_378"></a><a href="#fn_377"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_62"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 62</font></a></p>  
 <p>5. Nor (shall he eat) what has been smelt at by men or other (beings, as cats). <a name="fr_379"></a><a href="#fn_378"><font size="1">5</font></a></p>  
 <p>6. He shall not eat in a ship,</p>  
 <p>7. Nor on a wooden platform.</p>  
 <p>8. He may eat sitting on ground which has been purified (by the application of cowdung and the like).</p>  
 <p>9. (If he eats) out of an earthen vessel, he shall eat out of one that has not been used (for cooking).</p>  
 <p>10. (If he can get) a used vessel (only, he shall eat from it), after having heated it thoroughly.</p>  
 <p>11. A vessel made of metal becomes pure by being scoured with ashes and the like. <a name="fr_380"></a><a href="#fn_379"><font size="1">11</font></a></p>  
 <p>12. A wooden vessel becomes pure by being scraped. <a name="fr_381"></a><a href="#fn_380"><font size="1">12</font></a></p>  
 <p>13. At a sacrifice (the vessels must be cleaned) according to the precepts of the Veda.</p>  
 <p>14. He shall not eat food which has been bought or obtained ready-prepared in the market.</p>  
 <p>15. Nor (shall he eat) flavoured food (bought in the market) excepting raw meat, honey, and salt.</p>  
 <p>16. Oil and clarified butter (bought in the market) he may use, after having sprinkled them with water. <a name="fr_382"></a><a href="#fn_381"><font size="1">16</font></a></p>  
 <p>17. Prepared food which has stood for a night, must neither be eaten nor drunk. <a name="fr_383"></a><a href="#fn_382"><font size="1">17</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_63"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 63</font></a></p>  
 <p>18. Nor (should prepared food) that has turned sour (be used in any way). <a name="fr_384"></a><a href="#fn_383"><font size="1">18</font></a></p>  
 <p>19. (The preceding two rules do) not (hold good in regard to) the juice of sugar-cane, roasted rice-grains, porridge prepared with whey, roasted yava, gruel, vegetables, meat, flour, milk and preparations from it, roots and fruits of herbs and trees. <a name="fr_385"></a><a href="#fn_384"><font size="1">19</font></a></p>  
 <p>20. (Substances which have turned) sour without being mixed with anything else (are to be avoided). <a name="fr_386"></a><a href="#fn_385"><font size="1">20</font></a></p>  
 <p>21. All intoxicating drinks are forbidden.</p>  
 <p>22. Likewise sheep's milk, <a name="fr_387"></a><a href="#fn_386"><font size="1">22</font></a></p>  
 <p>23. Likewise the milk of camels, of does, of animals that give milk while big with young, of those that bear twins, and of (one-hoofed animals), <a name="fr_388"></a><a href="#fn_387"><font size="1">23</font></a></p>  
 <p>24. Likewise the milk of a cow (buffalo-cow or she-goat) during the (first) ten days (after their giving birth to young ones), <a name="fr_389"></a><a href="#fn_388"><font size="1">24</font></a></p>  
 <p>25. Likewise (food mixed) with herbs which serve for preparing intoxicating liquors,</p>  
 <p>26. (Likewise) red garlic, onions, and leeks, <a name="fr_390"></a><a href="#fn_389"><font size="1">26</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_64"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 64</font></a></p>  
 <p>27. Likewise anything else which (those who are learned in the law) forbid. <a name="fr_391"></a><a href="#fn_390"><font size="1">27</font></a></p>  
 <p>28. Mushrooms ought not to be eaten; that has been declared in a Brâhma<i>n</i>a; <a name="fr_392"></a><a href="#fn_391"><font size="1">28</font></a></p>  
 <p>29. (Nor the meat) of one-hoofed animals, of camels, of the Gayal, of village pigs, of <i>S</i>arabhas, and of cattle. <a name="fr_393"></a><a href="#fn_392"><font size="1">29</font></a></p>  
 <p>30. (But the meat) of milch-cows and oxen may be eaten.</p>  
 <p>31. The Vâ<i>g</i>asaneyaka declares 'bull's flesh is fit for offerings.'</p>  
 <p>32. Amongst birds that scratch with their feet for, food, the (tame) cock (must not be eaten). <a name="fr_394"></a><a href="#fn_393"><font size="1">32</font></a></p>  
 <p>33. Amongst birds that feed thrusting forward their beak, the (heron, called) Plava (or Saka<i>t</i>abila, must not be eaten). <a name="fr_395"></a><a href="#fn_394"><font size="1">33</font></a></p>  
 <p>34. Carnivorous (birds are forbidden), <a name="fr_396"></a><a href="#fn_395"><font size="1">34</font></a></p>  
 <p>35. Likewise the swan, the Bhâsa, the Brâhma<i>n</i>î duck, and the falcon. <a name="fr_397"></a><a href="#fn_396"><font size="1">35</font></a></p>  
 <p>36. Common cranes and Sâras-cranes (are not to <a name="fr_398"></a><a href="#fn_397"><font size="1">36</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_65"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 65</font></a></p>  
 <p>be eaten) with the exception of the leather-nosed Lakshma<i>n</i>a.</p>  
 <p>37. Five-toed animals (ought not to be eaten) with the exception of the iguana, the tortoise, the porcupine, the hedgehog, the rhinoceros, the hare, and the Pûtikhasha. <a name="fr_399"></a><a href="#fn_398"><font size="1">37</font></a></p>  
 <p>38. Amongst fishes, the <i>K</i>eta ought not to be eaten,</p>  
 <p>39. Nor the snake-headed fish, nor the alligator, nor those which live on flesh only, nor those which are misshaped (like) mermen. <a name="fr_400"></a><a href="#fn_399"><font size="1">39</font></a></p>

 <p><a name="fn_374"></a><a href="sbe0220.htm#fr_375">61:1</a> <b>17</b>. 'Some say, that this Sûtra indicates that the touch of a <i>S</i>ûdra does not defile at any other time but at dinner, whilst others hold that a <i>S</i>ûdra's touch defiles always, and that the Sûtra is intended to indicate an excess of impurity, if it happens at dinnertime.'--Haradatta.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_375"></a><a href="sbe0220.htm#fr_376">61:2</a> 'Unworthy people are those who are neither of good family, nor possess learning and virtue.'--Haradatta.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_376"></a><a href="sbe0220.htm#fr_377">61:3</a> According to Haradatta a person who misbehaves thus, is called 'a dinner-thorn.' This point of etiquette is strictly observed in our days also. Manu IV, 2 12.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_378"></a><a href="sbe0220.htm#fr_379">62:5</a> 'As the text has avaghrâta, "smelt at," it does not matter if they smell the food from a distance.'--Haradatta.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_379"></a><a href="sbe0220.htm#fr_380">62:11</a> 'It must be understood from other Sm<i>ri</i>tis, that brass is to be cleaned with ashes, copper with acids, silver with cowdung, and gold with water.'--Haradatta. Manu V, I 14.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_397"></a><a href="sbe0220.htm#fr_398">64:36</a> Manu V, 12; Yâ<i>g</i><i>ñ</i>. I, 172. Other commentators take the whole Sûtra as one compound, and explain it as an exception to Sûtra 34. In that case the translation runs thus: ('Carnivorous birds are forbidden) except the Kru<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>a, Krau<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>a, Vârdhrâ<i>n</i>asa, <a name="page_65"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 65</font></a> and Lakshma<i>n</i>a.'--Haradatta. This translation is objectionable, because both the Kru<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>a, now called Kulam or Kû<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>, and the Krau<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>a, the red-crested crane, now called Sâras (Cyrus), feed on grain. Kru<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>akrau<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>a is a Vedic dual and stands for kru<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>akrau<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>â or kru<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>akrau<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>au.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_398"></a><a href="sbe0220.htm#fr_399">65:37</a> Manu V, 18; Yâ<i>g</i><i>ñ</i>. I, 77. Pûtikhasha is, according to Haradatta, an animal resembling a hare, and found in the Himâlayas.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_399"></a><a href="sbe0220.htm#fr_400">65:39</a> Haradatta closes this chapter on flesh-eating by quoting Manu V, 56, which declares flesh-eating, drinking spirituous liquor, and promiscuous intercourse to be allowable, but the abstinence therefrom of greater merit. He states that the whole chapter must be understood in this sense.</p>
