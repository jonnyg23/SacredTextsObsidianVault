 <p><a name="page_211"><font size="1" color="green">p. 211</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">MA<i>N</i><i>D</i>ALA II, HYMN 7.</h3>  <h4 align="center">ASH<i>T</i>AKA II, ADHYÂYA 5, VARGA 28.</h4>  
 <p>1. Bring us, O youngest god, Bhârata <a href="sbe46057.htm#an_2:7:1:1">1</a>, Agni, the best, resplendent, much-desired wealth, O Vasu <a href="sbe46057.htm#an_2:7:1:2">2</a>!</p>  
 <p>2. May no malign power of a god or of a mortal overcome us. Help us across such hostile power.</p>  
 <p>3. And may we dive with thee across all hostile powers as across streams of water.</p>  
 <p>4. Bright, O purifier, worthy of adoration, Agni, thou shinest mightily; thou hast been worshipped by offerings of gh<i>ri</i>ta <a href="sbe46057.htm#an_2:7:4:1">1</a>.</p>  
 <p>5. Thou, O Bhârata <a href="sbe46057.htm#an_2:7:5:1">1</a>, Agni! hast been worshipped by us with offerings of heifers, of bulls, of eight-footed (cows) <a href="sbe46057.htm#an_2:7:5:2">2</a>.</p>  
 <p>6. The old excellent Hot<i>ri</i> who feeds on wood and drinks butter, he is the wonderful son of strength.</p>  <h4 align="center">NOTES.</h4>  
 <p>The same <i>Ri</i>shi and metre.—Verse 1 = TS. I, 3, 14, 3; MS. IV, 11, 4. Verse 4 = TS. I, 3, 14, 5. Verse 6 = VS. XI, 70; TS. IV, 1, 9, 2; MS. II, 7, 7.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 1.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_2:7:1:1">1</a>. Agni Bhârata is Agni as the protector of the Bharata tribe or as invoked by that tribe.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_2:7:1:2">2</a>. With the beginning of this verse, I, 44, 4 should be compared.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_212"><font size="1" color="green">p. 212</font></a></p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 4.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_2:7:4:1">1</a>. Comp. VIII, 19, 22. agní<i>h</i> gh<i>ri</i>tébhi<i>h</i> â´huta<i>h</i>.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 5.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_2:7:5:1">1</a>. See verse 1, note 1.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_2:7:5:2">2</a>. Roth (Petersb. Dictionary) supplies vâgbhi<i>h</i> or <i>ri</i>gbhi<i>h</i>; comp. VIII, 76, 12. vâ´<i>k</i>am ash<i>t</i>â´padîm. But there is no doubt that ash<i>t</i>â´padî, standing by the side of va<i>s</i>â´ and ukshán, has the same meaning as in the later ritual, viz. a cow with calf.</p>
