 <p><a name="page_153"><font size="1" color="green">p. 153</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">MA<i>N</i><i>D</i>ALA I, HYMN 142.</h3>  <h4 align="center">ASH<i>T</i>AKA II, ADHYÂYA 2, VARGA 10–11.</h4>  <h4 align="center">ÂPRÎ HYMN.</h4>  
 <p>1. Being inflamed, Agni, bring hither to-day the gods to the man who holds forth the (sacrificial) ladle. Spin out the ancient thread (of sacrifice) <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:1:1">1</a> for the sacrificer who has prepared Soma.</p>  
 <p>2. Measure out, O Tanûnapât <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:2:1">1</a>, the sacrifice rich in ghee, rich in honey, of a priest like me, of a sacrificer who has toiled hard.</p>  
 <p>3. The brilliant, purifying, wonderful Narâ<i>s</i>a<i>m</i>sa <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:3:1">1</a> mixes the sacrifice with honey three times a day, the god worthy of worship among the gods.</p>  
 <p>4. Agni, magnified <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:4:1">1</a> by us, bring hither the bright, beloved Indra. For this my prayer is addressed <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:4:2">2</a> to thee whose tongue is good.</p>  
 <p>5. (Priests) hold forth the (sacrificial) ladle, strewing the sacrificial grass at the decorous service of the sacrifice;—I <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:5:1">1</a> trim <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:5:2">2</a> (the sacrificial grass) which best receives the gods with its wide extent, a big shelter for Indra.</p>  
 <p>6 <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:6:1">1</a>. May the divine doors open themselves, the increasers of <i>Ri</i>ta, the never sticking, large ones, the purifying, much-desired (doors), that the gods may come forth.</p>  
 <p>7. May Night and Dawn, of glorious appearance, the two neighbouring (goddesses), wearing beautiful ornaments <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:7:1">1</a>, the young <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:7:2">2</a> mothers of <i>Ri</i>ta, sit down together on the sacrificial grass <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:7:3">3</a>.</p>  
 <p>8. May the two divine Hot<i>ri</i>s, eager in praising (the gods), the sages with lovely tongues, perform</p>  
 <p><a name="page_154"><font size="1" color="green">p. 154</font></a></p>  
 <p>for us to-day this successful sacrifice which attains to Heaven.</p>  
 <p>9. The brilliant (goddess) placed among the gods, among the Maruts <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:9:1">1</a>, Hotrâ Bhâratî <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:9:2">2</a>, I<i>l</i>â, Sarasvatî, and Mahî <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:9:3">3</a>: may these worshipful (goddesses) sit down on the sacrificial grass.</p>  
 <p>10. May Tvash<i>t</i><i>ri</i>, inclined towards us, pour forth for us, in our navel <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:10:1">1</a>, that wonderful seed with many treasures <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:10:2">2</a>, plentiful by itself, for the sake of prosperity and wealth <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:10:3">3</a>.</p>  
 <p>11. Letting go (the sacrificial food to the gods) sacrifice by thyself to the gods, O tree <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:11:1">1</a>. May <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:11:2">2</a> Agni make the offerings ready <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:11:3">3</a>, the god among the gods, the wise one.</p>  
 <p>12. For Him who is accompanied by Pûshan and by the Maruts, by the Vi<i>s</i>ve devâ<i>h</i>, (by) Vâyu <a href="sbe46040.htm#an_1:142:12:1">1</a>, who is moved by the Gâyatra song, for Indra pronounce the Svâhâ over the offering.</p>  
 <p>13. Come hither to the offerings over which the Svâhâ has been pronounced, in order to feast. Indra! Come hither! Hear our call! Thee they call at the worship.</p>  <h4 align="center">NOTES.</h4>  
 <p>The hymn is an Âprîsûkta. The same <i>Ri</i>shi. Metre, Anush<i>t</i>ubh. The whole hymn is closely related to I, 13.—Verse 10: cf. VS. XXVII, 20; TS. IV, 1, 8, 3; MS. II, 12, 6.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 1.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:1:1">1</a>. The third Pâda of this verse is identical with VIII, 13, 14.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 2.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:2:1">1</a>. Comp. I, 13, 2, note 1.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_155"><font size="1" color="green">p. 155</font></a></p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 3.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:3:1">1</a>. Comp. I, 13, 2, note 1.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 4.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:4:1">1</a>. 'Magnified' is î<i>li</i>tá<i>h</i>; comp. the note on I, 1, 1. The verse is addressed to the I<i>d</i>a<i>h</i>.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:4:2">2</a>. The text has á<i>k</i><i>kh</i>a … va<i>k</i>yáte. To me there seems to be no doubt that this is the passive of va<i>k</i>, not of va<i>ñ</i><i>k</i>. Comp. the name of the priest a<i>k</i><i>kh</i>âvâka, and the phrase á<i>k</i><i>kh</i>oktibhi<i>h</i> matînâ´m, I, 61, 3; 184, 2. The same passive of va<i>k</i> is found III, 39, 1. matí<i>h</i> hridá<i>h</i> â´ va<i>k</i>yámânâ (then follows á<i>k</i><i>kh</i>a … <i>g</i>igâti); X, 47, 7 (stómâ<i>h</i>) … mánasâ va<i>k</i>yámânâ<i>h</i>.—It may be observed that in our passage as well as in III, 39, 1 and X, 47, 7, the forms va<i>k</i>yáte, va<i>k</i>yámânâ, va<i>k</i>yámânâ<i>h</i> are preceded by a vowel; and we may infer that the poet did not say u<i>k</i>yáte, &amp;c., in order to avoid the hiatus.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 5.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:5:1">1</a>. The poet begins as if he intended to say, 'Priests … lay down the sacrificial grass.' But he continues, I lay down.' Dr. Neisser (Bezzenberger's Beiträge, XX, 60) tries to explain the difficulty in a way in which I cannot follow him.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:5:2">2</a>. On the verb v<i>ri</i><i>g</i> technically connected with barhí<i>h</i>, see Geldner, Vedische Studien, I, 152 seq., and compare vol. xxxii, I, 38, 1, note 2; I, 64, 1, note 2.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 6.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:6:1">1</a>. With the whole verse compare I, 13, 6.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 7.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:7:1">1</a>. Pischel's opinion (Vedische Studien, II, 113 seq.) that pé<i>s</i>a<i>h</i> means 'Gestalt,' 'Form,' 'Farbe,' 'rûpa,' does not convince me.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:7:2">2</a>. See Geldner, Kuhn's Zeitschrift, XXVIII, 195.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_156"><font size="1" color="green">p. 156</font></a></p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:7:3">3</a>. Comp. VIII, 87, 4. â´ barhí<i>h</i> sîdatam sumát. Geldner (Vedische Studien, II, 190) translates: 'das schöne Opfergras.'</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 9.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:9:1">1</a>. 'Should we read márteshu for marútsu?' M. M. This conjecture seems perhaps rather bold.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:9:2">2</a>. Hotrâ Bhâratî, i. e. the personified Offering of the Bharatas, seems to be one goddess, more usually called simply Bhâratî. Comp. I, 22, 10; II, 1, 11; III, 62, 3; Bergaigne, Religion Védique, I, 322; H. O., Religion des Veda, 243, note 2. Pischel's opinion (Vedische Studien, II, 85) is different.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:9:3">3</a>. See above, I, 13, 9, note 1.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 10.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:10:1">1</a>. On the navel as the symbol of the connection between father and son, see Bergaigne, I, 35, 36, and comp. the well-known name Nâbhânedish<i>th</i>a.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:10:2">2</a>. For purú vâ´ram very probably puruvâ´ram should be read (Grassmann). See II, 40, 4. puruvâ´ram … râyás pósham ví syatâm nâ´bhim asmé.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:10:3">3</a>. With the last Pâda compare II, 40, 4, quoted in note 2, and II, 3, 9. pra<i>g</i>â´m Tvásh<i>t</i>â ví syatu nâ´bhim asmé; see also Taittirîya Sa<i>m</i>hitâ IV, I, 8, 3. Tvash<i>t</i><i>ri</i> is generally considered as giving sons; see H. O., Religion des Veda, 234.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 11.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:11:1">1</a>. Comp. I, 13, 11, note 1.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:11:2">2</a>. The second hemistich recurs I, 105, 14.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:11:3">3</a>. See Neisser, Zur Vedischen Verballehre, 22.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 12.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_1:142:12:1">1</a>. The text has 'for Vâyu,' not 'for (the god) accompanied by Vâyu.' But there is no doubt that pûsha<i>n</i>váte, &amp;c., refers to Indra, and that Vâyu is named merely as a companion of Indra.</p>
