 <p><a name="page_405"><font size="1" color="green">p. 405</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">MA<i>N</i><i>D</i>ALA V, HYMN 18.</h3>  <h4 align="center">ASH<i>T</i>AKA IV, ADHYÂYA 1, VARGA 10.</h4>  
 <p>1. May Agni, beloved of many, the guest of the house <a href="sbe46122.htm#an_5:18:1:1">1</a>, be praised in the morning, the immortal who delights <a href="sbe46122.htm#an_5:18:1:2">2</a> in all offerings among the mortals.</p>  
 <p>2 <a href="sbe46122.htm#an_5:18:2:1">1</a>. To Dvita who by the liberal power of his dexterousness carries away injury <a href="sbe46122.htm#an_5:18:2:2">2</a>, this praiser of thine, O immortal, prepares Soma in the due way.</p>  
 <p>3. I call for your sake Him who flames through long life, with the speech that belongs to the liberal patrons <a href="sbe46122.htm#an_5:18:3:1">1</a> whose chariot moves uninjured, O giver of horses <a href="sbe46122.htm#an_5:18:3:2">2</a>;</p>  
 <p>4. And in whom (dwells) brilliant thought, who guard the hymns of praise in their mouth, (whose) sacrificial grass is spread in the realm of the sun: they have invested themselves with glory.</p>  
 <p>5. On the liberal patrons who have given me fifty horses for my song of praise <a href="sbe46122.htm#an_5:18:5:1">1</a>, bestow brilliant, mighty, high glory, O Agni; on those men (bestow glory) with (valiant) men, O immortal!</p>  <h4 align="center">NOTES.</h4>  
 <p>The <i>Ri</i>shi is M<i>ri</i>ktavâhas Dvita Âtreya (see verse 2); the metre is the same.—Verse 1 = SV. I, 85. Verse 5 = TB. II, 7, 5, 2.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 1.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:18:1:1">1</a>. Vi<i>s</i>á<i>h</i> … átithi<i>h</i>: cf. above, V, 3, 5.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:18:1:2">2</a>. On ran with the accusative, compare Gaedicke, p. 76.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_406"><font size="1" color="green">p. 406</font></a></p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 2.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:18:2:1">1</a>. Compare on this verse Macdonell, Journal Roy. As. Soc., 1893, p. 463 seq.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:18:2:2">2</a>. Dvita, who seems to be identified with Agni, is, in the same way as Trita (see Bloomfield, Proceedings Amer. Or. Soc., March, 1894, p. cxix seqq.), supposed to take away human sin and all sorts of mischief and misfortune (cf. VIII, 47, 16. Tritâ´ya <i>k</i>a Dvitâ´ya <i>k</i>a ûsha<i>h</i> dushvápnyam vaha). Thus he is invoked here as carrying away m<i>ri</i>ktá, i. e. injury.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 3.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:18:3:1">1</a>. The speech of the priest belongs to the sacrificer who has engaged him.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:18:3:2">2</a>. This seems to be Agni, with an evident allusion to the human giver of horses (see verse 5).</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 5.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:18:5:1">1</a>. Sadhástuti seems to be instrumental. Cf. Lanman, p. 381.</p>
