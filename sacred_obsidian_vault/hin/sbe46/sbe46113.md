 <p><a name="page_387"><font size="1" color="green">p. 387</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">MA<i>N</i><i>D</i>ALA V, HYMN 9.</h3>  <h4 align="center">ASH<i>T</i>AKA IV, ADHYÂYA 1, VARGA 1.</h4>  
 <p>1. Thee, O Agni, the god, mortals bringing offerings magnify. I deem thee the <i>G</i>âtavedas. Carry then the offerings (to the gods) in thy due way.</p>  
 <p>2. Agni is the Hot<i>ri</i> of the dwelling where they offer gifts and spread the sacrificial grass, he with whom sacrifices, with whom glorious gains assemble.</p>  
 <p>3. And he whom the kindling-stick has born, the young one, like a young (calf), the supporter of human clans, Agni the best sacrificer—</p>  
 <p>4. And thou showest thyself hard to seize like a son of … <a href="sbe46113.htm#an_5:9:4:1">1</a>, thou who art a burner of many woods, O Agni, like an animal (that consumes all grass) on a meadow <a href="sbe46113.htm#an_5:9:4:2">2</a>.</p>  
 <p>5 <a href="sbe46113.htm#an_5:9:5:1">1</a>. And he whose smoky <a href="sbe46113.htm#an_5:9:5:2">2</a> flames come together, when Trita in heaven blows upon him like a smelter, sharpens (him) as in smelting (him) <a href="sbe46113.htm#an_5:9:5:3">3</a>…</p>  
 <p>6. May I through thy protection, O Agni, and through the praises of Mitra—may we <a href="sbe46113.htm#an_5:9:6:1">1</a>, like dispellers of malice, overcome the dangers of mortals.</p>  
 <p>7. Bring this wealth to us, O powerful Agni, to (these our) men. May he <a href="sbe46113.htm#an_5:9:7:1">1</a> give us dwelling; may he <a href="sbe46113.htm#an_5:9:7:1">1</a> give us prosperity; may he <a href="sbe46113.htm#an_5:9:7:1">1</a> help us in winning booty. And help us to grow strong in fights!</p>  <h4 align="center">NOTES.</h4>  
 <p>The <i>Ri</i>shi is Gaya Âtreya (cf. V, 10, 3); the metre is Anush<i>t</i>ubh (verses 4 and 7, Paṅkti).—Verse 1 = TB. II, 4, 1, 4.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_388"><font size="1" color="green">p. 388</font></a></p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 4.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:9:4:1">1</a>. Putrá<i>h</i> ná hvâryâ´<i>n</i>âm. The meaning of hvâryá is conjectural. Cf. on hvârá, to which it very probably is related, I, 141, 7, note 1; 11, 24 4, note 1. Does hvâryá mean 'serpent,' or a kind of horse (VI, 2, 8. átya<i>h</i> ná hvâryá<i>h</i> <i>s</i>í<i>s</i>u<i>h</i>)?</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:9:4:2">2</a>. The last Pâda is identical with VI, 2, 9. Considering the occurrence of the word hvâryá here and in VI, 2, 8 (see note 1) we cannot believe that this is merely a casual coincidence.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 5.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:9:5:1">1</a>. On this verse, compare Neisser, Bezzenberger's Beiträge, XX, 40; Macdonell, Journal Roy. As. Soc., 1893, p. 446.</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:9:5:2">2</a>. Dhûmína<i>h</i> may be gen. sing.: 'he whose, the smoky (god's), flames.'</p>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:9:5:3">3</a>. Ludwig and Neisser (Bezz. Beitr., loc. cit.) regard dhmâtárî (Padap. dhmâtári) as a nom. sing. masculine. I think that Geldner (Vedische Studien, I, 146, note 1) and Bartholomae (Indogermanische Forschungen, I, 496, note 2) are right in explaining it as a locative infinitive. Compare also Johansson, Kuhn's Zeitschrift, XXX, 415; Joh. Schmidt, Pluralbildungen der Indogermanischen Neutra, p. 247. Macdonell translates, 'as in a smelting furnace.'</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 6.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:9:6:1">1</a>. The poet, who has begun his sentence in the first person singular ('may I'), goes on in the plural.</p>  <h5 align="center">Verse 7.</h5>  
 <p>Note <a name="an_5:9:7:1">1</a>. ‘He,’ i. e. Agni, or ‘it,’ i. e. the wealth?</p>
