 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">An Arthurian Miscellany at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <center> <h2 align="CENTER">THE RESCUE</h2>  <h3 align="CENTER">by</h3>  <h2 align="CENTER">ROBERT BUCHANAN</h2>  </center>  <br>  <br>                 I.<br>  <br>  The sun shines fair on holm and hill,<br>           On wood and waving corn,<br>  But there is wail in Carleil<br>           On that fair summer's morn.<br>  The noblest lady in the land<br>           Is doomed, ah, wo the while!<br>  To shameful death, with lighted brand,<br>           At yonder fatal pile.<br>  <br>  ***************************** <br>                 II.<br>  <br>  And hark, the dede-bell's dolesome sound!<br>           And pacing sad and slow,<br>  Two score were knights of Table Round<br>           Compass that place of wo.<br>  Sir Gareth and Sir Gaherys           1 <br>           Are of that knightly band,<br>  But loth I ween, and weaponless,<br>           To do the king's command.<br>  And helm'ed heads adown are hung<br>           For pity and for pain;<br>  And sobs are shrieked, and hands are wrung,<br>           And tears like summer-rain<br>  From gentle eyes are showering fast,<br>           To see that lady fair,<br>  Like Beauty's ghost, walk palely past<br>           With feet and tresses bare.<br>  Diskirtled to her snowy shift,<br>           She's reached that fatal spot,<br>  Then knelt her down and made her shrift --<br>           Ah! where was Launcelot?<br>  As up she rose, one hurried look<br>           To east, and then to west --<br>  Then mournfully her head she shook,<br>           And to her death address'd.<br>  O ne'er did sculptor's hand, I ween,<br>           Shape marble half so fair,<br>  As stood on green that matchless queen,<br>           Unkirtled Guinevere.<br>  And maidens swooned and warriors wept,<br>           As slowly to the stake,<br>  All in her smock, that lady stept<br>           Her doleful doom to take.<br>  <br>                 III.<br>  <br>  They've lit the brand -- they've bound her fast;<br>           But hark!  blown long and loud<br>  From far a blast!  and all aghast<br>           Disparts the flying crowd!<br>  "A Launcelot!  A Launcelot!"<br>           It nears their battle-cry --<br>  And man and horse all reeking hot<br>           Dash in that chivalry!<br>  All armèd knights of gallant strain,<br>           And of the Table Round;<br>  Sir Bors, Sir Blamor, Sir Lavayne,<br>           Sir Palamede renown'd:<br>  Sir Ector, and Sir Lionel,<br>           Sir Urre of Hungarie,<br>  And others more it needs not tell,<br>           A twenty knights and three.<br>  <br>                 IV.<br>  <br>  O madly right and madly left<br>           Sir Launcelot blindly strake,<br>  And helms and heads in sunder cleft,<br>           To reach that deadly stake.<br>  Nor wist not in that maddening press<br>           Two knights unarmed he slew,<br>  Sir Gareth and Sir Gaherys,<br>           And both his lovers true.<br>  That evil chance hath bred him pain,<br>           I ween, and meikle wo;<br>  For it hath made the brave Gawayne<br>           His fierce for ever foe.<br>  <br>                 V.<br>  <br>  With lance a'rest and sword a'sweep,<br>           So fell onslaught they made<br>  Of that two score, in mortal sleep<br>           A thirty knights are laid.<br>  And onward dashed Sir Launcelot<br>           To save his lady fair;<br>  With bloody brand the cords he cut --<br>           Joy, joy, for Guinevere!<br>  With haste his mantle's screen he wound<br>           To hap his lady's charms;<br>  She sobbed her thanks to Heaven, and swoon'd<br>           Within his clasping arms.<br>  Gently he's placed her on his selle,<br>           Then leapt him light afore,<br>  And spurred his steed o'er flood and fell --<br>           A precious freight he bore.<br>  And blythe that lady was I wot,<br>           And oft her circling arm<br>  Caressed her loving Launcelot,<br>           Nor foe she drad nor harm.<br>  And oft his ladye-love he cheered,<br>           And oft his gallant steed;<br>  Nor drew his rein until he neared<br>           His castle-bower on Tweed --<br>  His castle fair of Joyous Garde,<br>           Whereat a live-long year<br>  In worship, and in loving ward,<br>           Abode Queen Guinevere!<br>  <br>  <br>  <b> Footnotes</b> <br>  <br>  <a> 1.   Gareth and Gaherys were brothers of Sir Gawayne, and consequently nephews of King Arthur, their mother (wife of King Lot) being his half-sister.  Sir Gawayne had protested against the act of putting the Queen to death, and refused to be present at the execution of the sentence.<br>  <br>  <br> <br>
