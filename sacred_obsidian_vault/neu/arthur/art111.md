 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">An Arthurian Miscellany at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <center> <h2 align="CENTER">PALOMYDES' QUEST</h2>  <h3 align="CENTER">by</h3>  <h2 align="CENTER">WILLIAM MORRIS</h2>  </center>  <br>  About the middle of the month of June<br>       Sir Palomydes rode upon his quest,<br>  Twixt sunrise and the setting of the moon:<br>       Beast Glatysaunt did give him little rest<br>  <br>  At midday, and at midnight must he sleep,<br>       And still the beast trailed on unceasingly<br>  Waking strange echoes in the forest deep,<br>       Leaving strange scales on many a bush or tree.<br>  <br>  So the days went and no lovesickness came<br>       O'er the knight's heart to weaken it or bow<br>  His head; he rode on with the same<br>       Set purpose still in his unwrinkled brow.<br>  <br>  Until one day when that he rode thinking<br>       Whether the beast as they met face to face<br>  Would turn to fight  him with a sudden spring,<br>       Or creep away and whine in some dark place<br>  <br>  Until he bound his jaws and led him out--<br>       And then he thought until his heart grew hot<br>  Of how the folk would laugh and sing and shout<br>       And he should lead the beast through Camelot,<br>  <br>  The heralds crying, "Ho good people, see!<br>       For this is Palomydes the good knight<br>  Who hath achieved his quest most gloriously<br>       And won the Questing Beast in open fight!"<br>  <br>  Thereat in sooth he almost seemed to be<br>       There in the streets with all the bells ringing<br>  And all the folk at window him to see,<br>       Damsels and minstrels ready for to sing.<br>  <br>  Almost he heard the praises of the King<br>       And Launcelot saying "Now beyond all doubt<br>  Is Palomydes the best knight living<br>       Though Lamorak and Tristram are most stout."<br>  <br>  Abroad from thence the bruit shall go of me,<br>       And many a lord shall say, "Hold we high feast;<br>  Tomorrow an uncouth sight shall we see:<br>       Here cometh Palomydes and his beast."<br>  <br>  And so to Cornwall shall I come at last --<br>       But saying this he sighed, for well he thought:<br>  When all this noble fame has been compassed<br>       Shall Iseult's love be nearer to me brought?<br>  <br>  Now at that time the forest thinner grew<br>       On the left hand, and all between the trees<br>  The light of the green fields began to show,<br>       And ever fresher blew the western breeze,<br>  <br>  On either side of him the thrushes sang<br>       And as he drew his rein it seemed to him<br>  That from some far-off tower the bells rang.<br>       So he passed on to that great forest's rim<br>  <br>  And then beneath him by the meadows fair<br>       With their broad acres of the good green wheat<br>  Starred with the blood-red poppies burning clear,<br>       There sat he, and the smell of hay came sweet<br>  <br>  Upon the wind and therewithal the chimes<br>       Uncertain as the kisses of a maid<br>  Sang out their tale in sweet outlandish rymes<br>       Hard to remember.  Therefore down he laid<br>  <br>  His bridle, and he cried, How fair, how fair,<br>       You walk within the summer gardens [    ]<br>  O bright Iseult!--having but little care<br>       For Palomydes, as I full well know.<br>
