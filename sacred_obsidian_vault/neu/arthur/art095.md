 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">An Arthurian Miscellany at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <center> <h2 align="CENTER">A LEGEND OF TINTAGEL CASTLE</h2>  <h3 align="CENTER">by</h3>  <h2 align="CENTER">LETITIA ELIZABETH LANDON</h2>  </center>  <br>  Alone in the forest, Sir Lancelot rode,<br>  O'er the neck of his courser the reins lightly flowed,<br>  And beside hung his helmet, for bare was his brow<br>  To meet the soft breeze that was fanning him now.<br>  <br>  And "the flowers of the forest" were many and sweet,<br>  Which, crushed at each step by his proud courser's feet,<br>  Gave forth all their fragrance, while thick over-head<br>  The boughs of the oak and the elm-tree were spread.<br>  <br>  The wind stirred the branches, as if its low suit<br>  Were urged, like a lover who wakens the lute,<br>  And through the dark foliage came sparkling and bright,<br>  Like rain from the green leaves, in small gems of light.<br>  <br>  There was stillness, not silence, for dancing along,<br>  A brook went its way like a child with a song:<br>  Now hidden, where rushes and water-flags grow;<br>  Now clear, while white pebbles were glistening below.<br>  <br>  Lo, bright as a vision, and fair as a dream,<br>  The face of a maiden is seen in the stream;<br>  With her hair like a mantle of gold to her knee,<br>  Stands a lady as lovely as lady can be.<br>  <br>  Short speech tells a love-tale; --the bard's sweetest words<br>  Are poor, beside those which each memory hoards:<br>  Sound of some gentle whisper, the haunting and low,<br>  Such as love may have murmured--ah, long, long ago.<br>  <br>  She led him away to an odorous cave,<br>  Where the emerald spars shone like stars in the wave,<br>  And the green moss and violets crowded beneath,<br>  And the ash at the entrance hung down like a wreath.<br>  <br>  They might have been happy, if love could but learn<br>  A lesson from some flowers, and like their leaves turn<br>  Round their own inward world, their own lone fragrant nest,<br>  Content with its sweetness, content with its rest.<br>  <br>  But the sound of the trumpet was heard from afar,<br>  And Sir Lancelot rode forth again to the war;<br>  And the wood-nymph was left as aye woman will be,<br>  Who trusts her whole being, oh, false love, to thee.<br>  <br>  For months, every sun-beam that brightened the gloom,<br>  She deemed was the waving of Lancelot's plume;<br>  She knew not of the proud and the beautiful queen,<br>  Whose image was treasured as her's once had been.<br>  <br>  There was many a fair dame, and many a knight,<br>  Made the banks of the river like fairy-land bright;<br>  And among those whose shadow was cast on the tide,<br>  Was Lancelot kneeling near Genevra's side.<br>  <br>  With purple sails heavily drooping around,<br>  The mast, and the prow, with the vale lily bound;<br>  And towed by two swans, a small vessel drew near,<br>  But high on the  deck was a pall-covered bier.<br>  <br>  They oared with their white wings, the bark thro' the flood,<br>  Till arrived at the bank where Sir Lancelot stood:<br>  A wind swept the river, and flung back the pall,<br>  And there lay a lady, the fairest of all.<br>  <br>  But pale as a statue, like sunshine on snow,<br>  The bright hair seemed mocking the cold face below:<br>  Sweet truants, the blush and the smile both are fled--<br>  Sir Lancelot weeps as he kneels by the dead.<br>  <br>  And these are love's records; a vow and a dream,<br>  And the sweet shadow passes away from life's stream:<br>  Too late we awake to regret--but what tears<br>  Can bring back the waste to our hearts and our years!<br>  <br>
