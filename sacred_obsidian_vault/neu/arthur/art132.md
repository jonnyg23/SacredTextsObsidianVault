 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">An Arthurian Miscellany at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <center> <h2 align="CENTER">THE MARRIAGE OF SIR GAWAINE</h2>  <h3 align="CENTER">from </h3>  <h2 align="CENTER">BISHOP THOMAS PERCY'S RELIQUES OF ANCIENT ENGLISH POETRY</h2>  </center>  <br>  <br>  <center>PART THE FIRST</center>  <br>  King Arthur lives in merry Carleile,<br>     And seemely is to see;<br>  And there with him queene Guenever,<br>     That bride soe bright of blee.<br>  <br>  And there with him queene Guenever,<br>     That bride so bright in bowre:<br>  And all his barons about him stoode,<br>     That were both stiffe and stowre.<br>  <br>  The king a royale Christmasse kept,<br>     With mirth and princelye cheare;<br>  To him repaired many a knighte,<br>     That came both farre and neare.<br>  <br>  And when they were to dinner sette,<br>     And cups went freely round;<br>  Before them came a faire damsèlle,<br>     And knelt upon the ground.<br>  <br>  A boone, a boone, O kinge Arthùre,<br>     I beg a boone of thee;<br>  Avenge me of a carlish knighte,<br>     Who hath shent my love and me.<br>  <br>  At Tearne-Wadling his castle stands,<br>     Near to that lake so fair,<br>  And proudlye rise the battlements,<br>     And streamers deck the air.<br>  <br>  Noe gentle knighte, nor ladye gay,<br>     May pass that castle-walle:<br>  But from that foule discurteous knighte,<br>     Mishappe will them befalle.<br>  <br>  Hee's twyce the size of common men,<br>     Wi' thewes, and sinewes stronge,<br>  And on his backe he bears a clubbe,<br>     That is both thicke and longe.<br>  <br>  This grimme baròne 'twas our harde happe,<br>     But yester morne to see;<br>  When to his bowre he bare my love,<br>     And sore misused mee.<br>  <br>  And when I told him, king Arthùre<br>     As lyttle shold him spare;<br>  Goe tell, sayd hee, that cuckold kinge,<br>     To meete mee if he dare.<br>  <br>  Upp then sterted king Arthùre,<br>     And sware by hille and dale,<br>  He ne'er wolde quitt that grimme baròne,<br>     Till he had made him quail.<br>  <br>  Goe fetch my sword Excalibar:<br>     Goe saddle mee my steede;<br>  Nowe, by my faye, that grimme baròne<br>     Shall rue this ruthfulle deede.<br>  <br>  And when he came to Tearne Wadlinge<br>     Benethe the castle walle:<br>  "Come forth; come forth; thou proude baròne,<br>     Or yielde thyself my thralle."<br>  <br>  On magicke grounde that castle stoode,<br>     And fenc'd with many a spelle:<br>  Noe valiant knighte could tread thereon,<br>     But straite his courage felle.<br>  <br>  Forth then rush'd that carlish knight,<br>     King Arthur felte the charme:<br>  His sturdy sinewes lost their strengthe,<br>     Downe sunke his feeble arme.<br>  <br>  Nowe yield thee, yield thee, kinge Arthùre,<br>     Now yield thee, unto mee:<br>  Or fighte with mee, or lose thy lande,<br>     Noe better termes maye bee,<br>  <br>  Unless thou sweare upon the rood,<br>     And promise on thy faye,<br>  Here to returne to Tearne-Wadling,<br>     Upon the new-yeare's daye;<br>  <br>  And bringe me worde what thing it is<br>     All women moste desyre;<br>  This is thy ransome, Arthur, he sayes,<br>     Ile have noe other hyre.<br>  <br>  King Arthur then helde up his hande,<br>     And sware upon his faye,<br>  Then tooke his leave of the grimme barone<br>     And faste hee rode awaye.<br>  <br>  And he rode east, and he rode west,<br>     And did of all inquyre,<br>  What thing it is all women crave,<br>     And what they most desyre.<br>  <br>  Some told him riches, pompe, or state;<br>     Some rayment fine and brighte;<br>  Some told him mirthe; some flatterye;<br>     And some a jollye knighte.<br>  <br>  In letters all king Arthur wrote,<br>     And seal'd them with his ringe:<br>  But still his minde was helde in doubte,<br>     Each tolde a different thinge.<br>  <br>  As ruthfulle he rode over a more,<br>     He saw a ladye sette<br>  Betweene an oke, and a greene holléye,<br>     All clad in red scarlette.<br>  <br>  Her nose was crookt and turnd outwàrde,<br>     Her chin stoode all awrye;<br>  And where as sholde have been her mouthe,<br>     Lo! there was set her eye:<br>  <br>  Her haires, like serpents, clung aboute<br>     Her cheekes of deadlye hewe:<br>  A worse-form'd ladye than she was,<br>     No man mote ever viewe.<br>  <br>  To hail the king in seemelye sorte<br>     This ladye was fulle faine;<br>  But king Arthùre all sore amaz'd,<br>     No aunswere made againe.<br>  <br>  What wight art thou, the ladye sayd,<br>     That wilt not speake to mee;<br>  Sir, I may chance to ease thy paine,<br>     Though I be foule to see.<br>  <br>  If thou wilt ease my paine, he sayd,<br>     And helpe me in my neede;<br>  Ask what thou wilt, thou grimme ladyè<br>     And it shall bee thy meede.<br>  <br>  O sweare mee this upon the roode,<br>     And promise on thy faye;<br>  And here the secrette I will telle,<br>     That shall thy ransome paye.<br>  <br>  King Arthur promis'd on his faye,<br>     And sware upon the roode;<br>  The secrette then the ladye told,<br>     As lightlye well shee cou'de.<br>  <br>  Now this shall be my paye, sir king,<br>     And this my guerdon bee,<br>  That some yong fair and courtlye knight,<br>     Thou bringe to marrye mee.<br>  <br>  Fast then pricked king Arthùre<br>     Ore hille, and dale, and downe:<br>  And soone he founde the barone's bowre:<br>     And soone the grimme baroùne.<br>  <br>  He bare his clubbe upon his backe,<br>     Hee stoode bothe stiffe and stronge;<br>  And, when he had the letters reade,<br>     Awaye the lettres flunge.<br>  <br>  Nowe yielde thee, Arthur, and thy lands,<br>     All forfeit unto mee;<br>  For this is not thy paye, sir king,<br>     Nor may thy ransome bee.<br>  <br>  Yet hold thy hand, thou proud baròne,<br>     I praye thee hold thy hand;<br>  And give mee leave to speake once more<br>     In reskewe of my land.<br>  <br>  This morne, as I came over a more,<br>     I saw a ladye sette<br>  Betwene an oke, and a greene hollèye,<br>     All clad in red scarlètte.<br>  <br>  Shee sayes, all women will have their wille,<br>     This is their chief desyre;<br>  Now yield, as thou art a barone true,<br>     That I have payd mine hyre.<br>  <br>  An earlye vengeaunce light on her!<br>     The carlish baron swore:<br>  Shee was my sister tolde thee this,<br>     And shee's a mishapen whore.<br>  <br>  But here I will make mine avowe,<br>     To do her as ill a turne:<br>  For an ever I may that foule theefe gette,<br>     In a fyre I will her burne.<br>  <br>  <br>  <center>PART THE SECONDE</center> <br>  <br>  Homewarde pricked king Arthùre,<br>     And a wearye man was hee;<br>  And soon he mette queene Guenever,<br>     That bride so bright of blee.<br>  <br>  What newes! what newes! thou noble king,<br>     Howe, Arthur, hast thou sped?<br>  Where hast thou hung the carlish knighte?<br>     And where bestow'd his head?<br>  <br>  The carlish knight is safe for mee,<br>     And free fro mortal harme:<br>  On magicke grounde his castle stands,<br>     And fenc'd with many a charme.<br>  <br>  To bowe to him I was full faine,<br>     And yielde mee to his hand:<br>  And but for a lothly ladye, there<br>     I sholde have lost my land.<br>  <br>  And nowe this fills my hearte with woe,<br>     And sorrowe of my life;<br>  I swore a yonge and courtlye knight,<br>     Sholde marry her to his wife.<br>  <br>  Then bespake him sir Gawàine,<br>     That was ever a gentle knighte:<br>  That lothly ladye I will wed;<br>     Therefore be merrye and lighte.<br>  <br>  Nowe naye, nowe naye, good sir Gawàine;<br>     My sister's sonne yee bee;<br>  This lothlye ladye's all too grimme,<br>     And all too foule for yee.<br>  <br>  Her nose is crookt and turn'd outwàrde;<br>     Her chin stands all awrye;<br>  A worse form'd ladye than shee is<br>     Was never seen with eye.<br>  <br>  What though her chin stand all awrye,<br>     And shee be foule to see:<br>  I'll marry her, unkle, for thy sake,<br>     And I'll thy ransome bee.<br>  <br>  Nowe thankes, nowe thankes, good sir Gawàine;<br>     And a blessing thee betyde!<br>  To-morrow wee'll have knights and squires,<br>     And wee'll goe fetch thy bride.<br>  <br>  And wee'll have hawkes and wee'll have houndes,<br>     To cover our intent;<br>  And wee'll away to the greene forèst,<br>     As wee a hunting went.<br>  <br>  Sir Lancelot, sir Stephen bolde,<br>     They rode with them that daye;<br>  And foremoste of the companye<br>     There rode the stewarde Kaye:<br>  <br>  Soe did sir Banier and sir Bore,<br>     And eke sir Garratte keene;<br>  Sir Tristram too, that gentle knight,<br>     To the forest freshe and greene.<br>  <br>  And when they came to the greene forrèst,<br>     Beneathe a faire holley tree<br>  There sate that ladye in red scarlètte<br>     That unseemelye was to see.<br>  <br>  Sir Kay beheld that lady's face,<br>     And looked upon her sweere;<br>  Whoever kisses that ladye, he sayes,<br>     Of his kisse he stands in feare.<br>  <br>  Sir Kay beheld that ladye againe,<br>     And looked upon her snout;<br>  Whoever kisses that ladye, he sayes,<br>     Of his kisse he stands in doubt.<br>  <br>  Peace, brother Kay, sayde sir Gawàine,<br>     And amend thee of thy life:<br>  For there is a knight amongst us all,<br>     Must marry her to his wife.<br>  <br>  What marry this foule queane, quoth Kay,<br>     I' the devil's name anone;<br>  Gett mee a wife wherever I maye,<br>     In sooth shee shall be none.<br>  <br>  Then some tooke up their hawkes in haste,<br>     And some took up their houndes;<br>  And sayd they wolde not marry her,<br>     For cities, nor for townes.<br>  <br>  Then bespake him king Arthùre,<br>     And sware there by this daye;<br>  For a little foule sighte and mislikìnge,<br>     Yee shall not say her naye.<br>  <br>  Peace, lordinges, peace; sir Gawaine sayd;<br>     Nor make debate and strife;<br>  This lothlye ladye I will take,<br>     And marry her to my wife.<br>  <br>  Nowe thankes, nowe thankes, good sir Gawaine,<br>     And a blessinge be thy meede!<br>  For as I am thine owne ladyè,<br>     Thou never shalt rue this deede.<br>  <br>  Then up they took that lothly dame,<br>     And home anone they bringe:<br>  And there sir Gawaine he her wed,<br>     And married her with a ringe.<br>  <br>  And when they were in wed-bed laid,<br>     And all were done awaye:<br>  "Come turne to mee, mine owne wed-lord<br>     Come turne to mee I praye."<br>  <br>  Sir Gawaine scant could lift his head,<br>     For sorrowe and for care;<br>  When, lo! instead of that lothelye dame,<br>     Hee sawe a young ladye faire.<br>  <br>  Sweet blushes stayn'd her rud-red cheeke,<br>     Her eyen were blacke as sloe:<br>  The ripening cherrye swellde her lippe,<br>     And all her necke was snowe.<br>  <br>  Sir Gawaine kiss'd that lady faire,<br>     Lying upon the sheete:<br>  And swore, as he was a true knighte,<br>     The spice was never soe sweete.<br>  <br>  Sir Gawaine kiss'd that lady brighte,<br>     Lying there by his side:<br>  "The fairest flower is not soe faire:<br>     Thou never can'st bee my bride."<br>  <br>  I am thy bride, mine owne deare lorde,<br>     The same whiche thou didst knowe,<br>  That was soe lothlye, and was wont<br>     Upon the wild more to goe.<br>  <br>  Nowe, gentle Gawaine, chuse, quoth shee,<br>     And make thy choice with care;<br>  Whether by night, or else by daye,<br>     Shall I be foule or faire?<br>  <br>  "To have thee foule still in the night,<br>     When I with thee should playe!<br>  I had rather farre, my lady deare,<br>     To have thee foule by daye."<br>  <br>  What when gaye ladyes goe with their lordes<br>     To drinke the ale and wine;<br>  Alas! then I must hide myself,<br>     I must not goe with mine?<br>  <br>  "My faire ladyè, sir Gawaine sayd,<br>     I yield me to thy skille;<br>  Because thou art mine owne ladyè<br>     Thou shalt have all thy wille."<br>  <br>  Nowe blessed be thou, sweete Gawàine,<br>     And the daye that I thee see;<br>  For as thou seest mee at this time,<br>     Soe shall I ever bee.<br>  <br>  My father was an aged knighte,<br>     And yet it chanced soe,<br>  He tooke to wife a false ladyè,<br>     Whiche broughte me to this woe.<br>  <br>  She witch'd mee, being a faire yonge maide,<br>     In the greene forèst to dwelle;<br>  And there to abide in lothlye shape,<br>     Most like a fiend of helle.<br>  <br>  Midst mores and mosses; woods, and wilds;<br>     To lead a lonesome life:<br>  Till some yong faire and courtlye knighte<br>     Wolde marrye me to his wife:<br>  <br>  Nor fully to gaine mine owne trewe shape,<br>     Such was her devilish skille;<br>  Until he wolde yielde to be rul'd by mee,<br>     And let mee have all my wille.<br>  <br>  She witchd my brother to a carlish boore,<br>     And made him stiffe and stronge;<br>  And built him a bowre on magicke grounde,<br>     To live by rapine and wronge.<br>  <br>  But now the spelle is broken throughe,<br>     And wronge is turnde to righte;<br>  Henceforth I shall bee a faire ladyè,<br>     And hee be a gentle knighte.<br>
