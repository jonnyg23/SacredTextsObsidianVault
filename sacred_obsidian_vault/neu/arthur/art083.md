 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN">An Arthurian Miscellany at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr> <center><h3 align="CENTER">THE DOOM-WELL OF ST. MADRON</h3>  <h2 align="CENTER">by</h2>  <h3 align="CENTER">ROBERT STEPHEN HAWKER</h3>  </center> <br>  <br> 		I.<br> <br> "Plunge thy right hand in St. Madron's spring,<br> If true to its troth be the palm you bring:<br> But if a false sigil thy fingers bear,<br> Lay them rather on the burning share."<br> <br> 		II.<br> <br> Loud laughed King Arthur when-as he heard<br> That solemn friar his boding word:<br> And blithely he sware as a king he may,<br> "We tryst for St. Madron's at break of day."<br> <br> 		III.<br> <br> "Now horse and hattock, both but and ben,"<br> Was the cry at Lauds, with Dundagel men;<br> And forth they pricked upon Routorr side,<br> As goodly a raid as a king could ride.<br> <br> 		IV.<br> <br> Proud Gwennivar rode like a queen of the land,<br> With page and with squire at her bridle hand;<br> And the twice six knights of the stony ring,<br> They girded and guarded their Cornish king.<br> <br> 		V.<br> <br> Then they halted their steeds at St. Madron's cell:<br> And they stood by the monk of the cloistered well;<br> "Now off with your guantlets," King Arthur he cried,<br> "And glory or shame for our Tamar side."<br> <br> 		VI.<br> <br> 'Twere sooth to sing how Sir Gauvain smiled,<br> When he grasped the waters so soft and mild;<br> How Sir Launcelot dashed the glistening spray<br> O'er the rugged beard of the rough sir Kay.<br> <br> 		VII.<br> <br> Sir Bevis he touched and found no fear:<br> 'Twas a beniteé stoup to Sir Belvidere;<br> How the fountain flashed o'er King Arthur's Queen,<br> Say, Cornish dames, for ye guess the scene.<br> <br> 		VIII.<br> <br> "Now rede me my riddle, Sir Mordred, I pray,<br> My kinsmen, mine ancient, my Bien-aimé;<br> Now rede me my riddle, and rede it aright,<br> Art thou traitorous knave or my trusty knight?"<br> <br> 		IX.<br> <br> He plunged his right arm in the judgement well,<br> It bubbled and boiled like a cauldron of hell:<br> He drew and he lifted his quivering limb,<br> Ha!  Sir Judas, how Madron had sodden him.<br> <br> 		X.<br> <br> Now let Uter Pendragon do what he can,<br> Still the Tamar river will run as it ran:<br> Let king or let kaisar be fond or be fell,<br> Ye may harowe their troth in St. Madron's well.<br>
