 <h3 align="CENTER">L.</h3>
 <h3 align="CENTER">THE THREE QUESTIONS.</h3>
 <p align="CENTER">From the Brothers MacCraw, North Uist, 1859.</p>
 <p>THERE was once, long ago, a scholar; and when he had done learning, his master said that he must now answer three questions, or have his head taken off. The scholar was to have time to make ready, and being in a great fright, he went to a miller who was the master's brother, and asked his aid.</p>
 <p>The miller disguised himself and went instead of the scholar, and the first question put to him was this:--"How many ladders would reach the sky?"</p>
 <p>"Now," said the narrator, "can <i>you</i> answer that?"</p>
 <p>"One, if it were long enough."</p>
 <p>"That's right." The second was:--</p>
 <p>"Where is the middle of the world?"</p>
 <p>So the miller laid down a rod, and he said:--"Here, set a hoop about the world, and thou will find the middle here."</p>
 <p>The third was:--"What is the world's worth?"</p>
 <p>"Well," said the miller, "the Saviour was sold for thirty pieces of silver, I am sure the world is worth no more."</p>
 <p>"Oh," said the brother who was riding beside us, "that's not the way I have heard it. The second was,"</p>
 <p><a name="page_407"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 407</font></a></p>
 <p>"How long will it take to go round the world?" And the miller said:--</p>
 <p>"If I were as swift as the sun and moon, I would run it in twenty-four hours."</p>
 <p>And the third was:--</p>
 <p>"What is my thought?"</p>
 <p>And the miller answered:--</p>
 <p>"I can tell: Thou thinkest that I am thy scholar, but I am thy brother, the miller."</p>
 <p><font size="-1">This was told to me September 1, 1859, in North Uist, as I walked along the road. There are a great many similar wise saws current which are generally fathered on George Buchanan, the tutor of James VI.</font></p>
 <p>The following are a few riddles of the same kind, collected at Gairloch, for Osgood Mackenzie, Esq., by Mr. Donald MacDonald:</p>
 <p>1. Whether is older, the man or the beard?</p>
 <p>The beard is the older, for the work of creation was all finished before the man, and the beard was on the goat before the man was.</p>
 <p>2. What is the wood that is not bent nor straight?</p>
 <p>Sawdust. It is neither bent nor straight.</p>
 <p><font size="-1">This riddle forms part of a very long and curious story which I heard told at Inverary, at Easter, 1859, and which is written down.</font></p>
 <p>3. What is the thing which the Creator never saw, and that kings see but seldom, and that I see every day?</p>
 <p>There is but one Creator, for that he never saw his like. Kings are but scarce, for that they see each other but rarely; but I see my own like every day that I get up,--other sinners like myself.</p>
 <p><font size="-1">The riddle is very well known; but this is another view of it.</font></p>
 <p><a name="page_408"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 408</font></a></p>
 <p>4. There were three soldiers coming home on furlough, and their three wives with them; they came to a river over which there was a ferry, but the boat would take with it but two together. The question is, how did they make the passage, for no one of them would trust his wife with another man, unless he was himself beside her?</p>
 <p>Two women went over first, one went on shore, and the other came back with the boat, and she took the third with her, one of them went back and she stood beside her own husband, and the two husbands of the women who were over went back with the boat; one of them went on shore, and the wife of the man who was in the boat went into her along with him, and they went to the other side. His wife went on shore, and the man who was yonder came in the boat; then the two men went over; then there were three men over, and a woman; this woman took over the other women by the way of one and one; and there seem to be more solutions than one for the problem.</p>
 <p>This puzzle, in various shapes, is well known, <i>e.g.</i> the Fox, the Goose, and the Bag of Corn.</p>

 S o'n nach d' fhuair mi e thug mi leam dachaidh e.<br>
                                     Bior ann an cois.</font></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">40.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>A waveless well, it holds its fill of flesh and blood.<br>
                            A tailor's thimble.</p>
 <p><font size="-1">Tobar gun tonn, cumaidh e làn de dh' fhuil 'a de dh' fheoil.<br>
                                            Meuran tailleir.</font></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">41.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Blacky, blacky, out at the door and a human bone in her mouth.<br>
                                  A shoe on a foot.</p>
 <p><font size="-1">Dubhag, dubhag mach an dorusd 's cnaimh duine 'na beul.<br>
                                            Bròg air cois.</font></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">42.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Red below, black in the middle, and white above.<br>
                   Fire, griddle, and oatcake.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_421"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 421</font></a></p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p><font size="-1">Dearg foidhe, dugh 'na mheadhon, 's geal as a chionn.<br>
                       An gealbhan, a' ghreideal, 'a an t-aran.</font></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">43.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>I can go over on a bridge of glass,<br>
 And I can come over on a bridge of glass,<br>
 And if the glass bridge break,<br>
 There's none in Islay, nor in Eirinn,<br>
 Who can mend the bridge of glass.<br>
                                         Ice.</p>
 <p><font size="-1">Theid mi nunn air drochaid ghloine,<br>
 S thig mi nall air drochaid ghloine<br>
 S ma bhrisdeas an drochaid ghloine<br>
 Cha 'n 'eil an Ile na 'n Eirinn<br>
 Na chàras an drochaid ghloine.<br>
                                    Eitheandach.</font></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">44.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>A brown stag in the hill, and his car on fire.<br>
                                   The gun.</p>
 <p><font size="-1">Damh donn 's a' bheinn 's a chluas ra theinidh.<br>
                                     An gunna.</font></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">45.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>I will go out between two woods,<br>
 And I will come in between two lochs.<br>
                      A pair of pails.</p>
 <p>Theid mi mach eadar dha fhiodh,<br>
 S thig mi stigh eadar dha loch.<br>
                          Na cuinneagan.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">46.</p>
 <p align="CENTER">A green gentlewoman behind the door.<br>
 A broom, usually made of a bunch of some plant.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p><font size="-1">Bean nasal uaine cùl an doruisd.<br>
                            An gais sguabaidh.</font></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_422"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 422</font></a></p>
 <p align="CENTER">47.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Wiggle waggle about the river,<br>
 Iron its head, horse its neck,<br>
 Man its tail.<br>
                     A fishing-rod.</p>
 <p><font size="-1">Driobhal drabhal feadh na h-abhann, iarunn a cheann<br>
 each a mhuineal duin' a thòn.<br>
                                Slat iasgaich.</font></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">48.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>A sharp sharp sheep, and her entrails trailing.<br>
                              A big needle.</p>
 <p><font size="-1">Caora bhiorach bhiorach, 's a mionach slaodadh rithe.<br>
                                    Suathad mhor.</font></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">49.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>A red red sheep, red mad.<br>
                 The tongue.</p>
 <p><font size="-1">Caora dhearg dhearg, air an dearg choitheach.<br>
                                  An teanga.</font></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">50.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>I have a puzzle for thee:<br>
 It isn't thy hair, and it isn't thy locks,<br>
 It isn't a bit of the bits of thy trunk,<br>
 It is upon thee, and thou art no heavier.<br>
        The man's name.--The Gaelic expression being, 'What name is upon thee?"</p>
 <p><font size="-1">Tha toimhseagan agam ort,<br>
 Cha n' c t-fhionna 's cha 'n e t-fhalt,<br>
 Cha n' e ball de bhallaibh do chuirp,<br>
 S tha e ort 's cha truimid thu e.<br>
                                Ainm duine.</font></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_423"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 423</font></a></p>
 <p><font size="-1">Got these puzzles, riddles, or toimseagain, from Flora MacIntyre, and a little girl, Catherine MacArthur, at Ballygrant, twelve years of age.</font></p>
 <p align="CENTER">GLOSSARY.</p>
 <p>AIR AN T-SLIAIBH IS FHAIDE MUIGH, the farthest off hill or mountain.</p>
 <p>BEUM, a piece or bit.</p>
 <p>BLAIGH LIN, linen cloth.</p>
 <p>BREUNAGAN; this word may mean every filthy piece of ground over which the sickle passes.</p>
 <p>CATHADH, gen. of càith, corn seeds.</p>
 <p>CIR, the fore-part of the jams.</p>
 <p>COITILEAN, a garment somewhat of one piece, serving as the whole clothes; or perhaps a little boat or skiff, which suggests the form of the rainbow.</p>
 <p>CAS, fast.</p>
 <p>CUIRNEANACH, curled in ringlets.</p>
 <p>FHILE or Ile, or perhaps eibheal or eibhle, an ember.</p>
 <p>IONACHAG may be aonachag, from aon, a solitary little thing.</p>
 <p>LOMACHAG, a bareness, from lom.</p>
 <p>LOS, power of destruction.</p>
 <p>It will be observed that these riddles are all of a peculiar kind, such as the well known--</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Polly with a white pettiecoat and a red nose,<br>
 The longer she stands, the shorter she grows."</p></dir>
