 <p><a name="page_447"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 447</font></a></p>  <h1 align="center">TABLE OF PRONUNCIATION FOR THE MORE DIFFICULT WORDS</h1>  
 <p>A. Stress is of four kinds, and marked here:</p>  
 <p>(1) - (weak stress).</p>  
 <p>(2) : (medium).</p>  
 <p>(3) ' (strong).</p>  
 <p>(4) ; (emphatic).</p>  
 <p>B. Cymric words of more than one syllable have the stress regularly on the antepenult (although there are some words with the stress on the last; and the negative prefix <i>an</i> often takes full stress).</p>  
 <p>Cymric wy = ui; ðð = th in English <i>th</i>en; ll (łł) = a broad buzzed l, <i>i.e.</i> like the French l unvoiced, sounds like <i>hl</i>, and is unilateral (right side) with the tongue in the <i>i</i> position. The thorn letter þ = voiceless sound as in English wi<i>th</i>.</p>  
 <p>C. Nasality of vowels marked with ( ̨) next to the vowel.</p>  
 <p>Interdental (ṭ, ḍ, ḷ, ṇ) marked with dot underneath.</p>  
 <p>l mouillé, <i>i.e.</i> palatal, marked ł.</p>  
 <p>n <font size="-1" color="green">palatal</font> marked ñ (cf. Colo<i>gn</i>e).</p>  
 <p>ɛ = open <i>e</i>; e = close e; ɔ = o open; o = o close.</p>  
 <p>r = thin <i>r</i> (<i>i.e.</i> r after e, i).</p>  
 <p>œ = the mid-front narrow round (close); cf. German ö.</p>  
 <p>ə = mid-mixed indefinite sound at end of unstressed syllables, often alternating with mid-back (close) vowel a, which is the sound of u in Gaelic ag<i>u</i>s; the <i>a</i> has the sound of <i>a</i> in f<i>a</i>ther, but it occurs both long and short. Doubling of vowels indicates length. a is a more obscure sound.</p>  
 <p>ï = high-mixed open <i>i</i>; ĭ = high-front open <i>i</i>; i itself is in Gaelic a high-front narrow, <i>i.e.</i> close sound.</p>  
 <p>For basis cf. H. Sweet's <i>Phonetics</i>.</p>  
 <p>ʤ approximately like <i>dg</i> in English judge.</p>  
 <p>ʧ (<i>tch</i>), the voiceless sound of preceding.</p>  
 <p>pb is to indicate the peculiar quality of Gaelic initial <i>b</i>, which begins voiceless and ends voiced.</p>  
 <p>h stands for breath-glide, on or off, according as it precedes or follows the consonant concerned.</p>  
 <p>ç like ch in standard pronunciation of German i<i>ch</i>, "I". It is the forward palatal position of the guttural <i>ch</i>, here marked <i>x</i>. It is in Gaelic associated with thin or clear vowels (<i>i.e.</i> e, i).</p>  
 <p>c = forward palatal position of <i>k</i>.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_448"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 448</font></a></p>  
 <p>ɣ = voiced form of the voiceless x: it is ordinarily written <i>gh</i>, while the voiceless sound is written <i>ch</i> in the ordinary script. In the later form of Mag (a plain) it passes into <i>gh</i>, <i>i.e.</i> Ma<i>gh</i>, and is often but feebly felt, in which cases it is Moy in place names, c.f. Moylena.</p>
