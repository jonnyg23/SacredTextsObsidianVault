 <h3 align="CENTER">THE LAY OF YEEARMAID.</h3>
 <h3 align="CENTER">NO. 3.</h3>
 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1">I have tried to spell the names so as to convey some idea of the sound of the Gaelic words to English minds.--J. F. C.</font></p>
 <p align="CENTER">1.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>HEARKEN a space if you wish a lay<br>
 Of the tribe that from us are gone,<br>
 Of MacCooal and of the Feen,<br>
 And of the prince there's a woeful song.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">2.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Going to Vein Goolban to hunt<br>
 The boar that the weak arms cannot wound,<br>
 That venomous boar, and he so fierce<br>
 That Gray eyebrows had with her herd of swine.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">3.</p>
 <p align="CENTER">GRAINE.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Oh Yeearmaid slip not the hounds,<br>
 And trust not but this is a party of guile,<br>
 For it is hard to withstand MacCooal,<br>
 And he is in sorrow deprived of a wife."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_75"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 75</font></a></p>
 <p align="CENTER">LAOIDH DHIARMAID.</p>
 <p><font size="-1">The MS. came to me without division into verses, I have so divided it, being nearly certain that I am right. The people talk of "ceathrannan," quatrains, and the metre is clear, and fits the music.--J. F. C.</font></p>
 <p align="CENTER">1</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Eisdibh beag ma <a href="pt309.htm#xref_87-1"><font color="GREEN" size="1">87-1</font></a> 's aill leibh laoidh,<br>
 Air a' mhuinntir a dh' fhalbh uainn;<br>
 Air MacChumhail, 's air an Fhinn,<br>
 'S air mac an righ, gu 'm bheil sgial truagh.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">2</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Dol a Bheinn Ghulbann a shealg,<br>
 An tuirc nach dearg na h-airm chli; <a href="pt309.htm#xref_87-2"><font color="GREEN" size="1">87-2</font></a><br>
 'S e 'n torc nimhe, 's e ro gharg, <a href="pt309.htm#xref_87-3"><font color="GREEN" size="1">87-3</font></a><br>
 Bh' aig Mala liath <a href="pt309.htm#xref_87-4"><font color="GREEN" size="1">87-4</font></a> aig sealbh mhuc.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">3</p>
 <p align="CENTER">GRAIDHNE.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"A Dhiarmaid na leig na gadhair<br>
 'S na creid nach i 'n fhaghaid bhreige;  <a href="pt309.htm#xref_87-5"><font color="GREEN" size="1">87-5</font></a><br>
 'S gur deacair cur ri Mac Chumhail<br>
 A 's cumha leis 'bhith gun chéile."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_76"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 76</font></a></p>
 <p align="CENTER">4.</p>
 <p align="CENTER">DJEEARMAID.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"O Graine wilt thou not keep still,<br>
 And for thy first love earn not shame,<br>
 I would not let slip my share of the hunt,<br>
 For all the wrath of the men of the Feene."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">5.</p>
 <p align="CENTER">GRAINE.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Son of O Duine, valiant chief,<br>
 Since exploits were done through thee,<br>
 Be thou mindful of thy hand,<br>
 Here is the death to be shunned by thee."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">6.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>The monster awakened out of the sleep,<br>
 She went round about the glen,<br>
 And when she heard the din of the Feean,<br>
 East and west she turned her head.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">7.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>The well Skilled Shield withstood her then,<br>
 And the spear went into the belly of the boar,<br>
 It broke in the midst of the shaft again,<br>
 And the toughest head was upon the hog.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">8.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>The ancient blade was drawn from sheath,<br>
 With which each battlefield was won,<br>
 The son of king Guyne slew the beast,<br>
 And he withdrew himself whole again.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_77"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 77</font></a></p>
 <p align="CENTER">4</p>
 <p align="CENTER">DIARMAID.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"A Ghraidhne nach fuirich thu samhach<br>
 'S na coisinn naire dho d' cheud-ghaol;<br>
 Cha leiginn-sa mo chuid de 'n t-seilg<br>
 Air son feirge fir na Finne."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">5</p>
 <p align="CENTER">GRAIDHNE.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>A mhic O Duibhne, a fhlaith threin, <a href="pt309.htm#xref_87-6"><font color="GREEN" size="1">87-6</font></a><br>
 Nis o rinneadh euchdan <a href="pt309.htm#xref_87-7"><font color="GREEN" size="1">87-7</font></a> leat<br>
 Bi-sa cuimhneach air do laimh;<br>
 Seo an t-eug o 'n tearnar leat." <a href="pt309.htm#xref_87-8"><font color="GREEN" size="1">87-8</font></a></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">6</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Dhuisg an ulla-bheist <a href="pt309.htm#xref_87-9"><font color="GREEN" size="1">87-9</font></a> as an t-suain<br>
 Chaidh i mu chuairt air a' ghleann,<br>
 'L nuair chual i farum nam Fiann<br>
 Thug i 'n ear 's an iar a ceann.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">7</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Chaidh an sgiath urla <a href="pt309.htm#xref_87-10"><font color="GREEN" size="1">87-10</font></a> 'na dāil<br>
 'S Chaidh an t-sleagh an tar <a href="pt309.htm#xref_87-11"><font color="GREEN" size="1">87-11</font></a> an tuirc;<br>
 Bhrisd i eadar <a href="pt309.htm#xref_87-12"><font color="GREEN" size="1">87-12</font></a> an crann a rìs<br>
 'S bha'n ceann bu righn' air a' mhuic.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">8</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Thairneadh an t-seann lann a truaill<br>
 Leis am buinichte buaidh gach blair;<br>
 Mharbh mac righ Dhuibhn' a' bhéist;<br>
 Thiarainn <a href="pt309.htm#xref_88-13"><font color="GREEN" size="1">88-13</font></a> e fhin 'na déigh slan.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_78"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 78</font></a></p>
 <p align="CENTER">9.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>We sent the white hog to Leodrum,<br>
 And wishful we were to go to spoil. ( <a href="pt309.htm#xref_86-a"><font color="GREEN" size="1">86-a</font></a>)<br>
       .      .      .      .      .<br>
       .      .      .      .      .</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">10</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>The fairy glen and the glen by its side,<br>
 Where used to be voice of hero and bird,<br>
 Where was the hoarse sound of the Feean<br>
 Upon the mountain after their hounds.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">11.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>But anger settled on Fiun of the Feean,<br>
 And he sat moodily on the mound,<br>
 About Mac O Duine of the mighty arms,<br>
 It was torture that he came whole from the boar.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">12.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>After he had been silent a while<br>
 He spoke, and it was ill to say,<br>
 "Oh Yeearmaid, measure the boar,<br>
 How many feet from his snout to his tail?"</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">13.</p>
 <p align="CENTER">DJEEARMAID.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Let us take thy counsel, Een,<br>
 Since it was grievous coming from home;"<br>
 He measured the boar on his back,<br>
 King Guyne's son of the rounded foot.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">14.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Fifteen feet of the measure good<br>
 Are in the back of the wild swine;" ( <a href="pt309.htm#xref_86-b"><font color="GREEN" size="1">86-b</font></a>)<br>
       .      .      .      .      .<br>
       .      .      .      .      .</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_79"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 79</font></a></p>
 <p align="CENTER">9</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p> <a href="pt309.htm#xref_88-14"><font color="GREEN" size="1">88-14</font></a> Chuir sinn a' mhuc bhàn do Leodrum<br>
 'S bu deoSach sinn a dhol a 'reubadh;<br>
       .      .      .      .      .<br>
       .      .      .      .      .</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">10</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>An gleann sith, 'san gleann ri 'thaobh,<br>
 Far am biodh guth laoich a's loin;<br>
 Far am biodh torman nam Fiann<br>
 Air an t-sliabh an deigh nan con.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">11</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Gu 'n d' luidh sprochd air Fionn nam Fiann,<br>
 'S shuidh e gu cian air a' chnoc, <a href="pt309.htm#xref_88-15"><font color="GREEN" size="1">88-15</font></a><br>
 Mu mhac O Duibhne nan arm aidh<br>
 Bu chràiteach leis tighinn slan o 'n torc.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">12</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>A chionn e bhith treis 'na thosd <a href="pt309.htm#xref_88-16"><font color="GREEN" size="1">88-16</font></a><br>
 Labhair e, 's gu'm b' ole ri 'radh,<br>
 "A Dhiarmaid tomhais an torc<br>
 Co mhiad troidh o 'shoc g'a earr."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">13</p>
 <p align="CENTER">DIARMAID.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Gabham-sa do chomhairl' Fhinn<br>
 O b' aithreach leinn tigh'n on taigh."<br>
 Thomhais e'n torc air a dhriom;<br>
 Mac righ Dhuibhn' a bu chruinn troidh. <a href="pt309.htm#xref_88-17"><font color="GREEN" size="1">88-17</font></a></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">14</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Coig troidhe deug de'n deagh thomhas<br>
 Ann an driom na muice fiadhain." <a href="pt309.htm#xref_88-18"><font color="GREEN" size="1">88-18</font></a><br>
       .      .      .      .      .<br>
       .      .      .      .      .</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_80"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 80</font></a></p>
 <p align="CENTER">15.</p>
 <p align="CENTER">FIUN.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"That is not his measure at all,<br>
 Measure him again, Oh Yeearmaid.<br>
 Against his bristles measure him,<br>
 And thou get'st each jewel ( <a href="pt309.htm#xref_86-c"><font color="GREEN" size="1">86-c</font></a>) of a warrior proud"--</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">16.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Oh, Yeearmaid, measure again,<br>
 Softly against (the hair) the boar,<br>
 And thou shalt get thy prayer from the king,<br>
 Thy choice of the tough keen spears."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">17.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>He rose, and that was no journey of joy,<br>
 And, as he measured the boar for them,<br>
 The venomous spike of agony pierced<br>
 The heel of the hero not mild in strife.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">18.</p>
 <p align="CENTER">DJEEARMAID.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Give a draught from thy palms, Oh Een,<br>
 Son of my king for my succour,<br>
 For my life and my dwelling."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">FIUN.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Ochon! Oh king it is I that will not."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">19.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"I will not give to thee a draught,<br>
 And neither shall it quench thy thirst,<br>
 And never hast thou done me good,<br>
 That thou hast not helped my ruin."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_81"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 81</font></a></p>
 <p align="CENTER">15</p>
 <p align="CENTER">FIONN.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Cha'n e sin idir a thomhas,<br>
 Tomhais e rithis a Dhiarmaid.<br>
 Tomhais e 'n aghaidh a' chuilg,<br>
 'S gheibh thu 'laoich bhuirb gach seud."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">16</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"A Dhiarmaid tomhais a ris,<br>
 'Na aghaidh, gu min, an torc,<br>
 'S gheibh thu t' achanaich o'n righ,--<br>
 Rogha nan sleagh ruighinn goirt. <a href="pt309.htm#xref_88-19"><font color="GREEN" size="1">88-19</font></a></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">17</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Dh' eirich, 's cha b'e 'n turas aidh,<br>
 'S gu 'n do thomhais e dhaidh an torc;<br>
 Chaidh am bior nimh 'bu mhor cradh,<br>
 An sàil an laoich nach tlath 'san trod. <a href="pt309.htm#xref_88-20"><font color="GREEN" size="1">88-20</font></a></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">18</p>
 <p align="CENTER">DIARMAID.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Thoir deoch bhar do bhasan, Fhinn,<br>
  <a href="pt309.htm#xref_89-21"><font color="GREEN" size="1">89-21</font></a>A mhic mo righ, gus mo chobhair,<br>
  <a href="pt309.htm#xref_89-22"><font color="GREEN" size="1">89-22</font></a>Air son mo bhidh agus m' aodaich."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">FIONN.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Ochon, a righ, 's mi nach d' thobhair."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">19</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Cha d' thoir mise dhuitse deoch,<br>
 'S cha mhò a chaisgeas dhe t' iota;<br>
 'S cha d' rinn thu riamh dho m' leas<br>
 Nach do leasaich thu dho m' aimhleas."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_82"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 82</font></a></p>
 <p align="CENTER">20.</p>
 <p align="CENTER">DJEEARMAID.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"I have never done ill to thee,<br>
 Hither nor thither, nor east nor west<br>
 But travelling with Graine in a yoke,<br>
 While she planned to bring me under spells.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">21.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"In the rowan tree dwelling, and thou in straits,<br>
 Bold was I for thy succour;<br>
 At the time when death was upon thee,<br>
 It was I that went joyously to thee.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">22.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Een, 'tis to thee that my succour was good;<br>
 Hast mind of the day of the combat of Conal?<br>
 The Cairbre and his tribe was before thee,<br>
 And I was behind thee to help thee."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">23.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"How wretched my face towards Bein Goolban!<br>
 On another day was I good for thee;<br>
 When the white tooth was hitting thee,<br>
 I turned upon her from behind thee.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">24.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"And I succoured thee in that time!<br>
 If the women of the Feene should hear<br>
 That I was wounded on this ridge,<br>
 Dejected would be their faces.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">25.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>How wretched my face towards Bein Goolban! ( <a href="pt309.htm#xref_86-d"><font color="GREEN" size="1">86-d</font></a>)<br>
       .      .      .      .      .<br>
       .      .      .      .      .<br>
       .      .      .      .      .</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_83"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 83</font></a></p>
 <p align="CENTER">20</p>
 <p align="CENTER">DIARMAID. <a href="pt309.htm#xref_89-23"><font color="GREEN" size="1">89-23</font></a></p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Cha do rinn mis' ortsa cron<br>
 Thall, na bhos, an ear, na 'n iar;<br>
 Ach imeachd le Graidhn' ann am braid;<br>
 'S i tur gam thobhairt fo gheasaibh.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">21</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"S a' Bhruighin chaorainn, 's tu nad' chàs,<br>
 Bu dana mise gad' chobhair;<br>
 'S nuair a bha 'n t-eug air do mhuin<br>
 'S mis' a chaidh thugad le meodhail.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">22</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Fhinn 's ann duit bu mhath mo chobhair;<br>
 An cuimhneach leat latha cath Chonaill?<br>
 Bha 'n Cairbre romhad 's a mhuinntir?<br>
 'S bha mis' as do dheigh gad' chobhair!</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">23</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"S gur truagh m' aghaidh ri Beinn Ghulbann!<br>
 Lath' eile bu mhath dhuit mi<br>
 Nuair a bha 'n Deud-gheal gad' bhualadh;<br>
 Thionndaidh mi orr' as do dheaghainn,</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">24</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"S chobhair mi ort anns an uair sin!<br>
 Na 'n cluinneadh mnathan na Finne<br>
 Mis' a bhith leont' air an driom seo,<br>
 Bu tursach a bhiodh an adhart!</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">25</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"S gur truagh m' aghaidh ri Beinn Ghulbann!<br>
       .      .      .      .      .<br>
       .      .      .      .      .<br>
       .      .      .      .      .</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_84"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 84</font></a></p>
 <p align="CENTER">26.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"How wretched my face towards Bein Goolban!<br>
 As I pour in a flood forth my vigour:<br>
 And though I be the son of O Duine,<br>
 Farewell be to courting for ever."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">27.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>There was Djeearmaid on the hillock,<br>
 Heavy the hair was and fine,<br>
 And he ever losing the (life) blood<br>
 From the wound of the spike in his sole.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">28.</p>
 <p align="CENTER">FIUN.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Poor is the counsel that grew with me<br>
 To slay the son of my sister;<br>
 For Graine that ill of a woman<br>
 That never again will make my joy.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">29.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>That is great the loss on the hillock,<br>
 For the price of the wild swine,<br>
 Graine king's daughter of Coig Ullain,<br>
 Djeearmaid and the two hare hounds.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">30.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Djeearmaid, deceiver of women,<br>
 Son of king Guyne of the brilliant hue,<br>
 No maiden will raise her eye<br>
 Since the mould has gone over thy visage fair."</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">31.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Yellow without white in thy hair!<br>
 Long thy cheek! thine eyelash slow!<br>
 Blue without rashness in thine eye!<br>
 Passion ( <a href="pt309.htm#xref_86-e"><font color="GREEN" size="1">86-e</font></a>) and beauty behind thy curls!</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p><a name="page_85"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 85</font></a></p>
 <p align="CENTER">26</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Gur truagh m' aghaidh ri Beinn Ghulbann<br>
 'S mi ar tuil-bhruchdadh mo nearta!<br>
 'S gad a's mise mac O Duibhne.<br>
 Soraidh leis an t-suiridh am feasda!"</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">27</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Gu robh Diarmaid, air an tulaich,<br>
 Bu ghrinn am falt a's bu trom;<br>
 'S e ri sior chall na fala,<br>
 Le lot a bhior, air a bhonn.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">28</p>
 <p align="CENTER">FIONN.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"S bochd a' chomhairl' a chinn agam,<br>
 Mac mo pheathar a mharbhadh<br>
 Airson Ghraidhn', an t-olcas mnatha,<br>
 'S nach dean i rithis mo leanmhuinn.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">29</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"S mor sin a tha dh' easbhuidh na tulaich!<br>
 Air tàillibh na muice fladhain,<br>
 Graidhne, nighean righ Choig' Ullainn,<br>
 An da ghearr chuilean, agus Diarmaid. <a href="pt309.htm#xref_89-24"><font color="GREEN" size="1">89-24</font></a></p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p align="CENTER">30</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>"Diarmaid, fear mhealladh nam ban,--<br>
 Mac righ Dhuibhn' a bu ghlan snuadh!<br>
 Suireadh cha tog a suil <a href="pt309.htm#xref_89-25"><font color="GREEN" size="1">89-25</font></a><br>

 <p align="CENTER">NOTES--NUMBERS.--H. M<font size="-1">AC</font>LEAN.</p>
 <p><font size="-1"><a name="xref_87-1"><font color="GREEN">87-1</font></a> Laoidh, lay, ode, lyric; it differs from dan a poem, in being more melodious, and capable of being sung. It narrates rapidly a few events ending tragically, almost invariably the death of a hero.</font></p>
 <p><a name="xref_87-2"><font color="GREEN">87-2</font></a> Cli, weak, powerless. Duine gun chli, a man without strength. Airm chli, feeble weapons.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_87-3"><font color="GREEN">87-3</font></a> Garg, fierce.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_87-4"><font color="GREEN">87-4</font></a> Mala liath. The reciters pronounce this name Mala lìth, but the Irish pronounce ia, ee, so that the name means gray eyebrow, the old woman who owned the venomous boar and the swine, "aig sealbh mhuc," guarding her swine (herd of swine).</p>
 <p><a name="xref_87-5"><font color="GREEN">87-5</font></a> Faghaid bhreige, a lying hunting party, that is got up to deceive and destroy him.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_87-6"><font color="GREEN">87-6</font></a> Brave hero. Here the vocative is elegantly used.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_87-7"><font color="GREEN">87-7</font></a> Euchdan, exploits.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_87-8"><font color="GREEN">87-8</font></a> This is the death which you require to avoid.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_87-9"><font color="GREEN">87-9</font></a> A monster (feminine in Gaelic).</p>
 <p><a name="xref_87-10"><font color="GREEN">87-10</font></a> Sgiath urla or urlaimh. Expert shield, a name for Diarmid, from his adroitness in the use of the shield.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_87-11"><font color="GREEN">87-11</font></a> Tar, the belly.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_87-12"><font color="GREEN">87-12</font></a> Eadar an crann. Here eadar is used in a sense not common</p>
 <p><a name="page_88"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 88</font></a></p>
 <p><font size="-1">now; between the shaft, that is in the shaft, not between the shaft and the head.</font></p>
 <p><a name="xref_88-13"><font color="GREEN">88-13</font></a> Tèarainn, to come off without hurt.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_88-14"><font color="GREEN">88-14</font></a> This stanza is not known to all the reciters, given by Donald M'Phie, Breubhaig, Barra.</p><dir>
 <dir>
 
 <p>Gleann mo chridhe an gleann seo ri m' thaobh,<br>
 Far am binn guth laoigh a's loin;<br>
 Far am bi farum Dam Fiann,<br>
 Air an t-sliabh an deigh nan con.</p>
 <p>My heart's glen, this glen by my side,<br>
 Where sweet is the voice of calf and bird;<br>
 Where is the murmur of the Fean,<br>
 On the mountain side after their hounds.</p></dir>
 </dir>
 
 <p>(This is almost the same as a verse of an old manuscript poem, now in course of publication by Messrs. Edmonston and Douglas, 1861.--J. F. C.)</p>
 <p><a name="xref_88-15"><font color="GREEN">88-15</font></a> These are the lines according to Allan M'Phie, and Janet Currie, South Uist.</p>
 <p>"Gu 'n d' shuidh e siar air a' chnoc," according to some; That he sat west on the knoll.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_88-16"><font color="GREEN">88-16</font></a> A chionn e bhith treis na thosd. After, or at the end of his being a while silent.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_88-17"><font color="GREEN">88-17</font></a> Bu trom troidh, of heavy tread. This is the version used by most of them, and they explain it as referring to the warrior's strong firm step, and the largeness and strength of the leg; however, I have inserted bu chruinn troidh, which implies a well formed or fine foot, which is Boyd's version.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_88-18"><font color="GREEN">88-18</font></a> Fiadhain, more poetic than fiadhaich.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_88-19"><font color="GREEN">88-19</font></a> This is Janet Currie's version of the line, which I think is best. Allan M 'Phie gives,</p>
 <p>"Urram nan slighne ghear goirt;" a pretty line also, "The honour of the sharp keen spear."</p>
 <p>A great many give "Taghadh nan sleagh ruighinn, geur, goirt," Choice of the keen, tough, sharp spears, which I think is inferior in poetic merit to the other two.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_88-20"><font color="GREEN">88-20</font></a> Nach tlath 'san trod. That is, "not soft in fight." Here the poet very beautifully, in an abrupt manner, turns off to the present tense, so as to produce a vivid impression of the hero's great bravery on the mind of the hearer.</p>
 <p><a name="page_89"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 89</font></a></p>
 <p><font size="-1"><a name="xref_89-21"><font color="GREEN">89-21</font></a> "A dhealbhaich mo righ," Boyd.</font></p>
 <p><a name="xref_89-22"><font color="GREEN">89-22</font></a> "Air soil mo bheath' agus m' fhardoich," Boyd.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_89-23"><font color="GREEN">89-23</font></a> This beautiful dying speech of Diarmid is not known so full as this to any of the reciters, except to Donald M'Phie, Breubhaig.</p>
 <p>Cobhair is repeated here often, from the exasperated feeling at the ingratitude of the uncle. Such repetition is always the language of deep passion, as for instance in the case of Coriolanus in his reply, when called "thou boy of tears."</p>
 <p>The repetition of this line, and the abruptness with which he turns off from other subjects, is very pathetic. Whoever has been in the company of the dying can feel the truth of this line, how they refer constantly to some of their favourite haunts. The ruling passion, the last, a favourite theme with modern philosophers and novelists, is here finely illustrated.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_89-24"><font color="GREEN">89-24</font></a> "A dealbh-chuilean." This is the expression given by Janet Currie, who says it refers to the unborn child; Graidhne, according to her being with child to the Ciuthach. Boyd has da dhearbh chuilean. Macdonald gives the words inserted, who says they refer to <a href="errata.htm#4">the</a> two best dogs of the Feen, after Bran, which were killed by the boar.</p>
 <p><a name="xref_89-25"><font color="GREEN">89-25</font></a> Suireadh, a maid. "Suireadh cha tog a suil," no maid will raise her eye; that is with grief. The line is repeated no doubt in a corrupt manner, thus--</p>
 <p>"Suireach cha tog a shuil" no lover will raise his eye, which would make no sense.</p>
 <p>Leac, cheek. Rasg, eyelash, also the eye itself.</p>
 <p>"Guirme gun ghlaise," and "guirme le ghlaise." Janet Currie gives the line inserted, which is prettier, and at the same time a contrast to the piercing blue eye ascribed by Tacitus to the ancient Germans. Bulwer, in one of his novels, describes the French dark eye as milder and softer than the Italian.</p>
 <p>Cleachd, a ringlet.</p>
 <p>Cùis a's mais' air chul nan gleachd. Boyd.</p>
 <p>The poem is not the complete version of one reciter, but is built up and selected from several long versions, written from the dictation of the people named. If the evil spirit of the Ossianic controversy still cumbers the earth, the papers can be produced, and the authorities are alive. I will answer for the honest intention of the collector and the translator, and I can do no more.</p>
