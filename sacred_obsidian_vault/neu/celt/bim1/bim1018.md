 <p align="center"><a name="img_01100"></a>
 <img src="img/01100.jpg" alt="Symbol."></p>  <h3 align="center">THE ORIGIN OF LETTERS, ROLL, AND PAPER.--THE VIRTUE OF LETTERS.</h3>  
 <p>MAY it please your information, my beloved teacher; pray, tell me who was the first that made a Letter?</p>  
 <p>Einiged the Giant, son of Huon, son of Alser, <a name="fr_41"></a><a href="#fn_41"><font size="1">1</font></a> son of Javan, <a name="fr_42"></a><a href="#fn_42"><font size="1">2</font></a> son of Japheth, son of Noah the Aged, after the death of his father, for the purpose of preserving a memorial of what he did, and of his praiseworthy actions, warranted in respect of credibility and information. And because it was on wood (pren) that such belief was first placed, both the letters, and what they were inscribed on, were called <i>Coelbren</i>. <a name="fr_43"></a><a href="#fn_43"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_12"><font size="1" color="green">p. 12</font></a> <a name="page_13"><font size="1" color="green">p. 13</font></a></p>  
 <p>Who was the first that made a Roll <a name="fr_44"></a><a href="#fn_44"><font size="1">1</font></a> in connection with letters?</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_14"><font size="1" color="green">p. 14</font></a> <a name="page_15"><font size="1" color="green">p. 15</font></a></p>  
 <p>Bran the Blessed, <a name="fr_45"></a><a href="#fn_45"><font size="1">1</font></a> son of Llyr of Defective Speech, learned that mode at Rome, and brought it with him to Britain, where he taught it to the Cymry, as well as the manner of dressing the skins of kids and goats, so as to be suitable for written letters. And that mode became customary, so that the Bards alone practised, as it were by bare rescue, the old style of inscribing letters on wood, for the purpose of preserving the memorials of the old and primitive sciences of the nation of the Cymry; thence it came to be called <i>Coelbren of the Bards</i>. At present there are only the Bards that keep it in memory, by engraving their songs and records on wood, according to the ancient art, with the view of preserving in reliable memory the primitive sciences of the nation of the Cymry.</p>  
 <p>Who was the first that made paper?</p>  
 <p>A man from Constantinople, named Moran; he ground flax, which on its being thinly spread out, became paper.</p>  
 <p>What is the virtue of letters?</p>  
 <p>They are mute organs that speak--a body without a soul, and without life, guiding thought--dead ones, knowing more than the living--a hand speaking better than the tongue--an eye hearing better than the ear, without either noise or sound--speech without a tongue--hearing without an ear--</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_16"><font size="1" color="green">p. 16</font></a> <a name="page_17"><font size="1" color="green">p. 17</font></a></p>  
 <p>language without words--form of voice--a messenger uttering the truth, without knowing it--the dead teaching the living--memory with no one guiding it--the understanding of the dead--the principal skill of the art of the living--the preservation of all arts and sciences--and the demonstration of all that is demonstrable.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_41"></a><a href="bim1018.htm#fr_41">11:1</a> <a name="page_10"><font size="1" color="green">p. 10</font></a> Probably the same as Elishah, in Gen. x. 4.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_42"></a><a href="bim1018.htm#fr_42">11:2</a> It is remarkable that, contrary to the popular notion which represents Gomer as the progenitor of the Cymry, Nennius, the Genealogy of Gruffydd ab Cynan, in the 2nd volume of the Myvyrian Archaiology--and other Pedigrees registered by Lewis Dwnn, all support the view of the text as to the descent of that people from Javan. Nennius, indeed, asserts positively that his information was derived "ex traditione veterum, qui incolæ in primo fuerunt Britanniæ."</p>
 <p><a name="fn_43"></a><a href="bim1018.htm#fr_43">11:3</a> That is, <i>wood of credibility</i>. The ancient mode of cutting letters on wood is frequently alluded to in the poems of the Bards, both early and medieval. Thus;</p>

 <p>A long string of similar quotations might be adduced, but the foregoing are sufficient to show that the practice in question was known to the Bards from the 6th down to the 17th century.</p>  
 <p>It may be observed that several words in the Cymric language, which relate to knowledge or literature, have a primary reference to wood. Thus; <i>arwydd</i>, a sign; <i>cyfarwydd</i>, skilful; <i>cyfarwyddyd</i>, information; <i>cywydd</i>, a species of versification, also revelation; <i>dedwydd</i>, having recovered knowledge, happy; <i>derwydd</i>, a Druid; <i>egwyddawr</i>, a rudiment, an alphabet; <i>gwgddawr</i>, a rudiment; <i>gwyddon</i>, a man of science; <i>gwynwyddigion</i>, men of sacred knowledge.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_44"></a><a href="bim1018.htm#fr_44">13:1</a> Though Roll, as in the text, primarily refers to the schedule that was turned up with the hand in the form of a pipe, it came also to denote a system, or arrangement, as in the phraseologies, <i>Rhol y Crythor</i>, <i>Rhol y Telynor</i>, <i>Rhol y Mesurau</i>, <i>Rhol Iolo Goch</i>, <i>Rhol Achau</i>, <i>Rhol Cof a Chyfrif</i>, and <i>Rhol y Beirdd</i>. It is alluded to by many of the Bards; thus--</p>  
 <p align="center">DAVYDD AB GWILYM, 1300-1368.</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">Bydd yr un <i>Rhol</i> ag Iolo <br> Ddefod hardd, hen fardd y fro.  
 <p>He will be of the same <i>Roll</i> as Iolo,<br> Fair usage--the old Bard of the district.</p></div>  
 <p align="center">GRUFFYDD AB IEUAN AB LLYWELYN VYCHAN, 1470.</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">Y rhai na wyppont eu <i>Rhol</i><br> Yu csgud aent i ysgol. <a name="page_14"><font size="1" color="green">p. 14</font></a>  
 <p>They who know not their <i>Roll</i>,<br> Let them quickly go to school.</p></div>  
 <p align="center">HUW AP DAVYDD AP LLYWELYN AP MADAWC, 1480-1520.</p>  
 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">Goreuro <i>Rhol</i> geiriau rhawg,<br> <i>Grafio</i> dadl gref odidawg.  
 <p>He gilt a <i>Roll</i> of long words,<br> He <i>engraved</i> a controversy, strong and excellent.<br>                                           Mar. Tudur Aled.</p></div>

 <p>The degrees and Roll of <i>wood-knowledge</i>,<br> The root of sciences, for the weaving of a song of praise.<br>                                   Mar. Gwilym ap Ieuan Hen.</p></div>

 <p></p><div style="margin-left: 32px">Ai <i>Rhol</i> achau rhy lychwin.  
 <p>And their <i>Roll</i> of pedigrees, too much covered with mould.</p></div>
