Williams Ab Ithel, [1862], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">CYTHRAUL. <a name="fr_218"></a><a href="#fn_217"><font size="1">2</font></a></h3>  
 <p>There are three benefits to be had from Cythraul: the defection of evil; a view to goodness; and the triumph of victory over what is contrary to the beneficial.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_217"></a><a href="bim1088.htm#fr_218">213:2</a> <a name="page_212"><font size="1" color="green">p. 212</font></a> Bardism recognises another principle of evil, which is called "DERA," respecting which Iolo Morganwg has the following note:--"The old Bardic idea was that the DERA usurped the occupation of Cylch y Ceugant, and that he would suffer none but himself to occupy it, destroying all other beings;--that God, being more powerful, is able to dispossess him, and, with Himself to occupy it, creates beings highly susceptible of happiness, to enjoy it with Him to all <a name="page_213"><font size="1" color="green">p. 213</font></a> eternity, on condition of joining with Him to resist evil. The common proverb is hence derived--'Myn y diawl y cwbl iddo ei hunan, fal y cybydd: mae Duw, fal yr haul, yn rhoi than i bob un arall.'"</p>
