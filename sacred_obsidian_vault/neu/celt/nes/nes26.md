 <p><a name="page_169"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 169</font></a></p>  <h1 align="center">CHAPTER XXIII.</h1>  <h3 align="center">COUNTINGS-OUT.</h3>  
 <p><a name="img_qcinit"></a>
 </p><table align="LEFT"><tr><td align="CENTER" valign="CENTER"><img src="img/qcinit.jpg" alt="C"></td></tr></table>OUNTINGS-OUT" form a curious item of folklore and seem to be common among different nations in a variety of forms. The following have been collected in Banffshire and Aberdeenshire. Those who wish to look more into this subject may be referred to a paper on the "Anglo-Cymric Score" in the Transactions of the Philological Society, London (volume for 1877-79, pp. 316-372), by Mr. Ellis, one of the vice-presidents of the society. The following were in use in Fraserburgh:--  
 <p>The following were in use in Fraserburgh:--</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"Eenrie, twaarie, tickerie, teven,<br> Allaby, crockery, ten, or elaiven,<br> Peen, pan, fusky dam,<br> Wheedlum, whadlum, twenty-one."  
 <p>"Eetum, peetum, penny pump,<br> A the laadies in a lump;<br> Sax or saiven in a clew,<br> A made wi candy glue."</p>  
 <p>"One, two, three, four,<br> Tack a mell an ding im our."</p>  
 <p>"One, two, three, four, five, six, siven,<br> A that fisher dodds widna win t haven."</p>  
 <p>"Eerinnges, oranges, two for a penny,<br> Am a good scholar for coontin so many."</p>  
 <p>"Ink, pink,<br> Penny, stink."</p>  
 <p>"Hetum, petum, penny pie,<br> Pop a lorie, jinkie, jye.<br> An, tan, toap,<br> Stall ye oot by<br> For a bonny penny pie."</p></font></dir>  
 <p><a name="page_170"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 170</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">Anerie, twaarie, tickerie, saiven,<br> Hallabie, cockabie, ten, a belyven,<br> Pin, pan, muskie, dan,<br> Tweedlum, twadlum, twenty-one."  
 <p>"I saw a doo flee our the dam,<br> Wi silver wings an golden ban;<br> She leukit cast, she leukit west,<br> She leukit fahr t light on best;<br> She lightit on a bank o san<br> T see the cocks o Cumberlan.<br> Fite puddin, black trout,<br>         Ye're oot."</p>  
 <p>"As I geed up the brandy hill<br> I met my father--he geed wull,<br> He hid jewels, he hid rings,<br> He'd a cat wi ten tails,<br> He'd a ship wi saiven sails,<br> He'd a haimmer dreeve nails.<br> Up Jack, doon Tam,<br> Blaw the bellows, aul man."</p>  
 <p>"Mr. Smith's a very good man,<br> He teaches his scholars noo an than,<br> An fin he's deen, he tacks a dance<br> Up t London, doon t France.<br> He wears a green beaver wi a snoot,<br> Tarry cedle, ye're oot."</p>  
 <p>"Endy tendy, ticker a been,<br> I sent a letter to the Queen,<br> The Queen o Jerusalem sent it t me,<br> Ocus, pocus, one, two, three."</p>  
 <p>"Eentie, teentie, tippenny bun,<br> The cat geed oot to get some fun,<br> To get some fun played on a drum<br> Eentie, teentie, tippenny bun."</p>  
 <p>"Mr. Mundie, foos yir wife<br> 'Verra sick, an like t die.'<br> 'Can she eat ony butcher meat?'<br> 'Yes; more than I can buy.<br> Half a horse, half a coo,<br> Half three-quarters o a soo.<br> She maks her pottage very thin;<br> A pound o butter she puts in.'<br> Fite puddin, black troot,<br>            Ye're oot".</p></font></dir>  
 <p><a name="page_171"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 171</font></a></p>  
 <p>The following flourished long ago in the parish of Tyrie:--</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"Eetum, peetum, jinkie, jie,<br> Staan ye oot by."  
 <p>"Eetum, peetum, penny pie,<br> Staan ye oot by."</p>  
 <p>"Eetum for peetum,<br> The King cam t meet him,<br> And dang John Hamilton doon."</p></font></dir>  
 <p>Pitsligo gives:--</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"Item, peetun, peeny pie,<br> Pop a lorie, jinkie jye,<br> Ah day doot,<br> Staan ye oot by."</font></dir>  
 <p>Rathen gives:--</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"Anery, twaaery, tickery, seiven,<br> Allaby, crackaby, ten an eleiven,<br> Pin, pan, musky dan,<br> Tweedletum, twadletum, twenty-one."</font></dir>  
 <p>The following were in use in Portsoy:--</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"Eerie, aarie,<br> Biscuit Mary,<br> Pim, pam, pot."  
 <p>"Enerie, twaarie, tickerie, ten,<br> Allabie, crackabie, ten, or eleevin,<br> Pim, pam, musky dam,<br> Queevrie, quaavrie, English man."</p>  
 <p>"Eerie, aarie, ackertie, ann,<br> Feelicie, faalicie, mixin, John,<br> Queevrie, quaavrie, Irish man,<br> Stinklum, stanklum, buck."</p>  
 <p>"Eetum, peetum, penny pie,<br> Cock-a-lorie, jinky jye,<br> Staan ye oot by<br> For a bonnie penny pie."</p>  
 <p>"As I geed up the aipple tree,<br> A the aipples stack t me.<br> Fite puddin, black trout,<br> I choose you oot<br> For a dirty dish clout."</p></font></dir>  
 <p><a name="page_172"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 172</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"Eringies, orangies,<br> Two for a penny,<br> Come all ye good scholars<br> That counted so many.<br> The rose is red, the grass is green,<br> The days are gane<br> That I hae seen.<br> Kettie, my spinner,<br> Cum doon t yir dinner,<br> And taste a leg of frog.<br> Mr. Frog is a very good man,<br> He takes a dance up to France<br> Noo an than."</font></dir>  
 <p>The foregoing was in use when the number to be counted out was large.</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"Yokie, pokie, yankie, fun,<br> How do you like your potatoes done?<br> First in brandy, then in rum,<br> That's how I like my potatoes done."</font></dir>  
 <p>1n the following formula the syllable <i>ca</i> must be added to the end of each word:--</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"I wud gee a my livin<br> That my wife were as fite an as fair<br> As the swans that flee our the milldam."</font></dir>  
 <p>Keith furnishes the following:--</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"Anerie, twaarie, tickrie, ten,<br> Epsom, bobsum, gentle men,<br> Pim, pam, whisky dam,<br> Feedlum, fadlum, twenty-one."  
 <p>"Eerie, airie, ackertie ann,<br> Hunches, bunches, English man,<br> Back oot, back in,<br> Back throw the heelie pin.<br> Peter cam t oor door,<br> Playin at the pipes,<br> Cum a riddle, fizz oot."</p>  
 <p>"Anerie, twaarie, tickerie, ten,<br> Bobsie, munsie, gentle men,<br> Ting, tang, muskie dam,<br> Feedlum, fan, twenty-one."</p></font></dir>  
 <p><a name="page_173"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 173</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"Heetum, peetum, penny pie,<br> Pop a lorie, jinkie jye,<br> Eadie, ootside,<br> Staan ye oot by."  
 <p>"Anery, twaaery, tickery seven,<br> Halaby, clackaby, ten and eleven,<br> Teish, tosh, maca bosh,<br> Tid, taddle, tiddle, stink."</p></font></dir>  
 <p>Banff furnishes the following:--</p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"John, rod, tod, rascal."  
 <p>"Eenitie, feenitie, ficer, ta<br> Fae, el, del, domina,<br> Irky, birky, story, rock,<br> An, tan, toust."</p>  
 <p>"Eenitie, teenitie, tippinny bun,<br> The cat geed oot to get some fun,<br> She got some fun,<br> She played the drum,<br> Eenity, teenity, tippinny bun."</p>  
 <p>"Ease, ose, man's nose,<br> A potty fon o water brose."</p>  
 <p>"The moose ran up the clock,<br> The clock struck one,<br> Doon the moosie ran,<br> Ickety, dickety, dog, dan."</p>  
 <p>"Ane, twa, three, four,<br> Mary at the cottage door<br> Eating cherries off a plate,<br> Five, six, seven, eight."</p>  
 <p>"Eerie, aarie, ecertie, ann<br> Bobs in vinegar I began<br> Eat, at<br> Moose, rat,<br> I choose you oot for a pennie pie,<br> Pur."</p>  
 <p>"Mr. Mungo, foos yir wife?'<br> 'Very sick an like t die.'<br> 'Can she eat any butcher meat?'<br> 'Yes; more than I can buy. <a name="page_174"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 174</font></a><br> Half a sow,<br> Half an ox, half a quarter of a cow;<br> She likes her porridge very thin,<br> A pound of butter she puts in.'<br> I choose you oot<br> For a penny pie, put,"</p>  
 <p>"Fussle Beardie hid a coo<br> Black an fite aboot the moo,<br> Wizna that a dainty coo<br> Belongt to Fussle Beardie."</p>  
 <p>"Fussle Beardie hid a horse;<br> It hault the cairtie through the moss,<br> Broke the cairtie, hangt the horse<br> Wizna that a dainty horse<br> Belongt t Fussle Beardie."</p>  
 <p>"Rob Law's lum reeks<br> Roon about the chimney-cheeks."</p>  
 <p>"Rise, Sally Walker, rise if you can,<br> Rise, Sally Walker, an follow your gueedeman,<br> Come, choose to the East,<br> Come, choose to the West.<br> Come, choose to the very one I love best."</p>  
 <p>"As I gaed up the brandy hill<br> I met my father; he geed will.<br> He'd jewels, he'd rings,<br> Held mony fine things,<br> He'd a cat wi ten tails,<br> He'd a ship w' saiven sails.<br> Up Jack, down Tom,<br> Blow the bellows, old man.<br> Old man had a coat,<br> He rowed aboot i the ferry-boat;<br> The ferry-boat's our dear,<br> Ten poun i the year.<br> I've a cherry, I've a chest,<br> I've a bonny blue vest,<br> I've a dog amo' the corn,<br> Blawin Willie Buck's horn.<br> Willie Buck hiz a coo,<br> Black an fite aboot the moo,<br> It jumpit our the Brig o Muck,<br> An ran awa fae Willie Buck."</p></font></dir>  
 <p><a name="page_175"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 175</font></a></p>  
 <p></p><dir><font size="-1">"Anerie, twaarie, tickerie, teven,<br> Hallaby, crackeby, tenaby, leven,<br> Pim, pam, musky, dam,<br> Feedlum, faadlum, twenty-one."  
 <p>"Ees, aas, oos, ink,<br> Peas, pottage, sma drink,<br> Twa an twas a tippenny loaf,<br> Twa an twas it."</p>  
 <p>"Humble, humble, Mister Fumble,<br> Three score an ten,<br> Learn me to double a hundred<br> Over an over again."</p>  
 <p>Black fish, fite troot,<br> Eerie aarie, ye're oot."</p>  
 <p>"John Smith, a folla fine,<br> Cam t shoe a horse o mine.<br>     Shoe a horse,<br>     Ca a nail,<br> Ca a tacket in its tail.<br> Black fish, fite troot,<br> Eerie, aarie, ye're oot."<br> Mr. Dunn's a very good man,<br> He teaches scholars, now and than;<br> And when he's done he takes a dance<br> Up to London, down to France.<br> He wears a bonnet wi a green snoot<br> Eerie, aarie, yeer oot."</p>  
 <p>"'Mr. Murdoch, how's your wife?'<br> 'Very ill, and like to die.'<br> 'Can she eat any meat?'<br> 'Yes, as much as I can buy;<br> She makes her porritch very thin,<br> Pounds o butter she puts in.'<br> Black fish, fite troot,<br> Eerie, aarie, ye're oot."</p></font></dir>
