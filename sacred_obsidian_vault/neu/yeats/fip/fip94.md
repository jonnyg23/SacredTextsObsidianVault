 <h3 align="CENTER">SOME AUTHORITIES ON IRISH FOLK-LORE</h3>
 <p>Croker's <i>Legends of the South of Ireland</i>. Lady Wilde's <i>Ancient Legends of Ireland</i>. Sir William Wilde's <i>Irish Popular Superstitions</i>. McAnally's <i>Irish Wonders</i>. <i>Irish Folk-Lore</i>, by Lageniensis. Lover's <i>Legends and Stories of the Irish Peasantry</i>. Patrick Kennedy's <i>Legendary Fictions of the Irish Celts</i>, <i>Banks of the Boro</i>, <i>Legends of Mount Leinster</i>, and <i>Banks of the Duffrey</i>; Carleton's <i>Traits and Stories of the Irish Peasantry</i>; and the chap-books, <i>Royal Fairy Tales</i>, <i>Hibernian Tales</i>, and <i>Tales of the Fairies</i>. Besides these there are many books on general subjects, containing stray folk-lore, such as Mr. and Mrs.<font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font> </p>
 <p><a name="page_326"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 326</font></a></p>
 <p>S. C. Hall's <i>Ireland</i>; Lady Chatterton's <i>Rambles in the South of Ireland</i>; Gerald Griffin's <i>Tales of a Jury-room</i>; and the <i>Leadbeater Papers</i>. For banshee stories see Barrington's <i>Recollections</i> and Miss Lefanu's <i>Memoirs of my Grandmother</i>. In O'Donovan's introduction to the <i>Four Masters</i> are several tales. The principal magazine articles are in the <i>Dublin and London Magazine</i> for 1825-1828 (Sir William Wilde calls this the best collection of Irish folk-lore in existence); and in the <i>Dublin University Magazine</i> for 1839 and 1878, those in '78 being by Miss Maclintock. The <i>Folk-Lore Journal</i> and the <i>Folk-Lore Record</i> contain much Irish folk-lore, as also do the <i>Ossianic Society's</i> publications and the proceedings of the <i>Kilkenny Archæological Society</i>. Old Irish magazines, such as the <i>Penny Journal</i>, <i>Newry Magazine</i>, and <i>Duffy's Sixpenny Magazine</i> and <i>Hibernian Magazine</i>, have much scattered through them. Among the peasantry are immense quantities of ungathered legends and beliefs.</p>
