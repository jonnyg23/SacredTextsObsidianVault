 <p align="center">TO THE RIGHT HONORABLE</p>  
 <p align="center">WILLIAM WYNDHAM</p>  
 <p align="center">SECRETARY AT WAR. &amp;c. as &amp;e. SIR,</p>  
 <p>It was with great ƒatisfaction that I learned from a Friend that you coincided with me in the opinion, that the information contained in this performance would make a uƒeful impreƒƒion on the minds of my Countrymen.</p>  
 <p>I have preƒumed to inƒcribe it with your Name, that I may publicly espreƒs the pleaƒure which I felt, when I found that neither a ƒeparation for thirty years, nor the preƒƒure of the moƒt important buƒineƒs, had effaced your kind remembrance of a College Acquaintance, or abated that obliging and polite attention with which you favored au in thoƒe early days of life.</p>  
 <p>The friendƒhip of the accompliƒhed and the worthy is the higheƒt honor; and to him who is cut off, by want of health, from almoƒt every other enjoyment, it is an ineƒtimable bleƒƒing. Accept, therefore, I pray, of my grateful acknowledgements, and of my earneƒt wiƒhes for your Health, Proƒperity, and increaƒing Honor.</p>  
 <p>With ƒentiments of the greateƒt Eƒteem and Reƒpect,<br>          I am,<br>                  SIR,<br>                     Your moƒt obedient,<br>                        and moƒt humble Servant,<br>                             JOHN ROBISON.</p>  
 <p>EDINBURGH<br> September 5, 1797.</p>  
 <p>  QUOD ƒi quis verâ vitam ratione gubernet,<br> Divitiæ grandes homini ƒunt, vivere parcè<br> Æquo anima: neque enim eƒt unquam penuria parvi,<br> At claros ƒe homines voluêrunt atque potentes,<br> Ut fundamento: ƒtabili fortuna maneres,<br> Et placidam poƒƒent opulenti degere vitam:<br> Nequicquam.--quoniam ad ƒummum ƒuccedere honorem<br> Certantes, iter infeƒtum fecêre viaï,<br> Et tamen a ƒummo quaƒi fulmen dejicit ictos<br> Invidia interdum contemptim in Tartara tetra.</p>  
 <p>Ergo, Regibus occiƒis, ƒubverƒa jacebat<br> Priƒtina majeƒtas ƒoliorum, et ƒceptra ƒuperba;<br> Et capitis ƒummi præclarum inƒigne, cruentum,<br> Sub pedibus volgi magnum lugebat honorem:<br> Nam cupidè conculcatur nimis ante metutum.<br> Res itaqe ad ƒummam fæcem, turbaƒque redibat,<br> Imperium ƒibi cum ac ƒummatum quiƒque petebat.</p>  
 <p>                      LUCRETIUS, V. 1153.</p>  
 <p align="center"><font size="-1" color="green">Note: The following Latin and English texts of the above passage were not in the original book, they are supplied for reference.--JBH</font></p>  
 <p>...quod siquis vera vitam ratione gubernet,<br> divitiae grandes homini sunt vivere parce<br> aequo animo; neque enim est umquam penuria parvi.<br> at claros homines voluerunt se atque potentes,<br> ut fundamento stabili fortuna maneret<br> et placidam possent opulenti degere vitam,<br> ne quiquam, quoniam ad summum succedere honorem<br> certantes iter infestum fecere viai,<br> et tamen e summo, quasi fulmen, deicit ictos<br> invidia inter dum contemptim in Tartara taetra;...</p>  
 <p>Ergo regibus occisis subversa iacebat<br> pristina maiestas soliorum et sceptra superba,<br> et capitis summi praeclarum insigne cruentum<br> sub pedibus vulgi magnum lugebat honorem;<br> nam cupide conculcatur nimis ante metutum.<br> res itaque ad summam faecem turbasque redibat,<br> imperium sibi cum ac summatum quisque petebat.</p>  
 <p>But men wished glory for themselves and power<br> Even that their fortunes on foundations firm<br> Might rest forever, and that they themselves,<br> The opulent, might pass a quiet life-<br> In vain, in vain; since, in the strife to climb<br> On to the heights of honour, men do make<br> Their pathway terrible; and even when once<br> They reach them, envy like the thunderbolt<br> At times will smite, O hurling headlong down<br> To murkiest Tartarus, in scorn;</p>  
 <p>And therefore kings were slain,<br> And pristine majesty of golden thrones<br> And haughty sceptres lay o’erturned in dust;<br> And crowns, so splendid on the sovereign heads,<br> Soon bloody under the proletarian feet,<br> Groaned for their glories gone-for erst o’er-much<br> Dreaded, thereafter with more greedy zest<br> Trampled beneath the rabble heel. Thus things<br> Down to the vilest lees of brawling mobs<br> Succumbed, whilst each man sought unto himself<br> Dominion and supremacy.</p>  
 <p>(Lucretius, De Rerum Naturum, Book 5, William Ellery Leonard translation)</p>
