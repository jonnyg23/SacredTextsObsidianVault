 <p><a name="page_454"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 454</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER THE TWENTY-FOURTH, AND LAST</h3>  <h4 align="center">CABALISTIC PROFUNDITIES</h4>  
 <p>IT is an assertion of the occult philosophers that the meaning and purpose of life is altogether mistaken:--necessarily--that is, in the 'Necessity of Things'--mistaken. That, inasmuch as <i>he lives</i>, man is incapacitated for pronouncing upon the <i>nature of his life;</i> being it--<i>itself</i>. He being as a 'Liver'--'It'--(i.e. 'Life', 'Itself'). Philosophy and common sense take it for granted that life needs consciousness, or some form in which the consciousness may be, in order that the liver may 'live'. Abstract philosophy asserts that the liver (living), UNLIVES (in the true sense), <i>for the very purpose of living</i>. In other words, it is concluded that, as man is the 'thing seen', the individual cannot ever go out of himself, 'to see himself'; that the 'judged at the bar' cannot cease his character to become another character, and thus 'change places' with his judge, and thus become the judge on the bench, going out of 'himself', to become 'something other' than himself, and to judge of what he is, himself. Now this, obviously, cannot be in common-sense, or in any sense. Thus, this philosophy is applied in the hermetic sense. The alchemists contended that it is possible (by art) to obtain out of the boundless, holy, unappropriated eternal Youth of Nature, a wherewithal, by means of which to 'wreak'--to use a strange word. Thus there could be miraculous renewal, even out of the powers of nature. No one knows the purposes</p>  
 <p><a name="page_455"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 455</font></a></p>  
 <p>of God, nor can any one limit the powers of God.</p>  
 <p>'<i>Angelicarum</i> animarum <i>revolutionem</i>, quanquam ad terrestrem regionem proprie, dictam haud pertingit, ad superiorem tamen partem mundi <i>Asiathici</i> et atmosphœram extendi. Nec tamen nisi parcius et compendiosius hisce de rebus egimus in <i>Cabbala Philosophica;</i> in <i>Geneseos</i>, Cap. 2 &amp; 3.</p>  
 <p>Animas, quæ non <i>sua</i> quidem culpa, laborant, sed <i>Divino</i> quodam <i>Fato</i>, in mundum <i>Asiathicum</i> delabuntur. Divina quadam vi munitas ac agitatas tuto certoque in mundum Aziluthicum reverti.</p>  
 <p>Animam <i>Messiæ</i> in mundo Aziluthico tan tum profecisse et tam arcto amore ac unione cum Divino Intellectu, sive æterno <i>Logo</i> coaluisse ut tandem summo plane gradu <i>Aziluthico</i> vel potius <i>Hyperaziluthico</i>, et si scholastici loqui liceat Hypostatico, cum eo unitus esset, adeo ut Anima <i>Messiæ</i> et Divinus <i>Logos</i> unafieret ‏פרעוף‎, i.e. unapersona (ut anima et corpus unus Homo) quæ recte appellanda esset Filius DEI.</p>

 <p>There is nothing in the lower and sensible world, that is not produced, and hath its image, in the superior world. Since the form of the body, as well as the soul, is made after the image of the Heavenly Man, a figure of the forthcoming body which is to clothe the newly descending soul is sent down from the celestial regions to hover over the couch of the husband and wife when they copulate, in order that the conception may be formed according to this model. We have before declared in our chapter on the mystic anatomy, enlarged upon by Cornelius Agrippa, that the human 'act' by which the power of perpetuation</p>

This image receives us when we enter the world; it develops itself with us when we grow; and accompanies us when we depart this life, as it is written: 'Surely man walked in an image'.</p>  
 <p>The followers of this secret doctrine of the <i>Kabbalah</i> claim for it a pre-Adamite existence. 'All human countenances are divisible into the four primordial types of faces which appeared at the mysterious chariot-throne in the vision of the prophet Ezekiel; viz. the face of man, of the lion, the ox, and the eagle. Physiognomy does not consist in the external lineaments, but in the features which are mysteriously drawn in us.'</p>
