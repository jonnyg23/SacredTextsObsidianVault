 <p><a name="page_178"><font size="1" color="green">p. 178</font></a> <a name="page_179"><font size="1" color="green">p. 179</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">APPENDIX.</h3>  <h3 align="center">SOME LOST, DOUBTFUL, AND SPURIOUS WRITINGS.</h3>  
 <p><a name="img_17900"></a>
 </p><table align="LEFT"><tr><td align="CENTER" valign="CENTER"><img src="img/17900.jpg" align="LEFT"></td></tr></table>DOUBTLESS we should have expected every fragment of St. Francis’ writings to have been preserved with loving care throughout the ages. But when we consider the conditions under which some of them were composed and the vicissitudes they afterwards passed through, we need not be surprised if all of them have not come down to us. On the contrary. For if we may believe such writers as Ubertino da Casale, serious attempts were made in certain quarters toward the close of the thirteenth century to suppress altogether part of the Saint's writings. <a name="fr_579"></a><a href="#fn_579"><font size="1">1</font></a> Be this as it may, it is certain that several of these precious documents disappeared in the course of time. Among such lost treasures we must reckon the primitive Rule of the Friars in the form approved by Innocent III in 1209. <a name="fr_580"></a><a href="#fn_580"><font size="1">2</font></a> Again only two fragments seem to have survived of the "many writings" which, as has been already mentioned, St. Francis addressed to the Poor Ladies at St.  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_180"><font size="1" color="green">p. 180</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Damian's. <a name="fr_581"></a><a href="#fn_581"><font size="1">1</font></a> Whether or not either of these fragments is to be identified with a letter written by St. Francis to console the Clares, of which we read in the <i>Speculum</i> and the <i>Conformities</i>, it is well nigh impossible to determine. <a name="fr_582"></a><a href="#fn_582"><font size="1">2</font></a> Celano speaks <a name="fr_583"></a><a href="#fn_583"><font size="1">3</font></a> of a letter to St. Antony of Padua, different apparently from the one known to us, and of others to Cardinal Ugolino. <a name="fr_584"></a><a href="#fn_584"><font size="1">4</font></a> So, too, Eccleston <a name="fr_585"></a><a href="#fn_585"><font size="1">5</font></a> tells of letters written to the brothers in France and at Bologna. <a name="fr_586"></a><a href="#fn_586"><font size="1">6</font></a></p>  
 <p>As to the famous letter of St. Francis to St. Antony commissioning the latter to teach theology, there is no small diversity of opinion. It is given for the first time in the <i>Liber Miraculorum</i>, <a name="fr_587"></a><a href="#fn_587"><font size="1">7</font></a> and also in the <i>Chron. XXIV Generalium</i>. <a name="fr_588"></a><a href="#fn_588"><font size="1">8</font></a> M. Sabatier, who was, I believe, the first to call the authenticity of this letter into question, <a name="fr_589"></a><a href="#fn_589"><font size="1">9</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_181"><font size="1" color="green">p. 181</font></a></p>  
 <p>now seems less inclined to reject it. <a name="fr_590"></a><a href="#fn_590"><font size="1">1</font></a> Professor Goetz <a name="fr_591"></a><a href="#fn_591"><font size="1">2</font></a> has decided for, and Professor Boehmer <a name="fr_592"></a><a href="#fn_592"><font size="1">3</font></a> against it. The Quaracchi editors, in excluding this letter from their edition of the <i>Opuscula</i>, by no means intended to deny that St. Francis wrote to <i>fratri Antonio</i>, <a name="fr_593"></a><a href="#fn_593"><font size="1">4</font></a> but they were unable to determine which if any of the three different forms of this letter now in circulation might be the genuine one. Since the matter is <i>sub judice</i>, <a name="fr_594"></a><a href="#fn_594"><font size="1">5</font></a> so to say, I think, with Mr. Carmichael, this letter might find a place among the "Doubtful Works" of St. Francis. <a name="fr_595"></a><a href="#fn_595"><font size="1">6</font></a></p>  
 <p>Apropos of the Saint's doubtful works it seems proper to say a word as to the Rule of the Brothers and Sisters of Penance. Although this Rule—like that of the Clares—is wanting in all the early MS. collections of St. Francis’ writings, we know from Bernard of Besse <a name="fr_596"></a><a href="#fn_596"><font size="1">7</font></a> that St.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_182"><font size="1" color="green">p. 182</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Francis, with the coöperation of Cardinal Ugolino, wrote a Rule for these Tertiaries. What became of this document? It is generally conceded that the Rule of this Third Order as it stands in the Bull <i>Supra montem</i> of Nicholas IV in 1289 <a name="fr_597"></a><a href="#fn_597"><font size="1">1</font></a> is not the handiwork of St. Francis; and for the rest the early history of the Third Order is uncertain, as all Franciscan students are aware. <a name="fr_598"></a><a href="#fn_598"><font size="1">2</font></a> But what are we to think of the much older text of this Rule published by M. Sabatier in 1901, after MS. XX of the convent at Capistran in the Abruzzi? <a name="fr_599"></a><a href="#fn_599"><font size="1">3</font></a> Father Mandonnet, O.P., has tried to prove that the first twelve of the thirteen chapters comprising this document discovered by M. Sabatier, represent the Rule of 1221 in its primitive state. <a name="fr_600"></a><a href="#fn_600"><font size="1">4</font></a> I would fain share the opinion of the learned Dominican on this head, but the objection raised against it by the Quaracchi editors seems to me insuperable. It amounts to this: In Chapter VI, § 4, of this <i>Regula Antiqua</i> there is a clear allusion to a</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_183"><font size="1" color="green">p. 183</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Bull of March 30, 1228, <a name="fr_601"></a><a href="#fn_601"><font size="1">1</font></a> which it is difficult to regard as an interpolation. Moreover, as Fr. Ubald d’Alençon points out, <a name="fr_602"></a><a href="#fn_602"><font size="1">2</font></a> the mention of coin in circulation at Ravenna is also hard to explain in an Umbrian writer. Perhaps this document may prove to be St. Francis’ Rule for Tertiaries put into legislative form, with the addition of a few minor regulations. Meanwhile, following the example of the Quaracchi editors, I have abstained from including it among the authentic writings of St. Francis. <a name="fr_603"></a><a href="#fn_603"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>Coming next to St. Francis’ poems, although he doubtless wrote some few canticles besides the Canticle of the Sun, the two others given by Wadding can hardly be accepted as his, at least in their present form. I refer to the <i>Amor de caritade</i> <a name="fr_604"></a><a href="#fn_604"><font size="1">4</font></a> and <i>In foco l’amor mi mise</i>. <a name="fr_605"></a><a href="#fn_605"><font size="1">5</font></a> True, they are both attributed to St. Francis by St. Bernardine of Siena, <a name="fr_606"></a><a href="#fn_606"><font size="1">6</font></a> but they are also found among the works of Jacopone da Todi, <a name="fr_607"></a><a href="#fn_607"><font size="1">7</font></a> although Ozanam thinks that at most they were only retouched by the latter. <a name="fr_608"></a><a href="#fn_608"><font size="1">8</font></a> The tendency nowadays is to ascribe</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_184"><font size="1" color="green">p. 184</font></a></p>  
 <p>all the early Franciscan poetry to Jacopone. When the critical edition of this extraordinary man's works is published at Quaracchi, some needed light will no doubt be thrown on this delicate question; then too, perhaps, Pacifico, the "King of Verses," and " most courtly doctor of singers," may at length come into his own. Meanwhile a number of poems found in a fifteenth century manuscript at the National Library at Naples, once at the convent of Aquila in the Abruzzi, and lately ascribed to St. Francis, are clearly apocryphal, as Professor Ildebrando della Giovanna has sufficiently demonstrated.</p>  
 <p>Wadding himself regarded the seven sermons of St. Francis he gives as of doubtful authenticity. And rightly, for they are from the work of Fr. Louis Rebolledo, already mentioned. <a name="fr_609"></a><a href="#fn_609"><font size="1">1</font></a> The twenty-eight <i>Collationes</i> are, <i>pace</i> Fr. Mandonnet, who regards them as genuine, <a name="fr_610"></a><a href="#fn_610"><font size="1">2</font></a> rightly rejected by Professor Goetz, who points out how Wadding compiled them from various sources. <a name="fr_611"></a><a href="#fn_611"><font size="1">3</font></a> Many are translated from an Italian MS. at Fano in the Marches of which we know neither the age nor the parentage. <a name="fr_612"></a><a href="#fn_612"><font size="1">4</font></a> But they seem to be mere transcripts from the early legends. Thus <i>Collatio I</i> is an adaptation of Celano (1, 2)</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_185"><font size="1" color="green">p. 185</font></a></p>  
 <p>and <i>Collatio XIV</i> is taken almost verbatim from St. Bonaventure, while <i>Collatio V</i> is an accommodation of Celano and St. Bonaventure; <i>XXVI</i> and <i>XXVIII</i> are abridged from the <i>Speculum; XXIV</i> is found in the <i>Chron. XXI V Gen.</i>, and so on. It is therefore to the authors of these works and not to St. Francis that these conferences are to be ascribed.</p>  
 <p>At the end of his edition of the <i>Opuscula</i> Wadding has collected several "Prayers of St. Francis" of which the text is more than doubtful. Let us see why. Take for example the prayers said to have been used by St. Francis "at the beginning of his conversion" or "in time of sickness" or "at the elevation." One searches in vain among the early MS. collections for any trace of these prayers, nor is mention of them to be found <a name="fr_613"></a><a href="#fn_613"><font size="1">1</font></a> elsewhere. As regards the prayer "to obtain Poverty," it has long been known that it was not written by St. Francis himself. Wadding found it in the <i>Arbor Vitae</i> (l. v., cap. iii), but Ubertino da Casale is there quoting from the <i>Sacrum Commercium B. Francisci cum Domina Paupertate</i>. <a name="fr_614"></a><a href="#fn_614"><font size="1">2</font></a> The latter work is not an historical narrative, but an exquisite allegory in which St. Francis’ own tale of his mystic espousals with the Lady Poverty is most poetically expanded by one of his</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_186"><font size="1" color="green">p. 186</font></a></p>  
 <p>followers, <a name="fr_615"></a><a href="#fn_615"><font size="1">1</font></a> and consequently Ubertino did not pretend in citing such a work to give this prayer as the actual composition of Francis. <a name="fr_616"></a><a href="#fn_616"><font size="1">2</font></a></p>  
 <p>In some MS. collections and library catalogues certain works may be found ascribed to St. Francis which are obviously spurious. For example, the <i>Epistola B. Francisci ad Fr. Bernardum</i>, found in at least two fifteenth century codices, <a name="fr_617"></a><a href="#fn_617"><font size="1">3</font></a> is nothing else but the letter of St. Bonaventure <i>continens XXV memoralia</i>. <a name="fr_618"></a><a href="#fn_618"><font size="1">4</font></a></p>  
 <p>Sbaralea <a name="fr_619"></a><a href="#fn_619"><font size="1">5</font></a> mentions copies of a book of the "Sayings" of St. Francis as existing at Assisi and Ferrara, <a name="fr_620"></a><a href="#fn_620"><font size="1">6</font></a> but a careful search has failed to reveal any trace of them. He also refers to a MS. (B. 31) in the Vallicellian Library at Rome in which "the sayings of St. Francis are found with the Rule," <a name="fr_621"></a><a href="#fn_621"><font size="1">7</font></a> but this codex is also missing. In this library, however, there <i>is</i> a codex (B. 82, fol. 141 r) which contains a "Sermon delivered by St. Francis at the end of his life." <a name="fr_622"></a><a href="#fn_622"><font size="1">8</font></a> The</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_187"><font size="1" color="green">p. 187</font></a></p>  
 <p>number of patristic citations this work contains is alone sufficient to demonstrate its spuriousness.</p>  
 <p><i>The Francisci Collationes cum fratribus</i>, catalogued among the Latin MSS. of the Royal Library at Munich <a name="fr_623"></a><a href="#fn_623"><font size="1">1</font></a> as being contained in a fifteenth century MS. at that library (cod. 11354), are a selection from the <i>Dicta</i> of the Blessed Brother Giles, as is evident from the <i>Incipit</i> of the prologue and the text of the first collation. <a name="fr_624"></a><a href="#fn_624"><font size="1">2</font></a> Their attribution to St. Francis is therefore an error of the catalogue. The <i>Verba S. Francisci de Paupertate</i>, mentioned in the same catalogue as contained in Cod. 5998, fol. 189, are an excerpt from Chap. VI of the Second Rule of the Friars Minor. <a name="fr_625"></a><a href="#fn_625"><font size="1">3</font></a></p>  
 <p>This attribution of writings to St. Francis which clearly do not belong to him is rarely intentional; it is often the result of error. For the rest, it was easiest for compilers and librarians unacquainted with the authorship of certain Franciscan works, and not eager to undertake deep researches as to their origin, to ascribe them to the common father of all Franciscan literature and the source of its inspiration.</p>  
 <p>Since every new revelation of St. Francis must be a priceless gain, it is devoutly to be wished that the present energetic research work among</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_188"><font size="1" color="green">p. 188</font></a></p>  
 <p>the sources of Franciscan history may happily bring to light some of St. Francis’ writings not known to us save through the formal attestation of the early legends and chronicles, or at least put us in possession of complete copies of such as have come down to us only in fragmentary form.</p>  
 <p>Meanwhile I conclude this volume by wishing its readers their full share in the blessing which St. Francis himself has promised to those who receive his words kindly: <i>Omnes illi et illae, qui ea benigne recipient, benedicat eis Pater et Filius et Spiritus Sanctus. Amen</i>.</p>
