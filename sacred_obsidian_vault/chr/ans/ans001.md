 </p><p><a name="ii-p2"></a> 		</p><h1 align="center"><a name="ii-p2.1"></a><a name="ii-p2.2"></a>INTRODUCTION.</h1>   		
 <p><a name="ii-p3"></a>  		
 </p><p><a name="ii-p4"></a>  		
 </p><p><a name="ii-p5"></a><a name="ii-p5.1"></a>THE present volume of <a name="ii-p5.2"></a>St. Anselm’s<a name="ii-p5.3"></a> most important philosophical and theological writings contains: (1) The <i>Proslogium</i> (2) the <i>Monologium</i>, (3) the <i>Cur Deus Homo</i>, and (4) by way of historical complement, an Appendix to the <i>Monologium</i> entitled <a name="ii-p5.4"></a>In Behalf of the Fool<a name="ii-p5.5"></a> by <a name="ii-p5.6"></a>Gaunilon<a name="ii-p5.7"></a>, a monk of Marmoutiers. The <i>Proslogium</i> (which, though subsequent in point of time to the <i>Monologium</i>, is here placed first, as containing the famous ontological argument), the <i>Monologium</i> and the Appendix thereto were translated by <a name="ii-p5.8"></a>Mr. Sidney Norton Deane<a name="ii-p5.9"></a>, of New Haven, Conn.; the <a name="ii-p5.10"></a>Cur Deus Homo<a name="ii-p5.11"></a> was 		  rendered by <a name="ii-p5.12"></a>James Gardiner Vose<a name="ii-p5.13"></a>, formerly of Milton, Conn., and later of Providence, R. I., and published in 1854 and 1855 in the <a name="ii-p5.14"></a>Bibliotheca Sacra<a name="ii-p5.15"></a>, then issued at Andover, Mass., by <a name="ii-p5.16"></a>Warren F. Draper<a name="ii-p5.17"></a>. The thanks of the reading public are due to all these gentlemen for their gratuitous labors in behalf of philosophy.</p>  		
 <p><a name="ii-p6"></a><a name="ii-p6.1"></a>Welch’s recent book <a name="ii-p6.2"></a>Anselm and His Work, by its accessibility, renders any extended biographical notice of <a name="ii-p6.3"></a>Anselm unneccessary. We append, therefore, merely a few brief paragraphs from <a name="ii-p6.4"></a>Weber’s admirable <a name="ii-p6.5"></a>History of Philosophy on <a name="ii-p6.6"></a>Anselm’s position in the world of thought, and we afterwards add (this, at the suggestion of <a name="ii-p6.7"></a>Prof. George M. Duncan, of Yale University) a series of 		  quotations regarding <a name="ii-p6.8"></a>Anselm’s most characteristic contribution to philosophy ‑‑<i>the ontological argument</i> ‑‑from <a name="ii-p6.9"></a>Descartes, <a name="ii-p6.10"></a>Spinoza, <a name="ii-p6.11"></a>Locke, <a name="ii-p6.12"></a>Leibnitz, <a name="ii-p6.13"></a>Kant, <a name="ii-p6.14"></a>Hegel, <a name="ii-p6.15"></a>Dorner, <a name="ii-p6.16"></a>Lotze, and <a name="ii-p6.17"></a>Professor Flint. A bibliography also has been compiled. Thus the work will give full material and indications for the original study of one of the greatest exponents of 		  Christian doctrine.</p>
