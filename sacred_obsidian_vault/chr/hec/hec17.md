 <p><a name="page_78"><font size="1" color="green">p. 78</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">THE GREAT CANON,<br> <small>CALLED ALSO</small><br> THE KING OF CANONS.</h3>  
 <p align="center"><font size="-1" color="green">by S. Andrew of Crete.</font></p>  
 <p>It would be unpardonable not to give a portion of that which the Greeks regard as the King of Canons—the Great Canon of the Mid-Lent week. It is a collection of Scriptural examples, turned to the purpose of penitential Confession. It is impossible to deny the beauty of many stanzas, and the ingenuity of some tropological applications. But the immense length of the Canon, for it exceeds three hundred stanzas, and its necessary tautology, must render it wearisome, unless devotionally used under the peculiar circumstances for which it is appointed. The following is a part of the earlier portion.</p>  
 <p align="center">Πόθεν ἄρξομαι θρηνεῖν;</p>  
 <p>    Whence shall my tears begin?<br>     What first-fruits shall I bear<br>   Of earnest sorrow for my sin?<br>     Or how my woes declare? <a name="page_79"><font size="1" color="green">p. 79</font></a><br> O Thou! the Merciful and Gracious One<br> Forgive the foul transgressions I have done.</p>  
 <p>    With Adam I have vied,<br>     Yea, pass’d him, in my fall;<br>   And I am naked now, by pride<br>     And lust made bare of all;<br> Of Thee, O <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">God</span>, and that Celestial Band,<br> And all the glory of the Promised Land.</p>  
 <p>    No earthly Eve beguil’d<br>     My body into sin:<br>   A spiritual temptress smiled,<br>     Concupiscence within:<br> Unbridled passion grasp’d the unhallow’d sweet:<br> Most bitter—ever bitter—was the meat.</p>  
 <p>    If Adam's righteous doom,<br>     Because he dared transgress<br>   Thy one decree, lost Eden's bloom<br>     And Eden's loveliness: <a name="page_80"><font size="1" color="green">p. 80</font></a><br> What recompence, O <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Lord</span>, must I expect,<br> Who all my life Thy quickening laws neglect?</p>  
 <p>    By mine own act, like Cain,<br>     A murderer was I made:<br>   By mine own act my soul was slain,<br>     When Thou wast disobeyed:<br> And lusts each day are quickened, warring still<br> Against Thy grace with many a deed of ill.</p>  
 <p>    Thou formed’st me of clay,<br>     O Heav’nly Potter! Thou<br>   In fleshly vesture didst array,<br>     With life and breath endow.<br> Thou Who didst make, didst ransom, and dost know,<br> To Thy repentant creature pity show!</p>  
 <p>    My guilt for vengeance cries;<br>     But yet Thou pardonest all, <a name="page_81"><font size="1" color="green">p. 81</font></a><br>   And whom Thou lov’st Thou dost chastise,<br>     And mourn’st for them that fall:<br> Thou, as a Father, mark’st our tears and pain,<br> And welcomest the prodigal again.</p>  
 <p>    I lie before Thy door,<br>     O turn me not away!<br>   Nor in mine old age give me o’er<br>     To Satan for a prey!<br> But ere the end of life and term of grace,<br> Thou Merciful! my many sins efface!</p>  
 <p>    The Priest beheld, and pass’d<br>     The way he had to go:<br>   A careless glance the Levite cast,<br>     And left me to my woe:<br> But Thou, O <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Jesu</span>, Mary’s Son, console,<br> Draw nigh, and succour me, and make me whole!</p>  
 <p><a name="page_82"><font size="1" color="green">p. 82</font></a></p>  
 <p>    Thou Spotless Lamb divine,<br>     Who takest sins away,<br>   Remove, remove, the load that mine<br>     Upon my conscience lay:<br> And, of Thy tender mercy, grant Thou me<br> To find remission of iniquity!</p>  
 <p>[In Mr. Young's book: composed by Dr. Schroeder.]</p>
