 <p><a name="page_209"><font size="1" color="green">p. 209</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">CANON FOR ASCENSION DAY.</h3>  
 <p>This is the crowning glory of the poet Joseph; he has here with a happy boldness entered into the lists with S. John Damascene, to whom, on this one occasion, he must be pronounced superior. I have preserved the alphabetic arrangement, and "Joseph's Ode" at the end. All the Catavasias are in Iambics.</p>  <h3 align="center">ODE. I.</h3>  
 <p align="center"><font size="-1" color="green">by S. Joseph of the Studium</font></p>  
 <p align="center">ἀνέστης τριήμερος.</p>  
 <p>A   fter three days Thou didst rise<br>     Visible to mortal eyes:<br>     First the Eleven worshipped Thee,—<br>     Then the rest in Galilee:<br>     Then a cloud in glory bore<br>     Thee to Thine own native shore.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_210"><font size="1" color="green">p. 210</font></a></p>  
 <p>B   oldly David pour'd the strain:<br>     <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">God</span> ascends to Heav’n again:<br>     With the trumpet's pealing note<br>     Alleluias round Him float;<br>     As He now, by hard-won right,<br>     Seeks the Fount of purest Light!</p>  
 <p>C   rime on crime, and grief on grief,<br>     Left the world without relief:<br>     Now that aged, languid race,<br>     <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">God</span> hath quickened by His grace:<br>     As Thy going up we see,<br>     Glory to Thy Glory be!</p>  
 <p align="center"><i>Catavasía</i>.</p>  
 <p align="center">θέιῳ καλυφθείς.</p>  
 <p>D   arkness and awe, when Sinai's top he trod,<br>     Taught him of faltering tongue the Law of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">God</span>: <a name="page_211"><font size="1" color="green">p. 211</font></a><br>     The mist was scattered from his spirit's eye,<br>     He prais’d and hymn’d the Maker of the sky,<br>     When He That is and was and shall be, passed by.</p>
