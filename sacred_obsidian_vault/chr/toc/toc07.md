 <p><a name="page_38"><font size="1" color="green">p. 38</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER IV</h3>  <h3 align="center">REVERSIBLE ERRORS</h3>  
 <p>"Reversible Error" is defined in modern law as "such an error as warrants the appellate court in reversing the judgment and remanding the cause." <a name="fr_6"></a><a href="#fn_6"><font size="1">7</font></a> For generations an error has been held not reversible unless, at the time it was made, the party against whom it was made, through his attorney, made specific objection to the action of the trial court and saved a specific "exception" to the ruling; but in 1943 Missouri abolished exceptions and provided in its new Code for Civil Procedure that "it is sufficient that a party, at the time the ruling of the Court is made or sought, makes known to the court the action which he desires the court to take or his objection to the action of the court and his grounds therefor; and if a party has no opportunity to object to a ruling or order at the time it is made, the absence of an objection does not thereafter prejudice him." <a name="fr_7"></a><a href="#fn_7"><font size="1">8</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_39"><font size="1" color="green">p. 39</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> The said Code, Section 140, goes on to say that "No Appellate court shall reverse any judgment, unless it believes that error was committed by the trial court against appellant, <a name="fr_8"></a><a href="#fn_8"><font size="1">9</font></a> and materially affecting the merits of the action." <a name="fr_9"></a><a href="#fn_9"><font size="1">10</font></a> Exceptions still have to be saved in criminal causes in Missouri, and from a purely technical standpoint there may have been no reversible error in the Trial of Christ, merely because Jesus saved no exceptions, made no objection,"opened not his mouth." <a name="fr_10"></a><a href="#fn_10"><font size="1">11</font></a> The only statements Jesus did make were in the nature of admissions of His power. <a name="fr_11"></a><a href="#fn_11"><font size="1">12</font></a></p>  
 <p><b>IF</b>, however, errors had been pointed out to the trial courtsAnnas, Caiaphas, The Sanhedrin, Pilate and Herodthen IF an appeal had been had, Jesus would have been able to assign seventeen errors in the trials. <a name="fr_12"></a><a href="#fn_12"><font size="1">13</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_40"><font size="1" color="green">p. 40</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> We shall note these errors here and annotate them in the footnotes, before passing on to discuss the trials in detail.</p>  
 <p>These errors are:</p>  
 <p>1. <i>No process could take place on the Jewish Sabbath or on feast days</i>. <a name="fr_13"></a><a href="#fn_13"><font size="1">14</font></a></p>  
 <p>2. <i>No process could be started at night or even afternoon for a trial before a regular Sanhedrin court</i>. <a name="fr_14"></a><a href="#fn_14"><font size="1">15</font></a></p>  
 <p>3. <i>It was error for Caiaphas, acting as Judge, to have sought words from the mouth of Christ upon which to convict Him, without first making a prima-facie case with other witnesses</i>. <a name="fr_15"></a><a href="#fn_15"><font size="1">16</font></a></p>  
 <p>4. <i>Caiaphas Palace was not the meeting place of the Sanhedrin: it was error to hold a trial there</i>. <a name="fr_16"></a><a href="#fn_16"><font size="1">17</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_41"><font size="1" color="green">p. 41</font></a></p>  
 <p>5. <i>It was error for Caiaphas to have acted as Judge after having publicly declared that Christ deserved death</i>. <a name="fr_17"></a><a href="#fn_17"><font size="1">18</font></a></p>  
 <p>6. <i>It was error to have left Him unguarded, to the unrestrained license of the mob in the gallery of Caiaphas palace or court for an hour or more</i>. <a name="fr_18"></a><a href="#fn_18"><font size="1">19</font></a></p>  
 <p>7. <i>The Sanhedrin had no jurisdiction in Capital Cases, having been divested of that jurisdiction by the Romans forty years before</i>. <a name="fr_19"></a><a href="#fn_19"><font size="1">20</font></a></p>  
 <p>8. <i>The Sanhedrin, if existent, had no power except at a regular meeting</i>. <a name="fr_20"></a><a href="#fn_20"><font size="1">21</font></a></p>  
 <p>9. <i>It was error not to appoint someone to defend HimJesus had no counsel</i>. <a name="fr_21"></a><a href="#fn_21"><font size="1">22</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_42"><font size="1" color="green">p. 42</font></a></p>  
 <p>10. <i>It was error not to have "warned" the witnesses in this capital case, in a Sanhedrin court</i>. <a name="fr_22"></a><a href="#fn_22"><font size="1">23</font></a></p>  
 <p>11. <i>The courts erred by not taking into consideration the guilt or innocence of Jesus</i>. <a name="fr_23"></a><a href="#fn_23"><font size="1">24</font></a></p>  
 <p>12. <i>It was error to take Christ, as prisoner, before Annas</i>. <a name="fr_24"></a><a href="#fn_24"><font size="1">25</font></a></p>  
 <p>13. <i>In modern times it would have been error to require Christ to testify y as a witness against Himself</i>, <a name="fr_25"></a><a href="#fn_25"><font size="1">26</font></a> <i>but in those days in a trial for Blasphemy there seems to have been authority in favor of requiring what we know as "self incrimination"</i> <a name="fr_26"></a><a href="#fn_26"><font size="1">27</font></a><i>this will be discussed in detail in a later chapter</i>.</p>  
 <p>14. <i>Roman Law required trials to be public, and the private trial of Christ before Annas and Caiaphas was error</i>. <a name="fr_27"></a><a href="#fn_27"><font size="1">28</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_43"><font size="1" color="green">p. 43</font></a></p>  
 <p>15. <i>It was error to convict a man on the testimony of false witnesses;</i> <a name="fr_28"></a><a href="#fn_28"><font size="1">29</font></a><i>under modern law the Jury determines the credibility of the Witnesses</i>. <a name="fr_29"></a><a href="#fn_29"><font size="1">30</font></a></p>  
 <p>16. <i>Pilate having announced Jesus not guilty, erred in permitting the verdict of the "mob" to stand. <a name="fr_30"></a><a href="#fn_30"><font size="1">31</font></a> The record shows Christ, after Pilate found "no harm" in Him</i>, <a name="fr_31"></a><a href="#fn_31"><font size="1">32</font></a> <i>was sent to Herod, then back to Pilate, then turned over to be crucified</i>.</p>  
 <p>17. <i>It was unlawful and therefore error for the Sanhedrin to convict on the same day as the trial; they could acquit the same day but had to hold a verdict of "guilty" under advisement at least two days</i>. <a name="fr_32"></a><a href="#fn_32"><font size="1">33</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>
