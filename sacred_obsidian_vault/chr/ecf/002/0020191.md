 <p> <a name="v.ii.xxi"></a> </p><h5 align="center"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p0.1"></a>Chapter XXI.—Impure Loves Ascribed to the Gods.</h5>  
 <p><a name="v.ii.xxi-p1"></a>But should it be said that they only had fleshly forms, and possess blood and seed, and the affections of anger and sexual desire, even then we must regard such assertions as nonsensical and ridiculous; for there is neither anger, nor desire and appetite, nor procreative seed, in gods. Let them, then, have fleshly forms, but let them be superior to wrath and anger, that Athênâ may not be seen</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p1.1"></a> “Burning with rage and inly wroth with Jove;” <a name="fr_764"></a><a href="#fn_764"><font size="1">765</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p3"></a>nor Hera appear thus:—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p3.1"></a> “Junos breast<br>  Could not contain her rage.” <a name="fr_765"></a><a href="#fn_765"><font size="1">766</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p5"></a>And let them be superior to grief:—</p>  <a name="page_139"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 139</font></a> <a name="v.ii.xxi-Page_139"></a>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p5.1"></a> “A woful sight mine eyes behold: a man<br>  I love in flight around the walls! My heart<br>  For Hector grieves.” <a name="fr_766"></a><a href="#fn_766"><font size="1">767</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p7"></a>For I call even men rude and stupid who give way to anger and grief. But when the “father of men and gods” mourns for his son,—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p7.1"></a> “Woe, woe! that fate decrees my best belovd<br>  Sarpedon, by Patroclus hand to fall;” <a name="fr_767"></a><a href="#fn_767"><font size="1">768</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p9"></a>and is not able while he mourns to rescue him from his peril:—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p9.1"></a> “The son of Jove, yet Jove preservd him not;” <a name="fr_768"></a><a href="#fn_768"><font size="1">769</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p11"></a>who would not blame the folly of those who, with tales like these, are lovers of the gods, or rather, live without any god?  Let them have fleshly forms, but let not Aphrodité be wounded by Diomedes in her body:—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p11.1"></a> “The haughty son of Tydeus, Diomed,<br>  Hath wounded me;” <a name="fr_769"></a><a href="#fn_769"><font size="1">770</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p13"></a>or by Arês in her soul:—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p13.1"></a> “Me, awkward me, she scorns; and yields her charms<br>  To that fair lecher, the strong god of arms.” <a name="fr_770"></a><a href="#fn_770"><font size="1">771</font></a>
 <br>   “The weapon pierced the flesh.” <a name="fr_771"></a><a href="#fn_771"><font size="1">772</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p16"></a>He who was terrible in battle, the ally of Zeus against the Titans, is shown to be weaker than Diomedes:—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p16.1"></a> “He raged, as Mars, when brandishing his spear.” <a name="fr_772"></a><a href="#fn_772"><font size="1">773</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p18"></a>Hush! Homer, a god never rages. But you describe the god to me as blood-stained, and the bane of mortals:—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p18.1"></a> “Mars, Mars, the bane of mortals, stained with blood;” <a name="fr_773"></a><a href="#fn_773"><font size="1">774</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p20"></a>and you tell of his adultery and his bonds:—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p20.1"></a> “Then, nothing loth, th enamourd fair he led,<br>  And sunk transported on the conscious bed.<br>  Down rushed the toils.” <a name="fr_774"></a><a href="#fn_774"><font size="1">775</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p22"></a>Do they not pour forth impious stuff of this sort in abundance concerning the gods? Ouranos is mutilated; Kronos is bound, and thrust down to Tartarus; the Titans revolt; Styx dies in battle: yea, they even represent them as mortal; they are in love with one another; they are in love with human beings:—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p22.1"></a> “Æneas, amid Idas jutting peaks,<br>  Immortal Venus to Anchises bore.” <a name="fr_775"></a><a href="#fn_775"><font size="1">776</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p24"></a>Are they not in love? Do they not suffer? Nay, verily, they are gods, and desire cannot touch them! Even though a god assume flesh in pursuance of a divine purpose, <a name="fr_776"></a><a href="#fn_776"><font size="1">777</font></a>
  he is therefore the slave of desire.</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p25.2"></a> “For never yet did such a flood of love,<br>  For goddess or for mortal, fill my soul;<br>  Not for Ixions beauteous wife, who bore<br>  Pirithöus, sage in council as the gods;<br>  Nor the neat-footed maiden Danäe,<br>  A crisius daughter, her who Perséus bore,<br>  Th observd of all; nor noble Phœnix child;<br>  .  .  .  .  .  .  nor for Semele;<br>  Nor for Alcmena fair;  .  .  .<br>  No, nor for Ceres, golden-tressèd queen;<br>  Nor for Latona bright; nor for thyself.” <a name="fr_777"></a><a href="#fn_777"><font size="1">778</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p27"></a>He is created, he is perishable, with no trace of a god in him. Nay, they are even the hired servants of men:—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p27.1"></a> “Admetus halls, in which I have endured<br>  To praise the menial table, though a god.” <a name="fr_778"></a><a href="#fn_778"><font size="1">779</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p29"></a>And they tend cattle:—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p29.1"></a> “And coming to this land, I cattle fed,<br>  For him that was my host, and kept this house.” <a name="fr_779"></a><a href="#fn_779"><font size="1">780</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p31"></a>Admetus, therefore, was superior to the god.  prophet and wise one, and who canst foresee for others the things that shall be, thou didst not divine the slaughter of thy beloved, but didst even kill him with thine own hand, dear as he was:—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p31.1"></a> “And I believed Apollos mouth divine<br>  Was full of truth, as well as prophets art.”<br>  </blockquote>  
 <p class="continue"><a name="v.ii.xxi-p32"></a>(Æschylus is reproaching Apollo for being a false prophet:)—</p>  <blockquote><a name="v.ii.xxi-p32.1"></a> “The very one who sings while at  the feast,<br>  The one who said these things, alas!  is he<br>  Who slew my son.” <a name="fr_780"></a><a href="#fn_780"><font size="1">781</font></a>
 <br>  </blockquote>
