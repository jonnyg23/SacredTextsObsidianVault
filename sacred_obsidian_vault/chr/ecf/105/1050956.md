 </p><p class="c46"><a name="xix.iv.xxii-p1"></a> <span class="c2"><a name="xix.iv.xxii-p1.1"></a>Chapter 22 [X.]—Who is the Transgressor of the Law? The Oldness of Its Letter. The Newness of Its Spirit.</span></p>  
 <p><a name="xix.iv.xxii-p2"></a>Therefore, brethren, you ought by free will not do evil but do good; this, indeed, is the lesson taught us in the law of God, in the Holy Scriptures—both Old and New. Let us, however, read, and by the Lords help understand, what the apostle tells us: “Because by the deeds of the law there shall no flesh be justified in His sight; for by the law is the knowledge of sin.” <a name="fr_3071"></a><a href="#fn_3071"><font size="1">3072</font></a>
  Observe, he says “<i>the knowledge</i>,” not “the destruction,” of sin. But when a man knows sin, and grace does not help him to avoid what he knows, undoubtedly the law works wrath. And this the apostle explicitly says in another passage. His words are: “The law worketh wrath.” <a name="fr_3072"></a><a href="#fn_3072"><font size="1">3073</font></a>
  The reason of this statement lies in the fact that Gods wrath is greater in the case of the transgressor who by the law knows sin, and yet commits it; such a man is thus a transgressor of the law, even as the apostle says in another sentence, “For where no law is, there is no transgression.” <a name="fr_3073"></a><a href="#fn_3073"><font size="1">3074</font></a>
  It is in accordance with this principle that he elsewhere says, “That we may serve in newness of spirit, and not in the oldness of the letter;” <a name="fr_3074"></a><a href="#fn_3074"><font size="1">3075</font></a>
  wishing <i>the law</i> to be here understood by “the oldness of the letter,” and what else by “newness of spirit” than <i> grace</i>? Then, that it might not be thought that he had brought any accusation, or suggested any blame, against the law, he immediately takes himself to task with this inquiry: “What shall we say, then? Is the law sin? God forbid.” He then adds the statement: “Nay, I had not known sin but by the law;” <a name="fr_3075"></a><a href="#fn_3075"><font size="1">3076</font></a>
  which is of the same import as the passage above quoted: “By the law <a name="page_453"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 453</font></a> <a name="xix.iv.xxii-Page_453"></a>is the knowledge of sin.” <a name="fr_3076"></a><a href="#fn_3076"><font size="1">3077</font></a>
  Then: “For I had not known lust,” he says, “except the law had said, Thou shalt not covet. <a name="fr_3077"></a><a href="#fn_3077"><font size="1">3078</font></a>
  But sin, taking occasion by the commandment, wrought in me all manner of concupiscence. For without the law sin was dead. For I was alive without the law once; but when the commandment came, sin revived, and I died. And the commandment, which was ordained to life, I found to be unto death. For sin, taking occasion by the commandment, deceived me, and by it slew me. Wherefore the law is holy; and the commandment holy, just, and good. Was, then, that which is good made death unto me? God forbid. But sin, that it might appear sin, worked death in me by that which is good,—in order that the sinner, or <a name="fr_3078"></a><a href="#fn_3078"><font size="1">3079</font></a>
  the sin, might by the commandment become beyond measure.” <a name="fr_3079"></a><a href="#fn_3079"><font size="1">3080</font></a>
  And to the Galatians he writes: “Knowing that a man is not justified by the works of the law, except through faith in Jesus Christ, even we have believed in Jesus Christ, that we might be justified by the faith of Christ, and not by the works of the law; for by the works of the law shall no flesh be justified.” <a name="fr_3080"></a><a href="#fn_3080"><font size="1">3081</font></a>
 </p>
