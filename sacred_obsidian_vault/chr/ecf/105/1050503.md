 </p><p class="c46"><a name="xv.iii.xlviii-p1"></a> <span class="c2"><a name="xv.iii.xlviii-p1.1"></a>Chapter 48 [XLIV].—Ambrose is Not in Agreement with Pelagius.</span></p>  
 <p><a name="xv.iii.xlviii-p2"></a>I wish, indeed, that he would listen to the venerable bishop when, in the second book of his <i>Exposition of the Gospel according to Luke,</i> <a name="fr_1895"></a><a href="#fn_1895"><font size="1">1896</font></a>
 he expressly teaches us that the Lord co-operates also with our wills. “You see, therefore,” says he, “because the power of the Lord co-operates everywhere with human efforts, that no man is able to build without the Lord, no man to watch without the Lord, no man to undertake anything without the Lord. Whence the apostle thus enjoins: Whether ye eat, or whether ye drink, do all to the glory of God.” <a name="fr_1896"></a><a href="#fn_1896"><font size="1">1897</font></a>
  You observe how the holy Ambrose takes away from men even their familiar expressions,—such as, “We undertake, but God accomplishes,”—when he says here that “no man is able to undertake anything without the Lord.” To the same effect he says, in the sixth book of the same work, <a name="fr_1897"></a><a href="#fn_1897"><font size="1">1898</font></a>
  treating of the two debtors of a certain creditor: “According to mens opinions, he perhaps is the greater offender who owed most. The case, however, is altered by the Lords mercy, so that he loves the most who owes the most, if he yet obtains grace.” See how the catholic doctor most plainly declares that the very love which prompts every <a name="page_233"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 233</font></a> <a name="xv.iii.xlviii-Page_233"></a>man to an ampler love appertains to the kindly gift of grace.</p>
