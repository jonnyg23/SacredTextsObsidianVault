 <p class="c30"><a name="v.iii.i-p6"></a><span class="c1"><a name="v.iii.i-p6.1"></a>Chapter I.—Introductory. Heresies Must Exist, and Even Abound; They are a Probation to Faith.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="v.iii.i-p7"></a><span class="sc"><a name="v.iii.i-p7.1"></a>The</span> character of the times in which we live is such as to call forth from us even this admonition, that we ought not to be astonished at the heresies (which abound) <a name="fr_1850"></a><a href="#fn_1850"><font size="1">1851</font></a>
  neither ought their existence to surprise us, for it was foretold that they should come to pass; <a name="fr_1851"></a><a href="#fn_1851"><font size="1">1852</font></a>
  nor the fact that they subvert the faith of some, for their final cause is, by affording a trial to faith, to give it also the opportunity of being “approved.” <a name="fr_1852"></a><a href="#fn_1852"><font size="1">1853</font></a>
  Groundless, therefore, and inconsiderate is the offence of the many <a name="fr_1853"></a><a href="#fn_1853"><font size="1">1854</font></a>
  who are scandalized by the very fact that heresies prevail to such a degree. How great (might their offence have been) if they had not existed. <a name="fr_1854"></a><a href="#fn_1854"><font size="1">1855</font></a>
  When it has been determined that a thing must by all means be, it receives the (final) cause for which it has its being. This secures the power through which it exists, in such a way that it is impossible for it not to have existence.</p>

 <p class="endnote"><a name="v.iii.i-p3"></a> Of the various forms of the title of this treatise, <i>de Præscriptione Hæreticorum,</i> <i>de Præscriptionibus Hæreticorum,</i> <i>de Præscriptionibus adversus Hæreticos</i>, the first is adopted by Oehler after the oldest authorities, such as the Liber Argobardinus and the Codex Paterniacensis (or Seletstadiensis), and the Editio Princeps of Rhenanus. The term <i>præscriptio</i> is a legal one, meaning a <i>demurrer</i>, or formal objection. The genitive <i>hæreticorum</i> is used in an objective sense, as if <i>adversus hæreticos</i>. Tertullian himself, in <i>de Carne Christi</i>, ii. says, “Sed plenius ejusmodi præscriptionibus adversus omnes hæreses alibi jam usi sumus.” The title therefore means, “On the Churchs Prescriptive Rule against Heresies of all kinds.” [Elucidation I.]</p>
