 <p> <a name="vi.vii.x"></a>
 </p><p class="c30"><a name="vi.vii.x-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="vi.vii.x-p1.1"></a>Chapter X.—Of Revenge.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="vi.vii.x-p2"></a>There is, too, another chief spur of impatience, the lust of revenge, dealing with the business either of glory or else of malice. But “glory,” on the one hand, is everywhere “vain;” <a name="fr_9110"></a><a href="#fn_9110"><font size="1">9111</font></a>
  and malice, on the other, is always <a name="fr_9111"></a><a href="#fn_9111"><font size="1">9112</font></a>
  odious to the Lord; in this case indeed most of all, when, being provoked by a neighbours malice, it constitutes itself superior <a name="fr_9112"></a><a href="#fn_9112"><font size="1">9113</font></a>
  in following out revenge, and by paying wickedness doubles that which has once been done. Revenge, in the estimation of error, <a name="fr_9113"></a><a href="#fn_9113"><font size="1">9114</font></a>
  seems a solace of pain; in the estimation of truth, on the contrary, it is convicted of malignity. For what difference is there between provoker and provoked, except that the former is detected as prior in evil-doing, but the latter as posterior? Yet each stands impeached of hurting a man in the eye of the Lord, who both prohibits and condemns every wickedness. In evil doing there is no account taken of <i>order</i>, nor does <i>place</i> separate what <i>similarity</i> conjoins. And the precept is absolute, that evil is not to be repaid with evil. <a name="fr_9114"></a><a href="#fn_9114"><font size="1">9115</font></a>
  Like deed involves like merit. How shall we observe that principle, if in our loathing <a name="fr_9115"></a><a href="#fn_9115"><font size="1">9116</font></a>
  we shall not loathe <i>revenge</i>? What honour, moreover, shall we be offering to the Lord God, if we arrogate to ourselves the arbitrament of vengeance? We are corrupt <a name="fr_9116"></a><a href="#fn_9116"><font size="1">9117</font></a>
 —earthen vessels. <a name="fr_9117"></a><a href="#fn_9117"><font size="1">9118</font></a>
  With our own servant-boys, <a name="fr_9118"></a><a href="#fn_9118"><font size="1">9119</font></a>
  if they assume to themselves the right of vengeance on their fellow-servants, we are gravely offended; while such as make us the offering of their patience we not only approve as mindful of humility, of servitude, affectionately jealous of the right of their lords honour; but we make them an ampler satisfaction than they would have pre-exacted <a name="fr_9119"></a><a href="#fn_9119"><font size="1">9120</font></a>
  for themselves. Is there any risk of a different result in the case of a Lord so just in estimating, so potent in executing? Why, then, do we believe Him a Judge, if not an Avenger too? This He promises that He will be to us in return, saying, “Vengeance <i>belongeth</i> to me, and I will avenge;” <a name="fr_9120"></a><a href="#fn_9120"><font size="1">9121</font></a>
  that is, <i>Leave</i> patience to me, and I will reward patience. For when He says, “Judge not, lest ye be judged,” <a name="fr_9121"></a><a href="#fn_9121"><font size="1">9122</font></a>
  does He not require patience? For who will refrain from judging another, but he who shall be patient in not revenging himself? Who <i>judges</i> in order to <i>pardon</i>? And if he shall pardon, still he has taken care to indulge the impatience of a judger, and has taken away the honour of the one Judge, that is, God. How many mischances had impatience of this kind been wont to run into! How oft has it repented of its revenge! How oft has its vehemence been found worse than the causes which led to it!—inasmuch as nothing undertaken with impatience can be effected without impetuosity:  nothing done with impetuosity fails either to stumble, or else to fall altogether, <a name="page_714"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 714</font></a> <a name="vi.vii.x-Page_714"></a>or else to vanish headlong.  Moreover, if you avenge yourself too slightly, you will be mad; if too amply, you will have to bear the burden. <a name="fr_9122"></a><a href="#fn_9122"><font size="1">9123</font></a>
  What have I to do with vengeance, the measure of which, through impatience of pain, I am unable to regulate? Whereas, if I shall repose on patience, I shall not <i>feel</i> pain; if I shall not feel pain, I shall not <i>desire</i> to avenge myself.</p>
