 </p><p class="c30"><a name="iv.iv.xiii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="iv.iv.xiii-p1.1"></a>Chapter XIII.—Of the Observance of Days Connected with Idolatry.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="iv.iv.xiii-p2"></a>But why speak of sacrifices and priesthoods? Of spectacles, moreover, and pleasures of that kind, we have already filled a volume of their own. <a name="fr_253"></a><a href="#fn_253"><font size="1">254</font></a>
  In this place must be handled the subject of holidays and other extraordinary solemnities, which we accord sometimes to our wantonness, sometimes to our timidity, in opposition to the common faith and Discipline. The first point, indeed, on which I shall join issue is this:  whether a servant of God ought to share with the very <a name="page_69"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 69</font></a> <a name="iv.iv.xiii-Page_69"></a>nations themselves in matters of his kind either in dress, or in food, or in any other kind of their gladness. “To rejoice with the rejoicing, and grieve with the grieving,” <a name="fr_254"></a><a href="#fn_254"><font size="1">255</font></a>
  is said about <i>brethren</i> by the apostle when exhorting to unanimity. But, for <i>these</i> purposes, “There is nought of communion between light and darkness,” <a name="fr_255"></a><a href="#fn_255"><font size="1">256</font></a>
  between life and death or else we rescind what is written, “The world shall rejoice, but ye shall grieve.” <a name="fr_256"></a><a href="#fn_256"><font size="1">257</font></a>
  If we rejoice with the world, there is reason to fear that with the world we shall grieve too. But when the world rejoices, let us grieve; and when the world afterward grieves, we shall rejoice. Thus, too, Eleazar <a name="fr_257"></a><a href="#fn_257"><font size="1">258</font></a>
  in Hades, <a name="fr_258"></a><a href="#fn_258"><font size="1">259</font></a>
  (attaining refreshment in Abrahams bosom) and the rich man, (on the other hand, set in the torment of fire) compensate, by an answerable retribution, their alternate vicissitudes of evil and good.  There are certain gift-days, which with some adjust the claim of honour, with others the debt of wages. “Now, then,” you say, “I shall receive back what is mine, or pay back what is anothers.” If men have consecrated for themselves this custom from superstition, why do you, estranged as you are from all their vanity, participate in solemnities consecrated to idols; as if for you also there were some prescript about a day, short of the observance of a particular day, to prevent your paying or receiving what you owe a man, or what is owed you by a man? Give me the form after which you wish to be dealt with.  For why should you skulk withal, when you contaminate your own conscience by your neighbours ignorance?  If you are not unknown to be a Christian, you are tempted, and you act as if you were not a Christian against your neighbours conscience; if, however, you shall be disguised withal, <a name="fr_259"></a><a href="#fn_259"><font size="1">260</font></a>
  you are the slave of the temptation. At all events, whether in the latter or the former way, you are guilty of being “ashamed of God.” <a name="fr_260"></a><a href="#fn_260"><font size="1">261</font></a>
  But “whosoever shall be ashamed of Me in the presence of men, of him will I too be ashamed,” says He, “in the presence of my Father who is in the heavens.” <a name="fr_261"></a><a href="#fn_261"><font size="1">262</font></a>
 </p>
