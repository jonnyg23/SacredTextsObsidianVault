 <p> <a name="vi.ii.x"></a>
 </p><p class="c30"><a name="vi.ii.x-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="vi.ii.x-p1.1"></a>Chapter X.—Of Mens Shrinking from This Second Repentance and Exomologesis, and of the Unreasonableness of Such Shrinking.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="vi.ii.x-p2"></a>Yet most men either shun this work, as being a public exposure <a name="fr_8511"></a><a href="#fn_8511"><font size="1">8512</font></a>
  of themselves, or else defer it from day to day. I presume (as being) more mindful of modesty than of salvation; just like men who, having contracted some malady in the more private parts of the body, avoid the privity of physicians, and so perish with their own bashfulness. It is intolerable, forsooth, to modesty to make satisfaction to the offended Lord! to be restored to its forfeited <a name="fr_8512"></a><a href="#fn_8512"><font size="1">8513</font></a>
  salvation! Truly you are honourable in your modesty; bearing an open forehead for sinning, but an abashed one for deprecating! I give no place to bashfulness when I am a gainer by its loss; when itself in some son exhorts the man, saying, “Respect not me; it is better that I perish through <a name="fr_8513"></a><a href="#fn_8513"><font size="1">8514</font></a>
  you, <i>i.e. than you through me</i>.” At all events, the time when (if ever) its danger is serious, is when it is a butt for jeering speech in the presence of insulters, where one man raises himself on his neighbours ruin, where there is upward clambering over the prostrate.  But among brethren and fellow-servants, where there is common hope, fear, <a name="fr_8514"></a><a href="#fn_8514"><font size="1">8515</font></a>
  joy, grief, suffering, because there is a common Spirit from a common Lord and Father, why do you think these <i>brothers</i> to be anything other than yourself? Why flee from the partners of your own mischances, as from such as will derisively cheer them? The body cannot feel gladness at the trouble of any one member, <a name="fr_8515"></a><a href="#fn_8515"><font size="1">8516</font></a>
  it must necessarily join with one consent in the grief, and in labouring for the remedy. In a company of two <a name="fr_8516"></a><a href="#fn_8516"><font size="1">8517</font></a>
  is the church; <a name="fr_8517"></a><a href="#fn_8517"><font size="1">8518</font></a>
  but the church is Christ. <a name="fr_8518"></a><a href="#fn_8518"><font size="1">8519</font></a>
  When, then, you cast yourself at the brethrens knees, you are handling <i>Christ</i>, you are entreating <i>Christ</i>. In like manner, when they shed tears over you, it is <i>Christ</i> who suffers, <i>Christ</i> who prays the Father for mercy. What a son <a name="fr_8519"></a><a href="#fn_8519"><font size="1">8520</font></a>
  asks is ever easily obtained. Grand indeed is the reward of modesty, which the concealment of our fault promises us! to wit, if we do hide somewhat from the knowledge of man, shall we equally conceal it from God? Are the judgment of men and the knowledge of God so put upon a par?  Is it better to be damned in secret than absolved in public? <i>But you say</i>, “It is a miserable thing thus to come to <i><span class="c42"><a name="vi.ii.x-p11.2"></a>exomologesis</span></i>:” yes, for evil does bring to misery; but where repentance is to be made, the misery ceases, because it is turned into something <a name="page_665"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 665</font></a> <a name="vi.ii.x-Page_665"></a>salutary. Miserable it is to be cut, and cauterized, and racked with the pungency of some (medicinal) powder: still, the things which heal by unpleasant means do, by the benefit of the cure, excuse their own offensiveness, and make present injury bearable for the sake <a name="fr_8520"></a><a href="#fn_8520"><font size="1">8521</font></a>
  of the advantage to supervene.</p>
