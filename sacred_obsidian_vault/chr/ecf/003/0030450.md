 </p><p class="c30"><a name="v.iv.vi.iv-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="v.iv.vi.iv-p1.1"></a>Chapter IV.—Another Instance of Marcions Tampering with St. Pauls Text.  The Fulness of Time, Announced by the Apostle, Foretold by the Prophets. Mosaic Rites Abrogated by the Creator Himself. Marcions Tricks About Abrahams Name. The Creator, by His Christ, the Fountain of the Grace and the Liberty Which St. Paul Announced. Marcions Docetism Refuted.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="v.iv.vi.iv-p2"></a>“But,” says he, “I speak after the manner of men: when we were children, we were placed in bondage under the elements of the world.” <a name="fr_5321"></a><a href="#fn_5321"><font size="1">5322</font></a>
  This, however, was not said “after the manner of men.” For there is no figure <a name="fr_5322"></a><a href="#fn_5322"><font size="1">5323</font></a>
  here, but literal truth. For (with respect to the latter clause of this passage), what child (in the sense, that is, in which the Gentiles are children) is not in bondage to the elements of the world, which he looks up to <a name="fr_5323"></a><a href="#fn_5323"><font size="1">5324</font></a>
  in the light of a god? With regard, however, to the former clause, there <i>was</i> a figure (as the apostle wrote it); because after he had said, “I speak after the manner of men,” he adds), “Though it be but a mans covenant, no man disannulleth, or addeth thereto.” <a name="fr_5324"></a><a href="#fn_5324"><font size="1">5325</font></a>
  For by the figure of the permanency of a human covenant he was defending the divine testament. “To Abraham were the promises made, and to his seed. He said not to seeds, as of many; but as of one, to thy seed, which is Christ.” <a name="fr_5325"></a><a href="#fn_5325"><font size="1">5326</font></a>
  Fie on <a name="fr_5326"></a><a href="#fn_5326"><font size="1">5327</font></a>
  Marcions sponge! But indeed it is superfluous to dwell on what he has erased, when he may be more effectually confuted from that which he has retained. <a name="fr_5327"></a><a href="#fn_5327"><font size="1">5328</font></a>
  “But when the fulness of time was come, God sent forth His Son” <a name="fr_5328"></a><a href="#fn_5328"><font size="1">5329</font></a>
 —the God, of course, who is the Lord of that <a name="page_436"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 436</font></a> <a name="v.iv.vi.iv-Page_436"></a>very succession of times which constitutes <i>an age</i>; who also ordained, as “<i>signs</i>” of time, suns and moons and constellations and stars; who furthermore both predetermined and predicted that the revelation of His Son should be postponed to the end of the times. <a name="fr_5329"></a><a href="#fn_5329"><font size="1">5330</font></a>
  “It shall come to pass <i>in the last days</i>, that the mountain (of the house) of the Lord shall be manifested”; <a name="fr_5330"></a><a href="#fn_5330"><font size="1">5331</font></a>
  “and <i>in the last days</i> I will pour out of my Spirit upon all flesh” <a name="fr_5331"></a><a href="#fn_5331"><font size="1">5332</font></a>
  as Joel says. It was characteristic of Him (only) <a name="fr_5332"></a><a href="#fn_5332"><font size="1">5333</font></a>
  to wait patiently for the fulness of time, to whom belonged the end of time no less than the beginning. But as for that idle god, who has neither any work nor any prophecy, nor accordingly any time, to show for himself, what has <i>he</i> ever done to bring about the fulness of time, or to wait patiently its completion? If nothing, what an impotent state to have to wait for the Creators time, in servility to the Creator! But for what end did He send His Son? “To redeem them that were under the law,” <a name="fr_5333"></a><a href="#fn_5333"><font size="1">5334</font></a>
  in other words, to “make the crooked ways straight, and the rough places smooth,” as Isaiah says <a name="fr_5334"></a><a href="#fn_5334"><font size="1">5335</font></a>
 —in order that old things might pass away, and a new course begin, even “the new law out of Zion, and the word of the Lord from Jerusalem,” <a name="fr_5335"></a><a href="#fn_5335"><font size="1">5336</font></a>
  and “that we might receive the adoption of sons,” <a name="fr_5336"></a><a href="#fn_5336"><font size="1">5337</font></a>
  that is, the Gentiles, who once were not sons.  For He is to be “the light of the Gentiles,” and “in His name shall the Gentiles trust.” <a name="fr_5337"></a><a href="#fn_5337"><font size="1">5338</font></a>
  That we may have, therefore the assurance that we are the children of God, “He hath sent forth His Spirit into our hearts, crying, Abba, Father.” <a name="fr_5338"></a><a href="#fn_5338"><font size="1">5339</font></a>
  For “in the last days,” saith He, “I will pour out of my Spirit upon all flesh.” <a name="fr_5339"></a><a href="#fn_5339"><font size="1">5340</font></a>
 </p> 
 <p class="c20"><a name="v.iv.vi.iv-p22"></a>Now, from whom comes this grace, but from Him who proclaimed the promise thereof? Who is (our) Father, but He who is also our Maker?  Therefore, after such affluence (of grace), they should not have returned “to weak and beggarly elements.” <a name="fr_5340"></a><a href="#fn_5340"><font size="1">5341</font></a>
  By the Romans, however, the rudiments of learning are wont to be called <i>elements</i>. He did not therefore seek, by any depreciation of the mundane elements, to turn them away from their god, although, when he said just before, “Howbeit, then, ye serve them which by nature are no gods,” <a name="fr_5341"></a><a href="#fn_5341"><font size="1">5342</font></a>
  he censured the error of that physical or natural superstition which holds the elements to be god; but at the God of those elements he aimed not in this censure. <a name="fr_5342"></a><a href="#fn_5342"><font size="1">5343</font></a>
  He tells us himself clearly enough what he means by “<i>elements</i>,” even the rudiments of the law: “Ye observe days, and months, and times, and years” <a name="fr_5343"></a><a href="#fn_5343"><font size="1">5344</font></a>
 —the sabbaths, I suppose, and “the preparations,” <a name="fr_5344"></a><a href="#fn_5344"><font size="1">5345</font></a>
  and the fasts, and the “high days.” <a name="fr_5345"></a><a href="#fn_5345"><font size="1">5346</font></a>
  For the cessation of even these, no less than of circumcision, was appointed by the Creators decrees, who had said by Isaiah, “Your new moons, and your sabbaths, and your high days I cannot bear; your fasting, and feasts, and ceremonies my soul hateth;” <a name="fr_5346"></a><a href="#fn_5346"><font size="1">5347</font></a>
  also by Amos, “I hate, I despise your feast-days, and I will not smell in your solemn assemblies;” <a name="fr_5347"></a><a href="#fn_5347"><font size="1">5348</font></a>
  and again by Hosea, “I will cause to cease all her mirth, and her feast-days, and her sabbaths, and her new moons, and all her solemn assemblies.” <a name="fr_5348"></a><a href="#fn_5348"><font size="1">5349</font></a>
  The institutions which He set up Himself, you ask, did He then destroy? Yes, rather than any other. Or if another destroyed them, he only helped on the purpose of the Creator, by removing what even He had condemned. But this is not the place to discuss the question why the Creator abolished His own laws. It is enough for us to have proved that He intended such an abolition, that so it may be affirmed that the apostle determined nothing to the prejudice of the Creator, since the abolition itself proceeds from the Creator. But as, in the case of thieves, something of the stolen goods is apt to drop by the way, as a clue to their detection; so, as it seems to me, it has happened to Marcion: the last mention of Abrahams name he has left untouched (in the epistle), although no passage required his erasure more than this, even his partial alteration of the text. <a name="fr_5349"></a><a href="#fn_5349"><font size="1">5350</font></a>
  “For (it is written) that Abraham had two sons, the one by a bond maid, the other by a free woman; but he who was of the bond maid was born after the flesh, but he of the free woman was by promise: which things are allegorized” <a name="fr_5350"></a><a href="#fn_5350"><font size="1">5351</font></a>
  (that is to say, they presaged something besides <i>the literal history</i>); “for these are the <a name="page_437"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 437</font></a> <a name="v.iv.vi.iv-Page_437"></a>two covenants,” or the two exhibitions (of the divine plans), <a name="fr_5351"></a><a href="#fn_5351"><font size="1">5352</font></a>
  as we have found the word interpreted, “the one from the Mount Sinai,” in relation to the synagogue of the Jews, according to the law, “which gendereth to bondage”—“the other gendereth” (to liberty, being raised) above all principality, and power, and dominion, and every name that is named, not only in this world, but in that which is to come, “which is the mother of us all,” in which we have the promise of (Christs) holy church; by reason of which he adds in conclusion: “So then, brethren, we are not children of the bond woman, but of the free.” <a name="fr_5352"></a><a href="#fn_5352"><font size="1">5353</font></a>
  In this passage he has undoubtedly shown that Christianity had a noble birth, being sprung, as the mystery of the allegory indicates, from that son of Abraham who was born of the free woman; whereas from the son of the bond maid came the legal bondage of Judaism. Both dispensations, therefore, emanate from that same God by whom, <a name="fr_5353"></a><a href="#fn_5353"><font size="1">5354</font></a>
  as we have found, they were both sketched out beforehand. When he speaks of “the liberty wherewith Christ hath made us free,” <a name="fr_5354"></a><a href="#fn_5354"><font size="1">5355</font></a>
  does not the very phrase indicate that He is the Liberator who was once the Master? For Galba himself never liberated slaves which were not his own, even when about to restore free men to their liberty. <a name="fr_5355"></a><a href="#fn_5355"><font size="1">5356</font></a>
  By Him, therefore, will liberty be bestowed, at whose command lay the enslaving power of the law. And very properly. It was not meet that those who had received liberty should be “entangled again with the yoke of bondage” <a name="fr_5356"></a><a href="#fn_5356"><font size="1">5357</font></a>
 —that is, of the law; now that the Psalm had its prophecy accomplished: “Let us break their bands asunder, and cast away their cords from us, since the rulers have gathered themselves together against the Lord and against His Christ.” <a name="fr_5357"></a><a href="#fn_5357"><font size="1">5358</font></a>
  All those, therefore, who had been delivered from the yoke of slavery he would earnestly have to obliterate the very mark of slavery—even circumcision, on the authority of the prophets prediction. He remembered how that Jeremiah had said, “Circumcise the foreskins of your heart;” <a name="fr_5358"></a><a href="#fn_5358"><font size="1">5359</font></a>
  as Moses likewise had enjoined, “Circumcise your hard hearts” <a name="fr_5359"></a><a href="#fn_5359"><font size="1">5360</font></a>
 —not the <i>literal</i> flesh. If, now, he were for excluding circumcision, as the messenger of a new god, why does he say that “in Christ neither circumcision availeth anything, nor uncircumcision?” <a name="fr_5360"></a><a href="#fn_5360"><font size="1">5361</font></a>
  For it was his duty to prefer the rival principle of that which he was abolishing, if he had a mission from the god who was the enemy of circumcision.</p> 
 <p class="c20"><a name="v.iv.vi.iv-p44"></a>Furthermore, since both circumcision and uncircumcision were attributed to the same Deity, both lost their power <a name="fr_5361"></a><a href="#fn_5361"><font size="1">5362</font></a>
  in Christ, by reason of the excellency of faith—of that faith concerning which it had been written, “And in His name shall the Gentiles trust?” <a name="fr_5362"></a><a href="#fn_5362"><font size="1">5363</font></a>
 —of that faith “which,” he says “worketh by love.” <a name="fr_5363"></a><a href="#fn_5363"><font size="1">5364</font></a>
  By this saying he also shows that the Creator is the source of that grace. For whether he speaks of the love which is due to God, or that which is due to ones neighbor—in either case, the Creators grace is meant: for it is He who enjoins the first in these words, “Thou shalt love God with all thine heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy strength;” <a name="fr_5364"></a><a href="#fn_5364"><font size="1">5365</font></a>
  and also the second in another passage:  “Thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself.” <a name="fr_5365"></a><a href="#fn_5365"><font size="1">5366</font></a>
  “But he that troubleth you shall have to bear judgment.” <a name="fr_5366"></a><a href="#fn_5366"><font size="1">5367</font></a>
  From what God? From (Marcions) most excellent god? But he does not execute judgment. From the Creator? But neither will He condemn the maintainer of circumcision. Now, if none other but the Creator shall be found to execute judgment, it follows that only He, who has determined on the cessation of the law, shall be able to condemn the defenders of the law; and what, if he also affirms the law in that portion of it where it ought (to be permanent)? “For,” says he, “all the law is fulfilled in you by this:  Thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself.” <a name="fr_5367"></a><a href="#fn_5367"><font size="1">5368</font></a>
  If, indeed, he will have it that by the words “<i>it is fulfilled</i>” it is implied that the law no longer has <i>to be</i> fulfilled, then of course he does not mean that I should any more love my neighbour as myself, since this precept must have ceased together with the law. But no! we must evermore continue to observe this commandment. The Creators law, therefore, has received the approval of the rival god, who has, in fact, bestowed upon it not the sentence of a summary dismissal, <a name="fr_5368"></a><a href="#fn_5368"><font size="1">5369</font></a>
  but the favour of a compendious acceptance; <a name="fr_5369"></a><a href="#fn_5369"><font size="1">5370</font></a>
  <a name="page_438"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 438</font></a> <a name="v.iv.vi.iv-Page_438"></a>the gist of it all being concentrated in this one precept! But this condensation of the law is, in fact, only possible to Him who is the Author of it.  When, therefore, he says, “Bear ye one anothers burdens, and so fulfill the law of Christ,” <a name="fr_5370"></a><a href="#fn_5370"><font size="1">5371</font></a>
  since this cannot be accomplished except a man love his neighbour as himself, it is evident that the precept, “Thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself” (which, in fact, underlies the injunction, “Bear ye one anothers burdens”), is really “the law of Christ,” though literally the law of the Creator. Christ, therefore, is the Creators Christ, as Christs law is the Creators law.  “Be not deceived, <a name="fr_5371"></a><a href="#fn_5371"><font size="1">5372</font></a>
  God is not mocked.” <a name="fr_5372"></a><a href="#fn_5372"><font size="1">5373</font></a>
  But Marcions god <i>can</i> be mocked; for he knows not how to be angry, or how to take vengeance. “For whatsoever a man soweth, that shall he also reap.” <a name="fr_5373"></a><a href="#fn_5373"><font size="1">5374</font></a>
  It is then the God of recompense and judgment who threatens <a name="fr_5374"></a><a href="#fn_5374"><font size="1">5375</font></a>
  this. “Let us not be weary in well-doing;” <a name="fr_5375"></a><a href="#fn_5375"><font size="1">5376</font></a>
  and “as we have opportunity, let us do good.” <a name="fr_5376"></a><a href="#fn_5376"><font size="1">5377</font></a>
  Deny now that the Creator has given a commandment to do good, and then a diversity of precept may argue a difference of gods. If, however, He also announces recompense, then from the same God must come the harvest both of death <a name="fr_5377"></a><a href="#fn_5377"><font size="1">5378</font></a>
  and of life. But “in due time we shall reap;” <a name="fr_5378"></a><a href="#fn_5378"><font size="1">5379</font></a>
  because in Ecclesiastes it is said, “For everything there will be a time.” <a name="fr_5379"></a><a href="#fn_5379"><font size="1">5380</font></a>
  Moreover, “the world is crucified unto me,” who am a servant of the Creator—“the world,” (I say,) but not the God who made the world—“and I unto the world,” <a name="fr_5380"></a><a href="#fn_5380"><font size="1">5381</font></a>
  not unto the God who made the world. <i>The world</i>, in the apostles sense, here means life and conversation according to worldly principles; it is in renouncing these that we and they are mutually crucified and mutually slain. He calls them “persecutors of Christ.” <a name="fr_5381"></a><a href="#fn_5381"><font size="1">5382</font></a>
  But when he adds, that “he bare in his body the <i>scars</i> <a name="fr_5382"></a><a href="#fn_5382"><font size="1">5383</font></a>
  of Christ”—since scars, of course, are accidents of body <a name="fr_5383"></a><a href="#fn_5383"><font size="1">5384</font></a>
 —he therefore expressed the truth, that the flesh of Christ is not putative, but real and substantial, <a name="fr_5384"></a><a href="#fn_5384"><font size="1">5385</font></a>
  the scars of which he represents as borne upon his body.</p>
