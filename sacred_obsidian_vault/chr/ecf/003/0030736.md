 </p><p class="c30"><a name="vi.iii.xiv-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="vi.iii.xiv-p1.1"></a>Chapter XIV.—Of Pauls Assertion, that He Had Not Been Sent to Baptize.</span></p> 
 <p class="c56"><a name="vi.iii.xiv-p2"></a>But they roll back <i>an objection</i> from <i>that</i> apostle himself, in that he said, “For Christ sent me not to baptize;” <a name="fr_8684"></a><a href="#fn_8684"><font size="1">8685</font></a>
  as if by this argument baptism were done away!  For <i>if so</i>, why did he baptize Gaius, and Crispus, and the house of Stephanas? <a name="fr_8685"></a><a href="#fn_8685"><font size="1">8686</font></a>
  However, even if Christ had not sent <i>him</i> to baptize, yet He had given <i>other</i> apostles the precept to baptize. But these words were written to the Corinthians in regard of the circumstances of that particular time; seeing that schisms and dissensions were agitated among them, while one attributes <i>everything</i> to Paul, another to Apollos. <a name="fr_8686"></a><a href="#fn_8686"><font size="1">8687</font></a>
  For which reason the “peace-making” <a name="fr_8687"></a><a href="#fn_8687"><font size="1">8688</font></a>
  apostle, for fear he should seem to claim all <i>gifts</i> for himself, says that he had been sent “not to baptize, but to preach.” For preaching is the prior thing, baptizing the posterior.  Therefore the preaching came <i>first</i>: but I think baptizing withal was <i>lawful</i> to him to whom preaching was.</p>
