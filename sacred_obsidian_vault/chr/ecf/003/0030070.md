 <p> <a name="iv.iv.xii"></a>
 </p><p class="c30"><a name="iv.iv.xii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="iv.iv.xii-p1.1"></a>Chapter XII.—Further Answers to the Plea, How Am I to Live?</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="iv.iv.xii-p2"></a>In vain do we flatter ourselves as to the necessities of human maintenance, if—after faith sealed <a name="fr_237"></a><a href="#fn_237"><font size="1">238</font></a>
 —we say, “I have no means to live?” <a name="fr_238"></a><a href="#fn_238"><font size="1">239</font></a>
  For here I will now answer more fully that abrupt proposition. It is advanced <i>too late</i>. For after the similitude of that most prudent builder, <a name="fr_239"></a><a href="#fn_239"><font size="1">240</font></a>
  who first computes the costs of the work, together with his own means, lest, when he has begun, he afterwards blush to find himself spent, deliberation should have been made <i>before</i>. But even now you have the Lords sayings, as examples taking away from you all excuse.  For what is it you say? “I shall be in need.” But the Lord calls the needy “happy.” <a name="fr_240"></a><a href="#fn_240"><font size="1">241</font></a>
  “I shall have no food.” But “think not,” says He, “about food;” <a name="fr_241"></a><a href="#fn_241"><font size="1">242</font></a>
  and as an example of clothing we have the lilies. <a name="fr_242"></a><a href="#fn_242"><font size="1">243</font></a>
  “My work was my subsistence.” Nay, but “all things are to be sold, and divided to the needy.” <a name="fr_243"></a><a href="#fn_243"><font size="1">244</font></a>
  “But provision must be made for children and posterity.” “None, putting his hand on the plough, and looking back, is fit” for work. <a name="fr_244"></a><a href="#fn_244"><font size="1">245</font></a>
  “But I was under contract.” “None can serve two lords.” <a name="fr_245"></a><a href="#fn_245"><font size="1">246</font></a>
  If you wish to be the Lords disciple, it is necessary you “take your cross, and follow the Lord:” <a name="fr_246"></a><a href="#fn_246"><font size="1">247</font></a>
  <i>your cross</i>; that is, your own <i>straits</i> and <i>tortures</i>, or your <i>body</i> only, which is after the manner of a <i>cross</i>. Parents, wives, children, will have to be left behind, for Gods sake. <a name="fr_247"></a><a href="#fn_247"><font size="1">248</font></a>
  Do you hesitate about arts, and trades, and about professions likewise, for the sake of children and parents? Even there was it demonstrated to us, that both “dear pledges,” <a name="fr_248"></a><a href="#fn_248"><font size="1">249</font></a>
  and handicrafts, and trades, are to be quite left behind for the Lords sake; while James and John, called by the Lord, do leave quite behind both father and ship; <a name="fr_249"></a><a href="#fn_249"><font size="1">250</font></a>
  while Matthew is roused up from the toll-booth; <a name="fr_250"></a><a href="#fn_250"><font size="1">251</font></a>
  while even burying a father was too tardy a business for faith. <a name="fr_251"></a><a href="#fn_251"><font size="1">252</font></a>
  None of them whom the Lord chose to Him said, “I have no means to live.” Faith fears not famine. It knows, likewise, that hunger is no less to be contemned by it for Gods sake, than every kind of death. It has learnt not to respect <i>life</i>; how much more <i>food</i>? [You ask] “How many have fulfilled these conditions?” But what with men is difficult, with God is easy. <a name="fr_252"></a><a href="#fn_252"><font size="1">253</font></a>
  Let us, however, comfort ourselves about the gentleness and clemency of God in such wise, as not to indulge our “necessities” up to the point of affinities with idolatry, but to avoid even from afar every breath of it, as of a pestilence. [And this] not merely in the cases forementioned, but in the universal series of human superstition; whether appropriated to its gods, or to the defunct, or to kings, as pertaining to the selfsame unclean spirits, sometimes through sacrifices and priesthoods, sometimes through spectacles and the like, sometimes through holy-days.</p>
