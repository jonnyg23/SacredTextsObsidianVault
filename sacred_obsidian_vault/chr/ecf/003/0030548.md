 </p><p class="c30"><a name="v.vi.xxxiii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="v.vi.xxxiii-p1.1"></a>Chapter XXXIII.—These Remaining Chapters an Appendix to the Main Work. In This Chapter Tertullian Notices a Difference Among Sundry Followers of Ptolemy, a Disciple of Valentinus.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="v.vi.xxxiii-p2"></a>I shall now at last produce, by way of <i>finale</i>, <a name="fr_6915"></a><a href="#fn_6915"><font size="1">6916</font></a>
  after so long a story, those points which, not to interrupt the course of it, and by the interruption distract the readers attention, I have preferred reserving to this place. They have been variously advanced by those who have improved on <a name="fr_6916"></a><a href="#fn_6916"><font size="1">6917</font></a>
  the doctrines of Ptolemy. For there have been in his school “disciples above their master,” who have attributed to their Bythus two wives—Cogitatio (<i>Thought</i>) and Voluntas (<i>Will</i>). For Cogitatio alone was not sufficient wherewith to produce any offspring, although from the two wives procreation was most easy to him. The former bore him Monogenes (<i>Only-Begotten</i>) and Veritas (<i>Truth</i>). Veritas was a female after the likeness of Cogitatio; Monogenes a male bearing a resemblance to Voluntas. For it is the strength of Voluntas which procures the masculine nature, <a name="fr_6917"></a><a href="#fn_6917"><font size="1">6918</font></a>
  inasmuch as she affords efficiency to Cogitatio.</p>
