 </p><p class="c30"><a name="iv.viii.i.xix-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="iv.viii.i.xix-p1.1"></a>Chapter XIX. <a name="fr_749"></a><a href="#fn_749"><font size="1">750</font></a>
 —If Christians and the Heathen Thus Resemble Each Other, There is Great Difference in the Grounds and Nature of Their Apparently Similar Conduct.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="iv.viii.i.xix-p3"></a>Here end, I suppose, your tremendous charges of obstinacy against the Christians. Now, since we are amenable to them in common with yourselves, it only remains that we compare the grounds which the respective parties have for being personally derided. All our obstinacy, however, is with you a foregone conclusion, <a name="fr_750"></a><a href="#fn_750"><font size="1">751</font></a>
  based on our strong convictions; for we take for granted <a name="fr_751"></a><a href="#fn_751"><font size="1">752</font></a>
  a resurrection of the dead. Hope in this resurrection amounts to <a name="fr_752"></a><a href="#fn_752"><font size="1">753</font></a>
  a contempt of death. Ridicule, therefore, as much as you like the excessive stupidity of such minds as die that they may live; but then, in order that you may be able to laugh more merrily, and deride us with greater boldness, you must take your sponge, or perhaps your tongue, and wipe away those records of yours every now and then cropping out, <a name="fr_753"></a><a href="#fn_753"><font size="1">754</font></a>
  which assert in not dissimilar terms that souls will return to bodies. But how much more worthy of acceptance is our belief which maintains that they will return to the same bodies! And how much more ridiculous is your inherited conceit, <a name="fr_754"></a><a href="#fn_754"><font size="1">755</font></a>
  that the human spirit is to reappear in a dog, or a mule, or a peacock! Again, we affirm that a judgment has been ordained by God according to the merits of every man. This you ascribe to Minos and Rhadamanthus, while at the same time <i>you</i> reject Aristides, who was a juster judge than either.  By the award of the judgment, we say that the wicked will have to spend an eternity in endless fire, the pious and innocent in a region of bliss. In your view likewise an unalterable condition is ascribed to the respective destinations of Pyriphlegethon <a name="fr_755"></a><a href="#fn_755"><font size="1">756</font></a>
  and Elysium. Now they are not merely your composers of myth and poetry who write songs of this strain; but your philosophers also speak with all confidence of the return of souls to their former state, <a name="fr_756"></a><a href="#fn_756"><font size="1">757</font></a>
  and of the twofold award <a name="fr_757"></a><a href="#fn_757"><font size="1">758</font></a>
  of a final judgment.</p>
