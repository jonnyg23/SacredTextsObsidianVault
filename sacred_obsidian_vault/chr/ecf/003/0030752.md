 <p> <a name="vi.iv.viii"></a>
 </p><p class="c30"><a name="vi.iv.viii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="vi.iv.viii-p1.1"></a>Chapter VIII.—The Seventh or Final Clause.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="vi.iv.viii-p2"></a>For the completeness of so brief a prayer He added—in order that we should supplicate not touching the remitting merely, but touching the entire averting, of acts of guilt—“Lead us not into temptation:” that is, suffer us not to be led into it, by him (of course) who tempts; but far be the thought that the Lord should seem to tempt, <a name="fr_8815"></a><a href="#fn_8815"><font size="1">8816</font></a>
  as if He either were ignorant of the faith of any, or else were eager to overthrow it. Infirmity <a name="fr_8816"></a><a href="#fn_8816"><font size="1">8817</font></a>
  and malice <a name="fr_8817"></a><a href="#fn_8817"><font size="1">8818</font></a>
  are characteristics of the devil. For <i>God</i> had commanded even Abraham to make a sacrifice of his son, for the sake not of tempting, but proving, his faith; in order through him to make an example for that precept of His, whereby He was, by and by, to enjoin that he should hold no pledges of affection dearer than God. <a name="fr_8818"></a><a href="#fn_8818"><font size="1">8819</font></a>
  He Himself, when tempted by the devil, demonstrated who it is that presides over and is the originator of temptation. <a name="fr_8819"></a><a href="#fn_8819"><font size="1">8820</font></a>
  This passage He confirms by subsequent ones, saying, “Pray that ye be not tempted;” <a name="fr_8820"></a><a href="#fn_8820"><font size="1">8821</font></a>
  yet they <i>were</i> tempted, (as they showed) by deserting their Lord, because they had given way rather to sleep than prayer. <a name="fr_8821"></a><a href="#fn_8821"><font size="1">8822</font></a>
  The final clause, therefore, is consonant, and interprets the sense of “Lead us not into temptation;” for this <i>sense</i> is, “But convey us away from the Evil One.”</p>
