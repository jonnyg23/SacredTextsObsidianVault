 <p class="c28"><a name="v.iv.ii.i-p5"></a><span class="c6"><a name="v.iv.ii.i-p5.1"></a>Wherein is described the god of Marcion. He is shown to be utterly wanting in all the attributes of the true God.</span></p> 
 <p class="Centered"><a name="v.iv.ii.i-p6"></a> ————————————</p> 
 <p class="c30"><a name="v.iv.ii.i-p7"></a><span class="c1"><a name="v.iv.ii.i-p7.1"></a>Chapter I.—Preface. Reason for a New Work. Pontus Lends Its Rough Character to the Heretic Marcion, a Native. His Heresy Characterized in a Brief Invective.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="v.iv.ii.i-p8"></a><span class="sc"><a name="v.iv.ii.i-p8.1"></a>Whatever</span> in times past <a name="fr_2320"></a><a href="#fn_2320"><font size="1">2321</font></a>
  we have wrought in opposition to Marcion, is from the present moment no longer to be accounted of. <a name="fr_2321"></a><a href="#fn_2321"><font size="1">2322</font></a>
  It is a new work which we are undertaking in lieu of the old one. <a name="fr_2322"></a><a href="#fn_2322"><font size="1">2323</font></a>
  My original tract, as too hurriedly composed, I had subsequently superseded by a fuller treatise. This latter I lost, before it was completely published, by the fraud of a person who was then a brother, <a name="fr_2323"></a><a href="#fn_2323"><font size="1">2324</font></a>
  but became afterwards an apostate. He, as it happened, had transcribed a portion of it, full of mistakes, and then published it. The necessity thus arose for an amended work; and the occasion of the new edition induced me to make a considerable addition to the treatise.  This present text, <a name="fr_2324"></a><a href="#fn_2324"><font size="1">2325</font></a>
  therefore, of my work—which is the third as superseding <a name="fr_2325"></a><a href="#fn_2325"><font size="1">2326</font></a>
  the second, but henceforward to be considered the first instead of the third—renders a preface necessary to this issue of the tract itself that no reader may be perplexed, if he should by chance fall in with the various forms of it which are scattered about.</p> 
 <p class="c20"><a name="v.iv.ii.i-p15"></a>The Euxine Sea, as it is called, is self-contradictory in its nature, and deceptive in its name. <a name="fr_2326"></a><a href="#fn_2326"><font size="1">2327</font></a>
  As you would not account it hospitable from its situation, so is it severed from our more civilised waters by a certain stigma which attaches to its barbarous character. The fiercest nations inhabit it, if indeed it can be called <i>habitation</i>, when life is passed in waggons. They have no fixed abode; their life has <a name="fr_2327"></a><a href="#fn_2327"><font size="1">2328</font></a>
  no germ of civilization; they indulge their libidinous desires without restraint, and for the most part naked.  Moreover, when they gratify secret lust, they hang up their quivers on their car-yokes, <a name="fr_2328"></a><a href="#fn_2328"><font size="1">2329</font></a>
  to warn off the curious and rash observer.  Thus without a blush do they prostitute their weapons of war. The dead bodies of their parents they cut up with their sheep, and devour at their feasts.  They who have not died so as to become food for others, are thought to have died an accursed death. Their women are not by their sex softened to modesty.  They uncover the breast, from which they suspend their battle-axes, and prefer warfare to marriage. In their climate, too, there is the same rude nature. <a name="fr_2329"></a><a href="#fn_2329"><font size="1">2330</font></a>
  The day-time is never clear, the sun never cheerful; <a name="fr_2330"></a><a href="#fn_2330"><font size="1">2331</font></a>
  the sky is uniformly cloudy; the whole year is wintry; the only wind that blows is the angry North. Waters melt only by fires; their rivers flow not by reason of the ice; their mountains are covered <a name="fr_2331"></a><a href="#fn_2331"><font size="1">2332</font></a>
  with heaps of snow. All things are torpid, all stiff with cold. Nothing there has the glow <a name="fr_2332"></a><a href="#fn_2332"><font size="1">2333</font></a>
  of life, but that ferocity which has given to scenic plays their stories of the sacrifices <a name="fr_2333"></a><a href="#fn_2333"><font size="1">2334</font></a>
  of the Taurians, and the loves <a name="fr_2334"></a><a href="#fn_2334"><font size="1">2335</font></a>
  of the Colchians, and the torments <a name="fr_2335"></a><a href="#fn_2335"><font size="1">2336</font></a>
  of the Caucasus. Nothing, however, in Pontus is so barbarous <a name="page_272"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 272</font></a> <a name="v.iv.ii.i-Page_272"></a>and sad as the fact that Marcion was born there, fouler than any Scythian, more roving than the waggon-life <a name="fr_2336"></a><a href="#fn_2336"><font size="1">2337</font></a>
  of the Sarmatian, more inhuman than the Massagete, more audacious than an Amazon, darker than the cloud, <a name="fr_2337"></a><a href="#fn_2337"><font size="1">2338</font></a>
  (of Pontus) colder than its winter, more brittle than its ice, more deceitful than the Ister, more craggy than Caucasus.  Nay <a name="fr_2338"></a><a href="#fn_2338"><font size="1">2339</font></a>
  more, the true Prometheus, Almighty God, is mangled <a name="fr_2339"></a><a href="#fn_2339"><font size="1">2340</font></a>
  by Marcions blasphemies. Marcion is more savage than even the beasts of that barbarous region. For what beaver was ever a greater emasculator <a name="fr_2340"></a><a href="#fn_2340"><font size="1">2341</font></a>
  than he who has abolished the nuptial bond?  What Pontic mouse ever had such gnawing powers as he who has gnawed the Gospels to pieces? Verily, O Euxine, thou hast produced a monster more credible to philosophers than to Christians. For the cynic Diogenes used to go about, lantern in hand, at mid-day to find a man; whereas Marcion has quenched the light of his faith, and so lost the God whom he had found. His disciples will not deny that his first faith he held along with ourselves; a letter of his own <a name="fr_2341"></a><a href="#fn_2341"><font size="1">2342</font></a>
  proves this; so that for the future <a name="fr_2342"></a><a href="#fn_2342"><font size="1">2343</font></a>
  a heretic may from his case <a name="fr_2343"></a><a href="#fn_2343"><font size="1">2344</font></a>
  be designated as one who, forsaking that which was prior, afterwards chose out for himself that which was not in times past. <a name="fr_2344"></a><a href="#fn_2344"><font size="1">2345</font></a>
  For in <i>as</i> far as what was delivered in times past and from the beginning will be held as truth, in <i>so</i> far will that be accounted heresy which is brought in later.  But another brief treatise <a name="fr_2345"></a><a href="#fn_2345"><font size="1">2346</font></a>
  will maintain this position against heretics, who ought to be refuted even without a consideration of their doctrines, on the ground that they are heretical by reason of the novelty of their opinions. Now, so far as any controversy is to be admitted, I will for the time <a name="fr_2346"></a><a href="#fn_2346"><font size="1">2347</font></a>
  (lest our compendious principle of novelty, being called in on all occasions to our aid, should be imputed to want of confidence) begin with setting forth our adversarys rule of belief, that it may escape no one what our main contention is to be.</p>
