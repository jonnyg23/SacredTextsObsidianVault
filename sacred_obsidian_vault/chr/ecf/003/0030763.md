 </p><p class="c30"><a name="vi.iv.xix-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="vi.iv.xix-p1.1"></a>Chapter XIX.—Of Stations.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="vi.iv.xix-p2"></a>Similarly, too, touching the days of Stations, <a name="fr_8866"></a><a href="#fn_8866"><font size="1">8867</font></a>
  most think that they must not be present at the sacrificial prayers, on the ground that the Station must be dissolved by reception of the Lords Body. Does, then, the Eucharist cancel a service devoted to God, or bind it more to God?  Will not your <i>Station</i> be more solemn if you have withal <i>stood</i> at Gods <i>altar</i>? <a name="fr_8867"></a><a href="#fn_8867"><font size="1">8868</font></a>
  When the Lords Body has been received and reserved <a name="fr_8868"></a><a href="#fn_8868"><font size="1">8869</font></a>
  each point is secured, both the participation of the sacrifice and the discharge of duty. If the “Station” has received its name from the example of military life—for we withal are Gods military <a name="fr_8869"></a><a href="#fn_8869"><font size="1">8870</font></a>
 —of course no gladness or sadness chanting to the camp abolishes the “stations” of the soldiers: for gladness will carry out discipline more willingly, sadness more carefully.</p>
