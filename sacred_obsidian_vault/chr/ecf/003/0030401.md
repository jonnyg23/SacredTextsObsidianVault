 <p> <a name="v.iv.v.ii"></a>
 </p><p class="c30"><a name="v.iv.v.ii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="v.iv.v.ii-p1.1"></a>Chapter II.—St. Lukes Gospel, Selected by Marcion as His Authority, and Mutilated by Him.  The Other Gospels Equally Authoritative.  Marcions Terms of Discussion, However, Accepted, and Grappled with on the Footing of St. Lukes Gospel Alone.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="v.iv.v.ii-p2"></a>You have now our answer to the <i>Antitheses</i> compendiously indicated by us. <a name="fr_3515"></a><a href="#fn_3515"><font size="1">3516</font></a>
  I pass on to give a proof of the Gospel <a name="fr_3516"></a><a href="#fn_3516"><font size="1">3517</font></a>
 —not, to be sure, of Jewry, but of Pontus—having become meanwhile <a name="fr_3517"></a><a href="#fn_3517"><font size="1">3518</font></a>
  adulterated; and this shall indicate <a name="fr_3518"></a><a href="#fn_3518"><font size="1">3519</font></a>
  the order by which we proceed. We lay it down as our first position, that the evangelical Testament <a name="fr_3519"></a><a href="#fn_3519"><font size="1">3520</font></a>
  has apostles for its authors, <a name="fr_3520"></a><a href="#fn_3520"><font size="1">3521</font></a>
  to whom was assigned by the Lord Himself this office of publishing the gospel. Since, however, there are apostolic <a name="fr_3521"></a><a href="#fn_3521"><font size="1">3522</font></a>
  men also, <a name="fr_3522"></a><a href="#fn_3522"><font size="1">3523</font></a>
  they are yet not alone, but appear with apostles and after apostles; because the preaching of disciples might be open to the suspicion of an affectation of glory, if there did not accompany it <a name="fr_3523"></a><a href="#fn_3523"><font size="1">3524</font></a>
  the authority of the masters, which means that of Christ, <a name="fr_3524"></a><a href="#fn_3524"><font size="1">3525</font></a>
  for it was that which made the apostles their masters. Of the apostles, therefore, John and Matthew first instil <a name="fr_3525"></a><a href="#fn_3525"><font size="1">3526</font></a>
  faith into us; whilst of apostolic men, Luke and Mark renew it afterwards. <a name="fr_3526"></a><a href="#fn_3526"><font size="1">3527</font></a>
  These all start with the same principles of the faith, <a name="fr_3527"></a><a href="#fn_3527"><font size="1">3528</font></a>
  so far as relates to the one only God the Creator and His Christ, how that He was born of the Virgin, and came to fulfil <a name="fr_3528"></a><a href="#fn_3528"><font size="1">3529</font></a>
  the law and the prophets. Never mind <a name="fr_3529"></a><a href="#fn_3529"><font size="1">3530</font></a>
  if there does occur some variation in the order of their narratives, provided that there be agreement in the essential matter <a name="fr_3530"></a><a href="#fn_3530"><font size="1">3531</font></a>
  of the faith, in which there is disagreement with Marcion. Marcion, on the other hand, you must know, <a name="fr_3531"></a><a href="#fn_3531"><font size="1">3532</font></a>
  ascribes no author to his Gospel, as if it could not be allowed him to affix a title to that from which it was no crime (in his eyes) to subvert <a name="fr_3532"></a><a href="#fn_3532"><font size="1">3533</font></a>
  the very body. And here I might now make a stand, and contend that a work ought not to be recognised, which holds not its head erect, which exhibits no consistency, which gives no promise of credibility from the fulness of its title and the just profession of its author. But we prefer to join issue <a name="fr_3533"></a><a href="#fn_3533"><font size="1">3534</font></a>
  on every point; nor shall we leave unnoticed <a name="fr_3534"></a><a href="#fn_3534"><font size="1">3535</font></a>
  what may fairly be understood to be on our side. <a name="fr_3535"></a><a href="#fn_3535"><font size="1">3536</font></a>
  Now, of the authors whom we possess, Marcion seems to have singled out Luke <a name="fr_3536"></a><a href="#fn_3536"><font size="1">3537</font></a>
  for his mutilating process. <a name="fr_3537"></a><a href="#fn_3537"><font size="1">3538</font></a>
  Luke, however, was not an apostle, but only an apostolic man; not a master, but a disciple, and so inferior to a master—at least as far subsequent to <a name="fr_3538"></a><a href="#fn_3538"><font size="1">3539</font></a>
  him as the apostle whom he followed (and that, no doubt, was Paul <a name="fr_3539"></a><a href="#fn_3539"><font size="1">3540</font></a>
 ) was subsequent to the others; so that, had Marcion even published his Gospel in the name of St. Paul himself, the single authority of the document, <a name="fr_3540"></a><a href="#fn_3540"><font size="1">3541</font></a>
  destitute of all support from preceding authorities, would not be a sufficient basis for our faith. There would be still wanted that Gospel which St. Paul found in existence, to which he yielded <a name="page_348"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 348</font></a> <a name="v.iv.v.ii-Page_348"></a>his belief, and with which he so earnestly wished his own to agree, that he actually on that account went up to Jerusalem to know and consult the apostles, “lest he should run, or had been running in vain;” <a name="fr_3541"></a><a href="#fn_3541"><font size="1">3542</font></a>
  in other words, that the faith which he had learned, and the gospel which he was preaching, might be in accordance with theirs. Then, at last, having conferred with the (primitive) authors, and having agreed with them touching the rule of faith, they joined their hands in fellowship, and divided their labours thenceforth in the office of preaching the gospel, so that they were to go to the Jews, and St. Paul to the Jews and the Gentiles.  Inasmuch, therefore, as the enlightener of St. Luke himself desired the authority of his predecessors for both his own faith and preaching, how much more may not I require for Lukes Gospel that which was necessary for the Gospel of his master. <a name="fr_3542"></a><a href="#fn_3542"><font size="1">3543</font></a>
 </p>
