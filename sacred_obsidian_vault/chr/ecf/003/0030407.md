 <p> <a name="v.iv.v.viii"></a>
 </p><p class="c30"><a name="v.iv.v.viii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="v.iv.v.viii-p1.1"></a>Chapter VIII.—Other Proofs from the Same Chapter, that Jesus, Who Preached at Nazareth, and Was Acknowledged by Certain Demons as Christ the Son of God, Was the Creators Christ. As Occasion Offers, the Docetic Errors of Marcion are Exposed.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="v.iv.v.viii-p2"></a>The Christ of the Creator had <a name="fr_3676"></a><a href="#fn_3676"><font size="1">3677</font></a>
  to be called a <i>Nazarene</i> according to prophecy; whence the Jews also designate us, on that very account, <a name="fr_3677"></a><a href="#fn_3677"><font size="1">3678</font></a>
  <i>Nazerenes</i> <a name="fr_3678"></a><a href="#fn_3678"><font size="1">3679</font></a>
  after Him. For we are they of whom it is written, “Her Nazarites were whiter than snow;” <a name="fr_3679"></a><a href="#fn_3679"><font size="1">3680</font></a>
  even they who were once defiled with the stains of sin, and darkened with the clouds of ignorance. But to Christ the title Nazarene was destined to become a suitable one, from the hiding-place of His infancy, for which He went down and dwelt at Nazareth, <a name="fr_3680"></a><a href="#fn_3680"><font size="1">3681</font></a>
  to escape from Archelaus the son of Herod.  This fact I have not refrained from mentioning on this account, because it behoved Marcions Christ to have forborne all connection whatever with the <i>domestic</i> localities of the Creators Christ, when he had so many towns in Judæa which had not been by the prophets thus assigned <a name="fr_3681"></a><a href="#fn_3681"><font size="1">3682</font></a>
  to the Creators Christ. But Christ will be (the Christ) of the prophets, wheresoever He is found in accordance with the prophets. And yet even at Nazareth He is not remarked as having preached anything new, <a name="fr_3682"></a><a href="#fn_3682"><font size="1">3683</font></a>
  whilst in another <i>verse</i> He is said to have been rejected <a name="fr_3683"></a><a href="#fn_3683"><font size="1">3684</font></a>
  by reason of a simple proverb. <a name="fr_3684"></a><a href="#fn_3684"><font size="1">3685</font></a>
  Here at once, when I observe that they laid their hands on Him, I cannot help drawing a conclusion respecting His bodily substance, which cannot be believed to have been a phantom, <a name="fr_3685"></a><a href="#fn_3685"><font size="1">3686</font></a>
  since it was capable of being touched and even violently handled, when He was seized and taken and led to the very brink of a precipice. For although He escaped through the midst of them, He had already experienced their rough treatment, and afterwards went His way, no doubt <a name="fr_3686"></a><a href="#fn_3686"><font size="1">3687</font></a>
  because the crowd (as usually happens) gave way, or was even broken through; but not because it was eluded as by an impalpable disguise, <a name="fr_3687"></a><a href="#fn_3687"><font size="1">3688</font></a>
  which, if there had been such, would not at all have submitted to any touch.</p> 
 <p class="c34"><a name="v.iv.v.viii-p15"></a>“Tangere enim et tangi, nisi corpus, nulla potest res,” <a name="fr_3688"></a><a href="#fn_3688"><font size="1">3689</font></a>
 </p> 
 <p><a name="v.iv.v.viii-p17"></a>is even a sentence worthy of a place in the worlds wisdom. In short, He did himself touch others, upon whom He laid His hands, which were capable of being felt, and conferred the blessings of healing, <a name="fr_3689"></a><a href="#fn_3689"><font size="1">3690</font></a>
  which were not less true, not less unimaginary, than were the hands wherewith He bestowed them. He was therefore the very Christ of Isaiah, the healer of our sicknesses. <a name="fr_3690"></a><a href="#fn_3690"><font size="1">3691</font></a>
  “Surely,” says he, “He hath borne our griefs and <i>carried</i> our sorrows.” Now the Greeks are accustomed to use for <i>carry</i> a word which also signifies to <i>take away</i>. A general promise is enough for me in passing. <a name="fr_3691"></a><a href="#fn_3691"><font size="1">3692</font></a>
  Whatever were the cures which Jesus effected, He is mine. We will come, however, to the kinds of cures. To liberate men, then, from evil spirits, is a cure of sickness.  Accordingly, wicked spirits (just in the manner of our former example) used to go forth with a testimony, exclaiming, “Thou art the Son of God,” <a name="fr_3692"></a><a href="#fn_3692"><font size="1">3693</font></a>
 —of what God, is clear enough from the case itself.  But they were rebuked, and ordered not to speak; precisely because <a name="fr_3693"></a><a href="#fn_3693"><font size="1">3694</font></a>
  Christ willed Himself to be proclaimed by <i>men</i>, not by unclean spirits, as the Son of God—even that Christ alone to whom this was befitting, because He had sent beforehand men through whom He might become known, and who were assuredly worthier preachers. It was natural to Him <a name="fr_3694"></a><a href="#fn_3694"><font size="1">3695</font></a>
  to refuse the proclamation of an unclean spirit, at whose command there was an abundance of saints. He, however, <a name="fr_3695"></a><a href="#fn_3695"><font size="1">3696</font></a>
  who had never been foretold (if, indeed, he wished to be acknowledged; for if he did not wish so much, his coming was in vain), would not have spurned the testimony of an alien or any sort of substance, who did not happen to have a substance of his own, <a name="fr_3696"></a><a href="#fn_3696"><font size="1">3697</font></a>
  but had descended in an alien one. And now, too, as the destroyer also of the Creator, he would have desired nothing better <a name="page_355"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 355</font></a> <a name="v.iv.v.viii-Page_355"></a>than to be acknowledged by <i>His</i> spirits, and to be divulged for the sake of being feared: <a name="fr_3697"></a><a href="#fn_3697"><font size="1">3698</font></a>
  only that Marcion says <a name="fr_3698"></a><a href="#fn_3698"><font size="1">3699</font></a>
  that his god is not feared; maintaining that a good being is not an object of fear, but only a judicial being, in whom reside the grounds <a name="fr_3699"></a><a href="#fn_3699"><font size="1">3700</font></a>
  of fear—anger, severity, judgments, vengeance, condemnation. But it was from fear, undoubtedly, that the evil spirits were cowed. <a name="fr_3700"></a><a href="#fn_3700"><font size="1">3701</font></a>
  Therefore they confessed that (Christ) was the Son of a God who was to be feared, because they would have an occasion of not submitting if there were none for fearing.  Besides, He showed that He was to be feared, because He drave them out, not by persuasion like a good being, but by command and reproof. Or else did he <a name="fr_3701"></a><a href="#fn_3701"><font size="1">3702</font></a>
  reprove them, because they were making him an object of fear, when all the while he did not want to be feared? And in what manner did he wish them to go forth, when they could not do so except with fear? So that he fell into the dilemma <a name="fr_3702"></a><a href="#fn_3702"><font size="1">3703</font></a>
  of having to conduct himself contrary to his nature, whereas he might in his simple goodness have at once treated them with leniency. He fell, too, into another false position <a name="fr_3703"></a><a href="#fn_3703"><font size="1">3704</font></a>
 —of prevarication, when he permitted himself to be feared by the demons as the Son of the Creator, that he might drive them out, not indeed by his own power, but by the authority of the Creator. “He departed, and went into a desert place.” <a name="fr_3704"></a><a href="#fn_3704"><font size="1">3705</font></a>
  This was, indeed, the Creators customary region. It was proper that the Word <a name="fr_3705"></a><a href="#fn_3705"><font size="1">3706</font></a>
  should there appear in body, where He had aforetime, wrought in a cloud. To the gospel also was suitable that condition of place <a name="fr_3706"></a><a href="#fn_3706"><font size="1">3707</font></a>
  which had once been determined on for the law. <a name="fr_3707"></a><a href="#fn_3707"><font size="1">3708</font></a>
  “Let the wilderness and the solitary place, therefore, be glad and rejoice;” so had Isaiah promised. <a name="fr_3708"></a><a href="#fn_3708"><font size="1">3709</font></a>
  When “stayed” by the crowds, He said, “I must preach the kingdom of God to other cities also.” <a name="fr_3709"></a><a href="#fn_3709"><font size="1">3710</font></a>
  Had He displayed His God anywhere yet? I suppose as yet nowhere. But was He speaking of those who knew of another god also? I do not believe so. If, therefore, neither He had preached, nor they had known, any other God but the Creator, He was announcing the kingdom of that God whom He knew to be the only God known to those who were listening to Him.</p>
