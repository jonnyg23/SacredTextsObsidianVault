 </p><p class="c30"><a name="v.xi.vii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="v.xi.vii-p1.1"></a>Chapter VII.—Tatian, Cataphrygians, Cataproclans, Catæschinetans.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="v.xi.vii-p2"></a>To all these heretics is added one Tatian, a brother-heretic.  This man was Justin Martyrs disciple.  After Justins death he began to cherish different opinions from his. For he wholly savours of Valentinus; adding this, that Adam cannot even attain salvation:  as if, when the branches become salvable, <a name="fr_8408"></a><a href="#fn_8408"><font size="1">8409</font></a>
  the root were not!</p> 
 <p class="c20"><a name="v.xi.vii-p4"></a>Other heretics swell the list who are called Cataphrygians, but their teaching is not uniform. For there are (of them) <i>some</i> who are called Cataproclans; <a name="fr_8409"></a><a href="#fn_8409"><font size="1">8410</font></a>
  there are others who are termed Catæschinetans. <a name="fr_8410"></a><a href="#fn_8410"><font size="1">8411</font></a>
  These have a blasphemy common, and a blasphemy not common, but peculiar and special.  The common blasphemy lies in their saying that the Holy Spirit was in the apostles indeed, the Paraclete was not; and in their saying that the Paraclete has spoken in Montanus more things than Christ brought forward into (the compass of) the Gospel, and not merely more, but likewise better and greater. But the particular one they who follow Æschines have; this, namely, whereby they add this, that they affirm Christ to be Himself Son and Father.</p>
