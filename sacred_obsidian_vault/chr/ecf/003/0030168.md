 </p><p class="c30"><a name="iv.viii.ii.vii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="iv.viii.ii.vii-p1.1"></a>Chapter VII.—The Gods of the Mythic Class. The Poets a Very Poor Authority in Such Matters. Homer and the Mythic Poets. Why Irreligious.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="iv.viii.ii.vii-p2"></a>But to pass to the <i>mythic</i> class of gods, which we attributed to the poets, <a name="fr_880"></a><a href="#fn_880"><font size="1">881</font></a>
  I hardly know whether I must only seek to put them on a par with our own <i>human</i> mediocrity, or whether they must be affirmed to be gods, with proofs of divinity, like the African Mopsus and the Bœotian Amphiaraus. I must now indeed but slightly touch on this class, of which a fuller view will be taken in the proper place. <a name="fr_881"></a><a href="#fn_881"><font size="1">882</font></a>
  Meanwhile, that these were only human beings, is clear from the fact that you do not consistently call them gods, but heroes. Why then discuss the point? Although divine honours had to be ascribed to dead men, it was not to them as such, of course. Look at your own practice, when with similar excess of presumption you sully heaven with the sepulchres of your kings: is it not such as are illustrious for justice, virtue, piety, and every excellence of this sort, that you honour with the blessedness of deification, contented even to incur contempt if you forswear yourselves <a name="fr_882"></a><a href="#fn_882"><font size="1">883</font></a>
  for such characters? And, on the other hand, do you not deprive the impious and disgraceful of even the old prizes of human glory, tear up <a name="fr_883"></a><a href="#fn_883"><font size="1">884</font></a>
  their decrees and titles, pull down their statues, and deface <a name="fr_884"></a><a href="#fn_884"><font size="1">885</font></a>
  their images on the current coin? Will He, however, who beholds all things, who approves, nay, rewards the good, prostitute before all men <a name="fr_885"></a><a href="#fn_885"><font size="1">886</font></a>
  the attribute of His own inexhaustible grace and mercy? And shall men be allowed an especial mount of care and righteousness, that they may be wise <a name="fr_886"></a><a href="#fn_886"><font size="1">887</font></a>
  in selecting and multiplying <a name="fr_887"></a><a href="#fn_887"><font size="1">888</font></a>
  their deities? Shall attendants on kings and princes be more pure than those who wait on the Supreme God? <a name="fr_888"></a><a href="#fn_888"><font size="1">889</font></a>
  You turn your back in horror, indeed, on outcasts and exiles, on the poor and weak, on the obscurely born and the low-lived; <a name="fr_889"></a><a href="#fn_889"><font size="1">890</font></a>
  but yet you honour, even by legal sanctions, <a name="fr_890"></a><a href="#fn_890"><font size="1">891</font></a>
  unchaste men, adulterers, robbers, and parricides. Must we regard it as a subject of ridicule or indignation, that such characters are believed to be gods who are not fit to be men? Then, again, in this mythic class of yours which the poets celebrate, how uncertain is your conduct as to purity of conscience and the maintenance thereof!  For whenever we hold up to execration the wretched, disgraceful and atrocious (examples) of your gods, you defend them as mere fables, on the pretence of poetic licence; whenever we volunteer a silent contempt <a name="fr_891"></a><a href="#fn_891"><font size="1">892</font></a>
  of this said <a name="fr_892"></a><a href="#fn_892"><font size="1">893</font></a>
  poetic <i>licence</i>, then you are not only troubled with no horror of it, but you go so far as <a name="fr_893"></a><a href="#fn_893"><font size="1">894</font></a>
  to show it respect, and to hold it as one of the indispensable (fine) arts; nay, <a name="fr_894"></a><a href="#fn_894"><font size="1">895</font></a>
  you carry out the studies of your higher classes <a name="fr_895"></a><a href="#fn_895"><font size="1">896</font></a>
  by its means, as the very foundation <a name="fr_896"></a><a href="#fn_896"><font size="1">897</font></a>
  of your literature. Plato was of opinion that poets ought to be banished, as calumniators of the gods; (he would even have) Homer himself expelled from his republic, although, as you are aware, <a name="fr_897"></a><a href="#fn_897"><font size="1">898</font></a>
  he was the crowned head of them all. But <a name="page_136"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 136</font></a> <a name="iv.viii.ii.vii-Page_136"></a>while you admit and retain them thus, why should you not believe them when they disclose such things respecting your gods? And if you do believe your poets, how is it that you worship such gods (as they describe)?  If you worship them simply because you do not believe the poets, why do you bestow praise on such lying authors, without any fear of giving offence to those whose calumniators you honour? A regard for truth <a name="fr_898"></a><a href="#fn_898"><font size="1">899</font></a>
  is not, of course, to be expected of poets.  But when you say that they only make men into gods after their death, do you not admit that before death the said gods were merely human? Now what is there strange in the fact, that they who were once men are subject to the dishonour <a name="fr_899"></a><a href="#fn_899"><font size="1">900</font></a>
  of human casualties, or crimes, or fables?  Do you not, in fact, put faith in your poets, when it is in accordance with their rhapsodies <a name="fr_900"></a><a href="#fn_900"><font size="1">901</font></a>
  that you have arranged in some instances your very rituals? How is it that the priestess of Ceres is ravished, if it is not because Ceres suffered a similar outrage? Why are the children of others sacrificed to Saturn, <a name="fr_901"></a><a href="#fn_901"><font size="1">902</font></a>
  if it is not because he spared not his own?  Why is a male mutilated in honour of the Idæan goddess <i>Cybele</i>, unless it be that the (unhappy) youth who was too disdainful of her advances was castrated, owing to her vexation at his daring to cross her love? <a name="fr_902"></a><a href="#fn_902"><font size="1">903</font></a>
  Why was not Hercules “a dainty dish” to the good ladies of Lanuvium, if it was not for the primeval offence which women gave to him? The poets, no doubt, are liars. Yet it is not because <i>of their telling us that</i> <a name="fr_903"></a><a href="#fn_903"><font size="1">904</font></a>
  your gods did such things when they were human beings, nor because they predicated divine scandals <a name="fr_904"></a><a href="#fn_904"><font size="1">905</font></a>
  of a divine state, since it seemed to you more credible that gods should exist, though not of such a character, than that there should be such characters, although not gods.</p>
