 <p> <a name="vi.iv.vii"></a>
 </p><p class="c30"><a name="vi.iv.vii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="vi.iv.vii-p1.1"></a>Chapter VII.—The Sixth Clause.</span></p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="vi.iv.vii-p2"></a>It was suitable that, after contemplating the liberality of God, <a name="fr_8807"></a><a href="#fn_8807"><font size="1">8808</font></a>
  we should likewise address His clemency.  For what will aliments <a name="fr_8808"></a><a href="#fn_8808"><font size="1">8809</font></a>
  <a name="page_684"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 684</font></a> <a name="vi.iv.vii-Page_684"></a>profit us, if we are really <i>consigned</i> to them, as it were a bull destined for a victim? <a name="fr_8809"></a><a href="#fn_8809"><font size="1">8810</font></a>
  The Lord knew Himself to be the only guiltless One, and so He teaches that we beg “to have our debts remitted us.” A petition for pardon is a full confession; because he who begs for pardon fully admits his guilt. Thus, too, penitence is demonstrated acceptable to God who desires it rather than the death of the sinner. <a name="fr_8810"></a><a href="#fn_8810"><font size="1">8811</font></a>
  Moreover, <i>debt</i> is, in the Scriptures, a figure of <i>guilt</i>; because it is equally due to the sentence of judgment, and is exacted by it: nor does it evade the justice of exaction, unless the exaction be remitted, just as the lord remitted to that slave <i>in the parable</i> his debt; <a name="fr_8811"></a><a href="#fn_8811"><font size="1">8812</font></a>
  for hither does the scope of the whole parable tend. For the fact withal, that the same servant, after liberated by his lord, does not equally spare his own debtor; and, being on that account impeached before his lord, is made over to the tormentor to pay the uttermost farthing—that is, every guilt, however small: corresponds with our profession that “we also remit to our debtors;” indeed elsewhere, too, in conformity with this Form of Prayer, He saith, “Remit, and it shall be remitted you.” <a name="fr_8812"></a><a href="#fn_8812"><font size="1">8813</font></a>
  And when Peter had put the question whether remission were to be granted to a brother seven times, “Nay,” saith He, “seventy-seven times;” <a name="fr_8813"></a><a href="#fn_8813"><font size="1">8814</font></a>
  in order to remould the Law for the better; because in Genesis <i>vengeance</i> was assigned “seven times” in the case of Cain, but in that of Lamech “seventy-seven times.” <a name="fr_8814"></a><a href="#fn_8814"><font size="1">8815</font></a>
 </p>
