 <p> <a name="iv.iii.xi.xi"></a>  </p><h4 align="center"><a name="iv.iii.xi.xi-p0.1"></a>Chapter XI.</h4>  
 <p class="subh"><a name="iv.iii.xi.xi-p1"></a>The instance of King Uzziah who was overcome by the taint of the same malady.</p>  
 <p class="skip"><a name="iv.iii.xi.xi-p2"></a><span class="sc"><a name="iv.iii.xi.xi-p2.1"></a>Of</span> Uzziah, the ancestor of this king of whom we have been speaking, himself also praised in all things by the witness of the  <a name="page_278"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 278</font></a> <a name="iv.iii.xi.xi-Page_278"></a>Scripture, after great commendation for his virtue, after countless triumphs which he achieved by the merit of his devotion and faith, learn how he was cast down by the pride of vainglory. “And,” we are told, “the name of Uzziah went forth, for the Lord helped him and had strengthened him. But when he was made strong, his heart was lifted up to his destruction, and he neglected the Lord his God.” <a name="fr_1010"></a><a href="#fn_1010"><font size="1">1011</font></a>
  You behold another instance of a most terrible downfall, and see how two men so upright and excellent were undone by their very triumphs and victories. Whence you see how dangerous the successes of prosperity generally are, so that those who could not be injured by adversity are ruined, unless they are careful, by prosperity; and those who in the conflict of battle have escaped the danger of death fall before their own trophies and triumphs.</p>
