 <p> <a name="iv.iii.i.vi"></a>  </p><h4 align="center"><a name="iv.iii.i.vi-p0.1"></a>Chapter VI.</h4>  
 <p class="subh"><a name="iv.iii.i.vi-p1"></a>Of their Capes. <a name="fr_655"></a><a href="#fn_655"><font size="1">656</font></a>
 </p>  
 <p class="skip"><a name="iv.iii.i.vi-p3"></a><span class="sc"><a name="iv.iii.i.vi-p3.1"></a>Next</span> they cover their necks and shoulders with a narrow cape, aiming at modesty of dress as well as cheapness and economy; and this is called in our language as well as theirs <i>mafors</i>; and so they avoid both the expense and the display of cloaks and great coats.</p>   
 <hr>
 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <a name="fn_655"></a><a href="2110291.htm#fr_655">203:656</a> <a name="iv.iii.i.vi-p1.1"></a>
 <p class="endnote"><a name="iv.iii.i.vi-p2"></a> The mafors (<span class="Greek"><a name="iv.iii.i.vi-p2.1"></a>μαφώριον</span> or <span class="Greek"><a name="iv.iii.i.vi-p2.2"></a>μαφόριον</span>) is the monkish scapular, or working-dress. Cf. the Rule of S. Benedict, c. 55: “Scapulare propter opera.” In form it was a large, coarse cape, or hood.</p>
