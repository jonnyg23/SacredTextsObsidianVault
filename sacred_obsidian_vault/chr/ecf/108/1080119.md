 <p> <a name="ii.CVIII"></a>  
 </p><p class="c10"><a name="ii.CVIII-p1"></a> <a name="ii.CVIII-p1.1"></a><span class="c20"><a name="ii.CVIII-p1.2"></a>Psalm CVIII. <a name="fr_4895"></a><a href="#fn_4895"><font size="1">4896</font></a>
 </span></p>  
 <p class="c26"><a name="ii.CVIII-p3"></a>1. I have not thought that the <span class="c21"><a name="ii.CVIII-p3.1"></a>CVIII</span>th Psalm required an exposition; since I have already expounded it in the <span class="c21"><a name="ii.CVIII-p3.2"></a> LVII</span>th Psalm, <a name="fr_4896"></a><a href="#fn_4896"><font size="1">4897</font></a>
  and in the <span class="c21"><a name="ii.CVIII-p4.2"></a> LX</span>th, of the last divisions of which this Psalm consisteth. For the last part of the <span class="c21"><a name="ii.CVIII-p4.3"></a> LVII</span>th is the first of this, as far as the verse, “Thy glory is above all the earth.” Henceforth to the end, is the last part of the <span class="c21"><a name="ii.CVIII-p4.4"></a>LX</span>th: as the last part of the <span class="c21"><a name="ii.CVIII-p4.5"></a>CXXXV</span>th is the same as that of the <span class="c21"><a name="ii.CVIII-p4.6"></a>  CXV</span>th, <a name="fr_4897"></a><a href="#fn_4897"><font size="1">4898</font></a>
  from the verse, “The images of the heathen are but gold and silver:” as the <span class="c21"><a name="ii.CVIII-p5.2"></a> XIV</span>th[4899] and <span class="c21"><a name="ii.CVIII-p5.4"></a> LIII</span>d, <a name="fr_4898"></a><a href="#fn_4898"><font size="1">4899</font></a>
  with a few alterations in the middle, have everything the same from the beginning to the end. Whatever slight differences therefore occur in this <span class="c21"><a name="ii.CVIII-p6.2"></a>CVIII</span>th Psalm, compared with those two, of parts of which it is composed, are easy to understand; just as we find in the <span class="c21"><a name="ii.CVIII-p6.3"></a>LVII</span>th, <a name="fr_4899"></a><a href="#fn_4899"><font size="1">4900</font></a>
  “I will sing and give praise; awake, O my glory:” here,“ I will sing and give praise, with my glory.” <a name="fr_4900"></a><a href="#fn_4900"><font size="1">4901</font></a>
  Awake, is said there, that he may sing and give praise therewith. Also, there, “Thy mercy is great” (or, as some translate, “is lifted up”) “unto the heavens;” <a name="fr_4901"></a><a href="#fn_4901"><font size="1">4902</font></a>
  but here, “Thy mercy is great above the heavens.” <a name="fr_4902"></a><a href="#fn_4902"><font size="1">4903</font></a>
  For it is great unto the heavens, that it may be great in the heavens; and this is what he wished to express by “above the heavens.” Also in the <span class="c21"><a name="ii.CVIII-p10.2"></a>LX</span>th, “I will rejoice, I will divide Shechem:” <a name="fr_4903"></a><a href="#fn_4903"><font size="1">4904</font></a>
  here “I will be exalted, and will divide Shechem.” <a name="fr_4904"></a><a href="#fn_4904"><font size="1">4905</font></a>
  Where is shown what is signified in the division of Shechem, which it was prophesied should happen after the Lords exaltation, and that this joy doth refer to that exaltation; so that He rejoiceth, because He is exalted. Whence he elsewhere saith, “Thou hast turned my heaviness into joy; Thou hast put off my sackcloth and girded me with gladness.” <a name="fr_4905"></a><a href="#fn_4905"><font size="1">4906</font></a>
  Also there “Ephraim, the strength of my head:” <a name="fr_4906"></a><a href="#fn_4906"><font size="1">4907</font></a>
  but here, “Ephraim the taking up of my head.” <a name="fr_4907"></a><a href="#fn_4907"><font size="1">4908</font></a>
  But strength cometh from taking up, that is, He maketh men strong by taking up, causing fruit in us; for the interpretation of Ephraim is, bearing fruit. But “taking up” may be understood of us, when we take up Christ; or of Christ, when He, who is Head of the Church, taketh us up. And the words, “them that trouble us,” in the former Psalm, <a name="fr_4908"></a><a href="#fn_4908"><font size="1">4909</font></a>
  are the same with “our enemies,” in this. <a name="fr_4909"></a><a href="#fn_4909"><font size="1">4910</font></a>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="ii.CVIII-p18"></a>2. We are taught by this Psalm, that those titles which seem to refer to history are most rightly understood prophetically, according to the object of the composition of the Psalms.…And yet this Psalm is composed of the latter portions of two, <a name="fr_4910"></a><a href="#fn_4910"><font size="1">4911</font></a>
  whose titles are different. Where it is signified that each concur in a common object, not in the surface of the history, but in the depth of prophecy, the objects of both being united in this one, the title of which is, “A Song or Psalm of David:” <a name="fr_4911"></a><a href="#fn_4911"><font size="1">4912</font></a>
  resembling neither of the former titles, otherwise than in the word David. Since, “in many places, and in diverse manners,” as the Epistle to the Hebrews saith, “God spoke in former times to the fathers through the Prophets;” <a name="fr_4912"></a><a href="#fn_4912"><font size="1">4913</font></a>
  yet He spoke of Him whom He sent afterwards, that the words of the Prophets might be fulfilled: for “all the promises of God in Him are yea.” <a name="fr_4913"></a><a href="#fn_4913"><font size="1">4914</font></a>
 </p>
