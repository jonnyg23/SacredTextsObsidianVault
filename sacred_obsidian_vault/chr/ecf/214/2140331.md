 </p><p class="c46"><a name="xi.xviii.xiii-p1"></a> <span class="c4"><a name="xi.xviii.xiii-p1.1"></a>Canon XIII.</span></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="xi.xviii.xiii-p2"></a><span class="sc"><a name="xi.xviii.xiii-p2.1"></a>Strange</span> and unknown clergymen without letters commendatory from their own Bishop, are absolutely prohibited from officiating in another city.</p> 
 <p class="c31"><a name="xi.xviii.xiii-p3"></a><span class="c4"><a name="xi.xviii.xiii-p3.1"></a>Notes.</span></p> 
 <p class="c36"><a name="xi.xviii.xiii-p4"></a><span class="sc"><a name="xi.xviii.xiii-p4.1"></a>Ancient Epitome of Canon XIII.</span></p> 
 <p class="c35"><a name="xi.xviii.xiii-p5"></a>No cleric shall be received to communion in another city without a letter commendatory.</p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="xi.xviii.xiii-p6"></a>“Unknown clergymen.”  I have here followed the reading of the Greek commentators.  But the translators of the <i>Prisca</i>, and Dionysius, and Isidore must have all read <span class="Greek"><a name="xi.xviii.xiii-p6.1"></a>ἀναγνώστας</span> (i.e., Readers) instead of <span class="Greek"><a name="xi.xviii.xiii-p6.2"></a>ἀγνώστους</span>.  Justellus, Hervetus, and Beveridge, as also Johnson and Hammond, follow the reading of the text.  Hefele suggests that if “Readers” is the correct reading perhaps it means, “all clergymen even readers.”</p>
