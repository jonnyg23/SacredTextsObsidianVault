 </p><p class="c34"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p1"></a> <a name="page_99"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 99</font></a> <a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-Page_99"></a><span class="c4"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p1.1"></a>Canon XVI.</span></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p2"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p2.1"></a>If</span>, under any pretence of piety, any children shall forsake their parents, particularly [if the parents are] believers, and shall withhold becoming reverence from their parents, on the plea that they honour piety more than them, let them be anathema.</p> 
 <p class="c31"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p3"></a><span class="c4"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p3.1"></a>Notes.</span></p> 
 <p class="c36"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p4"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p4.1"></a>Ancient Epitome of Canon XVI.</span></p> 
 <p class="c35"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p5"></a>If children leave their parents who are of the faithful let them be anathema.</p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p6"></a>Zonaras notes that the use of the word “particularly” shews that the obligation is universal.  The commentators all refer here to <a href="../../../bib/kjv/mat015.htm#000">Matt. 15</a><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p6.1"></a>, where our Lord speaks of the subterfuge by which the Jews under pretext of piety defrauded their parents and made the law of God of none effect.</p> 
 <p class="c36"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p7"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p7.1"></a>Van Espen.</span></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p8"></a>Of the last clause this is the meaning; that according to the Eustathians “piety towards God” or “divine worship,” or rather its pretence, should be preferred to the honour and reverence due to parents.</p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="viii.v.iv.xvi-p9"></a>This canon, in Isidores version, is found in the <i>Corpus Juris Canonici</i>, Gratians <i>Decretum</i>, Pars I., Dist. xxx., c. i.  The Roman correctors advertize the reader that the version of Dionysius Exiguus “is much nearer to the original Greek, although not altogether so.”</p>
