 </p><p class="c34"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p1"></a> <a name="page_152"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 152</font></a> <a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-Page_152"></a><span class="c4"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p1.1"></a>Canon XL.</span></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p2"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p2.1"></a>Bishops</span> called to a synod must not be guilty of contempt, but must attend, and either teach, or be taught, for the reformation of the Church and of others.  And if such an one shall be guilty of contempt, he will condemn himself, unless he be detained by ill health.</p> 
 <p class="c31"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p3"></a><span class="c4"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p3.1"></a>Notes.</span></p> 
 <p class="c36"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p4"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p4.1"></a>Ancient Epitome of Canon XL.</span></p> 
 <p class="c35"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p5"></a>Whoso summoned to a synod shall spurn the invitation, unless hindered by the force of circumstances, shall not be free from blame.</p> 
 <p class="c36"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p6"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p6.1"></a>Hefele.</span></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p7"></a>By <span class="Greek"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p7.1"></a>ἀνωμαλία</span>, illness is commonly understood, and Dionysius Exiguus and Isidore translated it, the former <i>ægritudinem</i>, and the latter <i>infirmitatem</i>.  But Balsamon justly remarks that the term has a wider meaning, and, besides cases of illness includes other unavoidable hinderances or obstacles.</p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="viii.vii.iii.xlv-p8"></a>This Canon is found in the <i>Corpus Juris Canonici</i>, Gratians <i>Decretum</i>, Pars I., Dist. XVIII., c. v.</p>
