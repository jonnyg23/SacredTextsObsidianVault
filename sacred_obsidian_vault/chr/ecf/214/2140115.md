 <p> <a name="viii.v.iv.iv"></a>
 </p><p class="c34"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p1"></a> <span class="c4"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p1.1"></a>Canon IV.</span></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p2"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p2.1"></a>If</span> any one shall maintain, concerning a married presbyter, that is not lawful to partake of the oblation when he offers it, let him be anathema.</p> 
 <p class="c31"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p3"></a><span class="c4"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p3.1"></a>Notes.</span></p> 
 <p class="c36"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p4"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p4.1"></a>Ancient Epitome of Canon IV.</span></p> 
 <p class="c35"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p5"></a>Anathema to him who hesitates to receive communion from presbyters joined in matrimony.</p> 
 <p class="c36"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p6"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p6.1"></a>Hefele.</span></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p7"></a>As is well known, the ancient Church, as now the Greek Church, allowed those clergy who married before their ordination to continue to live in matrimony.  Compare what was said above in the history of the Council of Nicæa, in connection with Paphnutius, concerning the celibacy and marriage of priests in the ancient Church.  Accordingly this canon speaks of those clergy who have wives and live in wedlock; and Baronius, Binius, and Mitter-Müller gave themselves useless trouble <a name="page_94"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 94</font></a> <a name="viii.v.iv.iv-Page_94"></a>in trying to interpret it as only protecting those clergy who, though married, have since their ordination ceased to cohabit with their wives.</p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="viii.v.iv.iv-p8"></a>The so-called <i>Codex Ecclesiæ Romanæ</i> published by Quesnel, which, however, as was shown by the Ballerini, <a name="fr_156"></a><a href="#fn_156"><font size="1">157</font></a>
  is of Gallican and not Roman origin, has not this canon, and consequently it only mentions nineteen canons of Gangra.</p>
