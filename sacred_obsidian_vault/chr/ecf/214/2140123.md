 <p> <a name="viii.v.iv.xii"></a>
 </p><p class="c34"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p1"></a> <span class="c4"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p1.1"></a>Canon XII.</span></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p2"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p2.1"></a>If</span> any one, under pretence of asceticism, should wear a <i>peribolæum</i> and, as if this gave him righteousness, shall despise those who with piety wear the <i>berus</i> and use other common and customary dress, let him be anathema.</p> 
 <p class="c31"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p3"></a><span class="c4"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p3.1"></a>Notes.</span></p> 
 <p class="c36"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p4"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p4.1"></a>Ancient Epitome of Canon XII.</span></p> 
 <p class="c35"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p5"></a>Whoso despises those who wear beruses, let him be anathema.</p> 
 <p class="c36"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p6"></a><span class="sc"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p6.1"></a>Hefele.</span></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p7"></a>The <span class="Greek"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p7.1"></a>βήροι</span> (<i>lacernæ</i>) were the common upper garments worn by men over the tunic; but the <span class="Greek"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p7.2"></a>περιβόλαια</span> were rough mantles worn by philosophers to show their contempt for all luxury.  Socrates (<i>H. E.</i>, ii. 43) and the Synodal Letter of Gangra in its third article say that Eustathius of Sebaste wore the philosophers mantle.  But this canon in no way absolutely rejects a special dress for monks, for it is not the distinctive dress but the proud and superstitious over-estimation of its worth which the Synod here blames.</p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="viii.v.iv.xii-p8"></a>This canon is found in the <i>Corpus Juris Canonici</i>, Gratians <i>Decretum</i>, Pars I., Dist. xxx., c. xv.</p>
