 </p><p class="c46"><a name="v.iii.xxii-p1"></a>  <span class="c12"><a name="v.iii.xxii-p1.1"></a>Chapter XX.</span></p>  
 <p class="c31"><a name="v.iii.xxii-p2"></a><span class="c12"><a name="v.iii.xxii-p2.1"></a>Aristides <a name="fr_2412"></a><a href="#fn_2412"><font size="1">2413</font></a>
 </span> a most eloquent Athenian philosopher, and a disciple of Christ while yet retaining his philosophers garb, presented a work to Hadrian at the same time that Quadratus presented his. The work contained a systematic statement of our doctrine, that is, an <i>Apology</i> for the Christians, which is still extant and is regarded by philologians as a monument to his genius.</p>
