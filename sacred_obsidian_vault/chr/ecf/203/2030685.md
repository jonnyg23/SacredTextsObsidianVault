 <p> <a name="vi.xi.iii.i"></a>
 </p><p class="c65"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p1"></a>   <a name="page_460"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 460</font></a> <a name="vi.xi.iii.i-Page_460"></a><span class="c21"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p1.1"></a>Book II.</span></p>  
 <p class="c72"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p2"></a>1. Jerome says that the defenders of Origen are united in a federation of perjury.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p3"></a>2. Jeromes commentaries on Ephesians follow Origens interpretation of the texts about a secret federation to whom higher truths are to be told.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p4"></a>3. But I follow Christ in condemning all falsehood.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p5"></a>4. Jerome has not only allowed perjury but has practised it.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p6"></a>5. His treatise on Virginity (Ep. xxii to Eustochium) defames all orders of Christians.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p7"></a>6. In his anti-Ciceronian dream he promised never to read or possess heathen books.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p8"></a>7. Yet his works are filled with quotations from them.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p9"></a>8. In his “Best mode of Translation” he relies on the opinions of Cicero and Horace.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p10"></a>9. He confesses his obligations to Porphyry.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p11"></a>8 (2). Jerome at Bethlehem had heathen books copied and taught them to boys.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p12"></a>9 (2). He condemns as heathenish unobjectionable views which he himself holds.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p13"></a>10 (2). He spoke of Paula impiously as the mother-in-law of God.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p14"></a>11. Such impiety is unpardonable.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p15"></a>12. Jeromes boast of his teachers, Didymus and the Jew Baranina.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p16"></a>13. His extravagant praises of Origen.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p17"></a>14. Preface to Origen on Canticles.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p18"></a>15. Preface to Commentary on Micah.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p19"></a>16. Book of Hebrew Names.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p20"></a>17. A story of Origen.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p21"></a>18. Pamphilus the Martyr and his Library.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p22"></a>19. Jerome praises Origen but condemns others for doing the same.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p23"></a>20. Jerome praises the dogmatic as well as the expository works of Origen.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p24"></a>21. Contrast of Jeromes earlier and later attitude towards Origen.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p25"></a>22. The Book of Hebrew Questions.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p26"></a>23. Jeromes attack upon Ambrose.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p27"></a>24. Preface to Didymus on the Holy Spirit.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p28"></a>25. Jerome attacks one Christian writer after another.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p29"></a>26. His treatment of Melania.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p30"></a>27. I never followed Jeromes errors, for which he should do penance.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p31"></a>27 a. But I followed his method of translation.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p32"></a>28. Jerome in condemning me condemns himself.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p33"></a>29. He says I shew Origen to be heretical, yet condemns me.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p34"></a>30. His pretence that the Apology for Origen is not by Pamphilus needs no answer.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p35"></a>31. Others did not translate the <span class="Greek"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p35.1"></a>Περὶ ᾽Αρχῶν</span> because they did not know Greek.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p36"></a>32. Jeromes translation of the Scriptures impugned.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p37"></a>33. Authority of the LXX.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p38"></a>34. Has the Church had spurious Scriptures?</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p39"></a>35. Danger of altering the Versions of Scripture.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p40"></a>36. Origens Hexapla—Its object.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p41"></a>37. St. Pauls method of dealing with erring brethren.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p42"></a>38. How Jerome should have replied to Pammachius.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p43"></a>39. The Books against Jovinian.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p44"></a>40. My translation of the <span class="Greek"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p44.1"></a>Περὶ ᾽Αρχῶν</span> was meant to aid in a good cause.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p45"></a>41, 42, 43. Recapitulation of the Apology.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p46"></a>44. An appeal to Pammachius.</p>  
 <p class="c73"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p47"></a>45, 46. Why my translations of Origen had created offence, but Jeromes not.</p>  
 <p class="c74"><a name="vi.xi.iii.i-p48"></a>47. A Synod, if called on to condemn Origen, must condemn Jerome also.</p>
