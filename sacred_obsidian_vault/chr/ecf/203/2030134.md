 </p><p class="c46"><a name="iv.viii.iv.xxviii-p1"></a>  <span class="c12"><a name="iv.viii.iv.xxviii-p1.1"></a>Chapter XXVIII</span>.—<i>Of the letter written by Valens to the great Valentinianus about the war, and how he replied</i>.</p>  
 <p class="c31"><a name="iv.viii.iv.xxviii-p2"></a><span class="c12"><a name="iv.viii.iv.xxviii-p2.1"></a>The</span> Lord roused the Goths to war, and drew on to the Bosphorus him who knew only how to fight against the pious. Then for the first time the vain man became aware of his own weakness, and sent to his brother to ask for troops. But Valentinian replied that it were impious to help one fighting against God, and right rather to check his rashness. By this the unhappy man was filled with yet greater infatuation, yet he did not withdraw from his rash undertaking, <a name="page_130"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 130</font></a> <a name="iv.viii.iv.xxviii-Page_130"></a>and persisted in ranging himself against the truth. <a name="fr_800"></a><a href="#fn_800"><font size="1">801</font></a>
