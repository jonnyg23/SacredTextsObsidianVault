 </p><p class="c46"><a name="v.iv.lv-p1"></a>  <span class="c12"><a name="v.iv.lv-p1.1"></a>Chapter LIV.</span></p>  
 <p class="c31"><a name="v.iv.lv-p2"></a><span class="c12"><a name="v.iv.lv-p2.1"></a>Nestorius <a name="fr_2662"></a><a href="#fn_2662"><font size="1">2663</font></a>
 </span>  <a name="fr_2663"></a><a href="#fn_2663"><font size="1">2664</font></a>
  the heresiarch, was regarded, while presbyter of the church at Antioch, as a remarkable extemporaneous teacher, <a name="fr_2664"></a><a href="#fn_2664"><font size="1">2665</font></a>
  and <a name="page_395"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 395</font></a> <a name="v.iv.lv-Page_395"></a>composed a great many treatises on various <i>Questions,</i> into which already at that time <a name="fr_2665"></a><a href="#fn_2665"><font size="1">2666</font></a>
  he infused that subtle evil, which afterwards became the poison of acknowledged impiety, veiled meanwhile by moral exhortation. But afterwards, when commended by his eloquence and abstemiousness he had been made pontiff of the church at Constantinople, showing openly what he had for a long while concealed, he became a declared enemy of the church, and wrote a book <i>On the incarnation of the Lord,</i> formed of sixty-two passages from Divine Scripture, used in a perverted meaning. What he maintained in this book may be found in the catalogue of heretics.</p>
