 </p><p class="c53"><a name="vii.v-p1"></a>  <span class="c1"><a name="vii.v-p1.1"></a>Chapter IV.</span></p>  
 <p class="c54"><a name="vii.v-p2"></a>That there is no distinction in the scriptural use of these syllables.</p>  
 <p class="c20"><a name="vii.v-p3"></a>6.  <span class="c14"><a name="vii.v-p3.1"></a>We</span> acknowledge that the word of truth has in many places made use of these expressions; yet we absolutely deny that the freedom of the Spirit is in bondage to the pettiness of Paganism.  On the contrary, we maintain that Scripture varies its expressions as occasion requires, according to the circumstances of the case.  For instance, the phrase “<i>of</i> which” does not always and absolutely, as they suppose, indicate the material, <a name="fr_724"></a><a href="#fn_724"><font size="1">725</font></a>
  but it is more in accordance with the usage of Scripture to apply this term in the case of the Supreme Cause, as in the words “One God, of whom are all things,” <a name="fr_725"></a><a href="#fn_725"><font size="1">726</font></a>
  and again, “All things of God.” <a name="fr_726"></a><a href="#fn_726"><font size="1">727</font></a>
   The word of truth has, however, frequently used this term in the case of the material, as when it says “Thou shalt make an ark of incorruptible wood;” <a name="fr_727"></a><a href="#fn_727"><font size="1">728</font></a>
  and “Thou shalt make the candlestick of pure gold;” <a name="fr_728"></a><a href="#fn_728"><font size="1">729</font></a>
  and “The first man is of the earth, earthy;” <a name="fr_729"></a><a href="#fn_729"><font size="1">730</font></a>
  and “Thou art formed out of clay as I am.” <a name="fr_730"></a><a href="#fn_730"><font size="1">731</font></a>
   But these men, to the end, as we have already remarked, that they may establish the difference of nature, have laid down the law that this phrase befits the Father alone.  This distinction they have originally derived from heathen authorities, but here they have shewn no faithful accuracy of limitation.  To the Son they have in conformity with the teaching of their masters given the title of instrument, and to the Spirit that of place, for they say <i>in</i> the Spirit, and <i>through</i> the Son.  But when they apply “of whom” to God they no longer follow heathen example, but “go over, as they say, to apostolic usage, as it is said, “But of him are ye in Christ Jesus,” <a name="fr_731"></a><a href="#fn_731"><font size="1">732</font></a>
  and “All things of God.” <a name="fr_732"></a><a href="#fn_732"><font size="1">733</font></a>
   What, then, is the result of this systematic discussion?  There is one nature of Cause; another of Instrument; another of Place.  So the Son is by nature distinct from the Father, as the tool from the craftsman; and the Spirit is distinct in so far as place or time is distinguished from the nature of tools or from that of them that handle them.</p>
