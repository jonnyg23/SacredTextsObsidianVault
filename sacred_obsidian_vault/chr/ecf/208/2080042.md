 <p> <a name="vii.xiii"></a>
 </p><p class="c53"><a name="vii.xiii-p1"></a>  <span class="c1"><a name="vii.xiii-p1.1"></a>Chapter XII.</span></p>  
 <p class="c54"><a name="vii.xiii-p2"></a>Against those who assert that the baptism in the name of the Father alone is sufficient.</p>  
 <p class="c20"><a name="vii.xiii-p3"></a>28.  <span class="c14"><a name="vii.xiii-p3.1"></a>Let</span> no one be misled by the fact of the apostles frequently omitting the name of the Father and of the Holy Spirit when making mention of baptism, or on this account imagine that the invocation of the names is not observed.  “As many of you,” he says, “as were baptized into Christ have put on Christ;” <a name="fr_943"></a><a href="#fn_943"><font size="1">944</font></a>
  and again, “As many of you as were baptized into Christ were baptized into his death.” <a name="fr_944"></a><a href="#fn_944"><font size="1">945</font></a>
   For the naming of Christ is the confession of the whole, <a name="fr_945"></a><a href="#fn_945"><font size="1">946</font></a>
  shewing forth as it does the God who gave, the Son who received, and the Spirit who is, the unction. <a name="fr_946"></a><a href="#fn_946"><font size="1">947</font></a>
   So we have learned from Peter, in the Acts, of “Jesus of Nazareth whom God anointed with the Holy Ghost;” <a name="fr_947"></a><a href="#fn_947"><font size="1">948</font></a>
  and in Isaiah, “The Spirit of the Lord is upon me, because the Lord hath anointed me;” <a name="fr_948"></a><a href="#fn_948"><font size="1">949</font></a>
  and the Psalmist, “Therefore God, even thy God, hath anointed thee with the oil of gladness above thy fellows.” <a name="fr_949"></a><a href="#fn_949"><font size="1">950</font></a>
   Scripture, however, in the case of baptism, sometimes plainly mentions the Spirit alone. <a name="fr_950"></a><a href="#fn_950"><font size="1">951</font></a>
 </p>  
 <p class="c21"><a name="vii.xiii-p12"></a>“For into one Spirit,” <a name="fr_951"></a><a href="#fn_951"><font size="1">952</font></a>
  it says, “we were all baptized in <a name="fr_952"></a><a href="#fn_952"><font size="1">953</font></a>
  one body.” <a name="fr_953"></a><a href="#fn_953"><font size="1">954</font></a>
   And in harmony with this are the passages:  “You shall be baptized with the Holy Ghost,” <a name="fr_954"></a><a href="#fn_954"><font size="1">955</font></a>
  and “He shall baptize you with the Holy Ghost.” <a name="fr_955"></a><a href="#fn_955"><font size="1">956</font></a>
   But no one on this account would be justified in calling that baptism a perfect baptism wherein only the name of the Spirit was invoked.  For the tradition that has been given us by the quickening grace must remain for ever inviolate.  He who redeemed our life from destruction <a name="fr_956"></a><a href="#fn_956"><font size="1">957</font></a>
  gave us power of renewal, whereof the cause is ineffable and hidden in mystery, but bringing great salvation to our souls, so that to add or to take away anything <a name="fr_957"></a><a href="#fn_957"><font size="1">958</font></a>
  involves manifestly a falling away from the life everlasting.  If then in baptism the separation of the Spirit from the Father and the Son is perilous to the baptizer, and of no advantage to the baptized, how can the rending asunder of the Spirit from Father and from Son be safe for us? <a name="fr_958"></a><a href="#fn_958"><font size="1">959</font></a>
   Faith and baptism are two kindred and inseparable ways of salvation:  faith is perfected through baptism, baptism is established through faith, and both are completed by the same names.  For as we believe in the Father and the Son and the Holy Ghost, so are we also baptized in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Ghost; first comes the confession, introducing us to salvation, and baptism follows, setting the seal upon our assent.</p>
