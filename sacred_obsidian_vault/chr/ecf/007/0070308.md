 <p> <a name="iii.iv.xxi"></a>  </p><h4 align="center"><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p0.1"></a>General Note by the American Editor.</h4>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p1"></a><span class="sc"><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p1.1"></a>Just</span> here I economize a little spare room to note the cynical <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p1.2"></a>Gibbons ideas about <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p1.3"></a>Lactantius and his works. He quotes him freely, and recognises his Ciceronian Latinity, and even the elegance of his rhetoric, and the spirit and eloquence with which he can garnish the “dismal tale” of coming judgments, based on the Apocalypse. But then, again <a name="fr_1945"></a><a href="#fn_1945"><font size="1">1946</font></a>
  he speaks of him as an “obscure rhetorician,” and affects a doubt as to his sources of information, notably in doubting the conversation between <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p2.1"></a>Galerius and <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p2.2"></a>Diocletian which forced the latter to abdicate. This is before he decides to attribute the work on the <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p2.3"></a>Deaths of Persecutors to somebody else, or, rather, to quote its author ambiguously as <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p2.4"></a>Cæcilius. And here we may insert what he says on this subject, as follows:—  </p>  
 <p class="Blockquote"><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p3"></a>“It is certain that this…was composed and published while <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p3.1"></a>Licinius, sovereign of the East, still preserved the friendship of <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p3.2"></a>Constantine and of the Christians. Every reader of taste must perceive that the style is of a very different and inferior character to that of <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p3.3"></a>Lactantius; and such, indeed, is the judgment of <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p3.4"></a>Le Clerc <a name="fr_1946"></a><a href="#fn_1946"><font size="1">1947</font></a>
  and <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p4.2"></a>Lardner. <a name="fr_1947"></a><a href="#fn_1947"><font size="1">1948</font></a>
  Three arguments (from the title of the book and from the names of <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p5.2"></a>Donatus and <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p5.3"></a>Cæcilius) are produced by the advocates of <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p5.4"></a>Lactantius. <a name="fr_1948"></a><a href="#fn_1948"><font size="1">1949</font></a>
  Each of these proofs is, singly, weak and defective; <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p6.2"></a>but their concurrence has great weight</span>. I have often fluctuated, and shall <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p6.3"></a>tamely</span> <a name="fr_1949"></a><a href="#fn_1949"><font size="1">1950</font></a>
  follow the Colbert <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p7.1"></a>ms. </span>in calling the author, whoever he was, <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p7.2"></a>Cæcilius</span>.”  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p8"></a>After this the critic adheres to this ambiguity. I have no wish to argue otherwise. Quite as important are his notes on the <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p8.1"></a>Institutes. He states the probable conjecture of <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p8.2"></a>two</span> original editions,—the one under <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p8.3"></a>Diocletian, and the other under <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p8.4"></a>Licinius. Then he says: <a name="fr_1950"></a><a href="#fn_1950"><font size="1">1951</font></a>
 —  </p>  
 <p class="Blockquote"><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p10"></a>“I am <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p10.1"></a>almost</span> convinced that <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p10.2"></a>Lactantius dedicated his <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p10.3"></a>Institutions to the sovereign of Gaul at a time when <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p10.4"></a>Galerius, <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p10.5"></a>Maximin, and even <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p10.6"></a>Licinius, persecuted the Christians; that is, between the years <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p10.7"></a>a.d. </span>306 and <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p10.8"></a>a.d. </span>311.”  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p11"></a>On the dubious passages <a name="fr_1951"></a><a href="#fn_1951"><font size="1">1952</font></a>
  he remarks: <a name="fr_1952"></a><a href="#fn_1952"><font size="1">1953</font></a>
 —  </p>  
 <p class="Blockquote"><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p14"></a>“The first and most important of these is, indeed, wanting in twenty-eight <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p14.1"></a>mss.</span>, but is found in nineteen. If we weigh the comparative value of those <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p14.2"></a>mss.</span>, one, … in the King of Frances library, <a name="fr_1953"></a><a href="#fn_1953"><font size="1">1954</font></a>
  may be alleged in its favour. But the passage is omitted in the correct <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p15.1"></a>ms. </span>of Bologna, which the <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p15.2"></a>Père de Montfaucon <a name="fr_1954"></a><a href="#fn_1954"><font size="1">1955</font></a>
  ascribes to the sixth or seventh century. The taste of most of the editors <a name="fr_1955"></a><a href="#fn_1955"><font size="1">1956</font></a>
  has <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p17.2"></a>felt</span> the genuine style of <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p17.3"></a>Lactantius.”  </p>  
 <p class="BodyText"><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p18"></a>Do not many indications point to the natural suggestion of a <span><a name="iii.iv.xxi-p18.1"></a>third</span> original edition, issued after the conversion of <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p18.2"></a>Constantine? Or the questionable passages may be the interpolations of <a name="iii.iv.xxi-p18.3"></a>Lactantius himself.   </p>
