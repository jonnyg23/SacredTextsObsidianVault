 <p class="c35"><a name="x.v.xiv-p2"></a><span class="c1"><a name="x.v.xiv-p2.1"></a>On the Nature of Christ. <a name="fr_3634"></a><a href="#fn_3634"><font size="1">3635</font></a>
 </span></p> 
 <p class="c21"><a name="x.v.xiv-p4"></a>For there is no need, to persons of intelligence, to attempt to prove, from the deeds of Christ subsequent to His baptism, that His soul and His body, His human nature <a name="fr_3635"></a><a href="#fn_3635"><font size="1">3636</font></a>
  like ours, were real, and no phantom of the imagination.  For the deeds done by Christ after His baptism, and especially His miracles, gave indication and assurance to the world of the Deity hidden in His flesh.  For, being at once both God and perfect man likewise, He gave us sure indications of His two natures: <a name="fr_3636"></a><a href="#fn_3636"><font size="1">3637</font></a>
   of His Deity, by His miracles during the three years that elapsed after His baptism; of His humanity, during the thirty <i>similar</i> periods which preceded His baptism, in which, by reason of His low estate <a name="fr_3637"></a><a href="#fn_3637"><font size="1">3638</font></a>
  as regards the flesh, He concealed the signs of His Deity, although He was the true God existing before all ages.</p>
