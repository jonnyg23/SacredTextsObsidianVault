 <p class="c34"><a name="x.iii-p3"></a><span class="c6"><a name="x.iii-p3.1"></a>Quadratus, Bishop of Athens. <a name="fr_3529"></a><a href="#fn_3529"><font size="1">3530</font></a>
 </span></p> 
 <p class="c21"><a name="x.iii-p5"></a>[<span class="c9"><a name="x.iii-p5.1"></a>a.d.</span> 126.]  Quadratus <a name="fr_3530"></a><a href="#fn_3530"><font size="1">3531</font></a>
  is spoken of by Eusebius as a “man of understanding and of Apostolic faith.”  And he celebrates Aristides as a man of similar character.  These were the earliest apologists; both addressed their writings to Hadrian, and they were extant and valued in the churches in the time of Eusebius.</p> 
 <p class="c35"><a name="x.iii-p7"></a><span class="c1"><a name="x.iii-p7.1"></a>From the Apology for the Christian Religion. <a name="fr_3531"></a><a href="#fn_3531"><font size="1">3532</font></a>
 </span></p> 
 <p class="c21"><a name="x.iii-p9"></a><span class="c9"><a name="x.iii-p9.1"></a>Our</span> Saviours works, moreover, were always present:  for they were real, <i>consisting of</i> those who had been healed of their diseases, those who had been raised from the dead; who were not only seen whilst they were being healed and raised up, but were <i>afterwards</i> constantly present.  Nor did they remain only during the sojourn of the Saviour <i>on earth</i>, but also a considerable time after His departure; and, indeed, some of them have survived even down to our own times. <a name="fr_3532"></a><a href="#fn_3532"><font size="1">3533</font></a>
 </p>
