 </p><p class="c35"><a name="vi.iv.ix.xiv-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="vi.iv.ix.xiv-p1.1"></a>Chapter XIV.—Peleus and Thetis, Prometheus, Achilles, and Polyxena.</span></p> 
 <p class="c21"><a name="vi.iv.ix.xiv-p2"></a>“And the marriage banquet, at which Zeus held the feast on the occasion of the marriage of the Nereid Thetis and the beautiful Peleus, has in it this allegory, <a name="fr_1071"></a><a href="#fn_1071"><font size="1">1072</font></a>
 —that you may know, Appion, that you are not the only one from whom I have heard this sort of thing.  The banquet, then, is the world, and the twelve are these heavenly props of the Fates, <a name="fr_1072"></a><a href="#fn_1072"><font size="1">1073</font></a>
  called the Zodiac.  Prometheus is foresight (<span class="c9"><a name="vi.iv.ix.xiv-p4.1"></a>prometheia</span>), by which all things arose; Peleus is clay (<span class="c9"><a name="vi.iv.ix.xiv-p4.2"></a>pelos</span>), namely, that which was <span class="c9"><a name="vi.iv.ix.xiv-p4.3"></a>collected <a name="fr_1073"></a><a href="#fn_1073"><font size="1">1074</font></a>
 </span> from the earth and mixed with Nereis, or water, to produce man; and from the mixing of the two, i.e., water and earth, the first offspring was not begotten, but fashioned complete, and called Achilles, because he never put his lips (<span class="c9"><a name="vi.iv.ix.xiv-p5.2"></a>cheile</span>) to the breast. <a name="fr_1074"></a><a href="#fn_1074"><font size="1">1075</font></a>
   Still in the bloom of life, he is slain by an arrow while desiring to have Polyxena, that is, something other than the truth, and foreign (<span class="c9"><a name="vi.iv.ix.xiv-p6.1"></a>xene</span>) to it, death stealing on him through a wound in his foot.</p>
