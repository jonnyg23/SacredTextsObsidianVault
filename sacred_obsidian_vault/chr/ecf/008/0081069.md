 </p><p class="c35"><a name="vi.iv.xix.xvii-p1"></a> <span class="c1"><a name="vi.iv.xix.xvii-p1.1"></a>Chapter XVII.—The Nature of God.</span></p> 
 <p class="c21"><a name="vi.iv.xix.xvii-p2"></a>“We call Him God whose peculiar attributes cannot belong to the nature of any other; for, as He is called the Unbounded because He is boundless on every side, it must of necessity be the case that it is no other ones peculiar attribute to be called unbounded, as another cannot in like manner be boundless.  But if any one says that it is possible, he is wrong; for two things boundless on every side cannot co-exist, for the one is bounded by the other.  Thus it is in the nature <a name="fr_1302"></a><a href="#fn_1302"><font size="1">1303</font></a>
  of things that the unbegotten is one.  But if he possesses a figure, even in this case the figure is one and incomparable. <a name="fr_1303"></a><a href="#fn_1303"><font size="1">1304</font></a>
   Wherefore He is called the Most High, because, being higher than all, He has the universe subject to Him.”</p>
