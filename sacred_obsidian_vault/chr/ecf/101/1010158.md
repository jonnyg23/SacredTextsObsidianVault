 <p> <a name="vi.X.I"></a>  
 </p><p class="c41"><a name="vi.X.I-p1"></a><span class="c2"><a name="vi.X.I-p1.1"></a>Chapter I.—In God Alone is the Hope and Joy of Man.</span></p>  
 <p class="c10"><a name="vi.X.I-p2"></a>1. <span class="c9"><a name="vi.X.I-p2.1"></a>Let</span> me know Thee, O Thou who knowest me; let me know Thee, as I am known. <a name="fr_812"></a><a href="#fn_812"><font size="1">813</font></a>
  O Thou strength of my soul, enter into it, and prepare it for Thyself, that Thou mayest have and hold it without “spot or wrinkle.” <a name="fr_813"></a><a href="#fn_813"><font size="1">814</font></a>
  This is my hope, “therefore have I spoken;” <a name="fr_814"></a><a href="#fn_814"><font size="1">815</font></a>
  and in this hope do I rejoice, when I rejoice soberly. Other things of this life ought the less to be sorrowed for, the more they are sorrowed for; and ought the more to be sorrowed for, the less men do sorrow for them. For behold, “Thou desirest truth,” <a name="fr_815"></a><a href="#fn_815"><font size="1">816</font></a>
  seeing that he who does it “cometh to the light.” <a name="fr_816"></a><a href="#fn_816"><font size="1">817</font></a>
  This wish I to do in confession in my heart before Thee, and in my writing before many witnesses.</p>
