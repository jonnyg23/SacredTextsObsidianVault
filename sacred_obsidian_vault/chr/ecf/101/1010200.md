 </p><p class="c41"><a name="vi.X.XLIII-p1"></a><span class="c2"><a name="vi.X.XLIII-p1.1"></a>Chapter XLIII.—That Jesus Christ, at the Same Time God and Man, is the True and Most Efficacious Mediator.</span></p>  
 <p class="c10"><a name="vi.X.XLIII-p2"></a>68. But the true Mediator, whom in Thy secret mercy Thou hast pointed out to the humble, and didst send, that by His example <a name="fr_976"></a><a href="#fn_976"><font size="1">977</font></a>
  also they might learn the same humility—that “Mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus,” <a name="fr_977"></a><a href="#fn_977"><font size="1">978</font></a>
  appeared between mortal sinners and the immortal Just One—mortal with men, just with God; that because the reward of righteousness is life and peace, He might, by righteousness conjoined with God, cancel the death of justified sinners, which He willed to have in common with them. <a name="fr_978"></a><a href="#fn_978"><font size="1">979</font></a>
  Hence He was pointed out to holy men of old; to the intent that they, through faith in His Passion to come, <a name="fr_979"></a><a href="#fn_979"><font size="1">980</font></a>
  even as we through faith in that which is past, might be saved. For as man He was Mediator; but as the Word He was not between, <a name="fr_980"></a><a href="#fn_980"><font size="1">981</font></a>
  because equal to God, and God with God, and together with the Holy Spirit <a name="fr_981"></a><a href="#fn_981"><font size="1">982</font></a>
  one God.</p>  
 <p class="c10"><a name="vi.X.XLIII-p9"></a>69. How hast Thou loved us, <a name="fr_982"></a><a href="#fn_982"><font size="1">983</font></a>
  O good Father, who sparedst not Thine only Son, but deliveredst Him up for us wicked ones! <a name="fr_983"></a><a href="#fn_983"><font size="1">984</font></a>
  How hast Thou loved us, for whom He, who thought it no robbery to be equal with Thee, “became obedient unto death, even the death of the cross;” <a name="fr_984"></a><a href="#fn_984"><font size="1">985</font></a>
  He alone “free among the dead,” <a name="fr_985"></a><a href="#fn_985"><font size="1">986</font></a>
  that had power to lay down His life, and power to take it again; <a name="fr_986"></a><a href="#fn_986"><font size="1">987</font></a>
  for us was He unto Thee both Victor and Victim, and the Victor as being the Victim; for us was He unto Thee both Priest and Sacrifice, and Priest as being the Sacrifice; of slaves making us Thy sons, by being born of Thee, and serving us. Rightly, then, is my hope strongly fixed on Him, that Thou wilt heal all my diseases <a name="fr_987"></a><a href="#fn_987"><font size="1">988</font></a>
  by Him who sitteth at Thy right hand and maketh intercession for us; <a name="fr_988"></a><a href="#fn_988"><font size="1">989</font></a>
  else should I utterly despair. <a name="fr_989"></a><a href="#fn_989"><font size="1">990</font></a>
  For numerous and great are my infirmities, yea, numerous and great are they; but Thy medicine is greater. We might think that Thy Word was removed from union with man, and despair of ourselves had He not been “made flesh and dwelt among us.” <a name="fr_990"></a><a href="#fn_990"><font size="1">991</font></a>
 </p>  
 <p class="c10"><a name="vi.X.XLIII-p19"></a>70. Terrified by my sins and the load of my misery, I had resolved in my heart, and meditated flight into the wilderness; <a name="fr_991"></a><a href="#fn_991"><font size="1">992</font></a>
  but Thou didst forbid me, and didst strengthen me, saying, therefore, Christ “died for all, that they which live should not henceforth live unto themselves, but unto Him which died for them.” <a name="fr_992"></a><a href="#fn_992"><font size="1">993</font></a>
  Behold, O Lord, I cast my care upon Thee, <a name="fr_993"></a><a href="#fn_993"><font size="1">994</font></a>
  that I may live, and “behold wondrous things out of Thy law.” <a name="fr_994"></a><a href="#fn_994"><font size="1">995</font></a>
  Thou knowest my unskilfulness and my infirmities; teach me, and heal me. Thine only Son—He “in whom are hid all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge” <a name="fr_995"></a><a href="#fn_995"><font size="1">996</font></a>
 —hath redeemed me with His blood. Let not the proud speak evil of me, <a name="fr_996"></a><a href="#fn_996"><font size="1">997</font></a>
  because I consider my ransom, and eat and drink, and distribute; and poor, desire to be satisfied from Him, together with those who eat and are satisfied, and they praise the Lord that seek him. <a name="fr_997"></a><a href="#fn_997"><font size="1">998</font></a>
 </p>

 <p class="endnote"><a name="vi.X.XLIII-p5"></a> Not that our Lord is to be supposed, as some have held, to have been under the law of death in Adam, because “in Adam all die” (<a href="../../../bib/kjv/co1015.htm#022">1 Cor. 15.22</a><a name="vi.X.XLIII-p5.1"></a>; see the whole of c. 23, in <i>De</i> <i>Civ. This is the explanation Augustin gives in his comment on  <a href="../../../bib/kjv/psa085.htm#005">Ps. 85.5</a><a name="vi.X.XLIII-p5.4"></a> (quoted in the next section) of Christs being “free among the dead.” So also in his <i>De Trin.</i> xiii. 18, he says he was thus free because “solus enim a debito mortis liber est mortuus.” The true analogy between the first and second Adam is surely then to be found in our Lords being free from the law of death by reason of His divine nature, and Adam before his transgression being able to avert death by partaking of the Tree of Life. Christ was, it is true, a child of Adam, but a child of Adam miraculously born. See note 3, p. 73, above.</p>
