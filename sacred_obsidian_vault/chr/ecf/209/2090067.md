 </p><p class="c33"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p1"></a> <span class="c12"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p1.1"></a>Chapter XVI</span>.—<i>Concerning Anger.</i></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p2"></a>Anger is the ebullition <a name="fr_1827"></a><a href="#fn_1827"><font size="1">1828</font></a>
  of the hearts blood <a name="fr_1828"></a><a href="#fn_1828"><font size="1">1829</font></a>
  produced by bilious exhalation or turbidity. Hence it is that the words <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p4.2"></a>χολή</span> and <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p4.3"></a>χόλος</span> <a name="fr_1829"></a><a href="#fn_1829"><font size="1">1830</font></a>
  are both used in the sense of anger. Anger is sometimes lust for vengeance. For when we are wronged or think that we are wronged, we are distressed, and there arises this mixture of desire and anger.</p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p6"></a>There are three forms of anger:  rage, which the Greeks also call <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p6.1"></a>χολή</span> or <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p6.2"></a>χόλος</span>, <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p6.3"></a>μῆνις</span> and <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p6.4"></a>κότος</span>. When anger arises and begins to be roused, it is called rage or <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p6.5"></a>χολή</span> or <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p6.6"></a>χόλος</span>. Wrath again implies that the bile endures, that is to say, that the memory of the wrong abides:  and indeed the Greek word for it, <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p6.7"></a>μῆνις</span> is derived from <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p6.8"></a>μένειν</span>, and means what abides and is transferred to memory. Rancour, on the other hand, implies watching for a suitable moment for revenge, and the Greek word for it is <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p6.9"></a>κότος</span> from <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p6.10"></a>κεῖσθαι</span>.</p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-p7"></a>Anger further is the satellite of reason, the vindicator of desire. For when we long after anything and are opposed in our desire by some one, we are angered at that person, as though we had been wronged:  and reason evidently deems that there are just grounds for displeasure in what has happened, in the <a name="page_34b"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 34b</font></a> <a name="iii.iv.ii.xvi-Page_34b"></a>case of those who, like us, have in the natural course of things to guard their own position.</p>
