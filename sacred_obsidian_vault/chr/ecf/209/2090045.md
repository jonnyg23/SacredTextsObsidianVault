 <p> <a name="iii.iv.i.ix"></a>
 </p><p class="c33"><a name="iii.iv.i.ix-p1"></a> <a name="page_12b"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 12b</font></a> <a name="iii.iv.i.ix-Page_12b"></a><span class="c12"><a name="iii.iv.i.ix-p1.1"></a>Chapter IX</span>.—<i>Concerning what is affirmed about God.</i></p> 
 <p class="c18"><a name="iii.iv.i.ix-p2"></a>The Deity is simple and uncompound. But that which is composed of many and different elements is compound. If, then, we should speak of the qualities of being uncreate and without beginning and incorporeal and immortal and everlasting and good and creative and so forth as essential differences in the case of God, that which is composed of so many qualities will not be simple but must be compound. But this is impious in the extreme. Each then of the affirmations about God should be thought of as signifying not what He is in essence, but either something that it is impossible to make plain, or some relation to some of those things which are contrasts or some of those things that follow the nature, or an energy <a name="fr_1578"></a><a href="#fn_1578"><font size="1">1579</font></a>
 .</p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="iii.iv.i.ix-p4"></a>It appears then <a name="fr_1579"></a><a href="#fn_1579"><font size="1">1580</font></a>
  that the most proper of all the names given to God is “He that is,” as He Himself said in answer to Moses on the mountain, <i>Say to the sons of Israel, He that is hath sent Me</i> <a name="fr_1580"></a><a href="#fn_1580"><font size="1">1581</font></a>
 . For He keeps all being in His own embrace <a name="fr_1581"></a><a href="#fn_1581"><font size="1">1582</font></a>
 , like a sea of essence infinite and unseen. Or as the holy Dionysius says, “He that is good <a name="fr_1582"></a><a href="#fn_1582"><font size="1">1583</font></a>
 .” For one cannot say of God that He has being in the first place and goodness in the second.</p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="iii.iv.i.ix-p9"></a>The second name of God is <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.i.ix-p9.1"></a>ὁ</span> <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.i.ix-p9.2"></a>Θεός</span>, derived from <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.i.ix-p9.3"></a>θέειν</span> <a name="fr_1583"></a><a href="#fn_1583"><font size="1">1584</font></a>
 , to run, because He courses through all things, or from <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.i.ix-p10.2"></a>αἴθειν</span>, to burn:  <i>For God is a fire consuming all evil</i> <a name="fr_1584"></a><a href="#fn_1584"><font size="1">1585</font></a>
 :  or from <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.iv.i.ix-p11.2"></a>θεᾶσθαι</span>, because He is all-seeing <a name="fr_1585"></a><a href="#fn_1585"><font size="1">1586</font></a>
 :  for nothing can escape Him, and over all He keepeth watch. For He saw all things before they were, holding them timelessly in His thoughts; and each one conformably to His voluntary and timeless thought <a name="fr_1586"></a><a href="#fn_1586"><font size="1">1587</font></a>
 , which constitutes predetermination and image and pattern, comes into existence at the predetermined time <a name="fr_1587"></a><a href="#fn_1587"><font size="1">1588</font></a>
 .</p> 
 <p class="c19"><a name="iii.iv.i.ix-p15"></a>The first name then conveys the notion of His existence and of the nature of His existence:  while the second contains the idea of energy. Further, the terms without beginning, incorruptible, unbegotten, as also uncreate, incorporeal, unseen, and so forth, explain what He is not:  that is to say, they tell us that His being had no beginning, that He is not corruptible, nor created, nor corporeal, nor visible <a name="fr_1588"></a><a href="#fn_1588"><font size="1">1589</font></a>
 . Again, goodness and justice and piety and such like names belong to the nature <a name="fr_1589"></a><a href="#fn_1589"><font size="1">1590</font></a>
 , but do not explain His actual essence. Finally, Lord and King and names of that class indicate a relationship with their contrasts:  for the name Lord has reference to those over whom the lord rules, and the name King to those under kingly authority, and the name Creator to the creatures, and the name Shepherd to the sheep he tends.</p>
