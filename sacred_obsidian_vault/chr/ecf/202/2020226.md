 </p><p class="c32"><a name="ii.x.ix-p1"></a><span class="c11"><a name="ii.x.ix-p1.1"></a>Chapter IX</span>.—<i>The Bishops of Antioch and Rome.</i></p>  
 <p class="c24"><a name="ii.x.ix-p2"></a><span class="c11"><a name="ii.x.ix-p2.1"></a>During</span> this period upon the death of Flavian <a name="fr_927"></a><a href="#fn_927"><font size="1">928</font></a>
   Porphyry received the episcopate of Antioch, and after him Alexander <a name="fr_928"></a><a href="#fn_928"><font size="1">929</font></a>
   was set over that church. But at Rome, Damasus having held that bishopric eighteen years Siricius succeeded him; <a name="fr_929"></a><a href="#fn_929"><font size="1">930</font></a>
   and Siricius having presided there fifteen years, Anastasius held sway over the church for three years; after Anastasius Innocent [was promoted to the same see]. He was the first persecutor of the Novatians at Rome, and many of their churches he took away. </p>
