 </p><p class="c47"><a name="iii.ix.xx-p1"></a>  <span class="c11"><a name="iii.ix.xx-p1.1"></a>Chapter XX.—</span><i>The Rulers of the Church of Antioch.</i></p>  
 <p class="c18"><a name="iii.ix.xx-p2"></a>1. <span class="c13"><a name="iii.ix.xx-p2.1"></a>At</span> that time also in the church of Antioch, Theophilus <a name="fr_1212"></a><a href="#fn_1212"><font size="1">1213</font></a>
  was well known as the sixth from the apostles. For Cornelius, <a name="fr_1213"></a><a href="#fn_1213"><font size="1">1214</font></a>
  who succeeded Hero, <a name="fr_1214"></a><a href="#fn_1214"><font size="1">1215</font></a>
  was the fourth, and after him Eros, <a name="fr_1215"></a><a href="#fn_1215"><font size="1">1216</font></a>
  the fifth in order, had held the office of bishop.</p>

 <p class="endnote"><a name="iii.ix.xx-p4"></a> Of the life and character of Cornelius and Eros we know nothing. The <i>Chron.</i> of Eusebius puts the accession of Cornelius into the twelfth year of Trajan (128 <span class="c13"><a name="iii.ix.xx-p4.1"></a>a.d.</span>), and the accession of his successor Eros into the fifth year of Antoninus Pius (142). These dates, however, are quite unreliable, and we have no means of correcting them (see Harnacks <i>Zeit des Ignatius,</i> p. 12 sqq.). Theophilus, the successor of Eros we have reason to think became bishop about the middle of Marcus Aurelius reign and hence the <i>Chron.,</i> which puts his accession into the ninth year of that reign, (169 <span class="c13"><a name="iii.ix.xx-p4.2"></a>a.d.</span>) cannot be far out of the way. This gives us the approximate date for the death of Eros.</p>
