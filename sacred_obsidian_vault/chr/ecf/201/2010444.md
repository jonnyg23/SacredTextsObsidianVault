 </p><p class="c47"><a name="iv.vi.i.lix-p1"></a>  <span class="c11"><a name="iv.vi.i.lix-p1.1"></a>Chapter LIX</span>.—<i>That Maximin, blinded by Disease, issued an Edict in Favor of the Christians.</i></p>  
 <p class="c18"><a name="iv.vi.i.lix-p2"></a><span class="c11"><a name="iv.vi.i.lix-p2.1"></a>And</span> still the stroke of God continued heavy upon him, so that his eyes protruded and fell from their sockets, leaving him quite blind: and thus he suffered, by a most righteous retribution, the very same punishment which he had been <a name="page_499"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 499</font></a> <a name="iv.vi.i.lix-Page_499"></a>the first to devise for the martyrs of God. At length, however, surviving even these sufferings, he too implored pardon of the God of the Christians, and confessed his impious fighting against God: he too recanted, as the former persecutor had done; and by laws and ordinances explicitly acknowledged his error in worshiping those whom he had accounted gods, declaring that he now knew, by positive experience, that the God of the Christians was the only true God. These were facts which Licinius had not merely received on the testimony of others, but of which he had himself had personal knowledge: and yet, as though his understanding had been obscured by some dark cloud of error, persisted in the same evil course.</p>
