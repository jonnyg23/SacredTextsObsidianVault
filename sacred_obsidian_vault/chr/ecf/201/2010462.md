 </p><p class="c47"><a name="iv.vi.ii.xvii-p1"></a>  <span class="c11"><a name="iv.vi.ii.xvii-p1.1"></a>Chapter XVII</span>.—<i>Constantines Victory.</i></p>  
 <p class="c18"><a name="iv.vi.ii.xvii-p2"></a><span class="c11"><a name="iv.vi.ii.xvii-p2.1"></a>But</span> as soon as he perceived that his adversaries persisted in their resolution, and were already drawing their swords, he gave free scope to his indignation, and by a single charge <a name="fr_3176"></a><a href="#fn_3176"><font size="1">3177</font></a>
  overthrew in a moment the entire body of the enemy, thus triumphing at once over them and their gods.</p>

 <p class="endnote"><a name="iv.vi.ii.xvii-p3"></a> “With one shout and charge.” This does not agree with the account of the final struggle by which Licinius came into Constantines power, as generally given, and lends some probability to the view that after he had been captured he again revolted.</p>
