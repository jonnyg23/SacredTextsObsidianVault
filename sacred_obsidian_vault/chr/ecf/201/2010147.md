 </p><p class="c47"><a name="iii.x.vii-p1"></a>  <a name="page_221"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 221</font></a> <a name="iii.x.vii-Page_221"></a><span class="c11"><a name="iii.x.vii-p1.1"></a>Chapter VI.—</span><i>Catalogue of the Bishops of Rome.</i></p>  
 <p class="c18"><a name="iii.x.vii-p2"></a>1. “<span class="c13"><a name="iii.x.vii-p2.1"></a>The</span> blessed apostles <a name="fr_1437"></a><a href="#fn_1437"><font size="1">1438</font></a>
  having founded and established the church, entrusted the office of the episcopate to Linus. <a name="fr_1438"></a><a href="#fn_1438"><font size="1">1439</font></a>
  Paul speaks of this Linus in his Epistles to Timothy. <a name="fr_1439"></a><a href="#fn_1439"><font size="1">1440</font></a>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="iii.x.vii-p6"></a>2. Anencletus <a name="fr_1440"></a><a href="#fn_1440"><font size="1">1441</font></a>
  succeeded him, and after Anencletus, in the third place from the apostles, Clement <a name="fr_1441"></a><a href="#fn_1441"><font size="1">1442</font></a>
  received the episcopate. He had seen and conversed with the blessed apostles, <a name="fr_1442"></a><a href="#fn_1442"><font size="1">1443</font></a>
  and their preaching was still sounding in his ears, and their tradition was still before his eyes. Nor was he alone in this, for many who had been taught by the apostles yet survived.</p>  
 <p><a name="iii.x.vii-p10"></a>3. In the times of Clement, a serious dissension having arisen among the brethren in Corinth, <a name="fr_1443"></a><a href="#fn_1443"><font size="1">1444</font></a>
  the church of Rome sent a most suitable letter to the Corinthians, <a name="fr_1444"></a><a href="#fn_1444"><font size="1">1445</font></a>
  reconciling them in peace, renewing their faith, and proclaiming <a name="fr_1445"></a><a href="#fn_1445"><font size="1">1446</font></a>
  the doctrine lately received from the apostles.” <a name="fr_1446"></a><a href="#fn_1446"><font size="1">1447</font></a>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="iii.x.vii-p15"></a>4. A little farther on he says: <a name="fr_1447"></a><a href="#fn_1447"><font size="1">1448</font></a>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="iii.x.vii-p17"></a>“Evarestus <a name="fr_1448"></a><a href="#fn_1448"><font size="1">1449</font></a>
  succeeded Clement, and Alexander, <a name="fr_1449"></a><a href="#fn_1449"><font size="1">1450</font></a>
  Evarestus. Then Xystus, <a name="fr_1450"></a><a href="#fn_1450"><font size="1">1451</font></a>
  the sixth from the apostles, was appointed. After him Telesphorus, <a name="fr_1451"></a><a href="#fn_1451"><font size="1">1452</font></a>
  who suffered martyrdom gloriously; then Hyginus; <a name="fr_1452"></a><a href="#fn_1452"><font size="1">1453</font></a>
  then Pius; <a name="fr_1453"></a><a href="#fn_1453"><font size="1">1454</font></a>
  and after him Anicetus; <a name="fr_1454"></a><a href="#fn_1454"><font size="1">1455</font></a>
  Soter <a name="fr_1455"></a><a href="#fn_1455"><font size="1">1456</font></a>
  succeeded Anicetus; and now, in the twelfth place from the apostles, Eleutherus <a name="fr_1456"></a><a href="#fn_1456"><font size="1">1457</font></a>
  holds the office of bishop.</p>  
 <p><a name="iii.x.vii-p27"></a>5. In the same order and succession <a name="fr_1457"></a><a href="#fn_1457"><font size="1">1458</font></a>
  the tradition in the Church and the preaching of the truth has descended from the apostles unto us.”</p>

 <p class="endnote"><a name="iii.x.vii-p13"></a> <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.x.vii-p13.1"></a>ἀ</span><span class="Greek"><a name="iii.x.vii-p13.2"></a>νεοῦσα τὴν πίστιν αὐτῶν καὶ ἣν νεωστὶ ἀπὸ τῶν ἀποστόλων παρ€δοσιν εἰλήφει</span>. The last word being in the singular, the tradition must be that received by the Roman, not by the Corinthian church (as it is commonly understood), and hence it is necessary to supply some verb which shall govern <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.x.vii-p13.3"></a>παρ€δοσιν</span>, for it is at least very harsh to say that the Roman church, in its epistle to the Corinthians “renewed” the faith which <i>it</i> had received. The truth is, that both in Rufinus and in Irenæus an extra participle is found (in the former <i>exprimens,</i> in the latter <i>annuntians</i>), and Stroth has in consequence ventured to insert the word <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.x.vii-p13.4"></a>καταγγέλουσα</span> in his text. I have likewise, for the sake of the sense, inserted the word <i>proclaiming,</i> not thereby intending to imply, however, the belief that <span class="Greek"><a name="iii.x.vii-p13.5"></a>καταγγέλουσα</span> stood in the original text of Eusebius.</p>
 <a name="fn_1446"></a><a href="2010147.htm#fr_1446">221:1447</a> <a name="iii.x.vii-p13.6"></a>
 <p class="endnote"><a name="iii.x.vii-p14"></a> It is interesting to notice how strictly Eusebius carries out his principle of taking historical matter wherever he can find it, but of omitting all doctrinal statements and discussions. The few sentences which follow in Irenæus are of a doctrinal nature, and in the form of a brief polemic against Gnosticism.</p>
