 <h2 align="center"><i>XII<br>
 POVERTY AND RICHES</i></h2>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 192------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_192"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 192</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>293 <font size="1">(II. 56)</font><br>
 <big>POVERTY AND RICHES</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>The man who, what he hath, hath not,<br>
 To all things stands indifferent,<br>
 He is most poor when he is rich,<br>
 Most rich when he is indigent.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>294 <font size="1">(III. 69)</font><br>
 <big>THE SAINT'S WEALTH</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Be poor! On earth the Saint hath naught<br>
 Save one thing, which unwillingly<br>
 He needs must call his own, to wit,<br>
 This Body of Mortality.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>295 <font size="1">(II. 148)</font><br>
 <big>THE POOR IN SPIRIT</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>That man is truly poor who stands<br>
 Detached from all things, loose, adrift:<br>
 Were God to offer him Himself<br>
 I know he would refuse the gift.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>296 <font size="1">(III. 139)</font><br>
 <big>MAN FINDETH WHAT HE SEEKETH</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>The poor man seeketh God, the rich man seeketh wealth:<br>
 'Tis gold indeed the poor man finds, the rich man filth.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 193------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_193"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 193</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>297 <font size="1">(V. 157)</font><br>
 <big>THE RICH MAN IS TRULY POOR</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>When of his poverty he maketh much ado,<br>
 Doubt not the Rich Man's word—he speaketh what is true.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>298 <font size="1">(VI. 185)</font><br>
 <big>OUR WEALTH MUST BE WITHIN US</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Be all thy Wealth within thyself.<br>
 If lie thy riches otherwhere,<br>
 Yea, though thy fortune were the world,<br>
 Then art thou rich in naught but Care.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>299 <font size="1">(VI. 189)</font><br>
 <big>TO DESIRE EVERYTHING IS TO HAVE NOTHING</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Man, if desire for Everything<br>
 Possesseth all thy thought,<br>
 Then art thou poor as beggars are<br>
 And still possessest Naught.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>300 <font size="1">(VI. 86)</font><br>
 <big>WHO CRAVETH ALL HATH NAUGHT</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Who craveth Naught hath All. Who yearneth to possess<br>
 The riches of the world, he still is penniless.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 194------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_194"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 194</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>301 <font size="1">(VI. 84)</font><br>
 <big>THE GREATEST WEALTH AND GAIN</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Most Wealth hath he who Wealth doth most disdain,<br>
 And gaineth most who most renounceth Gain.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>302 <font size="1">(VI. 168)</font><br>
 <big>THE WISE MAN HATH NAUGHT IN COFFERS</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Naught doth the Wise Man heard in guarded treasuries:<br>
 Riches that can be lost he never counteth his.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>303 <font size="1">(VI. 99)</font><br>
 <big>THE TREASURY OF THE WISE MAN AND THE MISER</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Wise Men are wisely rich—the put<br>
 Their gold into a treasure-chest:<br>
 The Miser's gold is in his heart—<br>
 His heart hath never any rest.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>304 <font size="1">(VI. 167)</font><br>
 <big>HE WHO IS TRULY RICH</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Much having is not being rich.<br>
  The Wealthy Man is he<br>
 Who views the loss of all he hath<br>
  With equanimity.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>

 <a name="page_195"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 195</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>305 <font size="1">(VI. 100)</font><br>
 <big>THE WISE MAN FORESTALLS THE THIEF</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>The Wise Man doth not wait until<br>
  His fortune is bereft:<br>
 He steals his foretune from himself<br>
  And so forestalls the theft.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>306 <font size="1">(VI. 103)</font><br>
 <big>NO PROGRESS WHEN HEAVILY LADEN</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Storm-foundered seamen jettison<br>
 The weightiest cargo in the hold;<br>
 And thinkest thou to win to Heaven<br>
 O'erladen with a freight of gold?<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>307 <font size="1">(VI. 179)</font><br>
 <big>HOW MISERS AND WISE MEN ACT</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>The Miser leaves his gold at last,<br>
 Heirs seize on his inheritance:<br>
 The Wise Man to the other world<br>
 Sends on his riches in advance.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>308 <font size="1">(V. 132)</font><br>
 <big>THE UNCONCERNED MAN SUFFERETH NO HURT</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>The man who in this world claims nothing for his own,<br>
 Suffereth no great loss when all his house falls down.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>

 <a name="page_196"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 196</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>309 <font size="1">(V. 156)</font><br>
 <big>WHO DESIRETH MUCH, LACKETH MUCH</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Who hath enough, hath all. Who craveth more and more,<br>
 Betrayeth by his want how scanty is his store.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>310 <font size="1">(VI. 181)</font><br>
 <big>THE ESTIMATION OF THE FOOL AND THE WISE MAN</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>The Fool thinks he is rich enough<br>
 If he possess a sack of gold:<br>
 The Wise Man knoweth he is poor<br>
 With riches not the world can hold.<br></td></tr></table>
