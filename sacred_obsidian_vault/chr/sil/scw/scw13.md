 <h2 align="center"><i>XIII<br>
 MAN AND THE WORLD</i></h2>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 198------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_198"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 198</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>311 <font size="1">(I. 140)</font><br>
 <big>MAN IS ALL THINGS</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Man is the Sum of Things. If he lack but a jot,<br>
 The full scope of his wealth surely he knoweth not.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>312 <font size="1">(IV. 146)</font><br>
 <big>THE HIGHEST NOBILITY OF MAN</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>This is my true nobility,<br>
 That I have power to be, while still<br>
 On earth, a King, an Emperor,<br>
 A God, or anything I will.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>313 <font size="1">(III. 111)</font><br>
 <big>THE HUMAN HEART</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>God, Devil and the World would all<br>
 Invade my heart—such rivalry<br>
 Doth prove it to be wondrous fair<br>
 And of a high nobility.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>314 <font size="1">(V. 181)</font><br>
 <big>THE GREEDIEST</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>  How greedy is a Heart!<br>
 A thousand Worlds were all too few—<br>
 'Twould crave to have them all at once<br>
   Ay, and more too.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 199------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_199"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 199</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>315 <font size="1">(V. 170)</font><br>
 <big>ALL WORKS ARE ALIKE TO GOD</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Man's works are level before God.<br>
 To Him the Saint is full as dear<br>
 When he lifts up his cup to drink<br>
 As when he lifts his voice in prayer.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>316 <font size="1">(V. 334)</font><br>
 <big>GOD VALUETH THE WORK ACCORDING TO THE WORKER</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Know this for sure—the good man's sleep<br>
 Is more of worth in God's own sight<br>
 Than all the cry the sinner makes,<br>
 Chanting and praying through the night.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>317 <font size="1">(V. 174)</font><br>
 <big>WHAT THE SAINT DOTH, GOD DOTH IN HIM</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>'Tis God Himself who in the Saint<br>
 Enacts the Saint's activities,<br>
 God walks, stands, sleeps, wakes, eats and drinks,<br>
 And the Saint's courage too is His.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>318 <font size="1">(VI. 154)</font><br>
 <big>GOD DOETH ALL HIMSELF</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Himself God lays the cord to the shaft,<br>
 Himself draws back the bow—that's why,<br>
 When He Himself releases it,<br>
 The arrow cleaves the target's eye.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 200------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_200"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 200</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>319 <font size="1">(I. 194)</font><br>
 <big>WHAT ART THOU IN RESPECT OF GOD?</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Think not thou standest high with God<br>
 Because thy works His favour claim:<br>
 Even the labours of the Saints<br>
 To Him are trifling as a game.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>320 <font size="1">(I. 119)</font><br>
 <big>THOU MUST GO TO THE SOURCE</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Water is pure and clean when at the well-head quaffed:<br>
 Drink'st thou not at the Spring, there's danger in the draught.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>321 <font size="1">(IV. 190)</font><br>
 <big>THE CROSS MANIFESTETH WHAT IS HIDDEN</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Thou canst not know thyself in ease and cheer of heart;<br>
 The Cross first showeth thee the man thou inly art.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>322 <font size="1">(V. 275)</font><br>
 <big>SUFFERING IS MORE PROFITABLE THAN JOY</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Man, hadst thou only known the good<br>
 And profit Suffering can bestow,<br>
 Surely thou wouldst have chosen it<br>
 Rather than Pleasure, long ago.<br></td></tr></table>

 <p> </p>
 
 <p>323 <font size="1">(IV. 79)</font><br>
 <big>THE BEST FRIEND AND ENEMY</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>My Body is my dearest Friend,<br>
 Likewise my bitterest Enemy;<br>
 It bears me up and binds me down,<br>
 As it doth list, contrarily.<br>
 I hate it, yet I love it too,<br>
 And when death comes to part us twain,<br>
 How joyful will that parting be!<br>
 And how that parting will be pain!<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>324 <font size="1">(V. 135)</font><br>
 <big>READINESS MODIFIES THE BLOW</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Is not the Wise Man sad at heart<br>
 When Sorrow knocketh at his door?—<br>
 He's made all ready long ago<br>
 To welcome such a Visitor.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>325 <font size="1">(III. 88)</font><br>
 <big>THERE MUST BE CRUCIFIXION</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Who in the world-to-come would go rose-gathering<br>
 Must first know well how sharply this world's briars sting.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 202------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_202"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 202</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>326 <font size="1">(III. 89)</font><br>
 <big>BEAUTY</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Beauty I dearly love, and yet<br>
 I think that Beauty scarce adorns<br>
 Aught that I see, unless I find<br>
 It always set about with thorns.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>327 <font size="1">(V. 114)</font><br>
 <big>IT IS FOLLY TO STRIVE FOR HONOUR</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>What fools are we who keenly strive<br>
  Honour to win and keep.<br>
 God giveth Honour but to him<br>
  Who holdeth Honour cheap.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>328 <font size="1">(V. 141)</font><br>
 <big>THE ACTION OF THE WORLD IS A TRAGEDY</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Friend, envy not the World—it goes it own sweet way,<br>
 Yet is its Action nothing but a Tragic Play.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>329 <font size="1">(V. 263)</font><br>
 <big>HELL MUST BE TASTED</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>None can escape the throat of Hell.<br>
 The path thereto once must thou tread,<br>
 And if alive thou go not in,<br>
 Then surely shalt thou go in dead.<br></td></tr></table>

 <p> </p>
 
 <p>330 <font size="1">(III. 146)</font><br>
 <big>THE MIGHT OF SOULS</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>The Soul is very strong—God's self<br>
 Acknowledgeth 'tis even so.<br>
 He cannot break from her embrace<br>
 Unless she please to let Him go.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>331 <font size="1">(VI. 82)</font><br>
 <big>THY WILL MAKETH THEE LOST</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>By thy own Will thou'rt lost, by thy own Will thou'rt found,<br>
 Thou by thy Will art freed, and by thy Will art bound.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>332 <font size="1">(V. 98)</font><br>
 <big>GOD CANNOT CONTROL THE WILL</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Naught is there mightier than God;<br>
 Yet hath He not the might to turn<br>
 My Will from willing what it will,<br>
 My yearning as it needs must yearn?<br></td></tr></table>
