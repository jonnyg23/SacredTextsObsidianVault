 <h2 align="center"><i>XI<br>
 NATURE</i></h2>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 182------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_182"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 182</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>261 <font size="1">(I. 193)</font><br>
 <big>THE CREATURE IS TRULY IN GOD</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Rather in God than in itself<br>
 The Creature hath its true abode:<br>
 It perishes, yet evermore<br>
 Abides eternally in God.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>262 <font size="1">(II. 109)</font><br>
 <big>THE WORLD DOTH NOT PASS AWAY</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>The World doth pass away? Nay, the World stands its ground:<br>
 What God destroys is but the night that wraps it round.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>263 <font size="1">(I. 270)</font><br>
 <big>THE VOICE OF GOD</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>The Creatures are the utterance<br>
 Of the Eternal Word—now smooth<br>
 It sings itself in gentleness,<br>
 Now rings itself out loud in wrath.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>264 <font size="1">(V. 5)</font><br>
 <big>ZERO, IF IT PRECEDES, IS NAUGHT</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>The Creature, which is nullity,<br>
 Denoteth zero if it come<br>
 In front of God—placed after Him<br>
 It giveth value to the Sum.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 183------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_183"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 183</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>265 <font size="1">(I. 114)</font><br>
 <big>THE SUN IS ENOUGH</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Superfluous to scan the sky, if shines the Sun on thee,<br>
 Inquiring for the moon and stars of less degree.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>266 <font size="1">(IV. 218)</font><br>
 <big>THE TOKEN OF THE BRIDE OF GOD</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>The Bride doth love the Bridegroom<br>
  And loveth none beside:<br>
 If thou hast other lovers,<br>
  How canst thou be the Bride?<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>267 <font size="1">(II. 231)</font><br>
 <big>THE SUNFLOWER</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Friend, marvel not that I behold<br>
 Naught that my eyes can rest upon,<br>
 For I must turn myself about<br>
 And gaze all day upon my Sun.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>268 <font size="1">(II. 114)</font><br>
 <big>CREATURES ARE GOOD</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Dost thou complain that creatures thwart thy Godward road?<br>
 How so? To me all creatures are a way to God.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 184------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_184"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 184</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>269 <font size="1">(IV. 164)</font><br>
 <big>GOD'S COUNTERSIGN</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>I know God's countersign. His signature is writ<br>
 In every creature, canst thou but interpret it.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>270 <font size="1">(I. 275)</font><br>
 <big>MAN BRINGETH ALL INTO GOD</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>All things do love thee, Man, and thickly round thee throng:<br>
 They run to thee because they would to God belong.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>271 <font size="1">(II. 115)</font><br>
 <big>THE SPIRITUAL HUNT</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Christian, the hounds will hunt thee well, both high and low,<br>
 Wilt thou but willingly consent to be God's doe.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>272 <font size="1">(V. 110)</font><br>
 <big>ALL CREATURES RUN AFTER THE CREATOR.</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Who the Creator hath, all things run after him—<br>
 Man, Angel, Sun and Moon, Air, Fire, Earth and Stream.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 185------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_185"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 185</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>273 <font size="1">(II. 143)</font><br>
 <big>IN GOD ALL IS GOD</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>In God all things are God: one worm beneath the sod<br>
 Ranks with a thousand worms equivalent in God.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>274 <font size="1">(V. 61)</font><br>
 <big>EVERYTHING IS PERFECT</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Naught is imperfect, Man. Pebble is analogue<br>
 Of ruby, Seraph not more beautiful than frog.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>275 <font size="1">(I. 269)</font><br>
 <big>ALL IS THE SAME TO GOD</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>God listeth to the croak of frogs as heedfully<br>
 As to the meadow-lark's sweet-throated melody.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>276 <font size="1">(V. 203)</font><br>
 <big>THE WORLDLING IS BLINDED</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Open thine eyes and see! Heaven lieth all unfurled!<br>
 Thou seest it not? Then art thou blind drunk with the World.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 186------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_186"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 186</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>277 <font size="1">(IV. 160)</font><br>
 <big>GOD IS GLORIOUS EVERYWHERE</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>No motes of dust are so contemptible and small<br>
 But that the Wise see God all glorious in them all.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>278 <font size="1">(III. 172)</font><br>
 <big>THE FINEST IS THE COMMONEST</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Things which are commonest are also the most fine:<br>
 'Tis evident in God and in His broad Sunshine.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>279 <font size="1">(II. 198)</font><br>
 <big>GOD PLAYETH WITH THE CREATURE</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>All this is but a Game which God<br>
 Fashioneth for Himself alone:<br>
 He hath devised the World of Things<br>
 Not for the Things' sake but His own.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>280 <font size="1">(III. 216)</font><br>
 <big>GOD DOETH IT ALL HIMSELF</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>God, God is All, All utterly,<br>
 The lute-strings tremble at His touch;<br>
 'Tis He that plays and sings in us—<br>
 Is therefore thy performance much?<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 187------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_187"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 187</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>281 <font size="1">(IV. 71)</font><br>
 <big>HEAVEN ON EVERY SIDE.</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>All creatures live and move and have their being in God:<br>
 Why must thou then needs ask which is the heavenward road?<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>282 <font size="1">(V. 224)</font><br>
 <big>TO THE DEAD ALL IS DEAD</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>If thou art dead, my Man, it needs must seem as though<br>
 All creatures and the world itself were dead also.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>283 <font size="1">(VI. 101)</font><br>
 <big>ALL GOES WHEN DESIRE GOES</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Thy love and thy desire lend things their preciousness;<br>
 Take these away, then things are mean and valueless.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>284 <font size="1">(VI. 20)</font><br>
 <big>TEMPORAL THINGS ARE SMOKE</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Things temporal are like a smoke.<br>
 If thou dost let it blow about<br>
 Within thy house, for sure 'twill bite<br>
 The twain eyes of the spirit out.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 188------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_188"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 188</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>285 <font size="1">(I. 282)</font><br>
 <big>THE BEST STATION IS IN GOD</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>To hear the Morning Stars praise God<br>
 Is little profit to my ears<br>
 If I am not yet lifted up<br>
 To God above the Morning Stars.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>286 <font size="1">(I. 289)</font><br>
 <big>WITHOUT WHY</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>The Rose because she is Rose<br>
 Doth blossom, never asketh Why;<br>
 She eyeth not herself, nor cares<br>
 If she is seen of other eye.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>287 <font size="1">(III. 98)</font><br>
 <big>NOT TO DISSEMBLE IS NOT TO SIN</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>What is it not to sin? There is no need to ponder,<br>
 They'll tell you what it is—the dumb flowers yonder.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>288 <font size="1">(I. 290)</font><br>
 <big>LET GOD CARE</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Who gives the lilics grace? the daffodils who reeds?<br>
 Then, Christian, wherefore be so careful for thy needs?<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p>
 
 <!------------------------page 189------------------------>
 
 <a name="page_189"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 189</font></a>
 
 </p>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>289 <font size="1">(I. 127)</font><br>
 <big>ALL IS ALIKE TO GOD</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>All things are one thing unto God,<br>
  He knoweth no diversity.<br>
 Art thou in substance one with Him,<br>
  So is He also with the fly.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>290 <font size="1">(VI. 217)</font><br>
 <big>THE ANT'S MIND</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>To thee the World is very wide,<br>
 A lump of earth is adamant,<br>
 A molehill is a mountain range:<br>
 The reason is—thou art an ant.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>291 <font size="1">(VI. 213)</font><br>
 <big>TO THE SMALL, EVERYTHING SMALL IS GREAT</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Grow, Child, and become big! So long as thou art small,<br>
 Things little in themselves will show as great and tall.<br></td></tr></table>
 
 <p> </p>
 
 <p>292 <font size="1">(IV. 30)</font><br>
 <big>GOD ABOVE ALL GIFTS</big></p>
 <table align="center"><tr><td>Oft have I prayed, "O God, Thy gifts be given me!"<br>
 Yet knowest Thou, 'tis not Thy gifts I crave but Thee.<br>
 Give me eternal Life—give what Thou wilt—give aught,<br>
 Hast thou not given Thyself, still hast Thou given me naught.<br></td></tr></table>
