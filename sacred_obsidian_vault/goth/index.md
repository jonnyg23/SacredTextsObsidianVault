 <hr>
 
 <p>
 Gothic refers to a Romantic literary style which
 can include supernatural aspects, particularly vampires.
 This is because many of these stories are set in
 locales with Gothic architecture.
 This page includes significant non-fiction and fiction books
 about vampires and related subjects.
 </p><p>
 Why cover this subject at sacred-texts?
 From Lord Byrons' literary circle to Buffy the Vampire Slayer,
 vampires and their kin have evolved into a modern mythology
 about the presence of <a href="../evil/index.htm">evil</a>
 in a world governed by science.
 Gothic literature is an outlet for the ancient fears
 of humanity in an <a href="../aor/index.htm">age of reason</a>.
 These texts allow us to trace the evolution of this mythology.
 </p>
 <p>
 Gothic literature shouldn't be confused with
 texts <i>in</i> the Gothic language,
 the earliest written Germanic language, which
 includes the Codex Argentius, a translation of parts of the
 New Testament.
 For more information, 
 we recommend <a href="https://www.cs.tut.fi/~dla/gothic.html">Database of the Gothic Language</a> [External Site].
 </p>
 
 <hr>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="vkk/index.htm">The Vampire, his Kith and Kin</a></span>,
 by <span class="c_a">Montague Summers</span>. [<span class="c_d">1928</span>]<br>
 <span class="c_b">Summers, a devout and very literal Catholic,
 was apparently convinced of the reality of the undead.</span>
 This work is a serious, often pedantic, academic review of the literature.
 However, it is required reading for any prospective vampire experts.
 (An Internet rumor has it that Buffy
 Summers got her last name from this author; we welcome any
 comments on whether there is any truth to this.)
 This etext was scanned at sacred-texts.
 </span>
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="bow/index.htm">The Book of Were-Wolves</a></span>,
 by <span class="c_a">Sabine Baring-Gould</span>. [<span class="c_d">1865</span>]<br>
 <span class="c_b">All killer, some filler.</span>
 One of the best known studies of lycanthropy,
 written by the author of the hymn 'Onward Christian Soldiers.'
 The first ten chapters are a pretty good review of the shape-shifting
 literature, and then it takes a strange
 left turn into the topic of psychopathy.
 This etext was scanned at sacred-texts.
 </span>
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="frank/index.htm">Frankenstein</a></span>
 by <span class="c_a">Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley</span> [<span class="c_d">1831</span>]<br>
 <span class="c_b">It's alive!</span>
 Originaly published in 1818,
 this modern reworking of the Golem story
 is a cautionary tale about science.
 Frankenstein just keeps getting more relevant as we
 stumble forward into the 21st Century of clones, stem cells and 
 <a href="../dna/index.htm">genomics</a>.
 Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley, along with her husband,
 the poet Percy Bysshe Shelley, was a member of Lord Byron's circle;
 her mother, Mary Wollstonecraft,
 wrote one of the key feminist documents,
 <a href="../wmn/vind.txt">Vindication of the Rights Of Woman</a>
 (also at this site).
 </span>
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="polidori/vampyr.htm">The Vampyre</a></span>,
 by <span class="c_a">John Polidori</span> [<span class="c_d">1819</span>]<br>
 <span class="c_b">This short proto-gothic novel caused a sensation at the time, and spawned 
 a vampire craze.</span>
 Numerous books and plays based on
 or imitating its story line.
 Originally spuriously attributed to Lord Byron,
 this was actually written by Dr. John
 William Polidori, a physician friend of Lord Byron.
 The main character of Ruthven is reputedly based on Byron.
 For more information, refer to <a href="vkk/vkk07.htm">Chapter V of The Vampire, his Kith and Kin</a>.
 </span>
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="drac/index.htm">Dracula</a></span>
 by <span class="c_a">Bram Stoker</span> [<span class="c_d">1897</span>]<br>
 <span class="c_b">Dracula, not Ruthven, is the template for the modern vampire craze.</span>
 This novel, in the form of fictional journals and letters,
 was not the first or even the best 19th Century vampire tale,
 but is today the best known, due to the endless number of movies based on it.
 Today we know that Dracula had a medieval counterpart, Vlad the Impaler,
 who lived in Wallachia (which is to the south of Transylvania),
 today part of Romania.
 Although not an actual vampire, Vlad was said to
 have spilled quite a bit of blood in his day.
 </span>
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="lww/index.htm">Lair of the White Worm</a></span>
 by <span class="c_a">Bram Stoker</span> [<span class="c_d">1911</span>].
 <span class="c_b">An erotic thriller of demonic possession, this time by a giant invertebrate.</span>
 <cite>Lair</cite> was made into a dreadful movie by Ken Russell,
 which has become something of a cult classic.
 <i>Note that there are racist terms and characterizations in
 in this book, so be forewarned</i>.
 </span>
 </p>
 
 <p>
 <a href="#" onclick="window.open('../cdshop/index.htm','cartwin','');"><img src="../img/buycd.jpg" height="20" border="0"></a>
 
 <span class="c_e">
 <span class="c_t"><a href="vav/index.htm">Vikram and The Vampire</a></span>,
 translated by Sir <span class="c_a">Richard R. Burton</span>. [<span class="c_d">1870</span>]<br>
 <span class="c_b">The Vampire Scheherazade.</span>
 This collection of Hindu fables, which has an Arabian Nights-style
 backstory with a legendary Indian monarch playing Scheherazade to
 an undead being,
 doesn't have much to do with vampires, but this seemed
 the best place to put it.
 Burton, as it happens, also translated the
 <a href="../neu/index.htm#arabia">Arabian Nights</a>.
 Links to other Burton material at sacred-texts
 (most not suitable for children) can be found in the
 <a href="../sex/index.htm#kamasutra">Kama Sutra</a> section.
 </span>
 </p>
