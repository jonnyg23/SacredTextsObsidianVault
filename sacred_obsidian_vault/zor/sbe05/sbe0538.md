 <p>1. On [the <i>spiritual</i> chieftainship <a name="fr_638"></a><a href="#fn_633"><font size="1">2</font></a> of the regions <i>of the earth</i>] it says in revelation, that every one of those six chieftainships <a name="fr_639"></a><a href="#fn_634"><font size="1">3</font></a> has one <i>spiritual</i> chief; as the chief of Arzah is Ashâshagaha<i>d</i>-ê <i>H</i><i>v</i>a<i>n</i>d<i>k</i>ãn <a name="fr_640"></a><a href="#fn_635"><font size="1">4</font></a>, the chief of Savah is Hoazarôdathhri-hanâ Parê<i>s</i>tyarô <a name="fr_641"></a><a href="#fn_636"><font size="1">5</font></a>, the chief of Frada<i>d</i>afsh is Spîtôî<i>d</i>-i Aûspôsînân <a name="fr_642"></a><a href="#fn_637"><font size="1">6</font></a>, [the chief of Vîda<i>d</i>afsh is Aîrî<i>z</i>-râsp Aûspôsînân <a name="fr_643"></a><a href="#fn_638"><font size="1">7</font></a>,] the chief of Vôrûbar<i>s</i>t is Huvâsp <a name="fr_644"></a><a href="#fn_639"><font size="1">8</font></a>, the chief of Vôrûgar<i>s</i>t is <i>K</i>akhravâk <a name="fr_645"></a><a href="#fn_640"><font size="1">9</font></a>. 2.  <p>3. In the region of Khvanîras are many places, from which, in this evil time of violent struggling with the adversary, a passage (vi<i>d</i>arg) is constructed by the power of the spiritual world (maînôkîh), <i>and one</i> calls <i>them</i> the beaten tracks <a name="fr_647"></a><a href="#fn_642"><font size="1">2</font></a> of Khvanîras.</p>  
 <p>4. Counterparts of those other regions <a name="fr_648"></a><a href="#fn_643"><font size="1">3</font></a> are <i>such places</i> as Kangde<i>z</i>, the land of S<i>a</i>ukavastân, the plain of the Arabs (Tâ<i>z</i>îkân), the plain of Pê<i>s</i>yânsaî, the river Nâîvtâk <a name="fr_649"></a><a href="#fn_644"><font size="1">4</font></a>, Aîrân-vê<i>g</i>, the enclosure (var) formed by Yim, <i>and</i> Ka<i>s</i>mîr in India <a name="fr_650"></a><a href="#fn_645"><font size="1">5</font></a>. 5. Regarding them it says, they are <i>those</i> who are immortal, as are Narsih <a name="fr_662"></a><a href="#fn_657"><font size="1">5</font></a> <i>son</i> of Vîvanghâû, Tûs <a name="fr_663"></a><a href="#fn_658"><font size="1">6</font></a> <i>son</i> of Nô<i>d</i>ar <a name="fr_664"></a><a href="#fn_659"><font size="1">7</font></a>, Gîw <a name="fr_665"></a><a href="#fn_660"><font size="1">8</font></a> <i>son</i> of Gû<i>d</i>ar<i>z</i>, Ibairaz <a name="fr_666"></a><a href="#fn_661"><font size="1">9</font></a> the causer of strife, <i>and</i> Ashavazd son of Pourudhâkh<i>s</i>t <a name="fr_667"></a><a href="#fn_662"><font size="1">10</font></a>; and they will all <a name="fr_668"></a><a href="#fn_663"><font size="1">11</font></a> come forth, to the</p>

 <p>7. Regarding Sâm <a name="fr_669"></a><a href="#fn_664"><font size="1">1</font></a> it says, that he became immortal, <i>but</i> owing to his disregard of the Mazdayasnian religion, a Tûrk whom they call Nihâ<i>g</i> <a name="fr_670"></a><a href="#fn_665"><font size="1">2</font></a> wounded <i>him</i> with an arrow, when he was asleep there, in the plain of Pê<i>s</i>yânsaî; and it had brought upon him the unnatural lethargy (bûshasp) <i>which</i> overcame <i>him in</i> the midst of the heat <a name="fr_671"></a><a href="#fn_666"><font size="1">3</font></a>. 8. And the glory (far) of heaven stands over him <a name="fr_672"></a><a href="#fn_667"><font size="1">4</font></a> for the purpose that, when A<i>z</i>-i Dahâk <a name="fr_673"></a><a href="#fn_668"><font size="1">5</font></a> becomes unfettered (ara<i>z</i>ak), he may arise and slay him; and a myriad guardian spirits of the righteous are as a protection to him. 9. Of Dahâk, whom they call Bêvarâsp, this, too, it says, that Frê<i>d</i>ûn when he captured Dahâk was not able to kill <i>him</i>, and afterwards confined him in Mount Dimâvand <a name="fr_674"></a><a href="#fn_669"><font size="1">6</font></a>; when he becomes unfettered, Sâm arises, and smites and slays him.</p>  
 <p>10. As to Kangde<i>z</i>, <i>it is</i> in the direction of the east, at many leagues from the bed (var) <a name="fr_675"></a><a href="#fn_670"><font size="1">7</font></a> of the</p>

The plain of Pê<i>s</i>yânsaî is in Kâvulistân, as it says, that the most remarkable upland (bâlist) in Kâvulistân is where Pê<i>s</i>yânsaî is; there it is hotter, on the more lofty elevations there is no heat <a name="fr_676"></a><a href="#fn_671"><font size="1">1</font></a>. 12. Aîrân-vê<i>g</i> is in the direction of Âtarô-pâtakân <a name="fr_677"></a><a href="#fn_672"><font size="1">2</font></a>. 13. The land of S<i>a</i>ukavastân is on the way from Tûrkistân to <i>K</i>înistân, in the direction of the north. 14. [The enclosure] <a name="fr_678"></a><a href="#fn_673"><font size="1">3</font></a> formed by Yim is <i>in</i> the middle of Pârs, in Sruvâ <a name="fr_679"></a><a href="#fn_674"><font size="1">4</font></a>; thus, they say, that <i>what</i> Yim formed (Yim-kar<i>d</i>) is below Mount Yimakân <a name="fr_680"></a><a href="#fn_675"><font size="1">5</font></a>. 15. Ka<i>s</i>mîr is in Hindûstân.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_632"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_637">115:1</a> For this chapter, which is numbered XXX by previous translators, we have to depend only on K20 and TD (see the note on the heading of Chap. XXVIII); and the words enclosed in brackets are supplied from TD, being either illegible or omitted in K20.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_633"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_638">115:2</a> Perhaps 'patriarchate' or 'episcopate' would be a better translation of ra<i>d</i>îh, and 'patriarch' or 'bishop' of ra<i>d</i>, in this chapter, as the chief high-priest (dastûr-i dastûrân) and his office are evidently meant by these words.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_634"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_639">115:3</a> Of the six other regions, distinct from this one of Khvanîras, see Chap. XI, 2-4.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_635"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_640">115:4</a> TD has Ashashâg,h<i>d</i>-ê aîgh Nêva<i>n</i>dãn; both MSS. giving these names in a barbarous Pâzand form which cannot be relied on. Perhaps this Dastûr is the Av. Ashâvanghu Biva<i>n</i>dangha of Fravardîn Yt. 110.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_636"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_641">115:5</a> TD has Hôazarôkakhhr-hanâ Parê<i>s</i>tyrô, all in Pâzand in both MSS., except Huz. hanâ, which stands for Pâz. ê, here used for the idhâfat i. Perhaps this Dastûr is the Av. <i>G</i>arô-danghu Pairi<i>s</i>tîra of Fravardîn Yt. 110.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_637"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_642">115:6</a> So in TD; K20 has Pâz. Spaitanid-i Huspâsnyân. This Dastûr is, no doubt, the Av. (gen.) Spitôi<i>s</i> Uspãsnao<i>s</i> of Fravardîn Yt. 121.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_638"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_643">115:7</a> Omitted in K20, but, no doubt, this Dastûr is the Av. Erezrâspa Uspãsnu of Fravardîn Yt. 121.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_639"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_644">115:8</a> Av. Hvaspa of Fravardîn Yt. 122.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_640"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_645">115:9</a> So in both MSS. As in the case of each of the preceding two pair of regions, two consecutive names of Dastûrs have been taken from the Fravardîn Ya<i>s</i>t, it may be supposed that the names <a name="page_116"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 116</font></a> taken for this third pair of regions will also be consecutive, and this Dastûr must, therefore, be identified with the Av. <i>K</i>athwaraspa of Fravardîn Yt. 122.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_641"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_646">116:1</a> TD has 'Zaratû<i>s</i>t is chief of this region of Khvanîras, and also of the whole world of the righteous; all chieftainship, also, is from Zaratû<i>s</i>t, so that the whole religion,' &amp;c.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_642"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_647">116:2</a> Justi has 'zones, climates;' but transcribing Pâz. habâvanhâ back into Pahlavi we have a word which may be read khabânŏhâ, pl. of khabân, 'a trampling-place' (comp. Pers. khabîdan). TD has khvabî<i>s</i>nŏ-gâs, which has the same meaning.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_643"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_648">116:3</a> Meaning, probably, that they resemble the six smaller regions in being isolated and difficult of access; in other words, either mythical, or independent of Iranian rule.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_644"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_649">116:4</a> So in TD, which also omits the second, third, and fourth of these isolated territories. In K20 we might read ra<i>d</i> va khû<i>d</i>âk, 'chief and lord,' as an epithet of Aîrân-vê<i>g</i>. This river must be the Nâhvtâk of Chap. XXI, 6.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_645"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_650">116:5</a> Reading Ka<i>s</i>mîr-i andar Hindû, but TD has Ka<i>s</i>mîr-i andarûnŏ; perhaps the last word was originally anîrânak, in which case we should read 'the not-Iranian Ka<i>s</i>mîr.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_646"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_651">117:1</a> The Av. Peshôtanu of Vi<i>s</i>tâsp Yt. 4, where he is described as free from disease and death. TD has Pêshyôk-tanû. See also Chaps. XXXI, 29, XXXII, 5.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_647"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_652">117:2</a> TD has <i>K</i>itrô-mâônô, and it may be doubted whether the latter portion of the name be derived from Av. mainyu, 'spirit,' or m<i>a</i>unghô, 'moon.' The Dâ<i>d</i>istân-i-Dînîk (Reply 89) calls him 'Patshâyôtanû who is called from the <i>K</i>îtrôk-mâhanô (or mîyânô),' the <i>K</i>atru-mîyân river of Chap. XX, 7, 31.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_648"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_653">117:3</a> See § 10. TD has Kangde<i>z</i>-i bâmîk, 'Kangde<i>z</i> the splendid.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_649"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_654">117:4</a> The Av. Aghraêratha Narava of Gô<i>s</i> Yt. 18, 22, Fravardîn Yt. 131, Ashi Yt. 38, Zamyâd Yt. 77; he is Aghrîrath, brother of Afrâsiyâb, in the Shâhnâmah; see also Chap. XXXI, 15.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_650"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_655">117:5</a> TD has Pahl. Sakîkstân here, but Sôkapastân in § 13 (the letters îk and p being often much alike in Pahlavi writing). K20 has Pâz. Sâvkavatân, S<i>a</i>ukâvasta, and Sâvkavastãn.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_651"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_656">117:6</a> TD has Gôpat-malkâ, 'king of Gôpat;' and Dâ<i>d</i>. (Reply 89) states that 'the reign of Gôpatshah is over the country of Gôpatô, coterminous with Aîrân-vê<i>g</i>, on the bank of the water of the Dâîtîk; <i>and</i> he keeps watch over the ox Hadhayãs, on whom occurred the various emigrations of men of old.' Mkh. (LXII, 31-36) says, 'Gôpatshâh remains in Aîrân-vê<i>g</i>, within the region of Khvanîras; from foot to mid-body <i>he is</i> a bull, and from mid-body to top <i>he is</i> a man; at all times he stays on the sea-shore, and always performs the worship of God, and always pours holy-water into the sea; through the pouring of that holy-water innumerable noxious creatures in the sea will die; for if he should not mostly perform that ceremonial, and should not pour that holy-water into the sea, and those innumerable noxious creatures should not perish, then always when rain falls the noxious creatures would fall like rain.' In Chap. XXXI, 20, he is said to be a son of Aghrêra<i>d</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_652"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_657">117:7</a> So in K20; and Av. Parsha<i>d</i>g<i>a</i>u occurs in Fravardîn Yt. 96, 127; but TD has Fradakh<i>s</i>tar Khûmbîkân, and Dâ<i>d</i>. (Reply 89) mentions 'Fradhakh<i>s</i>tô son of Khûmbîkân' as one of the seven <a name="page_118"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 118</font></a> immortal lords of Khvanîras, which name corresponds with the Av. Fradhâkh<i>s</i>ti Khu<i>n</i>bya of Fravardîn Yt. 138.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_653"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_658">118:1</a> TD has always Pahl. Pê<i>s</i>ânsih. No doubt the Pi<i>s</i>în valley is meant (see § 11).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_654"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_659">118:2</a> Or it may be read Aêshm-i. This phrase occurs only in TD, but Dâ<i>d</i>. (Reply 89) mentions 'the Avesta Yakhmâyî<i>s</i>a<i>d</i>, son of the same Fryânô,' as one of the seven immortal lords of Khvanîras.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_658"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_663">118:6</a> Av. Tusa of Âbân Yt. 53, 58, and an Iranian warrior in the Shâhnâmah.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_659"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_664">118:7</a> Av. Naotara, whose descendants are mentioned in Âbân Yt. 76, 98, Fravardîn Yt. 102, Râm Yt. 35.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_660"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_665">118:8</a> Av. Gaêvani of Fravardîn Yt. 115 is something like this name of one of the Iranian warriors in the Shâhnâmah.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_661"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_666">118:9</a> TD has Pâz. Bairazd. Perhaps it is not a name but a Pâzand corruption of Pahl. aêvarz, 'warrior, trooper' (traditionally); in which case we should have to read 'the warrior who was a causer of strife.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_662"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_667">118:10</a> So in TD; K20 has 'Ashavand son of Porudakh<i>s</i>t' and Dâ<i>d</i>.  <p><a name="fn_663"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_668">118:11</a> So in TD, but K20 has 'always.'</p>

 <p><a name="fn_665"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_670">119:2</a> It can also be read Nihâ<i>v</i> or Nîyâ<i>g</i> in K20, and Nihâv or Nihân in TD.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_666"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_671">119:3</a> TD has '<i>as</i> he lay <i>in</i> the midst of the heat.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_667"></a><a href="sbe0538.htm#fr_672">119:4</a> TD has 'and the snow (vafar) has settled (nishast) over him.'</p>
