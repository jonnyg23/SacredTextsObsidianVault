 <p>0. The family of the Môbads ('priests').</p>  
 <p>1. Bahak <a name="fr_859"></a><a href="#fn_853"><font size="1">2</font></a> <i>was</i> son of Hûbakht, <i>son</i> of Âtarô-bôndak, <i>son</i> of Mâhda<i>d</i>, <i>son</i> of Mê<i>d</i>yôk-mâh, <i>son</i> of Frâh-vakhsh-vindâ<i>d</i> <a name="fr_860"></a><a href="#fn_854"><font size="1">3</font></a>, <i>son</i> of Mê<i>d</i>yôk-mâh, <i>son</i> of Kâ<i>d</i> <a name="fr_861"></a><a href="#fn_855"><font size="1">4</font></a>, <i>son</i> of Mê<i>d</i>yôk-mâh, <i>son</i> of Ârâstih, <i>son</i> of Paîtirâsp <a name="fr_862"></a><a href="#fn_856"><font size="1">5</font></a>. 2.  <p><a name="fn_853"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_859">145:2</a> Here written Bôhak, but it is Bahak or Bâk in § 2; compare Bâhak in § 3, and Av. B<i>a</i>ungha of Fravardîn Yt. 124.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_854"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_860">145:3</a> Compare Av. Frashâvakhsha of Fravardîn Yt. 109.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_856"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_862">145:5</a> See Chap. XXXII, 2, for the last three generations; TD has Pîrtarâsp here, like the variant of M6 in Chap. XXXII, 1.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_857"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_863">145:6</a> The Sasanian king Shâpûr II, who reigned A.D. 309-379.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_858"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_864">145:7</a> According to the chronology of the Bundahi<i>s</i> (Chap. XXXIV, 8, 9), Dârâî lived only some four centuries before Shâpûr II, for which period only seven generations of priests are here provided. This period, moreover, is certainly about three centuries less than the truth.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_859"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_865">145:8</a> This priest was prime minister of Shâpûr II.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_860"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_866">145:9</a> Compare Av. Fryâna of Yas. XLV, 12.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_861"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_867">145:10</a> This name is repeated in TD, probably by mistake (compare Bahak in §§ 1, 2).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_862"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_868">146:1</a> This is probably a semi-Huzvâri<i>s</i> form of Frashô<i>s</i>tar.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_863"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_869">146:2</a> Perhaps this name should be read along with the next one, so as to give the single Pâzand name <i>S</i>kina<i>s</i> or <i>S</i>kiva<i>s</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_864"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_870">146:3</a> See Chap. XXXII, 1, for the last three generations.  <p><a name="fn_865"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_871">146:4</a> No doubt Mânû<i>s</i><i>k</i>îhar is meant; if not, we must read Mânû<i>s</i>-dûrnâmîk in connection with § 5.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_866"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_872">146:5</a> Here written Nî<i>d</i>ar, but see Chaps. XXIX, 6, XXXI, 13.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_867"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_873">146:6</a> Here written Mar<i>d</i>-vêh, but see § 8.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_868"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_874">146:7</a> Here written Aê-vûkht, but see § 8; it may be Vîs-bûkht, or Vê<i>s</i>-bûkht.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_869"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_875">146:8</a> The text is amî<i>d</i>ar mûna<i>s</i> li a<i>g</i>a<i>s</i> zerkhûn<i>d</i> Hûmôî dûkht-i Freh-mâh-i<i>k</i> aharôb vûkht (dûkht?). We might perhaps read 'Freh-mâh <i>son</i> of <i>K</i>ahârôb-bûkht,' but it seems more probable that §§ 7, 8 should be connected, and that the meaning intended is that Hûmâî was daughter of Freh-mâh (of a certain family) and of Pûyi<i>s</i>n-shâ<i>d</i> (of another family); she was also the mother of the editor of that recension of the Bundahi<i>s</i> which is contained in TD; but who was his father? The singularly unnecessary repetition of the genealogy of the two brothers, Mitrô-akâvî<i>d</i> and Pûyi<i>s</i>n-shâ<i>d</i>, in §§ 6, 8, leads to the suspicion that if the latter <a name="page_147"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 147</font></a> were his mother's father, the former was probably his own father or grandfather. Unfortunately the text makes no clear statement on the subject, and § 10 affords further material for guessing otherwise at his name and connections.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_870"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_876">147:1</a> Compare Chap. XXXII, 4.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_871"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_877">147:2</a> Reading va lî<i>k</i> laband-i karîtûnd.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_872"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_878">147:3</a> This looks more like a complimentary title than a name, and if the editor of the TD recension of the Bundahi<i>s</i> were the son or grandson of Mitrô-akâvî<i>d</i> (§ 6) we have no means of ascertaining his name; but if he were not descended from Mitrô-akâvî<i>d</i> it is possible that §§ 10, 11 should be read together, and that he was the son of Yûdân-Yim. Now we know, from the heading and colophon of the ninety-two questions and answers on religious subjects which are usually called the Dâ<i>d</i>istân-i Dînîk, and from the colophons of other writings which usually accompany that work, that those answers were composed and certain epistles were written by Mânû<i>s</i><i>k</i>îhar, son of Yûdân-Yim, who was high-priest of Pârs and Kirmân in A.Y. 250 (A.D. 881), and apparently a more important personage than his (probably younger) brother Zâ<i>d</i>-sparham, who is mentioned in § 11 as one of the priests contemporary with the editor of the TD recension. If this editor, therefore, were a son of Yûdân-Yim (which is a possible interpretation of the text) he was most probably this same Mânû<i>s</i><i>k</i>îhar, author of the Dâ<i>d</i>istân-i Dînîk (see the Introduction. § 4).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_873"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_879">147:4</a> The last name is very probably superfluous, Zâ<i>d</i>-sparham <a name="page_148"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 148</font></a> having been written twice most likely by mistake. This Âtarô-pâ<i>d</i> son of Mâraspend was probably the one mentioned in the following extract from the old Persian Rivâyat MS., No. 8 of the collection in the Indian Office Library at London (fol. 142 a):</p>  
 <p>'The book Dînkar<i>d</i> which the dastûrs of the religion and the ancients have compiled, likewise the blessed Âdarbâd son of Mahrasfend, son of Asavahist of the people of the good religion, in the year three hundred of Yazda<i>g</i>ard Shahryâr, collected some of the more essential mysteries of the religion as instruction, and of these he formed this book.' That is, he was the last editor of the Dînka<i>r</i>d, which seems to have remained unrevised since his time, as the present copies have descended from the MS. preserved by his family and first copied in A.Y. 369.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_874"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_880">148:1</a> Zâ<i>d</i>-sparham was brother of the author of the Dâ<i>d</i>istân-i Dînîk; he was high-priest at Sîrkân in the south, and evidently had access to the Bundahi<i>s</i>, of part of which he wrote a paraphrase (see Appendix). His name is usually written Zâ<i>d</i>-sparam.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_875"></a><a href="sbe0542.htm#fr_881">148:2</a> In the history of the Dînka<i>r</i>d, given at the end of its third book (see Introd. to Farhang-i Oîm-khadûk, p. xxxiv), we are told as follows:</p>  
 <p>'After that, the well-meaning Âtarô-pâ<i>d</i> son of Hêmî<i>d</i>, who was the leader of the people of the good religion, compiled, with the assistance of God, through inquiry, investigation, and much trouble, a new means of producing remembrance of the Mazdayasnian religion.' He did this, we are further told, by collecting all the decaying literature and perishing traditions into a work 'like the great original Dînkar<i>d</i>, of a thousand chapters' (mânâk-i zak rabâ bûn Dînô-kartô 1000-darakŏ). We thus learn from external sources that the group of contemporary priests, mentioned in the text, was actively employed (about A.D. 900) in an attempted revival of the religious literature of the Mazdayasnians, to which we owe either the revision or compilation of such works as the Dînkar<i>d</i>, Dâ<i>d</i>istân-i Dînîk, and Bundahi<i>s</i>.</p>
