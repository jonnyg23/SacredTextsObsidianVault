 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>Pahlavi Texts, Part III (SBE24)</i>, E.W. West, tr. [1885], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER LXII.</h3>  
 <p>1. The sage asked the spirit of wisdom (2) thus: '<i>In</i> what place stands Kangde<i>z</i>? 3. Where is the enclosure formed by Yim constructed <a name="fr_592"></a><a href="#fn_584"><font size="1">8</font></a>? 4. <i>In</i> what</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_109"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 109</font></a></p>  
 <p>place lies <a name="fr_593"></a><a href="#fn_585"><font size="1">1</font></a> the body of Sâhm? 5. Where is the abode of Srôsh? 6. <i>In</i> what place stands the three-legged ass? 7. Where is the Hôm grown, the preparer of the dead, with which they restore the dead and produce the future existence? 8. In which place is <a name="fr_594"></a><a href="#fn_586"><font size="1">2</font></a> Gôpaîtôshah? 9. With what work is the Kar fish intrusted? 10. Where has the griffon bird a nest (â<i>s</i>îyân) <a name="fr_595"></a><a href="#fn_587"><font size="1">3</font></a>? 11. <i>In</i> what place sits <i>K</i>înâmrô<i>s</i>, and what is his work?'</p>  
 <p>12. The spirit of wisdom answered (13) thus: 'Kangde<i>z</i> <a name="fr_596"></a><a href="#fn_588"><font size="1">4</font></a> is intrusted with the. eastern quarter, near to Satavâyês <a name="fr_597"></a><a href="#fn_589"><font size="1">5</font></a>, (14) on the frontier of Aîrân-vê<i>g</i>ô <a name="fr_598"></a><a href="#fn_590"><font size="1">6</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>15. 'The enclosure formed by Yim <a name="fr_599"></a><a href="#fn_591"><font size="1">7</font></a> is constructed in Aîrân-vê<i>g</i>ô, below the earth <a name="fr_600"></a><a href="#fn_592"><font size="1">8</font></a>. 16. And every species and seed of all the creatures and creations of Aûha<i>r</i>ma<i>z</i><i>d</i>, the lord, whatever is better and more select of man and beast <i>of burden, of</i> cattle and flying creatures is brought thither <a name="fr_601"></a><a href="#fn_593"><font size="1">9</font></a>. 17. And every forty years one child is born from one woman and one man <a name="fr_602"></a><a href="#fn_594"><font size="1">10</font></a> who are of that place; (18) their life,</p>

 <p>too, is three hundred years <a name="fr_603"></a><a href="#fn_595"><font size="1">1</font></a>, (19) and their pain and disturbance are little <a name="fr_604"></a><a href="#fn_596"><font size="1">2</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>20. 'The body of Sâhm <a name="fr_605"></a><a href="#fn_597"><font size="1">3</font></a> is in the plain of Pê<i>s</i>ândas <a name="fr_606"></a><a href="#fn_598"><font size="1">4</font></a>, near to Mount Dimâvand <a name="fr_607"></a><a href="#fn_599"><font size="1">5</font></a>. 21. And on that plain, except corn and the eatable things they sow and reap <i>and</i> live upon, there is not so much as a single other tree <a name="fr_608"></a><a href="#fn_600"><font size="1">6</font></a>, <i>or</i> shrub, <i>or</i> plant; (22) and its golden colour is mostly wormwood <a name="fr_609"></a><a href="#fn_601"><font size="1">7</font></a>. 23. And the angels and archangels have appointed 99,999 guardian spirits of the righteous as a protection for the body of Sâhm <a name="fr_610"></a><a href="#fn_602"><font size="1">8</font></a>, (24) so that the demons and fiends <i>may</i> not injure it.</p>  
 <p>25. 'The abode of Srôsh <a name="fr_611"></a><a href="#fn_603"><font size="1">9</font></a> is mostly in Arzah <a name="fr_612"></a><a href="#fn_604"><font size="1">10</font></a>, and afterwards also in Savah and the whole world.</p>

 <p>26. 'The three-legged ass <a name="fr_613"></a><a href="#fn_605"><font size="1">1</font></a> sits <a name="fr_614"></a><a href="#fn_606"><font size="1">2</font></a> amid the sea Varkash <a name="fr_615"></a><a href="#fn_607"><font size="1">3</font></a>; (27) and <i>as to</i> water of every kind that rains on dead matter, the menstrual discharge, and other bodily refuse <a name="fr_616"></a><a href="#fn_608"><font size="1">4</font></a>, when it arrives at the three-legged ass, he makes every kind clean and purified, with watchfulness.</p>  
 <p>28. 'The Hôm <a name="fr_617"></a><a href="#fn_609"><font size="1">5</font></a>, which is the preparer of the dead, is grown in the sea Varkash, in that which is the deepest place; (29) and 99,999 guardian spirits of the righteous are appointed <i>as</i> its protection <a name="fr_618"></a><a href="#fn_610"><font size="1">6</font></a>. 30. The Kar fish <a name="fr_619"></a><a href="#fn_611"><font size="1">7</font></a>, too, ever circles around it, and always keeps the frog and other noxious creatures away from it.</p>  
 <p>31. 'Gôpaîtôshah <a name="fr_620"></a><a href="#fn_612"><font size="1">8</font></a> is in Aîrân-vê<i>g</i>ô, within the region of Khvanîras <a name="fr_621"></a><a href="#fn_613"><font size="1">9</font></a>. 32. From foot to mid-body <i>he</i> is an ox, and from mid-body to the top <i>he</i> is a man. 33. And <i>at</i> all times he sits on the sea-shore, (34) and always performs the ceremonial of the sacred beings, and pours holy-water into the sea. 35. On account of which <a name="fr_622"></a><a href="#fn_614"><font size="1">10</font></a>, through the pouring of that holy-water, innumerable noxious creatures in</p>

 <p>the sea will die. 36. Because, if he does not specially perform that celebration of the ceremonial, and does not pour that holy-water into the seawhere those innumerable noxious creatures shall utterly perish <a name="fr_623"></a><a href="#fn_615"><font size="1">1</font></a>then, whenever the rain shall rain, the noxious creatures <i>have</i> to rain just like rain.</p>  
 <p>37. 'The nest of the griffon bird <a name="fr_624"></a><a href="#fn_616"><font size="1">2</font></a> is on the tree. opposed to harm, the many-seeded <a name="fr_625"></a><a href="#fn_617"><font size="1">3</font></a>. 38. Whenever he rises aloft a thousand twigs will shoot out from that tree, (39) and when he alights he breaks off the thousand twigs and bites the seed from them. 40. And the bird <i>K</i>înâmrô<i>s</i> <a name="fr_626"></a><a href="#fn_618"><font size="1">4</font></a> alights likewise <i>in</i> that vicinity; (41) and his work is this, that he collects those seeds which are bitten from the tree of many seeds, which is opposed to harm, and he scatters (pargandê<i>d</i>) <i>them</i> there where Tî<i>s</i>tar <a name="fr_627"></a><a href="#fn_619"><font size="1">5</font></a> seizes the water; (42) so that, while Tî<i>s</i>tar shall seize the water, <i>together</i> with those seeds of all kinds, he shall rain <i>them</i> on the world with the rain <a name="fr_628"></a><a href="#fn_620"><font size="1">6</font></a>.'</p>

 <p><a name="fn_597"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_605">110:3</a> See Chap. XXVII, 49.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_598"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_606">110:4</a> L19 has Pu<i>s</i>t Gu<i>s</i>tâspã, 'the ridge of Vi<i>s</i>tâsp,' which appears, from Bd. XII, 18, 34, XVII, 8, to have been somewhere in the central desert of Persia. In Bd. XXIX, 7-9, 11 we are told that Sam lies asleep in the plain of Pê<i>s</i>yânsaî (evidently the Pê<i>s</i>ândas of our text) in Kâvulistân, till he is waked hereafter to slay Dahâk, who escapes from Mount Dimâvand. This legend may have led to the perplexing juxtaposition of Pê<i>s</i>ândas and Dimâvand in our text, and the perplexity occasioned by this may have led Nêr. to substitute Pû<i>s</i>t-î Vi<i>s</i>tâspân for the former name, as being nearer Dimâvand.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_599"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_607">110:5</a> The highest peak of the modern Albûr<i>z</i>, in which Dahâk is said to be confined (see Bd. XII, 31).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_600"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_608">110:6</a> Reading hânŏ drû-aê; L19 has han mûrd, 'another myrtle-bush.'</p>

 <p><a name="fn_605"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_613">111:1</a> A prodigious monster of benevolent character, described in Bd. XIX, 1-12; possibly some local divinity.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_606"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_614">111:2</a> L19 has 'stands,' as is also slated in Yas. XLI, 28.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_607"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_615">111:3</a> See Chap. XLIV, 15.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_608"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_616">111:4</a> L19 adds 'and pollution.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_609"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_617">111:5</a> The white Hôm or Gôkarn, the tree of immortality (see Bd. XVIII, 1, XXVIII 4). It is 'the preparer of the dead,' because the elixir of immortality is expected to be prepared from it at the, resurrection (see Bd. XXX, 25).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_610"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_618">111:6</a> Those who watch over the sea Vouru-kasha (see Fravar<i>d</i>in Yt. 59)</p>
 <p><a name="fn_611"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_619">111:7</a> Ten such fish, of enormous size and intense watchfulness, are said to be employed to protect the Gôkarn from a lizard or frog sent by Aharman to injure it (see Bd. XVIII, 2-6).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_612"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_620">111:8</a> See Chap. XLIV, 35 n.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_613"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_621">111:9</a> See Chap. XXVII, 40.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_614"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_622">111:10</a> L19 Omits these four words.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_615"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_623">112:1</a> L19 has 'and those innumerable noxious creatures do not utterly perish.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_616"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_624">112:2</a> The Sênô-mûrûv (Av. saênô mereghô) or Simurgh, a mythic flying creature said to suckle its young and to be of three natures like the bat (see Bd. XIV, 11, 24, XIX, 18).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_617"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_625">112:3</a> L19 has 'of all seeds.' This tree, from which all wild plants are supposed to spring, is said to grow in the sea near the Gôkarn tree, and also in Aîrân-vê<i>g</i>ô (see Bd. IX, 5, 6, XVIII, 9, XXVII, 2, XXIX, 5).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_618"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_626">112:4</a> The chief of mythic birds next to the Sênô-mûrûv; he is said to defend Irân from invasion by occasionally picking up foreign districts like grains of corn (see Bd. XIX, 15, XXIV, 29).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_619"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_627">112:5</a> The angel who personifies the star Tî<i>s</i>tar (Sirius, see Chap. XLIX, 5, 6), after a conflict with the demons of drought and thunder (see Bd. VII, 1-13), pours down rain from the cloud, in which he had brought the water from the sea.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_620"></a><a href="sbe24068.htm#fr_628">112:6</a> Originally, the archangel Ame<i>r</i>oda<i>d</i> (see Chap. II, 34) is said <a name="page_113"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 113</font></a> to have mingled the plants with the rain (see Bd. IX, 2); but afterwards this was done by the mythic bird (see Bd. XXVII, 3). This legend was evidently intended to account for the rapid appearance of wild plants after rain in dry climates, where all traces of vegetation often disappear during the summer droughts.</p>
