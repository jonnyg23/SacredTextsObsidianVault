 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>Pahlavi Texts, Part III (SBE24)</i>, E.W. West, tr. [1885], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER XXXII.</h3>  
 <p>1. The sage asked the spirit of wisdom (2) thus: 'What is the business of the well-endeavouring <a name="fr_371"></a><a href="#fn_366"><font size="1">6</font></a>, the artizans?'</p>  
 <p>3. The spirit of wisdom answered (4) thus: 'The business of the artizans is this, (5) that <i>as to</i> that work which they do not understand, they do not bring a hand to it; (6) and that which they well <a name="fr_372"></a><a href="#fn_367"><font size="1">7</font></a></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_69"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 69</font></a></p>  
 <p>understand (hû-dânend), they perform well <i>and</i> with attention; (7) and they demand wages lawfully. (8). For <i>as to him</i> who persists in doing that work which <a name="fr_373"></a><a href="#fn_368"><font size="1">1</font></a> he does not understand, it is <i>he by</i> whom that work is spoiled and becomes useless; and when, moreover, he is a man whose work makes himself satisfied, it then becomes even an origin of sin for him.'</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_366"></a><a href="sbe24038.htm#fr_371">68:6</a> The hûtûkhshân (Av. hûiti) are the fourth class of the community, and are very rarely mentioned in the Avesta, possibly because they were originally enslaved outcasts or aborigines, as in other ancient communities. The passage where they are specially mentioned (Yas. XIX, 46) is probably taken from the Bagh Nask (see Sls. X, 26 n; XIII, 1 n, 9 n).</p>
