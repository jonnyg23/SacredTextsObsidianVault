 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>Pahlavi Texts, Part III (SBE24)</i>, E.W. West, tr. [1885], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER XXIII.</h3>  
 <p>1. The sage asked the spirit of wisdom (2) thus: 'Is it possible to contend with destiny through wisdom <i>and</i> knowledge, or not?'</p>  
 <p>3. The spirit of wisdom answered (4) thus: 'Even with the might and powerfulness of wisdom <i>and</i> knowledge, even then it is not possible to contend with destiny. 5. Because, when predestination as to virtue, or as to the reverse <a name="fr_284"></a><a href="#fn_280"><font size="1">3</font></a>, comes forth, the wise becomes wanting (nîyâ<i>z</i>ân) in duty, and the astute in evil <i>becomes</i> intelligent; (6) the faint-hearted <i>becomes</i> braver, and the braver <i>becomes</i> faint-hearted;</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_55"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 55</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> (7) the diligent <i>becomes</i> lazy, and the lazy acts diligently <a name="fr_285"></a><a href="#fn_281"><font size="1">1</font></a>. (8) Just as is predestined as to the matter, the cause enters into it, (9) <i>and</i> thrusts out everything else.'</p>
