 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>Pahlavi Texts, Part II (SBE18)</i>, E.W. West, tr. [1882], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER LXII.</h3>  
 <p>1. <i>As to</i> the sixty-first question <i>and</i> reply, that which you ask is thus: How stand the shares in the inheritance (mîrâtŏ) of property among those of the good religion, and how is it necessary for them to stand therein?</p>  
 <p>2. The reply is this, that in the possession of wealth the wealth reaches higher <i>or</i> lower, just like water when it goes in a stream on a declivity, but when the passage shall be closed at the bottom it goes back on the running water (pûy-â<i>v</i>ŏ), <i>and</i> then it does not go to its after-<i>course</i> <a name="fr_712"></a><a href="#fn_709"><font size="1">2</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_195"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 195</font></a></p>  
 <p>3. When there is nothing <i>otherwise</i> in the will and private <a name="fr_713"></a><a href="#fn_710"><font size="1">1</font></a>, property <i>goes</i> to a wife <i>or</i> daughter <a name="fr_714"></a><a href="#fn_711"><font size="1">2</font></a> who is privileged; if <i>one</i> gives her anything by will then she does not obtain the share (dâ<i>s</i>) <i>pertaining</i> to her <a name="fr_715"></a><a href="#fn_712"><font size="1">3</font></a>. 4. Whenever <a name="fr_716"></a><a href="#fn_713"><font size="1">4</font></a> a share for a son is not provided by it, every one <i>has</i> so much and the wife who may be a privileged <i>one has</i> twice as much; and the share of that <i>one</i> of the sons <a name="fr_717"></a><a href="#fn_714"><font size="1">5</font></a>, <i>or</i> even the wife of a son, who is blind in both eyes, or crippled <a name="fr_718"></a><a href="#fn_715"><font size="1">6</font></a> in both feet, or maimed in both his hands, is twice as much as that <i>of one</i> who is sound.</p>  
 <p>5. And <i>it</i> is needful <i>that</i> he who was in the father's guardianship <i>shall</i> remain in guardianship, as when a father or mother is decrepit and causing awe (<i>k</i>agarîn), or <i>of</i> a nurture different from that of the guardian <a name="fr_719"></a><a href="#fn_716"><font size="1">7</font></a>--or a child of his brother or sister, or a father, or <i>one</i> <a name="fr_720"></a><a href="#fn_717"><font size="1">8</font></a> without nurture apart from him, is</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_196"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 196</font></a></p>  
 <p>without a guardian--the ready guardianship of a capable man, and the shelter <i>and</i> nourishment <i>that</i> have become inadequate <a name="fr_721"></a><a href="#fn_718"><font size="1">1</font></a> are as indispensably forth<i>coming</i> <a name="fr_722"></a><a href="#fn_719"><font size="1">2</font></a> from the possessors of wealth, of those who <i>have</i> taken the property, as that <i>taking</i> was indispensable <a name="fr_723"></a><a href="#fn_720"><font size="1">3</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>6. If <i>there</i> be no son of that <i>man</i>, but there be a daughter or wife of his, and if <i>some</i> of the <i>affairs</i> <a name="fr_724"></a><a href="#fn_721"><font size="1">4</font></a> of the man are such as render a woman not suitable for the guardianship, it is necessary to appoint a family guardian; if there be, moreover, no wife <i>or</i> daughter of his it is necessary to appoint an adopted son. 7. This--that is, when it is necessary to appoint a family guardian <i>and</i> who is the fittest, <i>and</i> when it is necessary to appoint an adopted son <i>and</i> which is the fittest--is written in the chapters <i>on</i> the question <a name="fr_725"></a><a href="#fn_722"><font size="1">5</font></a>.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_709"></a><a href="sbe18069.htm#fr_712">194:2</a> This metaphor seems to mean that property, like water, always <a name="page_195"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 195</font></a> descends until it meets with an obstruction to its downward progress in the shape of the nearest descendants, but, when once in their possession, it can again ascend (like the dammed stream) for the support of the survivors of an older generation (see § 5).</p>
