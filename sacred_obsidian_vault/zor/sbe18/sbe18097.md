 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>Pahlavi Texts, Part II (SBE18)</i>, E.W. West, tr. [1882], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER XC.</h3>  
 <p>1. <i>As to</i> the eighty-ninth question <i>and</i> reply, that which you ask is thus: Who, and how many are they who are without the religion (adînôîh) <i>but</i> are made immortal, and for what purpose is their immortality? 2. Where is the place they, each one, possess sovereignty, and <i>in</i> the place where they possess sovereignty are <i>there</i> people of the good religion of every kind, or how are they; are <i>there</i> sacred fires <a name="fr_959"></a><a href="#fn_956"><font size="1">2</font></a> and appointed worship, or how is</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_256"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 256</font></a></p>  
 <p>it; and for what purpose is each one of their sovereignties?</p>  
 <p>3. The reply is this, that the immortal rulers of the region of glory, Khvanîras <a name="fr_960"></a><a href="#fn_957"><font size="1">1</font></a>, are said <i>to be</i> seven: one is Yô<i>s</i>tô, son of Fryân  <a name="fr_961"></a><a href="#fn_958"><font size="1">2</font></a>; the Avesta name of one is Yakhmâyûs<i>a</i><i>d</i> <a name="fr_962"></a><a href="#fn_959"><font size="1">3</font></a>, son of the same Fryân; the name of one is Fradhakh<i>s</i>tŏ, son of the Khumbîks <a name="fr_963"></a><a href="#fn_960"><font size="1">4</font></a>; the name of one is Ashavazang, son of Pôrûdakhstôîh <a name="fr_964"></a><a href="#fn_961"><font size="1">5</font></a>; one is the tree opposed to harm <a name="fr_965"></a><a href="#fn_962"><font size="1">6</font></a>;</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_257"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 257</font></a></p>  
 <p>one is Gôpatshah <a name="fr_966"></a><a href="#fn_963"><font size="1">1</font></a>; <i>and</i> one is Peshyôtanû <a name="fr_967"></a><a href="#fn_964"><font size="1">2</font></a>, who is called after the <i>K</i>îtravôkŏ-mîyânŏ <a name="fr_968"></a><a href="#fn_965"><font size="1">3</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>4. The reign of Gôpatshah is over the land of Gôpatŏ <a name="fr_969"></a><a href="#fn_966"><font size="1">4</font></a>, coterminous with Aîrân-vê<i>g</i> <a name="fr_970"></a><a href="#fn_967"><font size="1">5</font></a>, on the bank (bar) of the water of the Dâîtîh <a name="fr_971"></a><a href="#fn_968"><font size="1">6</font></a>; <i>and</i> he keeps watch over the ox Hadhayã<i>s</i> <a name="fr_972"></a><a href="#fn_969"><font size="1">7</font></a>, through whom occurs the complete perfection of primitive man <a name="fr_973"></a><a href="#fn_970"><font size="1">8</font></a>. 5. The reign of Peshyôtanû is in Kangde<i>z</i> <a name="fr_974"></a><a href="#fn_971"><font size="1">9</font></a>, <i>and</i> he resides in the illustrious Kangde<i>z</i> which the noble Sîyâvash <a name="fr_975"></a><a href="#fn_972"><font size="1">10</font></a> formed through his glory, <i>he</i> who is called the erratic youth <a name="fr_976"></a><a href="#fn_973"><font size="1">11</font></a> of the illustrious Kayânians. 6. And through his powerful spirit <i>arose</i> increase of cultivation and the ruler Kaî-Khûsrôî <a name="fr_977"></a><a href="#fn_974"><font size="1">12</font></a> among the highest of the mountains in the countries of</p>

 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> Irân <i>and</i> Tûrân; the purity of the sacred fire <a name="fr_978"></a><a href="#fn_975"><font size="1">1</font></a> of great glory <i>and</i> the recital of the liturgy exist there, <i>and</i> the practice of religious rites (dînô) is provided. 7. The custom, also, of him (Peshyôtanû) and <i>his</i> companions <i>and</i> coadjutors (ham-bâr), in the appointed millenniums, is the great advancement of religion and good works in other quarters likewise <a name="fr_979"></a><a href="#fn_976"><font size="1">2</font></a>.</p>  
 <p>8. But, secondly, <i>as to</i> the whereabouts of the places which are theirs--just like his--of which <i>there</i> is no disquisition by me, this also is even owing to <i>my</i> not remembering <a name="fr_980"></a><a href="#fn_977"><font size="1">3</font></a>.</p>

 <h3 align="CENTER">Footnotes</h3>
 <p><a name="fn_956"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_959">255:2</a> Literally 'fires of Varahrân' (see Chap. XXXI, 7).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_957"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_960">256:1</a> The central one of the seven regions of the earth, which is supposed to contain all the countries best known to the Iranians, and to be as large as the other six regions put together (see Bd. XI, 2-6). The name is here corrupted into Khvanâîra<i>s</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_958"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_961">256:2</a> Av. Yôi<i>s</i>tô yô Fryananãm of Âbân Yt. 81, Fravar<i>d</i>în Yt. 120, who had to explain ninety-nine enigmas propounded to him by the evil Akhtya. In Pâz. this name is corrupted into Gô<i>s</i>t-î Fryânô, whose explanation of thirty-three enigmas propounded by Akht the wizard, and proposal of three enigmas in his turn form the subject of a Pahlavi tale published with AV. He is not included among the immortals mentioned in Bd. XXIX, 5, 6, and Dk. (see § 8, note), but is one of those specified in Byt. II, 1.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_959"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_962">256:3</a> No doubt, the Av. Ashem-yahmâi-u<i>s</i>ta ('righteousness for which be blessing') who precedes Yôi<i>s</i>tô in Fravar<i>d</i>în Yt. 120. He is the Asâm-î Yamâhu<i>s</i>t of Bd. XXIX, 5, who is said to reside in the district of the river Nâîvtâk; but he is not mentioned in Dk. The names Yakhmâyûs<i>a</i><i>d</i>, Fradhakh<i>s</i>tŏ, and Ashavazang are written in Pâzand, which accounts for their irregular spelling.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_960"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_963">256:4</a> Av. Fradhâkh<i>s</i>ti Khu<i>n</i>bya of Fravar<i>d</i>în Yt. 138. In Dk. he is said to be ruler on the Nâîvtâk waters, but in Bd. he is called Par<i>s</i>adgâ <i>H</i><i>v</i>embya residing in the plain of Pê<i>s</i>yânsaî.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_961"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_964">256:5</a> Av. Ashavazdang the Pourudhâkh<i>s</i>tiyan of Âbân Yt. 72, Fravar<i>d</i>în Yt. 112. He is the Ashavazd son of Pourudhâkh<i>s</i>t in Bd., and is said to rule in the plain of Pê<i>s</i>yânsaî in Dk.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_962"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_965">256:6</a> The many-seeded tree, said to grow in the wide-formed ocean, and also in Aîrân-vê<i>g</i>, on which the Saêna bird (simurgh) is sup-posed to sit and shake off the seeds, which are then conveyed by the bird <i>K</i>amrô<i>s</i> to the waters gathered by Tî<i>s</i>tar, who rains them down on the earth with the water; hence the growth of fresh vegetation when the rainy season commences (see Rashnu Yt. 17, Bd. XXXVI, 2, XXIX, 5, Mkh. LXII, 37-42).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_963"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_966">257:1</a> Either a title or son of Aghrêra<i>d</i>, brother of Frâsîyâ<i>v</i> of Tûr (see Bd. XXIX, 5, XXXI, 20-22). He is a righteous minotaur according to Mkh. LXII, 31-36.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_964"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_967">257:2</a> Here written Patshâyôtanû, but he is the Peshyôtanû of Chap. XXXVII, 36.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_965"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_968">257:3</a> The <i>K</i>atru-mîyân river in Kangde<i>z</i> (see Bd. XX, 31).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_966"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_969">257:4</a> Which is a non-Aryan country according to Dk., but Bd. (XXIX, 5) calls it 'the land of the S<i>a</i>ukavastân,' and Mkh. (XLIV, 24-35, LXII, 31) places Gôpatshah in Aîrân-vê<i>g</i>.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_967"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_970">257:5</a> See Chap. XXI, 2.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_968"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_971">257:6</a> Av. Dâitya, a river which flows out of Aîrân-vê<i>g</i> (see Bd. XX, 13).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_969"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_972">257:7</a> See Chap. XXXVII, 99.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_970"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_973">257:8</a> Referring either to the complete peopling of the earth by emigration on the back of this ox in ancient times, or to the immortality produced at the resurrection by tasting an elixir, of which the fat of this ox is one of the ingredients.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_971"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_974">257:9</a> A settlement east of Persia formed, or fortified, by Sîyâvash (see Bd. XXIX, 10, Byt. III, 25).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_972"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_975">257:10</a> See Chap. XXXVII, 36.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_973"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_976">257:11</a> Or kang-î raftâr may mean 'jaunty youth;' but it is evidently an attempt to account for the name Kangde<i>z</i> as 'the fortress of the kang ("youth").'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_974"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_977">257:12</a> The son of Sîyâvash (see Chap. XXXVI, 3, Bd. XXXI, 25).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_975"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_978">258:1</a> Literally 'fire of Varahrân.'</p>
 <p><a name="fn_976"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_979">258:2</a> He is expected to be summoned by the angels to restore the religious rites to the world, after the conflict of the nations in a future age (see Byt. III, 25-42).</p>
 <p><a name="fn_977"></a><a href="sbe18097.htm#fr_980">258:3</a> In the detailed account of the contents of the Sû<i>d</i>kar (or Stû<i>d</i>gar) Nask, given in the ninth book of the Dînka<i>r</i><i>d</i>, the latter part of its fifteenth fargar<i>d</i> is said to have been 'on the seven immortal rulers who are produced in the region of Khvanîras, and also about the determination of their glory, and the goodness, too, of <i>their</i> assistants and living sovereignty in both worlds. The tree opposed to harm is on Aîrân-vê<i>g</i> in the place of most excavations (frêh-nigânân?). Gôk-patŏ is in the non-Aryan countries. Peshyôtanû son of Vi<i>s</i>tâsp is in Kangde<i>z</i> of the hundred-ribbed shape (sa<i>d</i>-dandakŏ kerpîh?), in which a myriad of the exalted, who wear black marten <i>fur</i> (mûn sîyah samûr yakhsenund), are righteous listeners out of the retinue of Peshyôtanû son of Vi<i>s</i>tâsp. Frâ<i>d</i>akh<i>s</i>tŏ son of the mortal Khûmbîks, who is sovereign on the water of Nâîvtâk. Ashavazd son of Pôrû<i>d</i>akh<i>s</i>tŏ, who is sovereign over the most manifest among uplands, the plain, of Pê<i>s</i>inâs. Barâzd the causer of strife. And of the father-in-law's race (kha<i>s</i>t-tômagag?) of the famous Vi<i>s</i>tâsp is <i>he</i> who is called Kaî-Khûsrôî, who produces even an advance of thy religion of the Ma<i>z</i><i>d</i>a-worshippers, and also understands about <i>it; and</i> who gives my good practices further blessings, so that the world maintains my doings with benedictions. Perfect is the excellence of righteousness.'</p>  
 <p>In this list of the immortal rulers of ancient times, the names of Barâzd and Kaî-Khûsrôî are substituted for those of Yô<i>s</i>tô and <a name="page_259"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 259</font></a> Yakhmâyûs<i>a</i><i>d</i> in our text. Barâzd is the Ibairaz of Bd. XXIX, 6, and, possibly, the Av. Berezyar<i>s</i>ti of Fravar<i>d</i>în Yt. 101.</p>
