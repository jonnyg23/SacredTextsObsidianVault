 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><i>Pahlavi Texts, Part IV (SBE37)</i>, E.W. West, tr. [1892], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">CHAPTER XXXVII.</h3>  
 <p align="center"><i>Hûspâram Nask</i>.</p>  
 <p>1. One section <i>of</i> the seven <a name="fr_288"></a><a href="#fn_287"><font size="1">1</font></a> at the end <i>contains</i> particulars about the daily <i>food</i> of a grown-up man, a pregnant woman, her who is childless, <i>and</i> a child, as provided by law; <i>also</i> that of a shepherd's dog, a village dog, and a blood-hound; and the characteristics of these three kinds of dog.</p>  
 <p>2. About the sign of a person's conversion to the religion. 3. About association of several kinds, and one of them is that of the keepers (pa<i>d</i>ân) with the flocks (ramân), and the flocks in connection with the keepers; and of what kind is the meritoriousness of the keepers <i>of</i> those flocks, as to guardianship of every description; the happy effects of the flock, <i>and</i> those of the keeper, of every description; the advantage from this association, and whatever is on the same subject. 4. One is the association of priestly instructor (ra<i>d</i>ŏ) and pupil <a name="fr_289"></a><a href="#fn_288"><font size="1">2</font></a>, <i>and their</i> meritoriousness together; the fame of the priestly instructor for priestly instruction, <i>and</i> that of the disciple (hâvi<i>s</i>tô) for every kind of <i>learning derived</i> from the priestly instructor, and every kind that the priestly instructor <i>imparts</i> to the pupil; <i>and</i> the happy effects of the priestly instructor, of every kind, in similar matters. 5. One is the association</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_115"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 115</font></a></p>  
 <p>of ceremonial priests (ra<i>d</i>-pî<i>s</i>akânŏ), the worthiness of a man for the sacerdotal leadership, supplies for the whole of the ceremonial priests, <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject. 6. About the highest of all associations <a name="fr_290"></a><a href="#fn_289"><font size="1">1</font></a>, and about the lawful and virtuous existence of this same association, when <i>there are</i> two men in <i>a case</i> where he who is opulent is always necessary for him who is in innocence, and <i>has</i> given <i>him</i> the wealth that he asks for; or where, when the one shall commit sin, wealth is an affliction to the other; or the ownership, as to that which the one obtains, is as much even that of the other; or, on the passing away <i>of the one</i>, it is mingled with the wealth of the other; and whatever is on the same subject.</p>  
 <p>7. About the punishment of the sin of him for whom <i>one</i> lies <a name="fr_291"></a><a href="#fn_290"><font size="1">2</font></a> to him by whom provision is made, by thought or by word, <i>and</i> given to him who is worthy. 8. About a father's making a child aware of the sin at the time of the sin. 9. About the sin of taking the course of a false guide <i>and</i> exalting falsehood, <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject. 10. The sin of extorting supplies for a beast <i>of burden</i> from a lonely labouring person.</p>  
 <p>11. About important gifts to the worthy, atonement for deprival of food (atapdâ<i>d</i>ŏ-vi<i>g</i>âri<i>s</i>nîh) <a name="fr_292"></a><a href="#fn_291"><font size="1">3</font></a>, and disbursements (aûrûzdân) <i>of</i> that which is legally, and also <i>of</i> that which is derived from <i>what is</i> legally, property among impoverished (nyûrûzd) supplicants. 12. The depriver of food is he who is <i>for</i> early atonement, and <i>they</i> who severally <i>exist</i>,</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_116"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 116</font></a></p>  
 <p>through grazing <a name="fr_293"></a><a href="#fn_292"><font size="1">1</font></a> <i>and</i> bringing forth, are <i>they</i> who severally are also in loss of vitality, through deprival of the food of strength <i>and</i> intellect; <i>even</i> a powerful man is prostrated thereby; the food which is suitable as atonement for deprival of food, <i>and</i> that which is not suitable.</p>  
 <p>13. About that through which the indispensable creation <i>of</i> a debt arises, <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject. 14. Where <i>it is</i> the healing <i>of</i> the sick, the spiritual <i>debt</i> is unto the archangel Ashavahi<i>s</i>t <a name="fr_294"></a><a href="#fn_293"><font size="1">2</font></a>, <i>and</i> that which is worldly unto the physician's anteroom (dâlânakŏ).</p>  
 <p>15. About the worthiness <i>of</i> a good physician for every benefit, <i>and</i> the unworthiness <i>of</i> a bad physician for any benefit. 16. About each one of the plants <i>being</i> produced by Aûha<i>r</i>ma<i>z</i><i>d</i> for the subjugation of one disease at least. 17. About the protectiveness <i>and</i> preciousness of the profession of medicine; the advantage and reasoning thought of a physician due to the carrying on of <i>his</i> medical practice; the pleasant food, the handsome clothing, and the swift steed for a physician; and <i>his</i> wealth <i>being</i> as much as that of an average man in a house, village, community, <i>or</i> province. 18. About the diligently</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_117"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 117</font></a></p>  
 <p>remedial hand <i>of</i> the physician for the sick, opportunely mindful, yet without chastisement.</p>  
 <p>19. About the sin of a physician through handling (sû<i>d</i>akîh) and <i>having</i> spread <i>a disease</i> by walking up to the sick, because that is when <i>he would have been</i> innocent through not <i>having</i> gone. 20. About a great pestilence (s<i>ê</i><i>g</i>ŏ), <i>and</i> that which is trivial.</p>  
 <p>21. About the fee <a name="fr_295"></a><a href="#fn_294"><font size="1">1</font></a> of a physician for curing a sick <i>person</i> of disease of the whole body, <i>and</i> of each one of the members; even of him who <i>has</i> cured chieftains, both those of the lower grades <i>and</i> him who is the supreme king of kings, and so also various destitute people. 22. About the mode <i>and</i> extent of delivering up fees to a physician, after the declaration of the sick <i>person being</i> well; that is, from whom <i>comes</i> the physician's fee which is announced for the cure, <i>and</i> also that which is not announced; from whom that only which is announced for it, from whom a meal (pishôn-1), and from whom nothing whatever <i>of</i> worldly reward comes.</p>  
 <p>23. About the physician whom <i>one</i> hears <a name="fr_296"></a><a href="#fn_295"><font size="1">2</font></a> <i>and</i> asks for medical treatment. 24. About a test as to the competency of a physician; that is, how <i>it is</i> to be made, how it is when it is possible to test <i>it, and</i> how it is when it is not possible to test <i>it</i>. 25.</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_118"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 118</font></a></p>  
 <p><span class="contnote"><font size="-2" color="GREEN">[paragraph continues]</font></span> About the sin of a physician who is not tested, <i>and</i> also <i>of</i> him whom it is not possible to test, when he shall undertake the medical treatment of others, <i>and</i>, as regards a limb of any one, there is not anything which is another's test of him, <i>nor</i> even that which is not another's test of him, <i>nor</i> that which is a trial of him.</p>  
 <p>26. About how long is the duration of <i>having</i> sought a physician in Irân whereafter <i>it</i> is allowable, through not obtaining <i>one</i>, to seek <i>him</i> even from foreigners. 27. The sin of <i>having</i> sought <i>one</i> from foreigners, when <i>one can</i> obtain a physician in Irân. 28. About the fee for a foreign physician, and much else on the same subject. 29. The medical treatment of mankind, and also about the medical treatment of beasts <i>of burden and</i> cattle.</p>  
 <p>30. About the sin owing to intrusting him who is unfit for a duty. 31. About the greater suitability of a priest than <i>of</i> a disciple for duty and position; a trusty <i>person</i> is also obtaining the important <i>rather</i> than obtaining a desire for the important, and even so far as <i>being</i> a potter <i>rather</i> than an astrologer, and being careful <i>rather</i> than a potter; and the reason of it.</p>  
 <p>32. About preparing an unauthorised (<i>a</i>-dastôbar) dwelling in the locality of other persons, <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject. 33. About boundaries where <i>there</i> is a place of residence for people, and whatever is on the same subject. 34. About what description of testimony of <i>one of</i> the good religion is <i>received</i> as evidence regarding an infidel, <i>and</i> of an infidel as regards <i>one of</i> the good religion.</p>  
 <p>35. About the greatness of eminence <i>of</i> the abode of priestly authorities (ra<i>d</i>ânŏ), <i>both</i> for procedure</p>  
 <p><a name="page_119"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 119</font></a></p>  
 <p>and for petitions <a name="fr_297"></a><a href="#fn_296"><font size="1">1</font></a>; the openness of the doors of a priestly authority; the want of eminence of any one through every kind of offence to others, which is owing to his closed doors and evil eminence in every mode; <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject. 36. About the extent of splendour (lîyânŏ) and pomp-diffusing (vafsh-<i>a</i>fgânŏ) tokens from the abode of fires, <i>and</i> the arrangement as regards him who casts the allotted twigs <i>and</i> charcoal (khâr akhgar) into <i>them</i>. 37. About conveying prosperity (pa<i>d</i>îkhûîh) <a name="fr_298"></a><a href="#fn_297"><font size="1">2</font></a> to the abode of fires appropriately <i>to</i> the capability of every one.</p>  
 <p>38. About the quality (sâmân) of water oozing out (aîrî<i>d</i>ŏ) and that which is flowing in a channel (nâêv-tâk). 39. About the characteristics of specified works which are contiguous in a place between two frontiers (mar<i>z</i>ŏ).</p>  
 <p>40. About a decision as to a sheep <i>free</i> from unlawful influenceand so also <i>as to one under</i> unlawful influencewhich goes to the pasture of others with thievish intention, neglecting its own; and as to that which <i>does so</i> not with thievish intention. 41. About the quantity which <i>one has</i> to provide, in the duration of a day and night, on admitting to pasture <i>and</i> corn, in the case of an ox without defect (anâgânŏ), or <i>of</i> another kind, or a horse, or a sheep, or a goat, or a pig, or an animal of any other kind.</p>  
 <p>42. About the distance of a residence of mankind from a river flowing in a channel. 43. About the period for letting a sheep graze at pleasure in a pasture, <i>and</i> that for restraining <i>it;</i> the time for not</p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_120"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 120</font></a></p>  
 <p>cutting trees, <i>and</i> that for little slaughter of sheep. 44. About an article of clothing which is associated with defence, for fear of enemies, <i>and</i> becomes quite a good omen (sukûn) <i>among</i> mankind, <i>being</i> imperceptible <i>and</i> appropriate. 45. About a tree with stem uprooted, where <i>and</i> how <i>it is</i> allowable.</p>  
 <p>46. About a leader's causing a march of whatever kind, the people <i>being</i> in motion through fear, and they drive the sheep which are with the army on account of molestation; <i>also</i> making the sheep decide as to the pasture near to the road within reach, the pasturing <i>of</i> the first of the species of sheep, <i>and</i> letting <i>them</i> forth to pasture in succession unto the last, and the reason of it.</p>  
 <p>47. About a person who is of note <a name="fr_299"></a><a href="#fn_298"><font size="1">1</font></a> on account of wealth, <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject. 48. About this intermixture <i>of</i> with-the-stream <i>and</i> against-the-stream, with banks and without banks, and waters running <i>and</i> down-pouring (nîy<i>â</i>pân), on the road; that is, which of the waters, running <i>or</i> down-pouring, is to be earlier reverenced <i>by him</i> who is returning from the road, and the reason of it. 49. About the subordination of the disciple unto the priest, as to eating, drinking, and plenty, goodness and preciousness; <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject.</p>  
 <p>50. About that which <i>occurs</i> when foreigners come to the frontier of Irân, <i>and</i> shall do damage to Irân; <i>and</i> the frontier governors <i>and</i> fellow-champions <i>have</i> to repel the foreigners by fighting, to save the Irânian people and property which <i>were</i></p>  
 <p>
 </p>  
 <p><a name="page_121"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 121</font></a></p>  
 <p>to be made foreign; <i>and</i> whatever is on the same subject.</p>  
 <p>51. About the advantage of punishing a violent thief by the members of the assembly, that owing to reliance upon the actions <i>and</i> convictions of the ancients, that owing to forming many priestly assemblies, that owing to providing a disciple for a priest, that through passing away after <i>being</i> high-priest, that through <i>doing so</i> without <i>being</i> high-priest, <i>and</i> that of much information on similar statements prior to any other resources.</p>  
 <p>52. Perfect is the excellence <i>of</i> righteousness.</p>

 <p><a name="fn_293"></a><a href="sbe37040.htm#fr_294">116:2</a> The personification of 'perfect righteousness' (Av. ashavahi<i>s</i>ta) whose special duty is stated to be the care of fire (see Sls. I, 3) which included extensive commentaries, adapting the original Avesta statements to the altered circumstances of Sasanian times.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_295"></a><a href="sbe37040.htm#fr_296">117:2</a> Or 'satisfies' (shnâyê<i>d</i>ŏ).</p>

 <p><a name="fn_297"></a><a href="sbe37040.htm#fr_298">119:2</a> By providing fuel and other necessaries.</p>
 <p><a name="fn_298"></a><a href="sbe37040.htm#fr_299">120:1</a> Reading mûn sakhûnag. Another guess would be min nî<i>s</i>ôn-î (for nî<i>s</i>ân-î), in which case the translation would be 'a person <i>free</i> from indications relating to wealth.'</p>
