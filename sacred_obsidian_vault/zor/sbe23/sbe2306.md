 <p><a name="page_21"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 21</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">I. ORMAZD YA<i>S</i>T.</h3>  
 <p><font size="-1">The Ormazd Ya<i>s</i>t, properly so called, ends with § 23. The rest of the Ya<i>s</i>t, from § 24 to the end, is wanting in several manuscripts, and is supposed by the Parsis to be a fragment of the Bahman Ya<i>s</i>t.</font></p>  
 <p>The Ormazd Ya<i>s</i>t is exclusively devoted to an enumeration of the names of Ahura and to a laudation of their virtues and efficacy: the recitation of these names is the best defence against all dangers.</p>  
 <p>§§ 1-6. The names of Ahura Mazda are the most powerful part of the Holy Word.</p>  
 <p>§§ 7-8. The twenty names of Ahura Mazda are enumerated.</p>  
 <p>§§ 9-11. Efficacy of these names.</p>  
 <p>§§ 12-15. Another list of names.</p>  
 <p>§§ 16-19. Efficacy of Ahura's names.</p>  
 <p>§§ 20-23. Sundry formulas of invocation.</p>  
 <p>As may be seen from this summary, the subject has been treated twice over, first in §§ 1-11, and then in §§ 12-19; yet it does not appear that this Ya<i>s</i>t was formed out of two independent treatises, and it is more likely that the vague and indefinite enumeration in §§ 12-15, which interrupts so clumsily the train of ideas, is due either to an interpolation or simply to the literary deficiency of the writer himself.</p>  
 <p>The Ormazd Ya<i>s</i>t is recited every day at the Hâvan Gâh, after the morning prayer (Anquetil, Zend-Avesta, II, 143): it is well also to recite it when going to sleep and when changing one's residence (§ 17).</p>  
 <p>Speculations on the mystical powers of God's names have always been common among Orientals. The number of these names went on increasing: Dastûr Nôshîrvân wrote on the roc names of God; Dastûr Marzbân on his 125 names. With the Musulmans, Allah had 1001 names. On the names of God among the Jews, see Zeitschrift der Deutschen Morgenländischen Gesellschaft, XXXV, pp. 162, 532.</p>  
 <p>We have three native translations of this Ya<i>s</i>t; one in Pahlavi (East India Office, XII, 39, and St. Petersburg, XCIX, 39; edited by Carl Salemann), one in Persian (East India Office, XXII, 43), and one in Sanskrit (Paris, fonds Burnouf, V, 66); the last two edited in our Études Iraniennes, II, 255).</p>  
 <p><a name="page_22"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 22</font></a></p>  
 <p><font size="-1">The second part of the Ya<i>s</i>t, the so-called Bahman Ya<i>s</i>t fragment, is in a state of the utmost corruption. It is difficult to trace any connection in the ideas, yet §§ 28, 29, 30 seem to point rather clearly to the final struggle between Ormazd and Ahriman and to the annihilation of the Daêvas, and, thereby, some connection is established between this fragment and the Pahlavi Bahman Ya<i>s</i>t <a name="fr_103"></a><a href="#fn_98"><font size="1">1</font></a>, which deals with the same subject. If that correspondence be real, § 26 might refer to the beginning of the Pahlavi Bahman Ya<i>s</i>t, in which Zarathu<i>s</i>tra is shown by Ahura the times to come and the end of the world.</font></p>  
 <p>Of this fragment we have only a bad Pahlavi translation in the St. Petersburg manuscript mentioned above.</p>
