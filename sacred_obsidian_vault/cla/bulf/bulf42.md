 <h2 align="CENTER">PROVERBIAL EXPRESSIONS</h2>
 
 
                         No. 1<br>
             MATERIEM superabat opus.- Ovid.<br>
             The workmanship surpassed the material.<br>
 
                         No. 2.<br>
                 Facies non omnibus una,<br>
       Nec diversa tamen, qualem decet esse sororum.- Ovid.<br>
 <p>Their faces were not all alike, nor yet unlike, but such as those of
 sisters ought to be.
 
 </p>
                         No. 3.<br>
            Medio tutissimus ibis.- Ovid.<br>
            You will go most safely in the middle.<br>
 
                         No. 4.<br>
      Hic situs est Phaeton, currus auriga paterni,<br>
      Quem si non tenuit, magnis tamen excidit ausis.- Ovid.<br>
 <p>Here lies Phaeton, the driver of his father's chariot, which if he
 failed to manage, yet he fell in a great undertaking.
 
 </p>
                         No. 5.<br>
               Imponere Pelio Ossam.- Virgil.<br>
               To pile Ossa upon Pelion.<br>
 
                         No. 6.<br>
          Timeo Danaos et dona ferentes.- Virgil.<br>
          I fear the Greeks even when they offer gifts.<br>
 
                         No. 7.<br>
           Non tali auxilio nec defensoribus istis<br>
           Tempus eget.- Virgil.<br>
     Not such aid nor such defenders does the time require.<br>
 
                         No. 8.<br>
        Incidit in Scyllam, cupiens vitare Charybdim.<br>
        He runs on Scylla, wishing to avoid Charybdis.<br>
 
                         No. 9.<br>
        Sequitur patrem, non passibus aequis.- Virgil.<br>
        He follows his father with unequal steps.<br>
 
                         No. 10.<br>
    Monstrum horrendum, informe, ingens, cui lumen ademptum.- Virgil.<br>
  A horrible monster, misshapen, vast, whose only eye had been put out.
 
                         No.11.<br>
         Tantaene animis coelestibus irae?- Virgil.<br>
         In heavenly minds can such resentments dwell?<br>
 
                         No. 12.<br>
       Haud ignara mali, miseris succurrere disco.- Virgil.<br>
 <p>Not unacquainted with distress, I have learned to succour the
 unfortunate.
 
 </p>
                         No. 13.<br>
      Tros, Tyriusve mihi nullo discrimine agetur.- Virgil.<br>
    Whether Trojan or Tyrian shall make no difference to me.<br>
 
                         No. 14.<br>
     Tu ne cede malis, sed contra audentior ito.- Virgil.<br>
 <p>Yield thou not to adversity, but press on the more bravely.
 
 </p>
                         No. 15.<br>
                         Facilis descensus Averni;<br>
          Noctes atque dies patet atri janua Ditis;<br>
          Sed revocare gradum, superasque evadere ad auras,<br>
          Hoc opus, hic labor est.- Virgil.<br>
 <p>The descent to Avernus is easy; the gate of Pluto stands open
 night and day; but to retrace one's steps and return to the upper air,
 that is the toil, that the difficulty.
 
 </p>
                         No. 16.<br>
            Uno avulso non deficit alter.- Virgil.<br>
           When one is torn away another succeeds.<br>
 
                         No. 17.<br>
      Quadrupedante putrem sonitu quatit ungula campum.- Virgil.<br>
 <p>Then struck the hoofs of the steeds on the ground with a four-footed
 trampling.
 
 </p>
                         No. 18.<br>
         Sternitur infelix alieno vulnere, coelumque<br>
         Adspicit et moriens dulces reminiseitur Argos.- Virgil.<br>
 <p>He falls, unhappy, by a wound intended for another; looks up to
 the skies, and dying remembers sweet Argos.
