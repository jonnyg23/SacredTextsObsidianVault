 <p align="CENTER"><font size="-1" color="GREEN"><a href="index.htm"><img src="img/tease.jpg"></a><br><i>Ancilla to the Pre-Socratic Philosophers</i>, by Kathleen Freeman, [1948], at sacred-texts.com</font></p><hr>  <h3 align="center">70. MÊTRODÔRUS OF CHIOS</h3>  
 <p><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Mêtrodôrus</span> of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Chios</span> lived at some time during the fourth century B.C. He wrote a book <i>On Natural Science</i>; and perhaps works on <i>The History of Ionia</i> and <i>The Trojan War</i>.</p>  
 <p>1. None of us knows anything, not even whether we know or do not know, nor do we know whether not knowing and knowing exist, nor in general whether there is anything or not.</p>  
 <p><a name="page_121"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 121</font></a></p>  
 <p>2. Everything exists which anyone perceives.</p>  
 <p>3-5. (<i>From a work on 'The Trojan War'</i>).</p>  
 <p>3. (<i>Marsyas discovered the reed pipe among the Celaenae</i>).</p>  
 <p>4. (<i>Divine visitants command that an acropolis shall be built for the Spartan king, in which he is to live</i>).</p>  
 <p>5. (<i>Grammatical comment on Homer, ascribed to 'Metrodorus', who may be Metrodorus of Lampsacus</i>).</p>  
 <p>6. (<i>History of Ionia: the people of Smyrna, who are Aeolians by origin, sacrifice a black bull to Boubrôstis: they cut it up and burn it, hide and all</i>).</p>
