 <p><a name="page_140"><font size="1" color="GREEN">p. 140</font></a></p>  <h3 align="center">84. PRODICUS OF CEOS</h3>  
 <p><span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Prodicus</span> of <span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Ceos</span> lived in the latter half of the fifth century B.C.</p>  
 <p>He wrote on correct terminology, and was credited with a book <i>On Nature</i>, or <i>On the Nature of Man</i>. He also wrote exercises on set themes, meant for display, one of which, The Choice of Heracles, was paraphrased by Xenophon in the <i>Memorabilia</i>, and mentioned by Plato in the <i>Symposium</i>.</p>  
 <p>1. (<span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Scholiast</span> on Aristophanes, Clouds: <i>The book describing the Choice of Heracles is called 'Horae'</i>).</p>  
 <p>2. (<span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Xenophon</span>, <i>Memorabilia</i>, II.1.21: <i>The Choice of Heracles. Heracles, having come to the time when the young choose their way of life, is confronted by two women, Virtue and Vice, who set before him the rival claims of the way of pleasure and the way of honourable toil</i>).</p>  
 <p>3. (<i>Title: 'On the Nature of Things'</i>).</p>  
 <p>4. (<span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Galen</span>: <i>Prodicus in his book 'On the Nature of Man' called the heated and as it were overcooked element in the bodily humours the 'phlegm', and the mucous discharge which is universally named 'phlegm' he called 'blenna', slime'</i>).</p>  
 <p>5. (<i>Things from which benefits to human life have been derived have come to be considered deities, such as Demeter and Dionysus</i>).</p>  
 <p>6. (<i>Sophists are</i>) on the borderline between the philosopher and the statesman.</p>  
 <p>7. Desire when doubled is love, love when doubled is madness.</p>  
 <p><i>Doubtful</i></p>  
 <p>8. (<span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Ps.-Plato</span>, <i>Eryxias</i> 397D: <i>Prodicus said that things were good or bad according to their users, for example, wealth</i>).</p>  
 <p>9. (<span style="font-variant:small-caps;">Ps.-Plato</span>, <i>Axiochus</i> 366B: <i>Prodicus teaches nothing free</i>).</p>  
 <p><i>Spurious</i></p>  
 <p>10. Fire is the best of the perfumes.</p>  
 <p>11. Milk is best if one draw it actually from the female.</p>
