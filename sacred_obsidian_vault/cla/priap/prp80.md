 <h1 align="CENTER">80</h1>
 <h3 align="CENTER">To Priapus</h3>
 <p>Priape, quod sis fascino gravis tento,<br>
 quod exprobravit hanc tibi suo versu<br>
 poeta noster, erubescere hoc noli:<br>
 non es poeta sarcinosior nostro.</p>
 <p>Although with yard distent (Priapus!) weighted<br>
 (Wherewith our poet did reprove thee here<br>
 In verse), on no wise deign thereat to blush;<br>
 Thou be not heavier than our poet hung.</p>
 <p>Priapus, though thou mayst be weighty with turgid <i>fascinum</i>, albeit our poet in his verses has cast this in thy teeth, blush not for it. Thou art not more heavily hung than is that poet of ours.</p>
 <font size="2"><p>[1. The word <i>fossis</i> (ditch) being plural in the original, Scaliger suggests that Labdace had been overworking both in anus and coynte.]</p>
