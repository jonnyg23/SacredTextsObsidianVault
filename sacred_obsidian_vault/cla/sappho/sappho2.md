 <center><h1>The Poems of Sappho, Part III</h1></center>
 
 <center><h1><a name="44">44</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Ge'llws paidofilwte'ra.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>More fond of children than Gello.</p>
 
 <p>Zenobius, about A.D. 130, quotes this as a proverb.
 The ghost of Gello was said by the Lesbians to pursue
 and carry off young children.
 </p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="45">45</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Ma'la dh` kekorhme'nas Go'rgws.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Very weary of Gorgo.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Choeroboscus about A.D. 600 to show the Aeolic
 genitive with <tt>-ws</tt>. Gorgo is mentioned by Maximus
 Tyrius with Andromeda as being friends of Sappho.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="46">46</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 E?'gw d? e?pi` malða'kan tu'lan spole'w me'lea.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>But upon a soft cushion I dispose my limbs</p>
 
 <p>From Herodian.</p>
 
 <p>This is a good example of the choice of words which
 combine meaning and sound poetically.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="47">47</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Kh^ d? a?mbrosi'as me`n kra'thr e?ke'krato,
 ?Erma^s d? e?'len o?'lpin ðe'ois oi?noxo'hsai.
 kh^noi d? a?'pa pa'ntes karxh'sia t? h?^xon
 ka'leibon a?ra'santo de` pa'mpan e?'sla
 twj ga'mbrwj.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>And there the bowl of ambrosia was mixed and Hermes
 took the ladle to pour out for the gods; and then all held
 goblets and made libation, and wished good fortune to the
 bridegroom.</p>
 
 <p>Athenaeus quotes this fragment in two portions in different
 places. Lachmann first joined the two parts. The poem was
 evidently one of the Epithalamia.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="48">48</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 De'duke men a? sela'nna
 kai` Plhï'ades, me'sai de`
 nu'ktes pa'ra d? e?'rxet? w?'ra,
 e?'gw de` mo'na kateu'dw.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>The moon has set, and the Pleiades; it is midnight, the
 time is going by and I recline alone.</p>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 The sinking moon has left the sky,
 The Pleiades have also gone.
 Midnight comes--and goes, the hours fly
 And solitary still, I lie.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 The Moon has left the sky,
 Lost is the Pleiads' light;
      It is midnight,
      And time slips by,
 But on my couch alone I lie.
              <i>J. A. Symonds</i>, 1883.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>This singularly beautiful fragment is quoted by Hephaestion
 as an example of metre. With the "Hymn to Aphrodite" it
 was the first portion of the Poems of Sappho to be printed
 in 1554.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="49">49</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Plh'rhs me`n e?fai'net? a? sela'nna
 ai? d? w?s peri` Bw^mon e?staðhsan.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>The moon rose full, and as around an altar, stood the women.</p>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Now rose the moon, full and argentine,
 While round stood the maidens, as at a shrine.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Quoted by Hephaestion as an example of the metre known as the
 Ionic <i>a majore</i> trimeter brachycatalectic. Poetically
 the figure is a fine one, and shows Sappho's wonderful power of
 visualizing a scene in a few unerringly chosen words. The moon
 and its light had a great attraction for her, as a number of
 fragments shows.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="50">50</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Krh'ssai nu' pot? w?^d? e?mmele'ws po'dessin
 w?rxeu^nt? a?pa'lois a?mf? epo'enta Bw^mon
 po'as te'pen a?'nðos ma'lakon ma'teisai.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Thus sometimes, the Cretan women, tender footed, dance
 in measure round the fair altar, crushing the fine bloom of
 the grass.</p>
 
 <p>From Hephaestion as an example of metre. Blass thinks that this
 and the preceeding framgment belong together. The whole is another
 example of the delicate imagery of Sappho.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="51">51</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 A?'bra dhu?^te paxh'aj spo'laj a?llo'man.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Then lightly, in an enfolding garment I sprang.</p>
 
 <p>From Herodian as a specimen of metre. It may not be by Sappho.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="52">52</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Fai^si dh` pota Lh'dan u?akinði'nwn
 [u?p? a?nðe'wn] pepukadme'non
 eu?'rhn w?'ion.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>They say that Leda once found an egg under the hyacinths.</p>
 
 <p>From the "Etymologicum Magnum." It is uncertain
 what flower the Greeks described by the word "hyacinth."
 In this case the iris may be meant.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="53">53</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 O?fða'lmois de` me'lais nu'ktos a?'wros.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>And dark-eyed Sleep, child of Night.</p>
 
 <p>From the "Etymologicum Magnum."</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="54">54</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Xrusofa'h ðera'painan 'Afrodi'tas.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>The handmaiden of Aphrodite, shining like gold.</p>
 
 <p>In a manuscript of Philodemas about 60 B.C., found at
 Herculaneum, in which it is said that Sappho thus addresses
 <tt>Peiðw'</tt>. There is some doubt about this as the
 manuscript is defective.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="55">55</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 E?'xei me`n Androme'da ka'lan a?moi'ban.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Andromeda has a fair reward.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="56">56</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Psa'pfoi ti' ta`n polu'olbon A?fro'ditan;
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Sappho, why [celebrate or worship] most happy Aphrodite?</p>
 
 <p>Both of these are quoted by Hephaestion.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="57">57</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Deu^te' nun a?'brai Xa'rites, kalli'komoi' te Moi^sai.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Come now gentle Graces, and fair-haired Muses.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Hepaestion, Attilius Fortunatianus, and Servius as an example of the choriambic tetrameter used
 by Sappho.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="58">58</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Pa'rðenon a?du'fwnon.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>A sweet-voiced maiden.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Attilius, about the fifth century A.D.
 
 </p><hr>
 <center><h1><a name="59">59</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Katðna'skei Kuðe'rh?, a?'bros A?'dwnis, ti' ke ðei^men,
 Kattu'ptesðe ko'rai kai` katerei'kesðe xi'twnas.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Gentle Adonis is dying, O Cythera, what shall we do?<br>
 Beat your breasts, O maidens, and rend your garments.</p>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Gentle Adonis wounded lies, dying, dying.
 What message, O Cythera, dost thou send?
 Beat, beat your white breasts, O ye weeping maidens,
 And in wild grief your mourning garments rend.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Quoted by Hephaestion and presumed to be written by Sappho
 from a passage in Pausanias.</p>
 
 <p>The reverbrating beat of the repetitions of the letter <tt>k</tt>
 is very remarkable.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="60">60</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 O?' to`n A?'dwnin.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>O for Adonis.</p>
 
 
 <p>Quoted by Marius Plotinus about A.D. 600. It appears to be the
 refrain of an ode.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="61">61</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 E?'lðont? e?ks o?ra'nw porfuri'an [e?'xonta]
      perðe'menon xla'mun.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Coming from heaven, clad in a purple mantle.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Pollux about A.D. 180 to illustrate Sappho's use
 of he word <tt>xlamu's</tt>, which she is said to be the first to use.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="62">62&gt;A</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Brodopa'xees a?'gnai Xa'rites, deu^te Dios ko'rai.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Come rosy-armed Graces, virgin daughters of Zeus.</p>
 
 <p>The Idyll on a Distaff by Theocritis, according to the
 argument before it, was written in the metre of this fragment.
 Philostratus, about A.D. 220, refers to this as indicating
 Sappho's love for the rose.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="63">63</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 ...O? d? A?'reus fai^si ken A?'faiston a?'gnh Bi'aj.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>But Ares said he would forcibly drag Hephaestus.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="64">64</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 ---- Polla` d? a?na'riðma
 poth'ria kalai'fis.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Innumerable drinking cups thou drainest.</p>
 
 <p>From Athenaeus.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="65">65</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Katða'noisa de` kei'seai po'ta, kwu? mnamosu'na se'ðen
 e?'sset? ou?'te to't? ou?'t? u'?steron. ou? ga`r pede'xeis bro'down
 tw^n e?k Pieri'as a?ll? a?fa'nhs kh?n' ?Ai^da do'mois
 foita'seis ped? a?mau'rwn ne'kuwn e?kpepotame'na.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>But thou shalt ever lie dead nor shall there be any remembrance
 of thee then or ever, for thou hast none of the roses of
 Pieria; but thou shalt wander unnoticed, even in the houses
 of Hades, flitting among the shadowy dead.</p>
 
 <p>Forever shalt thou lie dead, nor shall there be any remembrance
 of thee now or hereafter, for never has thou had any of the roses
 of Pieria; but thou shalt wander, eternally unregarded in the
 houses of Hades, flitting among the insubstantial shades.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Stobaeus about A.D. 500 as addressed to a woman
 of no education. Plutarch also quotes this fragment, twice in fact,
 once as if written to a rich woman, and again when he says that 
 the crown of roses was assigned to the Muses, for he remembers
 that Sappho had said these same words to some uneducated woman.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="66">66</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Ou?d? i?'an doki'moimi prosi'doisan fa'os a?li'w
 e?'ssesðai sofi'an pa'rðenon e?is ou?de'na pw xro'non toiau'tan.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>I think that no maiden whall ever see the sunlight, who
 shall have thy wisdom.</p>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 No maiden, I think, more wise than thou
 Shall ever see the sun.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Quoted by Chrysippus, and may be part of the preceding poem.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="67">67</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Ti's d? a?groiw^ti's toi ðe'lgei no'on,
 ou?k e?pistame'na ta` bra'ke? e?'lkhn
      e?pi' tw^n sfu'rwn;
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>What rustic girl bewitches thee who knows not how to draw
 her dress about her ankles?</p>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 What rustic girl bewitches thee,
 Who cannot even draw
 Her garments neat as they should be,
 Her ankles roundabout?
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Athenaeus and others quote these lines.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="68">68</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 H?'rwn e?ksedi'daks? ek Gua'rwn ta`n tanusi'dromon.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Hero of Gyara, that swift runner, I taught.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Choeroboscus to show an Aeolic form of the accusative.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="69">69</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 A?'lla' tis ou?k e?'mmi paligko'twn
 o?'rgan, a?ll? a?ba'khn ta`n fre'n? e?'xw.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>I am not of a malign nature but have a calm temper.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted in the "Etymologicum Magnum" to show the
 meaning of <tt>a?ba'khs</tt>, "innocent", "unsophisticated."
 
 </p><hr>
 <center><h1><a name="70">70</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Au?ta`r o?rai^ai stefanhplo'keun.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Then sweet maidens wove garlands.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by the Scholiast upon the "Thermophoriazusae"
 of Aristophanes to show that the weaving of floral garlands
 is a sign of being in love.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="71">71</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 ----- Su' te ka?'mos ðero'pwn E?'ros.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Thou and my servant, Eros.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Maximus Tyrius.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="72">72</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 ?All? ?'ewn fi'los a?'mmin [a?'llo]
 le'xos a?'rnusw new'teron
 ou? ga`r tla'som? e?'gw ksunoi'khn
 newj g? e?'ssa geraitera.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>For if thou lovest us, choose another and a younger spouse,
 for I will not endure to live with thee, old woman with young man.</p>
 
 <p>From the anthology of Stobaeus.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="73">73</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Eu?morfote'ra Mnascidi'ka ta^s a?pa'las Guri'nnws.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>More shapely is Mnasidica, than gentle Gyrinno.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Hephaestion as an example of metre.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="74">74</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Acarote'ras ou?'gdam? e?p w?^ p?a'nna seðen tu'xoica.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>One more scornful than thee, O Eranna, I have never found.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Hephaestion. The reading is doubtful.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="75">75</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Su` de` stefa'nois, a Di'ka perðe'sað? e?ra'tais fo'baisin,
 o?'rpakas a?nh'toio sun r?rais? a?pa'laisi xe'pcin,
 e?ga'nðesin e?'k ga`r pe'letai kai` xa'ritos makaira^n
 ma^llon prote'rhn, a?sterfanw'toisi d? a?pystere'fontai.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Do thou, O Dica, set garlands upon thy lovely hair,
 weaving sprigs of dill with thy delicate hands; for those
 who wear fair blossoms may surely stand first, even in the
 presence of Goddesses who look without favour upon those
 who come ungarlanded.</p>
 
 <p>Athenaeus quotes this fragment, saying that according to
 Sappho those who approach the gods should wear garlands,
 as beautiful things are acceptable to them.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="76">76</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 E?'gw de` fi'lhm? a?brosu'nan, kai` moi to` la'mpron
 e?'ros a?eli'w kai` to` ka'lon le'logxen.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>I love refinement and for me Love has the splendour and
 beauty of the sun.</p>
 
 <p>From Athenaeus.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="77">77</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Ka`m me'n te tu'lan kaspole'w.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>And down I set the cushion.</p>
 <p>From Herodian.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="78">78</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 O? plou^tos a?'neu seu^ g? a?re'ta c?t? ou?k a?si'nhs pa'roikos,
 [h` d e?ks a?mfote'rwn kra^sis eu?daimoni'as e?'xei to a?'kron.]
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Wealth without thee, Worthiness is no safe neighbor,
 [but the mixture of both is the height of happiness].</p>
 
 <p>From the Scholiast on Pindar. The second line is apparently

 Auta de` su' Kallio'pa.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>And thou thyself, Calliope.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Hephaestion when discussing a metre of Archilochus.
 
 </p><hr>

 </pre>
 </blockquote>

 <center><h1><a name="81">81</a></h1></center>

 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Hither now, ye Muses, leaving golden [surroundings].</p><p>

 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="82">82</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 E?'sti moi ka'la pa'is xrusi'oisin a?nðe'moisin
 e?mfe'rhn e?'xoisa mo'rfan, Klh^is a?gapa'ta,
 a?nti ta^s e?'gw ou?de` Ludi'an pai^san ou?d? e?'rannan.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>I have a fair daughter with a form like golden flowers,

 Like a golden flower she seems to me.
 Far more than all Lydia, her do I love,
 Or Lesbos shimmering in the sea.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Quoted and commented upon by Hephaestion.</p>

 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="85">85</a></h1></center>

 <pre>
 Ti' me Pandi'onis w?^ p?'anna xeli'dwn;
 </pre>
 </blockquote>

 </p><hr>
 <center><h1><a name="86">86</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 A?mfi` d? a?'brois lasi'ois eu?^ ve pu'kassen.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>She wrapped herself well in gossamer garments.</p>
 
 <p>Pollux says that the line refers to finely woven linen.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="87">87</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Glu'keia ma^ter, ou?' toi dy'namai kre'khn to`n i?'ston,
 po'ðwj da'meisa pai^dos bradi'nan di? A?fro'ditan.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>My sweet mother, broken by soft Aphrodite's spell,
 longing for a youth, I can no more weave the cloth.</p><p>
 
 </p><blockquote>
 <pre>
 My sweet mother! Fair Aphrodite's spell
 Has from me sense and reason all bereft,
 And, yearning for that dear beloved youth,
 No longer can I see the warp or weft.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Quoted by Hephaestion as an example of metre.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="88">88</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 I?'psoi dh` to` me'laðron,
      U?mh'naon
 a?e'rrete te'ktones a?'ndres,
      U?mh'naon
 ga'mbros e?'rxetai i?^sos A'?reuï,
      [U?mh'naon]
 andros mega'lo po'lu mei'zwn
      [U?mh'naon]
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Raise high the roof beams, Workmen!
      Hymenaeus!
 Like Ares comes the bridgroom!
      Hymenaeus!
 Taller than all tall men!
      Hymenaeus!
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Quoted by Hephaestion as an example of mes-hymnic poem.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="89">89</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Pe'rroxos w?s o?'t? a?'oidos o? Le'sbois a?lloda'poisin.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Towering like the singer of Lesbos among men of other lands.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Demetrius about A.D. 150. It is possible that
 Terpander is meant, but the line may be merely a reference to
 Lesbian poets in general.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="90">90</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Oi?^on to` gluku'malon e?reu'ðetai a?'krwj e?p? u?'sdwj
 a?'kron e?p? a?krota'twj lela'ðonto de` malodro'pnes,
 ou? ma`n e?klela'ðont?, a?ll? ou?k e?du'nant? e?pi'kesðai.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>As the sweet apple blushes on the end of the bough, the very
 end of the bough which gatherers missed, nay, missed not,
 but could not reach.</p>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 At the end of the bough--its uttermost end,
 Missed by the harvesters, ripens the apple,
 Nay, not overlooked, but far out of reach,
      So with all best things.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Quoted by the Scholiast on Hermogenes and elsewhere.
 The "sweet-apple" to which Sappho refers was probably the
 result of a a graft of apple on quince.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="91">91</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Oi?'an ta`n u?a'kinðon e?n ou?'resi poi'menes a?'ndres.
 po'ssi katastei'boisi, xamai d? e?piporfu'rei a?'nðos.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>As on the hills the shepherds trample the larkspur (?)
 under foot and the flower lies empurpling in decay on the ground.</p>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 O'er the hills the heedless shepherd,
   Heavy footed, plods his way;
 Crushed behind him lies the larkspur,
   Soon empurpling in decay.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Quoted by Demetrius, who comments on the ornament and beauty of
 the lines.  Bergk was the first to assign the lines to Sappho.
 The last three words contain a picture of a crushed flower decaying
 on the ground, which would perhaps be impossible to put in so
 few words in any language but Greek. The Greek word <tt>u?a'kinðos</tt>
 does not mean the flower which at the present day is called "hyacinth".
 The Greek name was applied to several flowers of which one was
 almost certainly the larkspur, and another, as noted elsewhere,
 the iris.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="92">92</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Ve'spere, pa'nta fe'rwn, o?'sa fai'nolis e?ske'das? agws,
 fe'reis oi?'n, fe'reis ai?^ga, fe'reis a?'pu mate'ri pai^da.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Evening, thou that bringst all that bright morning
 scattered, thou bringst the sheep, the goat, and the child
 back to its mother.</p>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Hail, gentle Evening, that bringst back
 All things that bright morning hath beguiled.
 Thou bringst the lamb, thou bringst the kid,
 And to its mother, her drowsy child.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>From the "Etymologicum Magnum," where the meaning
 of <tt>au?'ws</tt> ("dawn") is discussed. The beauty of the
 fragment needs no emphasising.</p>

 A?ïpa'rðenos e?'ssomai.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>

 <p>From a manuscript in Paris, edited by Cramer.</p>

 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="95">95</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Ðurw'rwj po'des e?ptoro'guioi
 ta` de` sa'mbala pempebo'na,
 pi'suggoi de` de'k? e?ksepo'nasan.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>To the door-keeper, feet seven fathoms long, and sandals
 of five bulls' hides, work for ten cobblers.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Hephaestion as a specimen of metre.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="96">96</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 O?'lbie ga'mbre, coi` me`n dh` ga'mos, w?s a?'rao
 e?ktete'lest? e?'xeis de` pa'rðenon, a?'n a?'rao.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Happy bridegroom! Now has come thy wedding as thou
 wished, and thou hast the maiden of thy desire.</p>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Thou happy bridegroom! Now has dawned
   That day of days supreme,
 When in thine arms thou'lt hold at last
   The maiden of thy dream.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>From Hepaestion.</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="97">97</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Melli'xios d' e?p? i?mme'rtwj ke'xutai prosw'powj.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>And a sweet expression spreads over her fair face.</p>
 
 <p>From Hepaestion. Compare Catullus, "Mellitos oculos."
 and "Pulcher es neque te Venus negligit."</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="98">98</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 O? me`n ga`r ka'los, o?'sson i?'dhn, pe'letai [a?'gaðos]
 o? de` ka?'gaðos au?'tika kai` ka'los e?'ssetai.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>He who is fair to look upon is good, and he who is good,
 will soon be fair also.</p>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 He should be good who is fair of face,
 And he will be fair whose soul has grace.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Galen, writing about A.D. 160, says: "It is better
 therefore, knowing as we do that youthful beauty is like the
 flowers of spring, its allurement lasting but a short time,
 to agree with the Lesbian poetess, and to believe Solon when
 he points out the same."</p>
 
 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="99">99</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 H?^r? e?'ti parðeni'as epiba'llomai;
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Do I still long for maidenhood?</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Apollonius to show the Aeolic form <tt>h?^ra</tt>,
 the interrogative particle <tt>a?^ra</tt>, and also as a specimen
 of metre.</p>

 <center><h1><a name="100">100</a></h1></center>

 Xai'roisa hu'mfa, xaire'tw d? o? ga'mbros.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>

 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 Ti'wj, s?, w?^ fi'le ga'mbre, ka'lws e?'ika'sw;

 Ou? ga`r h?^n a?te'ra pai^s, w?^ ga'mbre, toau'ta.

 <hr>
 <center><h1><a name="104">104</a></h1></center>
 
 <blockquote>
 <pre>
 A. Parðeni'a, parðeni'a, poi^ me li'pois? a?poi'xhj;
 B. Ou?ke'ti h?'ksw pro`s ce', ou?ke'ti h?'ksw.
 </pre>
 </blockquote>
 
 <p>Maidenhood, maidenhood, whither art thou gone from me?<br>
 Never, O, never again, shall I return to thee.</p>
 
 <p>Quoted by Demetrius, to show the beauty of Sappho's style,

 <center><h1><a name="107">107</a></h1></center>

 Mh't? e?'moi me'li mh'te me'lissa.
